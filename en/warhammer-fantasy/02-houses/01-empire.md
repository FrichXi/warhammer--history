# 01 · The Empire

## Overview

The Empire of Man is the largest and strongest human nation in the Old World, and the first line of defence against Chaos. Founded by Sigmar in the year 1 IC, it endured for over two and a half millennia — through schism, civil war, plague, Greenskin invasions, the Vampire Wars, the Storm of Chaos — and crumbled at last in the End Times. At its heart lay the Elector Count system: a fragile equilibrium that granted the provinces autonomy while just barely sustaining central authority. Its religion was a dual order split between the cults of Sigmar and Ulric. Its armies were built on state troop halberdiers, knightly orders, handgunners, and steam tanks. Its magical tradition was forged by Teclis of the High Elves, who tamed the raw energies of Chaos into the eight Winds of Magic and institutionalised them in the Colleges of Magic. The capital, Altdorf, sits upon the River Reik; upon the imperial throne rests the legendary warhammer Ghal Maraz — which only a true Emperor can lift.

## Origin: Sigmar's Unification

The root of the Empire is Sigmar Heldenhammer himself.

Sigmar was born the son of an Unberogen chieftain, ripped prematurely from his mother's womb by an Orc raid, the cord wrapped round his neck — an ill omen. Yet he grew into a monstrous warrior. As a youth he killed a giant boar with his bare hands. After his father fell in battle, Sigmar cut a one-man path through an entire Orc battle line and was acclaimed king by his tribe. Then he infiltrated a Greenskin camp and rescued the Dwarf High King Kurgan Ironbeard from captivity. In gratitude the Dwarfs gifted him the rune-hammer Ghal Maraz. That hammer became the symbol of imperial authority — no man who cannot lift it may claim the throne.

Over the next decade and more, Sigmar walked a three-legged path of marriage, alliance, and conquest to forge the twelve scattered human tribes of the central Old World into a single nation. The Teutogens he fought into submission. He married the queen of the Asoborns. The Brigundians and Merogens he brought in through diplomacy. The Thuringians he bonded with while they held the line against Nurgle together. The Udoses he stood beside against Chaos. In 1 IC, after the Battle of Black Fire Pass, the chieftains of the twelve tribes became the first twelve Elector Counts, and Ar-Ulric himself placed the crown upon Sigmar's head. The Empire was born.

But Sigmar's departure fifty years into his reign planted a fuse that would smoulder for two millennia. His cousin Geronius secretly worshipped Nurgle and sought to spread pestilence across the Empire. Sigmar slew him with his own hand — but a blade of Nurgle pierced his shoulder, leaving a wound that never healed. Knowing Chaos had tainted him, Sigmar summoned the Elector Counts, announced his abdication, named no heir, took Ghal Maraz, and walked east alone into the Worlds Edge Mountains, vanishing among the peaks. For the next two and a half thousand years, the Empire lived under the legend that he might return at any moment — and waited in vain for a legitimate blood heir.

## Deeds

### The Elector Counts, the Emperor, and the Two Faiths

The Empire is not a centralised state. It is best understood as a loose federation — the Emperor is elected, not born.

The Electoral Council holds fifteen votes: one for each of the ten provincial Elector Counts, two for the Grand Theogonist of the Cult of Sigmar, one for the Ar-Ulric of the Cult of Ulric, and one for the Elder of the Moot (the Halfling autonomous region). The Elector Count of Talabecland has at various points in history wielded an additional weighted vote. Upon the death of each Emperor, the Electors convene behind closed doors in Altdorf — not always with civil ballots; sometimes with daggers and gold.

The dual religious settlement of Sigmarite and Ulrican cults forms the deepest substrate of imperial politics. The Cult of Sigmar is rooted in the Empire's centre and south, with Altdorf as its heart. The Cult of Ulric holds sway in the north, with Middenheim as the seat of the White Wolf. The two faiths have rarely coexisted in peace. Sigmar is the god of southern civilisation and imperial authority; Ulric is the god of the north — war, wolves, winter. The Empire's most stable periods tend to coincide with the moments the two cults reached an understanding; every time religion tore itself open, the Empire tore with it.

Beyond this, the Empire has law and roads. Sigmar left behind the first Imperial Code and the great Imperial Roads that crisscross the nation — the physical hardware of governance. Two and a half thousand years of civil war, plague, and invasion have patched and re-patched that hardware. Altdorf maintains an official called the Chancellor of the Roads, whose sole duty is road maintenance — one of the most thankless posts in the Empire. The roads are never finished, the budget is never enough, and armies are forever fighting each other at both ends of them.

### Army of the Empire: Infantry, Knights, Gunpowder, and Magic

The armies of the Empire are a patchwork monstrosity in the Old World.

The baseline regular force is the State Troops, raised and maintained by each provincial Elector Count. Their equipment is far from fine — a combination of halberdiers, swordsmen, and handgunners, reliant on formation discipline and numbers. Armour is mostly half-plate and feathered hats: decorative to the eye, but an Orc axe will still cleave through it.

Above them stand the Knightly Orders. The most famous are the Knights of the Blazing Sun, the Knights Panther, the Knights of the White Wolf, and the Knights of Sigmar's Holy Order. Some are more esoteric — such as the Knights of Morr, who specialise in destroying the Undead. Knights are largely noble-born, heavily armoured, well-mounted, and when charging they form a wall of steel. But an Empire knight is not on the same level as a Bretonnian Grail Knight — Empire knights fight on morale and combined arms; Bretonnian Grail Knights are superhuman warriors blessed by a goddess.

Gunpowder is the Empire's trump card. Handgunners firing ranked volleys can turn an Orc warband into a sieve in an afternoon — provided the halberdier front rank holds. What truly gives the Empire fire superiority over its neighbours is the Steam Tank, rolling out of the forges of Nuln. A Steam Tank is essentially a cannon mounted inside a steam-driven iron can, the product of Dwarf-taught engineering fused with human madness. At its peak the entire Empire fielded no more than a dozen — a single tank costs as much to build as a regiment to equip, and the driver is very likely to be scalded to death by the boiler.

The founding of the Colleges of Magic was a watershed in imperial military history. In 2304 IC, after Magnus the Pious defeated the great Chaos incursion, he established the Imperial Colleges of Magic in Altdorf with the help of the High Elf archmage Teclis. Teclis split Chaos magic into eight "Winds" — Aqshy (Fire), Azyr (Heavens), Chamon (Metal), Ghyran (Life), Hysh (Light), Ghur (Beasts), Ulgu (Shadow), Shyish (Death) — one Wind per College, each training specialised wizards. It was a stroke of genius: rather than let humans dabble in secret and fall to corruption, legalise magic, institutionalise it, systematise it. The cost was that every wizard was bound under strict lifetime control, owing dual loyalty to the Emperor and the Cult of Sigmar. A Bright Wizard and a Celestial Wizard dare not stay in the same room — the inherent antipathy of the Winds makes it so, regardless of personal feelings.

### Key Campaigns

**The Battle of Black Fire Pass (1 IC).** This was the Empire's birth-bed. Sigmar led the newly united twelve-tribe coalition against the Orc Warboss Bonecrusher's colossal WAAAGH!, personally shattering the Orc's skull with Ghal Maraz. After this day, the Empire faced no truly existential Greenskin invasion for over a thousand years.

**The Vampire Wars (c. 1797–2145 IC).** The greatest internal threat the Empire ever faced was neither Greenskin nor Chaos: it was the vampires beneath Sylvania. Vlad von Carstein — a former prince of Nehekhara who had lived three thousand years — seized Sylvania by marriage and launched the first Vampire War, marching his undead legions to the very gates of Altdorf and coming within a hair's breadth of turning the Empire into a kingdom of the dead. Grand Theogonist Wilhelm III grappled Vlad to mutual destruction holding Sigmar's hammer — only for Vlad to start regenerating from his Carstein Ring. The ring was stolen from his corpse before he could fully rise. Later, Konrad and Mannfred would each launch their own wars. Half the Empire's lasting scars in the south were carved by vampires.

**The Storm of Chaos (2522 IC).** The Everchosen Archaon marched the full northern hordes south. The north prong besieged Praag in Kislev; the centre prong drove straight for Middenheim. At the Siege of Middenheim, the avatar of Sigmar — Valten — met Archaon head-on. The Chaos forces failed to take the city and were forced to retreat. But the Empire's cost was catastrophic — the northern defence line was shredded, a wound that would dictate the course of the End Times three years later.

**The End Times (c. 2525–2528 IC).** The full legions of Chaos, Nagash's undead hosts, the Skaven Under-Empire, and the Beastmen warherds struck simultaneously in the same narrow window. Emperor Karl Franz, imbued with Sigmar's divine power, personally led the counter-attack — the last Emperor in history to lift Ghal Maraz. Province after province fell. Altdorf was breached from below by the Skaven. Middenheim collapsed the moment Archaon severed the world's final magical node beneath it. Twenty-five hundred years of the Empire ended there.

## Fate & Present Status

In the ashes of the End Times, the Old World's Empire was annihilated. But its genetic code — Elector-style city-state autonomy, Sigmarite faith, ranked handgun formations, the tradition of the Eight Winds — was carried by Sigmar into the Mortal Realms, taking root anew in the Free Cities. In this sense, the Empire is not fully dead. It has merely changed continents and resumed its endless programme of patching and repair.

## Verdict

The most captivating thing about the Empire is its imperfection. It is not sheltered by a goddess like Bretonnia. It has no ten-thousand-year inheritance like the Dwarfs. Its people do not live a thousand years like the Elves. The Empire is a ramshackle contraption Sigmar knocked together with fists and diplomacy, spending two and a half millennia on patchwork repairs, forever hovering at the edge of collapse. The Elector Counts have spent more time fighting each other than fighting foreign enemies. The Emperor has been a rubber stamp for the noble council half the time.

Yet this fragile resilience is the most human quality in the Old World. The Empire has no Dwarf Book of Grudges, no Dragon Princes of Caledor, no Lizardmen relics of the Old Ones. What it has are ranks of halberd-wielding farmers standing in squares, hastily levied handgunners, Steam Tanks that might blow their own boilers at any moment, and wizards locked in colleges testing the Eight Winds with their lives. These things together somehow held the line against Chaos for two and a half thousand years. Every time the Empire was beaten broken. Every time it got back up — until the last time.

When Sigmar walked east into the mountains without naming an heir, posterity faulted him for two millennia. But perhaps he understood better than anyone: an empire dependent on a single bloodline might not have lived two millennia at all. The Elector Count system, for all its inefficiency and chaos, at least averted the Old World's most common fatal flaw — three generations of an idiot king squandering a nation.
