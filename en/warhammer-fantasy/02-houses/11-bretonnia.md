# 11 · Bretonnia

## Overview

Bretonnia is the most distinctive human kingdom in the western Old World — a feudal society built entirely upon the chivalric ideal and the worship of the Lady of the Lake. It endured for over fifteen hundred years, facing the Empire across the Grey Mountains to the east, the two nations locked in a relationship somewhere between neighbours, allies, and mutual scorn. The Imperials think the Bretonnians are medieval fossils whose brains have been washed by a fairy. The Bretonnians think the Imperials are faithless gunpowder peddlers. But when Chaos truly comes calling, the knights of both realms invariably find themselves on the same battlefield.

Bretonnia's core is a three-tiered structure fixed in iron and blood: King (Royarch) — Duke — Knight — Peasant. There is almost no channel of movement between these tiers. A peasant's son is a peasant forever. A knight's son, no matter how worthless, is at the very least a Knight Errant. What allows this rigid caste system to function for fifteen hundred years is not administrative efficiency, but the real, visible power of the Lady of the Lake. A knight who sets out upon the Quest for the Grail without sufficient purity will die horribly along the way. Those who truly complete the Quest do indeed gain superhuman strength, a lifespan doubled beyond its natural span, and a semi-divine aura of majesty. A Grail Knight charging alone into an Orc warband, killing the Warboss, and charging back out — this is not myth in Bretonnia; it is a common battlefield occurrence.

## Origin: Gilles and the Twelve Great Battles

Bretonnia's founding reaches back to roughly nine centuries before the Imperial Calendar. At that time, the western side of the Grey Mountains was not yet a unified nation, but a patchwork of human lordlings and petty domains constantly at war with one another. Each patch of land had its castle, its peasants, its so-called knights with spears and skinny horses — in truth, bandits wearing iron scraps. Worse still, a Greenskin WAAAGH! was sweeping up from the south across the land, black tides of Orc tribes flooding forward, fortress after fortress falling.

Gilles le Breton was the son of the lord of Bastonne. He rose in the hour of despair as the Greenskins closed in. Legend holds that after a harrowing battle, he came alone to the shore of a hidden lake in the territory of Bastonne. There, the Lady of the Lake — a female deity clad in white robes, her face veiled — rose from the water, extended the Grail to him, and blessed him with her divine power. Gilles drank from the Grail and became the first Grail Knight of Bretonnia.

This was no isolated boon. The Lady gave Gilles a clear task: unify this land, and build a kingdom of knights. Over the years that followed, Gilles and his twelve companions — later called the Grail Companions — fought side by side, waging twelve decisive battles across the length of Bretonnia to crush the main Greenskin host. His companions included Landuin, who would become Duke of Bastonne; Balduin, who would become Duke of Brionne; and ten others, each having been chosen by the Lady upon the battlefield and drunk from the Grail.

After the Twelve Great Battles, Gilles was crowned the first Royarch of Bretonnia. But he did not rule for long. In a battle to sweep up the Greenskin remnants, Gilles took a mortal wound. His companions carried him to the lakeshore where he had first beheld the Lady, and there Gilles breathed his last. But the Lady did not let him truly die. She transformed him into the Green Knight — an undying, wandering guardian. Whenever Bretonnia faces an existential threat, the Green Knight rides forth from the deep forest, challenging and slaying unworthy knights and protecting the Lady's flock.

## Deeds

### The System: King, Duke, Knight, and Peasant

Bretonnia's political structure is a web woven from the Lady's faith and oaths sworn in blood.

The King (Royarch) is the highest authority, answering directly to the Lady, ruling from the throne city of Couronne. His legitimacy requires no election, no noble vote — he needs only to be recognised at his coronation by the Fay Enchantress, the mortal voice of the Lady of the Lake. The Fay Enchantress is a lifelong oracle-sorceress who holds the power to veto a king, appoint a duke, or declare an Errantry War (a holy crusade). In theory, her status exceeds even the king's — she is the Lady's mouth; the king is merely the Lady's hand.

The Dukes are the supreme lords of each duchy, holding their own fiefs and armies. They swear fealty to the king but pay no taxes. The king's effective radius of power barely extends past the walls of Couronne. Each duke is a petty emperor on his own lands — levying taxes, raising troops, pronouncing death sentences, all on his own authority. Blood feuds and border skirmishes between duchies are the normal state of affairs, though the Lady's strictures forbid open warfare among nobles; grievances may be settled in the duelling ground.

The knightly class is the true face of Bretonnia. Knights are divided into several ranks: at the lowest are the Knights Errant — young, untitled noble sons who charge into the wilds on raw nerve and hot blood to prove themselves against Orcs. In the middle are the Knights of the Realm — officially enfeoffed by a liege lord, holding land and peasants, forming the backbone of Bretonnia's armies. At the apex are the Grail Knights — those who have completed the Quest for the Grail. They renounce all property and title, wander the wilderness in search of the Lady's vision, proving themselves every step of the way through combat and devotion, until they drink again from the Grail in a divine vision. They emerge with superhuman physique, a demigod's presence, and a majesty that mortals cannot withstand. When a Grail Knight speaks, even the king must listen.

And the peasant — the peasant is the bottommost foundation of this edifice. A Bretonnian peasant's legal rights total approximately zero. Nine-tenths of the harvest goes to the lord; the peasant keeps one-tenth to survive. Without the lord's permission, a peasant may not leave his village. He may not bear a longsword or ride a warhorse — this is the legally codified "arms-class prohibition." Peasant soldiers carry spears, longbows, and dung-slings; if they die, their names need not be recorded. But, conversely, the Bretonnian peasant is not entirely without dignity. When the Greenskins come, the peasant longbow-storm can pin an entire rank of Orcs to the ground in a single volley. In a siege, the peasants' will to carry earth and fill moats is sometimes more use than a knight's charge. The Lady does not, in theory, extend her direct blessing to peasants — but a peasant brave and devout enough may, after death, find a corner somewhere in the Lady's domain. At least, the teachings say so, though no one has seen it with their own eyes.

### Military: The Charge, Pegasi, and the Arrow-Storm

Bretonnia's military logic is brutally simple: the knightly charge solves everything.

A typical Bretonnian army deploys as follows: the front line is Men-at-Arms, peasant spearmen whose job is not to win — it is to hold. To buy time with their bodies for the knights behind them to form up and begin the charge. On the flanks are Peasant Bowmen. The Bretonnian longbow's range and penetrating power rank among the best in the Old World, because peasants begin drawing bows at age ten — this is a mandatory legal requirement. Behind them are Trebuchets. Bretonnia does not use gunpowder; the trebuchet is their long-range heavy firepower.

The core striking power is the wedge of heavily armoured knights formed behind the line. A Bretonnian knightly charge is a physical phenomenon that every race of the Old World has learned to fear. A thousand barded warhorses start up, accelerate, and slam into the enemy line simultaneously. The metallic crash is deafening. Lances shatter like matchsticks at the moment of impact; the first rank of the charged enemy is usually hurled bodily through the air rather than cut down. When Grail Knights form the tip of the wedge, the shock doubles — their divine power grants their steeds supernatural endurance, allowing them to smash through the first line, wheel, and charge a second.

Pegasus Knights are the most elite force among Bretonnia's young nobility. Pegasi are native flying beasts of the Grey Mountains, tameable only by the purest noble virgins of either sex. Pegasus Knights attack from the air in plunging dives, specialising in ripping up the enemy's rear — harvesting archers, toppling trebuchet batteries, hunting fleeing mages. Among the aerial units of the Old World, the shock power of Pegasus Knights is second only to the Dragon Princes of the High Elves.

Bretonnia's greatest military deficiency is the lack of gunpowder weapons and battle magic. The Lady forbids the use of firearms and crossbows (crossbows are for shooting knights — dishonourable; gunpowder is the invention of cowards). Thus Bretonnia has no Steam Tanks, no Dwarf cannon, no ranked handgun volleys. As for magic — Bretonnia's only lawful spellcasters are the Damsels of the Lady, girls born with magical talent who are taken as children by the Fay Enchantress to study the lores of Water and Life at the Lady's hidden lake. Damsels are exceedingly few, unlike the Empire's eight Colleges of Magic which mass-produce battle wizards. In most campaigns, Bretonnia must bridge the magic gap with knightly steel and peasant blood alone.

## Fate & Present Status

For Bretonnia, the End Times meant not merely the death of a nation, but the theological annihilation of its faith.

Chaos hosts poured into Bretonnia simultaneously from the north and through the mountain passes. The last great king was Louen Leoncoeur, a Grail Knight of heroic stature, who mounted his Hippogryph and led his knights in desperate, suicidal holding actions at the Grey Mountain passes. Louen ultimately fell on the field of Altdorf, fighting the Undead legions — one of the very few Bretonnian kings to die on Imperial soil rather than his own kingdom's.

But as the rhythm of the world's collapse accelerated, the Bretonnians discovered a truth they could not bear: the Lady of the Lake's true identity is Lileath — an Elven goddess, a member of the High Elf pantheon. The "Lady of the Lake" whom Bretonnians had worshipped in piety for fifteen hundred years was, in essence, an Elven deity wearing a different mask, cultivating human knights as pieces in a larger scheme. The revelation was devastating to Bretonnia's faith. Some knights went mad on the spot and threw their grails into the river. Some chose denial and kept fighting. Most, as Chaos burned town after village, no longer had the luxury to ask whether their god was even a god.

The Green Knight played a final mythic role in the End Times. When the spirit of Gilles manifested once more, he chose not to guard any city, but to ride straight for the centre of the Chaos battle-line and engage the Chaos champions in a last duel. The ultimate Bretonnian reckoning was this: whatever the Lady's origins, the divine power she and Gilles bestowed upon the knights was real, the Grail was real, and the grace that had shielded Bretonnia's peasants for fifteen hundred years was real. But the Old World did not grant Bretonnia time to finish the argument. The castles, chapels, vineyards, and knightly tombs on the western slopes of the Grey Mountains turned to rubble along with the Chaos tide behind them.

## Verdict

Bretonnia is one of the most "anachronistic" and yet most unforgettable powers in all of Warhammer Fantasy. Its institutions, viewed through Imperial eyes, are a joke — a medieval fossil with no gunpowder, no representative government, and no social mobility. How could it possibly exist west of the Grey Mountains? But Bretonnia did exist, and it stood firm. Not because its institutions were rational, but because the Lady's power was real and the Grail Knight's strength was absolute.

This model of "divine legitimacy descending from above" is unique in the Old World. The Empire rests on the Elector Counts' votes and Sigmar's legend from a thousand years past. The Dwarfs rest on the Book of Grudges and ten thousand years of runic tradition. The High Elves rest on the ordeal of the Sacred Flame. Only Bretonnia was, from beginning to end, directly governed by a goddess — she chose the king, she anointed the knights, she manifested, she blessed. The moment the godhead proved faulty (as in the End Times, when she was revealed to be an Elven deity), the entire kingdom shut down like a machine whose plug had been pulled.

The Green Knight that Gilles left behind is Bretonnia's most intriguing design. Other gods departed (the Old Ones left), other gods were imprisoned (Nagash seized the Realm of Death), other gods are too lofty to bother with mortals — but the Lady (or Lileath) turned her first champion into an undying sentinel, forever patrolling Bretonnia's darkest corners. The Green Knight will not fight on anyone's behalf — he does only one thing: finds those unworthy of the title of knight, and cuts them down. This demigod-bodyguard-inspector's existence kept Bretonnia's fifteen-hundred-year knightly culture from degenerating into a pure tyranny of the strong. You never know whether there is a green shadow in the forest behind you, watching everything you do.

Seen from another angle, Bretonnia's peasants are among the most wretched human populations in the Old World. An Imperial peasant can at least move to the city, become a citizen, earn a soldier's pay, even rise through the Colleges of Magic to become a battle wizard. A Bretonnian peasant cannot even leave the village. Being killed by Greenskins is a duty; being eaten by Beastmen is fate. But the cruelty of Warhammer lies in this: on this land, without the Bretonnian knights standing in front of them, the peasants west of the Grey Mountains would have been consumed by Greenskins and Chaos long ago. The kingdom's entire structure is, in essence, a transaction: the peasants trade absolute obedience and nine-tenths of their harvest for the knights trading their lives against Chaos on the battlefield. That transaction held the map together for fifteen centuries, until the End Times finally broke the contract.

In the new world of the Mortal Realms, Bretonnia did not undergo a rebirth like the Empire's "Free Cities." Lileath's whereabouts after the End Times are unknown; the tradition of the Grail appears to have been severed. Yet the Lumineth Realm-Lords (the inheritors of the High Elves) in the Age of Sigmar still preserve the worship of Lileath. Perhaps, one day, on the shore of some new lake in some Mortal Realm, a knight will meet a woman in white and drink from a new Grail. Bretonnia is not the sort of thing that stays completely dead. So long as anyone is still willing to kneel upon a single knee for an ideal, some form of it remains.
