# 03 · High Elves

## Overview

The High Elves, who call themselves the Asur, are the most orthodox of the three Elven kindreds. They dwell upon the continent of Ulthuan — a ring-shaped isle floating in the centre of the Great Ocean, veiled in magic and guarded by the sea. Their armies are armoured in dragon-scale and ithilmar. Their mages wield High Magic, the simultaneous channelling of all eight Winds. Their fleets are, in quality, the finest in the world. Their purpose, by their own account, is to stand sentinel for the world — the first line of defence against Chaos, and the last.

They are not entirely boasting. Millennia ago, the Elves built the Great Vortex, drawing the excess Chaos energy streaming into the world from the polar rifts. Without it, the world would have been consumed long ago. Ever since, Ulthuan has been the stabiliser of the Old World's entire magical order. This gives the High Elves ample ground to look down on all others: we do the heavy lifting; you are not entitled to stand alongside us.

Naturally, this arrogance has helped them alienate every ally they have ever had, one after another, across twenty centuries.

## Origin: The Age of Aenarion

Elven civilisation began on Ulthuan. In an age when Men had not yet learned to smelt iron, the Elves were already building the white cities of the isle, studying magic, taming Dragons, and dividing the continent into ten meticulously ordered kingdoms. It was an age without urgency — an Elf's lifespan runs into thousands of years; a century passes like a flick of the wrist; a three-generation plan was, to them, the work of a moment.

The first true emergency came from Chaos.

No record can precisely fix the date of the first great Chaos incursion. Roughly five millennia before the Imperial Calendar, the polar gates collapsed, and the Winds of Magic, freighted with Daemons, poured into the world. Ulthuan took the brunt. Defensive line after defensive line was torn open. Daemon legions surged onto the island from the northern seas, and white cities turned to black ash one after another.

Just as the Elves faced extinction, a warrior named Aenarion walked into the Sacred Flame of Asuryan — a shrine-flame said to be the mortal embodiment of the Elven creator-god, which burns the unworthy alive. Aenarion drew forth a sword from the fire: the Sword of Khaine, also called the Widowmaker. It was the weapon of the Elven god of war, Khaine, planted in the shrine of Asuryan and waiting for a hand to claim it. The cost: whoever wields it, and his bloodline, shall be cursed forever.

Aenarion took it. Armed with that blade, mounted upon the Dragon Indraugnir, he waged a one-man counter-war. The Daemon host was hurled back. Chaos was driven from Ulthuan.

But he himself was changed. The curse of Khaine seeped into his flesh, making him ever more violent, ever more bloodthirsty. His first wife — the Everqueen Astarielle — vanished without trace in the chaos of war. He later married Morathi, a sorceress of dubious origin and terrifying beauty. They bore a son: Malekith.

Aenarion's end was death in battle. In a war that could not be won, he led every warrior who would follow him into the deepest heart of the Chaos host and immolated himself and the enemy together in the flame of Asuryan. He was thereafter revered as the eternal guardian of the Elves — the ancestor and symbol of every Phoenix King after him. But the curse he left behind, and the cursed son born of it, would tear Elven history in two.

### The Great Vortex

Aenarion's death did not mean Chaos had truly withdrawn. The Daemons had been beaten back, but the polar rifts remained open; the Winds of Magic kept pouring Chaos energy into the world day and night. So long as the rifts stayed unsealed, the Daemons would return sooner or later.

The one who sealed them was a mage named Caledor Dragontamer. He was the archmage of the kingdom of Caledor and by consensus the greatest magical genius in all Elven history. He proposed a plan that could only be called lunatic: to construct a magical vortex at the centre of Ulthuan that would inhale the world's surplus Chaos energy, channel it, purify it, and thereby plug the influx at the source.

This plan required the full cooperation of every Elven archmage and had to be executed while holding off a direct Chaos assault. Caledor led his mage-host into the central mountains of the island, raising wards and conducting rituals simultaneously. The Daemons, of course, were not stupid — they threw everything they had at the vortex before it could be completed. Elven warriors and Dragon Princes fought and died by the half-generation to buy the mages the time they needed.

The Great Vortex succeeded. The Winds of Magic were drawn into the vortex and expelled, Chaos energy reduced to a manageable level. The world drew breath. Caledor and his mages were locked permanently into the spatial rift at the centre — their bodies trapped there, their consciousness eternally maintaining the vortex's operation. Caledor Dragontamer, the genius who single-handedly saved the world, never walked out of that chamber. And every mage of Caledor chosen thereafter to inherit his burden would enter that same room and join the eternal watch.

The completion of the Great Vortex established the High Elves' self-conception for the next several millennia: it is not that we are the masters of the world — it is that we have held this world together. You owe us, whether you admit it or not.

### The Ten Kingdoms of Ulthuan

High Elf society is composed of ten kingdoms, divided into the Inner Ring and the Outer Ring.

**The Inner Kingdoms** — Caledor, Saphery, Eataine, Avelorn — lie in the warm central region of the continent. Caledor is the homeland of the Dragon Princes: the hardest temper, the fiercest in war, the lowest in output — a Caledorian noble would sooner ride a Dragon into a volcano than speak a single soft word to an outsider. Saphery is the city of magic; the White Tower of Hoeth is the highest academy of High Magic, and it was here that Teclis trained the first heads of the Empire's Colleges of Magic. Avelorn is the seat of the Everqueen, charged with perpetuating Aenarion's bloodline. Eataine is the breadbasket, the gentlest of tempers.

**The Outer Kingdoms** — Nagarythe, Tiranoc, Chrace, Cothique, Ellyrion, Yvresse — ring the island's periphery, facing down pirates, Dark Elf invasions, and every peril of the seas. Nagarythe is the homeland of the Shadow Warriors, those scout-assassins who move like ghosts through the hills. Yvresse produces the finest Mist-walker spearmen, who guard the island's fogbound passes generation after generation. Tiranoc was largely sunk during the Sundering.

The ten kingdoms squabble ceaselessly — the question of whether Caledor or Saphery holds primacy in matters of war versus magic has been debated for thousands of years without resolution. But by and large, when an external enemy comes calling, the ten kingdoms can still face in the same direction. At least they did for the first two millennia.

### The Line of the Phoenix Kings

The Phoenix King is never hereditary. The Elven system is election by the Council of Princes. In theory, any Elf can be chosen; in practice, the office rotates among a handful of the great kingdoms.

**Bel Shanaar** was the first Phoenix King elected after Aenarion. Malekith — the prior king's own son — had the loudest support, but the Council chose Bel Shanaar. Malekith accepted publicly and began walking a separate path in secret. Bel Shanaar was not incompetent; he was simply born at the wrong time. The Phoenix Throne was mined from day one. He later dropped dead in the middle of a court session, the exact cause of which no one has ever satisfactorily explained — Malekith was, of course, impossible to exclude.

**Caledor II** presided over the single stupidest war in Elven history: the War of the Beard. The cause was simple enough — Dark Elves, disguised as High Elves, attacked a Dwarf trade caravan and shaved the Dwarf ambassadors' beards. The Dwarf High King sent a delegation to Ulthuan demanding the perpetrators. Caledor II not only refused to hand them over, but publicly shaved the Dwarf envoys as well. You do not shave a Dwarf's beard — that is a deeper insult than beheading. War erupted. The two sides butchered each other for centuries, nearly wiping out each other's entire leadership. By the end, the Elves could not sustain it. The newly elevated Phoenix King **Caradryel** made a brutal but practical decision: abandon all colonies in the Old World, pull the troops back to the island. The High Elf losses in this war were no lighter than the Dwarfs'. Nearly every Elven city on the Old World mainland that had stood for thousands of years was abandoned.

Subsequent Phoenix Kings each had their own character. **Tethlis** was a military Phoenix King through and through, pouring every ounce of energy into the war against the Dark Elves — he took his meals at inspections. He died in battle, having discovered too late that his inner circle had been thoroughly infiltrated by Malekith's spies. **Morvael** suffered grievous losses during a Dark Elf invasion and committed suicide in shame — the only Phoenix King to take his own life in office. After him, **Bel-Korhadris** rebuilt the White Tower of Saphery, restoring the prestige of magic to its apex and making the Elves the intellectual giant of the world for two centuries. **Aethis** had no head for military affairs and spent his days writing poetry and playing music; under him, Elven culture bloomed to its peak, and border defences slackened to their nadir — it was during this period that the Dark Elves raided most savagely.

**Finubar**, the eleventh Phoenix King, was called the Seafarer. He was the first High Elf king to actively sail to the Old World and re-establish contact with Men and Dwarfs. His vision extended beyond Ulthuan's self-regarding insularity — he began to take the rise of Man seriously. It was during his reign that Teclis was dispatched overseas to assist the Empire in founding the Colleges of Magic, winning for the High Elves their stoutest diplomatic foothold in the Old World.

The last Phoenix King, **Tyrion**, was a product of the End Times. He was Aenarion's descendant, Ulthuan's greatest living general, thrust onto the throne during the apocalyptic Chaos invasion. He drew the Sword of Khaine seeking to replicate his ancestor's myth. But the sword's curse ultimately turned him into a new Chaos threat himself. His brother Teclis, in desperation, sought out Malekith — the enemy banished for a millennium — to end the cycle.

### The Sundering and the Dark Elves

The war between the High Elves and the Dark Elves is not a war between two nations. It is a race at war with itself for two thousand years.

After his failed coup, Malekith fled to Naggaroth and built the Dark Elf kingdom there. His invasions of Ulthuan never ceased thereafter — the most famous being seven great invasions. Each was a catastrophe: the Outer Kingdoms were repeatedly sacked, Tiranoc was largely sunk, the coastline of Cothique was trampled flat by Black Arks over and over. The High Elves' response was to take it on the chin — they never launched a counter-invasion of Naggaroth proper (the place was frozen wasteland, useless even if captured), merely hunkering down on Ulthuan and fighting for their lives every time the Dark Elf ships appeared.

The real loser in this long war of attrition was the High Elf commoner. Every Dark Elf fleet that came took another haul of living slaves. High Elf population numbers never recovered over the millennia. At the same time, the Dwarfs seized the chance to settle accounts while the Elves were weakened — the War of the Beard caught the Elves between two fronts, inflicting devastating losses. After the war the High Elves withdrew from the Old World, effectively ceding the entire continental sphere of influence to Men and Dwarfs.

## Fate & Present Status

In the End Times, Chaos flooded into the world on a scale never seen before. Ulthuan was the first to be breached. The Great Vortex was corrupted. Daemon legions rose up directly from the inner sea. What the Phoenix Kings had sustained across thousands of years collapsed in the final hour.

Tyrion drew the sword and fought alone — and fell. Teclis moved in shadows and brought back Malekith. Malekith entered the Flame of Asuryan a second time — and this time he did not burn. His scars healed. The black armour melted away. The trial the sacred fire had not completed a thousand years before was now complete. The three Elven kindreds — High, Dark, and Wood — merged at the edge of extinction and faced Chaos with a unity that had never existed before.

The Old World shattered in the end. But the Elves did not die.

In the Mortal Realms of the Age of Sigmar, the bloodline of the High Elves flows primarily into the Lumineth Realm-Lords — a "new High Elves" that inherits every virtue and every flaw of the old. There is also Malerion (Malekith) and his shadow-kin in the Realm of Shadow, and the Idoneth Deepkin in the depths of the oceans. Ulthuan is gone, the Great Vortex is gone — but the Asur spirit of "we are the most correct" has been inherited into the new world without the slightest dilution.

## Verdict

The story of the High Elves is like a parent who carries the heaviest burden but can never learn to speak a soft word. They held the line for the world for how many years — and they genuinely did. The mage-host of the Vortex, the sacrifice of Phoenix King after Phoenix King, the grinding war of flesh against the Dark Elves, the senseless losses in the war against the Dwarfs — they piled the finest magic, the mightiest Dragons, the most elite troops on the front line against Chaos. Without them holding the magical order together, it would never have lasted long enough for Mankind to rise.

But their arrogance is equally real. The spark of the War of the Beard was not a Dark Elf frame-up — it was Caledor II publicly shaving the Dwarf envoys. That act was not necessity — it was pure, bone-deep contempt. They fell out with the Dwarfs, withdrew from the Old World, gave the rise of Men a grudging half-acceptance — every step had its rational excuse of self-preservation, and every step had its component of being blinded by pride.

The most painful part is the population question. The High Elves fought too many wars, lost too many lives, and could not breed fast enough to replace them. Turn the pages of Elven history: in the beginning Ulthuan was everywhere crowning kings, ten kingdoms flourishing; by the later centuries, every battle required a miser's calculation with lives, and every fallen Dragon Prince was irreplaceable. The High Elves won many battles. The war they lost was a slow war of attrition measured in millennia.

And the starting point of it all — Aenarion walking into the flame, drawing the Sword of Khaine — had written the ending from the beginning: this race was fated to be cursed, and fated to hold this world together. They did both.
