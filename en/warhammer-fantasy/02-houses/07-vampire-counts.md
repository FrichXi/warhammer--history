# 07 · Vampire Counts

## Overview

Among all the races of the Old World, none is more aristocratic — or more fundamentally untrustworthy — than the vampires. They possess human appearance, immortal lifespans, superhuman martial prowess, and an affinity for dark magic, yet are shackled for eternity to the blood-curse, forever wandering the narrow ground between civilisation and savagery.

Vampires are not the product of natural evolution. They are the by-product of Nagash's necromancy — a failed immortality experiment that crawled out of a court conspiracy in the desert of Nehekhara over four thousand years ago and has lodged itself in the dark places of the Old World ever since. To the world, they are daemons, lords of the Undead, man-eating monsters in ancient castles. In their own eyes, they are a race that seized immortality by its own strength. The gap between this self-conception and the world's assessment is the key to understanding the entire history of the vampires.

## Origin: The Elixir of Blood

Everything begins with Nagash.

After usurping the throne of Khemri, Nagash invented necromancy from the fusion of dark magic and Nehekharan mortuary ritual. He then formulated an elixir of life — compounded from human blood and herbs, requiring repeated doses to sustain immortality. The side effects were pronounced: the drinker's flesh gradually desiccated into that of a living corpse.

He bestowed the elixir upon his ally, Queen Neferata of Lahmia. Neferata was the first female pharaoh of Nehekhara. She regarded Nagash with a mixture of awe and wariness and was not content merely to be his subordinate. In the depths of the Lahmian court, she secretly refined the formula, creating the Elixir of Blood — purer and more seductively lethal than Nagash's original.

Around 1350 years before the Imperial Calendar, Neferata drank from the elixir alongside her most trusted followers: her champion Abhorash, Prince Vashanesh (later renamed Vlad von Carstein), and others.

The elixir granted them immortality, superhuman strength, and an affinity for necromantic magic. But it left them with a fatal flaw the original Nagash-formula lacked: the blood-thirst. Without the drinking of blood, their power waned and their reason unravelled, until they degenerated into mindless, blood-crazed beasts. The first vampires were born.

## Deeds

### The Fall of Lahmia and the Six Bloodlines

Neferata's secret could not remain hidden forever. The vanishing servants of the Lahmian court and the queen's perpetually ageless beauty gradually drew suspicion from the other city-states. The truth was unearthed. The Nehekharan city-states formed a coalition and besieged Lahmia. The ancient city fell in fire and ruin. The vampires scattered in every direction in what history calls the First Diaspora — and it was this scattering that laid down the pattern of the Six Bloodlines.

**The von Carstein Bloodline**, progenitor Vlad. After the fall of Lahmia he fled east, arriving in Sylvania, the most benighted province of the Empire's eastern frontier. He changed his name, married the mad daughter of the local count, Isabella, and seized power through his marriage bed. He turned Sylvania into the vampire stronghold. The von Carsteins are the bloodline with the greatest ambition to rule — they do not seek survival in shadows, but a throne in the open light.

**The Blood Dragon Bloodline**, progenitor Abhorash. Former Captain of the Guard of Lahmia, Abhorash could not accept his dependency on human blood after transformation. He wandered the Old World for centuries, seeking a path of purification, and at last slew and drank the blood of a great red dragon in the north. Dragon's blood diluted the thirst for human blood; henceforth he fed on dragon's blood alone. He founded the way of the Blood Dragons — taming the curse through martial discipline and a knight's code. They are the knights among vampires.

**The Neferata Bloodline**, progenitor Neferata herself — the first vampire, the Queen of Lahmia. She fled north with her closest blood-kin, building a hidden court in the Silver Pinnacle deep in the Worlds Edge Mountains. The Neferata bloodline is the oldest direct descent among all vampires, preserving the purest pride and lineage of the vampire aristocracy, forming a rigidly hierarchical court society centred on the queen.

**The Lahmian Bloodline**, infiltrators named for the fallen city of Lahmia. They are spread through the shadows of every great city of the Old World, maintaining an impenetrable spy network — the Lahmian Sisterhood. They use beauty, charm, and blood-bondage to control nobles and temple priests, manipulating the political chessboards of nations from the dark. This is the most insidious, the most skilled at remaining hidden, and the most widely distributed of the Six Bloodlines, with the best intelligence.

**The Strigoi Bloodline**, the most tragic. A band of vampire refugees fled deep into the wilderness and survived among ruins and graveyards. Prolonged isolation from civilisation, inbreeding, and the accumulated weight of the curse gradually devolved them into bestial forms. Their raw physical strength ranks among the highest of the Six Bloodlines, but they are the most hideous in appearance and were jointly purged by the other bloodlines.

**The Necrarch Bloodline**, the scholars among vampires. They seclude themselves in remote towers and underground crypts, their faith necromancy itself, their desire knowledge. They are indifferent to their own increasingly cadaverous appearance. They are the most sombre of all the bloodlines, and the most magically destructive — still researching, to this day, how to surpass Nagash.

The Six Bloodlines each have their own gifts, each their own weaknesses. They war constantly among themselves and hold one another in mutual contempt. But they share one common wound: Nagash hangs over every one of them. Their power originates with him; the curse was set in motion by him; and not one of them — in their bones — accepts this willingly.

### The Vampire Wars

In 1797 IC, Vlad von Carstein declared war on the neighbouring provinces of Sylvania.

This was no hidden infiltration — it was open conquest. Vlad intended to become Emperor. His army, composed of skeletal undead, Ghouls, and vampire lieutenants, surged out of Sylvania and rolled across the Empire's east. The Elector Counts initially fought individually and were broken one by one. By the time the Empire assembled a coalition, Vlad was at the gates of Altdorf.

The siege lasted a full winter. Vlad breached the city multiple times and was thrown back each time. His willingness to gamble everything rested on a single artefact: the Carstein Ring. So long as it stayed on his finger, no fatal wound could truly destroy him. The Grand Theogonist Wilhelm III eventually decoded this secret. In the final battle, Wilhelm was impaled through the chest on Vlad's blade — but as he fell, he struck the ring from Vlad's hand with his last blow. The ring gone, Vlad's accumulated wounds detonated simultaneously, and his body disintegrated on the spot.

After Vlad's death, his mad get, Konrad, launched the Second Vampire War — sweeping across the Empire's east on sheer brutality rather than military skill, and was finally slain by a Dwarf rune-weapon. Then the youngest apprentice, Mannfred von Carstein, took up the banner.

Mannfred was the most meticulous planner of the three Vampire Wars. His military scale did not match Vlad's, but his political infiltration and sorcerous arrangements far outstripped his predecessors. He came within a hair's breadth of detonating a crisis in the heart of the Empire that would have paralysed the Elector Count system. The coalition defeated him in the end but could not kill him — Mannfred vanished from the world in defeat and went underground.

None of the three Vampire Wars truly eliminated the vampires. They always withdrew into shadow, awaiting the next opportunity.

### The Relationship with Nagash

The vampires' relationship with Nagash is a debt spanning four thousand years.

On the surface, Nagash is the "ancestor" of all vampires. In reality, he has never regarded them as true offspring. To him, they are a by-product of necromancy, an inferior variant created by Neferata's unauthorised improvements. The vampires, too, have never truly submitted to him. Neferata spent her entire existence evading Nagash's summons. Vlad, while building an empire in Sylvania, had zero interaction with Nagash. The Blood Dragons wrapped themselves in the knight's code, unwilling to be associated with the tyrant of the dead. The Necrarchs viewed Nagash as a rival, not a master.

Mannfred alone was an exception. He considered himself Nagash's most loyal servant — but his record of betraying allies was so high that no one believed he would not sell out Nagash at the crucial moment.

The essential relationship between vampires and Nagash is a proposition of inheritance and rebellion: every generation of vampires wishes to inherit Nagash's power; none wishes to inherit his will.

## Fate & Present Status

As the Chaos hosts descended from the north and the Old World tottered, the vampires faced their most fundamental choice.

Mannfred moved first. He sought out Nagash's most faithful servant, Arkhan the Black, and assisted in gathering Nagash's relics scattered across the world. To swell the ranks, he resurrected Vlad. After being brought back, Vlad merely smiled at Mannfred's calculations and agreed to join — not out of loyalty to Nagash, but because he understood clearly: if Chaos wins, not even the dead will be left.

Nagash was successfully resurrected and achieved apotheosis, becoming Death itself. But before his divinity could stabilise, the sun-god Ptra used the power of the Nehekharan kings to drag him deep into the Realm of Death and imprison him.

Mannfred betrayed the alliance again at a critical battle. His habitual treachery reached its zenith in the End Times, leading ultimately to his own destruction — and indirectly to the consummation of the Old World's end. Vlad, by contrast, walked willingly to his end to protect Isabella, writing an unexpectedly tragic final line for the von Carstein bloodline.

The other bloodlines could not escape the reckoning. The Silver Pinnacle was consumed by Chaos; Neferata's fate is unknown. The Blood Dragons sought out single-combat arenas everywhere, treating the end of the world as the ultimate test. The Strigoi perished unremarked in forgotten wilderness corners. The Necrarch towers were torn apart by the Winds of Chaos.

## Verdict

The story of the vampires is the story of a people trying to build an identity of their own in the shadow of Nagash — and never succeeding.

Neferata wanted immortality; she got it, at the cost of forever depending on the plundered lives of others. Abhorash wanted honour; he found a path of purification — but dragon's blood did not break the curse, only swapped it for a rarer addiction. Vlad wanted the Empire; he nearly grasped the crown, only to be dragged down by a defeated enemy's dying blow. Mannfred wanted to be the chess-player; he spent his life weaving the moves, and only at the end realised he had been Nagash's piece all along.

The Six Bloodlines each have their pride, their gifts, their aesthetics — more elegant than Dwarfs, more pragmatic than Elves, longer-lived than Men. But they share one soft spot: they have never truly broken free of Nagash. Nagash's magic runs in their veins. Their power is hitched to Nagash's system. Their immortality is built on Nagash's invention. No matter how far they walk, how mighty the forces they build, how many identity-narratives they weave, they are always pulled back to the fact: they are Nagash's creation, not Nagash's master.

The End Times revealed everything. Every bloodline structure disintegrated in the world's collapse. All that remained in the end was the one branch that fell back under Nagash's banner. For a race whose ultimate pursuit was freedom and immortality, this is nearly the greatest irony imaginable.

The tragedy of the vampires is not that they are monsters — it is that they have never been willing to be monsters.
