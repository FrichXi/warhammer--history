# 13 · Wood Elves

## Overview

The Wood Elves, who call themselves the Asrai, are the most marginalised and most mysterious of the three Elven kindreds. Their domain lies not among the white spires of Ulthuan, not on the black cliffs of Naggaroth, but deep within a forest in the heart of the Old World's mainland — Athel Loren. The woodcutters on its fringes say that those who walk into its depths never walk out. The knights of Bretonnia say the forest eats men. The Dwarfs, with characteristic directness, simply mark it on their maps as "Not Recommended."

The Asrai's philosophy of existence is entirely different from the other two kindreds. The High Elves see themselves as the world's guardians. The Dark Elves see themselves as wronged by the world. The Wood Elves see none of these things. They neither guard nor hate. They simply live. Whoever enters the forest, dies. And they have lived this way for a full five thousand years.

## Origin: The Elves Who Chose No Side

The Sundering is the fissure in Elven history. Malekith entered the Flame of Asuryan, was burned, and launched his civil war. The Elves of Ulthuan split in two: those standing with the Phoenix King on the left; those standing with the Witch King on the right. But there was one group of Elves who chose to stand with neither.

These were colonists who had withdrawn from Ulthuan's colonial possessions to the Old World mainland at the start of the Sundering. Once the civil war ignited, these colonies were conscripted in turn by High Elves and Dark Elves; those who refused to help one side were punished by the other. So they made the simplest possible decision: walk into the forest. What walked in were Elves. What walked out was a different people.

Athel Loren had been wrong long before the Elves arrived. The trees grew taller than elsewhere, the roots deeper; at night you could hear sounds that were not the wind. This forest was conscious. It did not speak, but it saw, it remembered, and it expressed its opinion with vines and bogs. The first immigrants did not have an easy time of it. The forest drove them with nightmares and phantoms, ground them down with disorientation and starvation. But in the end the forest judged that these outsiders were not like the others out there — the ones who cut trees and burned land. These did not cut, did not burn, only hid. The forest accepted them.

The result of acceptance was mutual transformation. The Elves learned to live with the trees. The forest absorbed the spark of civilisation. Ancient trees quickened, growing limbs and faces — this was the awakening of the Treemen. The Elves themselves changed: their skin paled, their ears lengthened, and their gaze, when it fell upon outsiders, increasingly resembled that of a forest beast, carrying a vacancy that no civilised species possessed. They no longer called themselves Asur, nor acknowledged themselves as Druchii. Their name was Asrai — "the people of the woods."

In time, Athel Loren ceased to be a refuge. It became them. When the forest was wounded, the Elves felt it. When an Elf died here, the soul returned beneath the roots. This was not possession. It was not worship. It was two heartbeats inside one body.

## Deeds

### The Dual Throne: King in the Woods and Queen Eternal

Asrai society is not a hierarchical pyramid in the fashion of Men and Dwarfs, nor a noble council in the fashion of the High Elves. It has only two persons: a king and a queen.

Orion is the King in the Woods. He is not elected. He is not inherited. He is not elevated by debate and conspiracy. He is burned into being. Every winter, Orion immolates himself beneath the great Oak of Ages in the deepest heart of Athel Loren. His flesh turns to ash. His life is returned to the forest. Then, come spring — at the first thunderclap and the first new rain — he grows back out of the hollow of the great oak. Younger than the time before. Wilder. Hungrier. His horns are larger. His eyes burn brighter. Every vessel in his body is urging him to do one thing: hunt.

Orion's season is spring and summer. In these months, he is the living weapon of the forest — taller than any Elf, his muscles like coiled oak-roots in human form, below his antler-helm a roaring face that an Elf ought not to possess. The company he leads is not called an army. It is the Wild Hunt. They charge out of the forest, killing everything they see — Chaos, Orcs, Men, occasionally even Dwarfs. Orion's hunt is not driven by hatred or strategy, but by something more primal: the forest is hungry. In spring, as all things quicken, the forest needs to taste blood.

Ariel is the Queen Eternal. She is Orion's exact opposite. She does not cycle, does not die and return. She has been there from the beginning. Ariel is an Elven queen who was once a butterfly; the wings remain at her back as shimmering gossamer, but her power does not come from them. It comes from the root-system of the forest itself. Her season is autumn and winter. When Orion dies in the fire, Ariel reigns alone. All things withdraw. Leaves wither. Her magic is nothing like the academic sorcery of the High Elves: it is a force grown from soil, sap, and mycelium — no incantations, no scrolls, only perception and intent.

The relationship between the two is deeply complex. In mythic narrative they are husband and wife, the two faces of Athel Loren's single body. In practical terms, Ariel often disagrees with whatever Orion is doing while he runs wild through the summer, and Orion can contribute nothing when she governs the forest quietly through the winter. After each death and rebirth, Orion sometimes comes back with a temperament wilder than the last, to the point where even Ariel cannot restrain him. Five hundred years ago, Orion, in a killing frenzy, nearly stormed the Bretonnian royal city, and Ariel had to haul him back into the deep woods using the roots of the entire forest.

And yet the dual structure has never broken. The two of them could not break it if they tried. Orion is the forest's pulse. Ariel is the forest's breath. The Asrai accept this arrangement. No one seeks to become a third monarch.

### Military: The Forest Is the Battle-Line

The Asrai have no standing army. No barracks, no drill-grounds, no standardised equipment. Their soldiers are the forest itself.

The baseline combat strength is the Glade Guard — Asrai Elves wielding longbows of living wood, their quivers holding arrows with over a dozen different functional heads: armour-piercing, starfire, poison, rope-cutters. A trained Glade Guard can identify the correct arrowhead, string, aim for the head, and release in three seconds — then vanish behind the next tree. The more refined marksmen are the Waywatchers, the silent reapers of Athel Loren, capable of standing as a tree through an entire night without motion, striking only when the enemy cannot see them.

The cavalry are the Wild Riders, attached to Orion's Wild Hunt. Their mounts are not horses, but forest-striders — half horse, half deer, their eyes green, their passage stirring not a single leaf. In battle they fall into a shaman-like possession; Orion's will pours directly into their bodies. They become no longer Elves, but the claws of the forest.

But the true heavyweights are the trees. Treemen are the oldest inhabitants of Athel Loren, standing for millennia before the Elves arrived. They tower six to eight metres; their arms are thick boughs, their fingers vine-tendrils. They cannot shout and feel no fear — they only walk forward and tear people in two. Dryads are the Treemen's close kin: smaller, more agile, female in form, half-human and half-tree. In peacetime they murmur soft songs. In war they become a wildly spinning mass of claws and thorns. When invaders approach, they emit an unsettling giggling laugh, then press their face-bark against the tree trunk.

In the air there are Warhawk Riders — giant hawks whose wingspan can hold three Elves standing abreast, shooting downward from above the canopy. The men on the ground look up and see nothing but an infinity of leaves; from above, the riders see every step the enemy takes with perfect clarity.

The coordination of all these forces needs no herald. The root network of Athel Loren is itself an information-transmission system, the trees exchanging intelligence through their roots. A human cavalry regiment accelerates through a marsh — the news travels from the first root onward, reaches the forest-heart within fifteen seconds, and within twenty, Orion has begun laying a kill-trap. The entire forest is a single living war machine.

### The Symbiosis: Ecology as Faith

The Asrai's relationship with Athel Loren, in the eyes of any other race, is not normal. The High Elves have a cooperative relationship with magic. The Dwarfs have a craftsman's relationship with stone. Men have a consumptive relationship with the land. The Asrai have been eaten by the forest, digested, and become part of it.

This has physiological expression. After an Elf dies, the body does not rot. It is enveloped by tree roots and drawn beneath the earth. Eventually a new tree grows there — sometimes its shape faintly resembles the deceased's face. The Asrai do not find this horrifying. Death is becoming a new organ of the forest. Conversely, the Asrai can feel when the forest is wounded. A stretch of ground is burned — that night, half the kindred suffers nightmares, children run fevers, the old ones' hands tremble. The forest is poisoned, the river runs black — the Asrai begin to sicken. Cut Athel Loren down entirely, and the Asrai cannot live — not in the political sense of "cannot live," but in the physiological sense of organ failure.

From this relationship flows the fact that the Asrai have no traditional religion. They worship no god, build no temples, burn no incense. The only law they follow is called the Law of the Forest: do not cut living wood; do not gorge on game; do not lead outsiders into the wood. The price of violation is not a trial — it is the forest's estrangement. An alienated Asrai grows colder, weaker, until at last it is no longer Asrai at all.

The external stance is a single sentence: just don't come in. The sole exception is Bretonnia. The Bretonnian worship of the woodland spirits overlaps religiously with the forest-consciousness of Athel Loren. Their Green Knight — the eternal rider who haunts the wilds — is said to be, in his true form, one of Orion's incarnations. The two powers do not garrison together, but they frequently coordinate when Beastmen and Orcs attack.

## Fate & Present Status

The defining characteristic of the End Times was this: Chaos stopped playing border skirmishes and simply flipped the table. Hiding was no longer an option.

Athel Loren's defensive logic had always been obscurity and trickery — enter and you cannot see, see and you cannot leave, and by the time you cannot leave you are already dead. Effective against Orcs, Beastmen, and lost knights. But Nurgle rotted from the inside out. Pestilence invaded through the rivers. Corruption spread through the mycelial web. Chaos magic fouled the trees from the roots. The Asrai's greatest advantage — their symbiosis with the forest — became their greatest vulnerability. As the forest was corrupted, they ulcerated with it.

Ariel poured every last reserve of strength into preserving the heartland, but her power drained in lockstep with the dying forest. When Orion emerged from the oak-hollow that spring, there was no wildness in his eyes — only grey death. He led the Wild Hunt in one last charge against Chaos and did not return. The forest burned for forty days.

In the final phase, Ariel made a decision no one could have predicted: she opened the root-gates of Athel Loren, permitting the survivors of the High Elves and Dark Elves to enter the sanctum that had been absolutely forbidden to outsiders. The three Elven kindreds — divided for ten thousand years — stood together in the ashes. Without the Asrai unlocking the forest-gates at the final hour, the tripartite alliance would not have formed.

After the end of the Old World, the Asrai's legacy split down two paths. The soul of the forest — the Treemen, Dryads, the root-pulse — evolved into the Sylvaneth. The Elven bloodline of the Asrai flowed into the Lumineth Realm-Lords, merging with the other two kindreds.

## Verdict

The Asrai are the least-written-about of the three Elven kindreds and the easiest to reduce to "scenery." Orcs attack — shoot arrows. Beastmen attack — shoot arrows. Humans get lost — shoot arrows. It is tempting to simplify them into three words: "keepers of the wood." But their distinctiveness lies not in their war-record, but in an extreme existential choice: they voluntarily withdrew from the stage of history.

At the time of the Sundering, Malekith said "choose me," the Phoenix King said "do not choose him" — both sides were fighting for the narrative of "who is the true Elf." The Asrai's answer was: who is true does not matter. Argue among yourselves; we are going into the trees. The price of abandoning the battle for the narrative was to be forgotten. Leaf through the history of the Old World and the Asrai appear in almost no proactive role. But the other side of that price was this: they evaded the High Elves' population crash, the Dark Elves' thousand-year cycle of rebellion, the Dwarfs' War of the Beard, the Empire's three-cornered civil wars — and lived quietly beneath the trees for five thousand years. This forest offered all Elves a single answer: you do not have to fight. Aenarion's curse bore no fruit in this soil.

Most interesting of all is that in the End Times, this "least involved" group provided the ground for the great Elven reconciliation. The High Elves and Dark Elves had fought for ten thousand years; neither could walk through the other's door. The Asrai's forest-gates became, perversely, the only possible neutral space. After five thousand years of refusing outsiders, the Asrai became, in the final hour, the womb of the three kindreds.

A footnote for Bretonnia: Bretonnian scholars are fond of co-opting the Asrai into their own religious system — "the Elves favoured by the woodland goddess," "the kin of the Green Knight" — as though the Asrai were a subset of their faith. The Asrai have never contradicted them. Not because they agree. Because they cannot be bothered to speak. Watching a knight kneel in prayer before a tree, the corner of an Asrai's mouth might twitch slightly — then they turn and vanish into the tree-shadows.
