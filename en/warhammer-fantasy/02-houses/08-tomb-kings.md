# 08 · Tomb Kings

## Overview

The Tomb Kings are the most peculiar Undead power in the Warhammer Fantasy world. They are not mindless puppets summoned by some necromancer, but dead beings who retain their full living memories, personalities, and desires. Millennia ago, they were the pharaohs, princes, priests, and warriors of the ancient civilisation of Nehekhara — one of the earliest great human civilisations. Nagash's Great Ritual of Awakening annihilated the living world of Nehekhara, yet paradoxically allowed its dead to persist in another form. The awakened pharaohs crawled from their pyramids to find their kingdoms reduced to bone-cities in the desert. So they took up their weapons and re-established order — the order of the dead.

The Tomb Kings despise the undead of the vampires, viewing them as the twisted spawn of Nagash. In their own sight, they are the legitimate heirs of Nehekhara, rulers who have returned after receiving the judgement of the death-gods. Towards living outsiders — whether Imperials, Bretonnian knights, or Greenskins — they essentially have one attitude: you are trampling upon the lands of the pharaohs, and therefore you must die.

## Origin: The Nehekharan Civilisation and the Mortuary Cult

Nehekhara was a great river-valley civilisation south of the Old World, its history more ancient than the Empire, Bretonnia, or even the Dwarf empire. Nehekhara consisted of a series of independent city-states — Khemri, Lahmia, Zandri, Numas, Quatar, and others — each ruled by its own king, sharing a common pantheon headed by the sun-god Ptra. The Nehekharans held a distinctive attitude towards death: they believed the soul, after the body's death, would undergo judgement, and those who passed would enter the realm of the gods to receive genuine eternal life.

This belief gave birth to the Mortuary Cult. The mortuary priests mastered the techniques of mummification and performed complex rituals within the pyramids to ensure that the pharaoh's soul would enjoy the same status and wealth in the afterlife as in life. Gold, jewels, chariots, servants — everything was carried into the tomb, because the Nehekharans believed the dead would continue their rule in another world.

Yet the one who drove all of this to its extreme was Settra the Imperishable.

## Deeds

### Settra: Rise of the Imperishable

Settra the Imperishable was the greatest conqueror in Nehekharan history. Born in the city of Khemri, he unified the whole of Nehekhara within his single mortal lifespan through iron will, bringing all the city-states under one pharaoh, and crowned himself "King of Kings." His dominions stretched from the Araby coast to the foothills of the Worlds Edge Mountains. Any city-state that had resisted him in life was flattened.

But Settra had one fatal obsession: he could not accept death. He commanded the Mortuary Cult priests to research the art of true immortality, and left behind an oath that sums up his entire being: **"By my name, I vow I will conquer death itself."**

The priests did not complete the research before his death, but Settra was nonetheless prepared with the most magnificent mummification and interred in the Great Pyramid of Khemri. He left an inscription, still engraved upon his throne to this day:

> "Gold is my right; jewels are my property. The living grovel before me; the dead serve behind me. I am Settra, the Imperishable, King of Kings. Behold my works, ye mighty, and despair."

### Nagash and the Great Ritual

Nagash was the first prince of Khemri, a descendant born centuries after Settra. He was the diametric opposite of Settra: Settra sought the eternity of order and rule; Nagash sought absolute power over death itself. He learned Dark Magic (Dhar) during captivity among the Dark Elves, and upon returning to Nehekhara fused it with the rituals of the Mortuary Cult to invent necromancy — a forbidden art capable of overturning the very order of life and death.

Nagash murdered his brother to seize the throne, constructed the colossal Black Pyramid to absorb death-energy, and then launched a war of conquest across the entirety of Nehekhara. Seven city-state kings formed a coalition that besieged Khemri and defeated him the first time, but his soul fled into the Black Pyramid and returned centuries later. This time, he enacted the largest-scale necromantic ritual in history — the **Great Ritual of Awakening**.

Nagash's intent was to simultaneously reanimate every dead soul in all of Nehekhara and marshal them into his Undead legion. The ritual succeeded. The dead of an entire continent stirred beneath his incantations. Bones thrust through sand. Mummified pharaohs snapped their eyes open inside the sarcophagi of their pyramids. But before Nagash could seize control of this host of the dead, he was counterattacked by the last living army, led by King Alcadizaar the Conqueror of the city of Alidia. Alcadizaar infiltrated the Black Pyramid and slew Nagash's physical form with a sword forged from meteoric iron. Nagash fell once more.

Yet the residual effect of the Great Ritual did not fade. Nagash was dead, but his incantation still reverberated across the land. Over the centuries that followed, all the dead of Nehekhara — from the greatest pharaoh to the lowliest servant — awoke, one after another. This is the origin of the Tomb Kings: an out-of-control, over-successful necromantic ritual that returned the dead of an entire civilisation to the world.

### The Great Dynasties and Pharaohs

The social structure of the Tomb Kings rigidly preserves the hierarchy of living Nehekhara: the pharaoh (king) at the apex, then the princes (lords) and liche priests (mortuary clergy), then warriors and servants below. The only difference is that everyone is now bone.

When Settra awoke, his first act was not to mourn the passing of the living, but to look around and see a Nehekhara that had shattered into fragments after his death — the kings of the city-states he had conquered had also awoken, each declaring themselves sovereign. Settra immediately launched a war of re-unification. He defeated, in succession, the infantry legions of Numas, the naval fleets of Zandri, the desert chariots of Quatar, the vampiric descendants of Lahmia — the Lahmian royal house, tainted by Nagash's power, had mutated into the first vampires long before the mass awakening of the Tomb Kings.

Not all Tomb Kings bow to Settra. The Ember-Pharaoh of Ka-Sabar, the Mechanist-Queen of Lybaras, the ancient sacred host of Mahrak — every city-state has its own pharaoh, its own ambitions, and its own list of people it wants to destroy first upon waking. But the fearsome thing about Settra is this: no matter the opponent, he can, in the end, make them kneel. Not through any magical compulsion, but because over the long span of Nehekharan history, the title "King of Kings" has belonged to one man alone — in life, and in death.

### The Armies of the Tomb Kings

The armies of the Tomb Kings are not slaves driven by terror, but ancient regiments that retain full discipline and tactical memory:

**Skeleton Warriors**: The bedrock of the Tomb King host. Armed with bronze-verdigris spears and curved khopesh blades, advancing in dense blocks, their tactical discipline far exceeds that of ordinary undead. Every skeleton was once a soldier who served in the armies of Nehekhara.

**Chariot Legions**: The core striking arm of Nehekharan warfare. Light chariots, drawn by pairs of skeletal steeds, with crews wielding composite bows and spears, specialise in high-speed flank attacks. Settra's favourite mode of combat was personally driving his chariot across the enemy line.

**Ushabti Constructs**: The supreme achievement of the Mortuary Cult — semi-mechanical, semi-magical constructs carved from great stone in the images of the gods, imbued by the priests with soul-fragments of fallen warriors. Far more powerful than ordinary skeletons, they fight with giant khopesh blades or ritual staves, each step cracking the earth.

**Warsphinxes**: Colossal beasts cast from stone, bone, and incantation, capable of carrying an archer-platform or a command dais upon their backs for the pharaoh to direct the battle in person.

**Bone Giants**: Colossal skeletons assembled from the remains of multiple warriors, wielding great bows or halberds, each arrow capable of transfixing an entire rank of enemies.

**Screaming Skull Catapults**: Siege engines that hurl fire-wreathed skulls saturated with death-magic; the impact zone triggers the dual effect of terror and conflagration.

**Liche Priests**: The priests of the Mortuary Cult persist after death. Their corpses have been made into mummies, and they continue to carry out the sole duty of their existence: serving the pharaoh. Liche Priests command the Lore of Nehekhara, the Mortuary Cult's own magical tradition — an ancient system independent of the Eight Winds, working through ritual incantations to summon sandstorms, reinforce bone, and accelerate chariot charges.

### War Against Nagash

The relationship between the Tomb Kings and Nagash can only be expressed in four words: irreconcilable unto death.

In the eyes of the Tomb Kings, Nagash is not simply a "fallen descendant" or a "historical traitor." He is the direct culprit who destroyed Nehekhara. In life, he usurped the throne, butchered cities, and twisted death into a weapon. After death, he used the Great Ritual to violently drag every dead soul in Nehekhara back into the world. The Tomb Kings do not hate that they became undead — they hate that Nagash did not let them rest. In their view, had Nagash never existed, Nehekhara could have sustained its glory, and they, the pharaohs, could have enjoyed true immortality in the realm of the gods, rather than being trapped in dry bones wandering the world for eternity.

This is why, when Settra faced the Chaos invasion, he could temporarily ally with rival city-state kings. When vampires, Imperials, Greenskins, and other powers appeared in Nehekhara, he could treat them all as secondary threats. But Nagash — Nagash is always number one.

## Fate & Present Status

In the End Times, as the world approached its terminus and Chaos flooded in from both poles, Settra faced a question he had never confronted in life: how to preserve Nehekhara when the world itself was being destroyed.

The answer was bitter. He chose to submit to Nagash — a choice he would never have contemplated while alive. To gain the strength to oppose Chaos, Settra folded his Undead legions into Nagash's grand alliance of death. But he never truly accepted it.

When Nagash, at the decisive battle of the End Times, demanded every last ounce of strength from everyone, Settra pulled his forces at the crucial moment. He had no intention of being anyone's chess piece — including the god of death's. The famous line — "Settra does not serve" — is not merely a declaration. It is the bone-deep pride that sustained him for thousands of years and still would not die.

Nagash was an object of transaction born of despair, but Settra never regarded him as a master. After the End Times, the Old World destroyed, the Mortal Realms formed in the void. Settra and the death-culture of Nehekhara were not directly incorporated into the order of the Age of Sigmar as an independent race, but rather absorbed into the Realm of Death (Shyish) under Nagash's dominion — a silent, cold conclusion, fitting for a pharaoh who had kept his silence for thousands of years.

## Verdict

The Tomb Kings are among the closest things to pure "tragedy" in Warhammer Fantasy. They did not become undead by choice. They were not corrupted by Chaos. They made no dark bargain. They simply died too early, and a madman's ambition violently dragged them back from their rest.

But their story is not merely one of misery. The Tomb Kings preserved the order, the glory, the dignity, and the ambition of life — and simultaneously preserved life's arrogance, stubbornness, cruelty, and belligerence. Settra is not a victim to be pitied. He is a man who, after dying, still insists the whole world kneel before him again. What the Tomb Kings demonstrated to the world is this: death does not necessarily mean an ending. Sometimes, it merely turns a person into the purest version of their own nature.

In the Undead spectrum of the Warhammer world, the Tomb Kings occupy a singular position. They are not the soulless tools manufactured by Nagash. They are not the blood-drinking predators who sustain life by consuming others. They are the skeletal remains of a civilisation. Their tragedy lies in the fact that they remember clearly who they once were, and know with equal clarity that they can never be that again. And the only thing they can do is carry that memory forward — and continue to rule, continue to wage war, continue to command the world to kneel.

Settra's inscription is still read aloud by the winds across the yellow sands of Nehekhara: **"Behold my works, ye mighty, and despair."** He delivered — just in a way he never expected.
