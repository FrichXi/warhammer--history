# 14 · Beastmen

## Overview

In the narrative of human civilisation, the forest is the savage frontier — an obstacle to be felled, tilled, and walled. But there is one force that has never accepted this narrative: the Beastmen. They are the beast-children of Chaos, the eternal, unsleeping threat within the forests of the Old World. They make no demands of civilisation, conduct no negotiations, offer no compromise. They have only a single instinctive mission: turn the towns built of stone back into mud and carrion.

The dark treeline an Empire farmer sees at the end of his field — any day, tens of thousands of Bestigor warbands may pour forth from it. They come without sound, leave behind nothing but rubble, as though civilisation were merely a brief hallucination. This is the Beastmen: not an enemy, but a natural disaster.

## Origin

The origin of the Beastmen, stripped to its core, is the sustained pollution of nature by Chaos. The Old World has two moons — the normal moon, Mannslieb, and that baleful green moon, Morrslieb. Morrslieb is no ordinary moon. It is a window through which the Realm of Chaos bleeds into the material world; every beam of its unholy light is saturated with undiluted, primordial Chaos energy.

When a human infant is born beneath the full light of Morrslieb, it may grow horns, hooves, and fur, and become a "mutant" — a Gor. The parents will typically throw the thing into the forest, and the forest happens to be full of waiting kin. The same logic applies to animals: wild goats, bulls, and wolves bathed in Morrslieb's light will twist into upright, walking monsters. Thus the Beastmen are not a race that was "created." They are the ongoing, casually generated by-product of Chaos. So long as Morrslieb hangs in the sky, the Beastmen can never be killed off.

There is another, more ancient account of their origins involving the Old Ones. It is said that when Chaos first flooded into the world, the Old Ones attempted to engineer a self-adapting biological weapon, but the collapse of the polar gates interrupted the process. The half-finished genomes left on the planet iterated themselves under Chaos corruption and ultimately produced the forms of the Beastmen. This theory remains unproven, but it would explain why the Beastmen feel an instinctive hatred for the ruins of the Old Ones — their very existence is an abandoned, failed experiment.

## Deeds

### Society

The social organisation of the Beastmen is staggeringly primitive — but far from without order. That order rests on a single word: strength.

The supreme leader of a Beastmen warband is the **Beastlord**. A Beastlord is not elected. He does not inherit. He hacks his way to the top — whoever kills the previous Beastlord in the tribal melée becomes the new one. The process is so savage that even Chaos Warriors would raise an eyebrow: a Gor challenging a Beastlord never ambushes him; it must be a frontal impact — with horns, with hooves, with bare hands — until one is dead. The defeated Beastlord is usually torn apart; his bones are nailed to the Herdstone by the new lord.

Below the Beastlord, the Beastmen hierarchy is crude but effective. The **Bestigors** are the strongest, the main battle strength — bodies of solid muscle and horn, a single adult Bestigor can punch through an Imperial state-troop shieldwall with its bare hands. The **Ungors** are the hornless underclass, handling menial labour, corpse-collection, and serving as meat-shields during charges. At the very bottom are the **Warhounds** — hunting dogs twisted by Chaos, not even fully Beastmen, purely expendable creatures loosed to bite.

The central sacred object of the entire society is the **Herdstone**. A Herdstone is a great boulder consecrated with blood and magic by a Bray-Shaman, erected in the darkest part of the forest. Every Beastman hammers the skulls, weapons, and armour-fragments of slain enemies onto this rock, layer upon layer, until the stone itself is sheathed in rotting flesh and rusted iron, a stinking totem. Every Morrslieb full-moon, the Beastmen gather around the Herdstone to conduct blood-rituals, dancing in frenzy beneath the unholy light, their strength surging. Imperial scholars generally agree: if a Herdstone appears in a region, that region can know no peace again, unless the stone and the entire forest around it are burned together.

Special mention must be made of the **Bray-Shamans**. They are the very few Beastmen who possess intelligence. The average Gor's intellect runs somewhere between "want to hit" and "going to hit right now," but a Bray-Shaman can commune with the Chaos Gods, interpret Morrslieb's unholy light, and channel the raw, feral magic of the Beastmen — the Lore of the Wild — to direct the herd's berserk energies. Bray-Shamans do not rule the tribe, but they are far more dangerous than any Beastlord: the Beastlord only wants to kill people; the Bray-Shaman understands why to kill them, whom to kill, and how to kill them in the way most pleasing to Chaos.

### Military

A Beastmen army, at first glance, is a mob of frenzied animals. But Imperial generals have learned through bitterest experience: do not underestimate their tactical diversity.

**Gors** are the infantry core, the most frequently encountered. They carry cleavers, axes, and great clubs. They have no concept of formation, but their explosive power is terrifying. A Gor-tide can drown the first rank of an Empire spear-line in three minutes.

**Minotaurs** are the most terrifying shock force among the Beastmen. These bull-headed giants tower over three metres; their two fists can smash a city gate. A single Minotaur can tear a breach in an Empire infantry line by itself. Minotaurs are so blood-mad they border on mindless; in battle they will crush one man after another while stuffing the flesh into their mouths — not from any need to eat, but from pure instinct. They do not even consider themselves Beastmen, regarding Gors as merely food or temporary teammates.

**Centigors** provide mobility. These half-man, half-beast monsters run faster through the forest than Imperial cavalry, and possess an immense thirst for alcohol — if they discover drink on a battlefield, they will stop and drain it dry before resuming the charge. This unreliability drives even Beastlords to despair, but the Centigor's charge impact is genuinely irreplaceable.

**Harpies** are air-scouts and assault forces. These winged female monsters dive from the air to snatch isolated soldiers and then drop the corpses from altitude back onto the enemy formation to sow terror. Their intelligence is lower than a Gor's; they are driven almost entirely by hunger.

**Dragon Ogres** are not, strictly speaking, Beastmen. They are creatures from an ancient age — Dragons who signed pacts with Chaos. But because they frequently operate alongside Beastmen warbands and hold the same view of civilisation as food, the Empire's classification system lumps them under "Beastmen and Symbiotic Monsters." A single Dragon Ogre can face down an artillery piece and a knightly formation simultaneously on the same battlefield.

It must be stressed that Beastmen tactics are not limited to "charge." They are among the finest ambush and night-fighting races in the world. When the Empire marches through a forest, the vanguard will notice the trees are moving — then the trees reveal horns, and the horns plunge into men. The marching speed of a warherd is also absurdly fast: a Beastman host can appear in Nordland today and be within sight of Middenheim's walls three nights later. Mankind perpetually underestimates their mobility through the woodlands.

### Famous Beastlords

**Gorthor the Beastlord** is universally considered the most destructive Beastlord in history. In the sixteenth century IC, Gorthor unified the tens of thousands of warherds in the Forest of Shadows and launched the great slaughter known as "Gorthor's Rampage." He destroyed multiple towns in Ostland and Hochland in succession; no force could hold back his tide. Gorthor was said to be a foredoomed beast — on the night of his birth, Morrslieb cast a column of unholy light straight down upon his herd-nest. After his death, the warherds splintered into dozens of small bands, but Gorthor's skull remains to this day mounted on the oldest Herdstone in the Forest of Shadows, treated as a holy relic.

**Khazrak the One-Eye** was the Beastlord who tormented Mankind most during the Empire's middle and late periods. He laired in the Drakwald Forest and waged a decades-long private war against the Elector Count of Middenland, Boris Todbringer. Boris gouged out one of Khazrak's eyes; Khazrak ambushed Boris's carriage train multiple times and nearly succeeded in killing him. What made Khazrak terrifying was that he learned. He studied human marching routes, supply patterns, and defensive weaknesses — a rare strategic brain among the Beastmen. The feud between the two men became one of the longest-running grudges in the Old World, ending only with the End Times.

**Morghur the Shadowgave** cannot be classified under any ordinary definition of "Beastlord." He is Chaos's favoured child, regarded as a tentacle of Chaos extending into the material world. Morghur's physical form cannot truly die — each time he is killed, he is reborn in some other forest, accompanied by the frenzied mutation of all living things around him. He does not even need to organise an army: wherever he is, the earth rots, animals warp, humans go mad. Morghur has a blood-feud with the Wood Elves, because Athel Loren considers him the foulest source of pollution. The Wood Elf lords have assassinated him repeatedly, but every time he returns — like a corrosion that will not wash off.

Additionally, there is **Taurox the Brass Bull**, a Minotaur war-king whose entire body was sheathed in living brass — a favourite of Khorne. His whole form was remade by Chaos into an impermeable skin of brass, proof against all blades, and he was said to have slaughtered tens of thousands on the battlefield without suffering a single wound.

## Fate & Present Status

When Archaon led the Chaos hosts south in full force and the powers of the Old World toppled one after another, the Beastmen greeted the most glorious and violent moment in their race's history.

In the structure of the End Times, the Beastmen were Chaos's natural vanguard. They needed no recruitment, no persuasion — the moment Archaon's horn sounded, every forest in the Old World trembled. From the Reikwald to the Forest of Shadows, from the Drakwald to the fringes of Athel Loren, hundreds of thousands of warherds simultaneously surged out of the treeline and fell upon the towns of Men, the castles of Bretonnia, the strongholds of the Dwarfs.

The Beastmen offensive was chaotic in nature — they were not a disciplined force under Archaon's command, but a primal storm triggered by the Winds of Chaos. Yet that was precisely their value: while the regular armies of the Empire faced Archaon's iron tide head-on, the Beastmen chewed through the Empire's villages, supply lines, and civilians from behind.

The most brutal battlefield was Athel Loren. In the End Times, Chaos corruption penetrated the sacred wood of the Wood Elves. The trees rotted. The Dryads went mad. Orion led the Wild Hunt against the surging warherds and fought to his death. Ariel was wound in shadow. Athel Loren — that living forest that had once struck terror into every invader — was finally trampled to pieces by Beastmen and Chaos together.

But the Beastmen's "victory" did not last. When the Old World collapsed entirely and the Mortal Realms took form, the Beastmen discovered they had merely moved from one destroyed stage to another, larger one. In the Mortal Realms, they endure under the name of "Brayherds," continuing to do the only thing they know how to do: find the traces of civilisation, and reduce them to ruin.

## Verdict

The Beastmen occupy a unique position in the Warhammer world. They are not the "great villains" — that role belongs to Archaon and the Chaos Gods. They are not the "conspirators" — that role belongs to the Skaven and the vampires. They are a pure, unarguable force of nature. You do not need to understand why they hate you, any more than you need to understand why it rains or thunders. When you meet them, fight or die. There is no third option.

The irony is that the Beastmen consider themselves the defenders of the "natural order." In their view, human cities are stony cancers, Dwarf strongholds are scars upon the earth, and Elven magical forests are a perversion of the true forest. They believe they are "restoring the world to its original state" — though this "original state" has itself been thoroughly twisted by Chaos. It is a paradox that would choke a philosopher: the most unnatural creatures on the planet claiming to defend nature.

In the framework of the Warhammer annals, the necessity of the Beastmen's record is this: they are the bottom layer of the Chaos ecosystem. Without the Beastmen, the Empire and the Dwarfs would not know what it means for the enemy to be "everywhere." Without the Beastmen, the forests of the Old World would be mere scenery. It is their presence that turned the wilderness into a threat, that made every tree a place where death might be hiding. In this sense, the Beastmen defined the Old World — not by defining its order, but by defining its boundary.
