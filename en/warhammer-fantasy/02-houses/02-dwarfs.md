# 02 · Dwarfs

## Overview

The Dwarfs are among the oldest races of the Old World — and the most stubborn, the most grudging, and the most reliable. Their realm is called the Karaz Ankor, the "Everlasting Realm" in the Dwarf tongue. In the Golden Age their underground tunnels spanned the entire length of the Worlds Edge Mountains, karak after karak, the sound of the anvil ringing day and night. But today's Dwarfs live in the last light of that glory — karaks fall one by one, the population dwindles year on year, and only the Book of Grudges grows ever thicker. They know they will eventually perish, but they have no intention of making it easy.

## Origin

The Dwarfs are not the product of natural evolution. Their ancestor gods — Grungni, god of the forge, and Grimnir, god of warriors — crafted the first Dwarfs with their own hands deep within the mountains. Grungni taught them mining, smelting, and the carving of runes. Grimnir taught them to grip the axe, form the shieldwall, and never retreat. The third ancestor goddess, Valaya, taught them brewing, hearth-building, and the defence of the home. The three Ancestor Gods shaped not merely the Dwarf body, but an entire uncompromising code of survival.

To the Dwarfs, the mountain tunnels were not obstacles — they were home. They dug downward, inward, forward, turning the entire Worlds Edge Mountains into a single colossal underground metropolis.

## Deeds

### Runesmiths

Dwarfs are innately immune to the Winds of Magic — they cannot wield sorcery as Elves or Men do. But this does not mean they lack magic. They fix magic into matter, and that is rune-magic. Grungni struck the first rune with his own hand. Every Runesmith since has transmitted the art blow by blow — not through books, but from father to son, master to apprentice.

Runes are not decoration. A warding rune on a shield lets the bearer absorb a Troll's charge head-on. A burning rune on an axe sets the target alight with every strike. The mightiest rune-weapon of all — Ghal Maraz, borne by Sigmar — was forged by Alaric the Mad and remains the unassailable symbol of the Empire's sovereignty.

Runesmiths hold status in Dwarf society nearly equal to kings. But as the ages pass, the craft is dying. Every master who falls takes with him several runes that no one will ever strike again. This is among the deepest Dwarf anxieties — technology is not progressing; it is receding.

### The Engineers Guild

If runes are the Dwarf soul, gunpowder and steam are the fist. The Engineers Guild possesses the most advanced firearms technology in the Old World: handguns, cannon, organ guns, flame cannons, and the signature Gyrocopter — a steam-powered single-pilot flyer that rains lead across the battlefield. The human engineers of Nuln have learned a few tricks, but the real goods remain in Dwarf hands.

Relations between the Engineers Guild and the Runesmiths are delicate. The old-guard Runesmiths look down on these "powder-playing upstarts"; the Engineers consider runes too slow, too conservative, and impossible to mass-produce. The two factions may argue, but when battle is joined, the cannon fire and the runes do not fail to arrive together.

### The Book of Grudges (Dammaz Kron)

The Book of Grudges is the most fundamental institution in Dwarf society — bar none. Every slight against the Dwarfs, great or small, must be recorded. A merchant's overdue debt, a human village's mockery of a Dwarf smith, an Elven army shaving the beard of a Dwarf ambassador — all go in. To the Dwarf mind, forgetting an insult is not magnanimity; it is degeneracy.

Every recorded grudge has a price. That price must be assessed and exacted by the Dwarf king or the High King — it may be gold, it may be territory, it may be war. If a grudge goes unsettled, it does not vanish. It passes to the descendants. Some grudges have lain in the book for two thousand years. The Dwarfs are waiting.

The War of the Beard is the Book's most famous product. Around 2000 years before the Imperial Calendar, the Phoenix King of the High Elves sent envoys to a Dwarf stronghold. Through some diplomatic slight, the Elves shaved the Dwarf ambassadors' beards — the ultimate insult to Dwarf dignity. The High King roared for war, and that war lasted over four hundred years. Both sides' high commands were nearly wiped out. The end of the Dwarf Golden Age began here.

Dwarf grudging is not mere temper — it is a near-pathological fixation. But it is precisely this fixation that refuses surrender even in the direst straits, because surrender would mean adding yet one more unpayable debt.

### The Karaks and the Decline

Dwarf strongholds — karaks — are engineering marvels. Each is a three-dimensional city carved inside a mountain: mines and forges at the bottom, living quarters and halls in the middle, command chambers and air-docks at the top. Karaks were once linked by the Underway, a subterranean transit network stretching thousands of miles. In the Golden Age, a Dwarf could walk from Karak Eight Peaks in the far south to Karak Kadrin in the far north without ever glimpsing the sky.

Then everything collapsed.

Around 1500 years before the Imperial Calendar, a series of great earthquakes tore the Worlds Edge Mountains apart. Vast sections of the Underway caved in. Karak lost contact with karak. Countless Dwarfs were entombed in the rubble and starved to death. This was the Time of Woes in Dwarf history.

The earthquakes had barely subsided when the Greenskins arrived. Orcs and Goblins flooded through the fissures into the deeps, overrunning Karak Eight Peaks, Karak Drazh, and several other major strongholds. Then the Skaven boiled up from even deeper below, seizing collapsed tunnels and emptied cities. The Dwarfs, once the hegemons of the Golden Age, became cornered beasts fighting guerrilla actions among the ruins.

For two thousand years thereafter, every Dwarf karak bled out slowly. Some fell to Greenskins and remain unrecovered to this day. Others were hollowed out by the Skaven. Others were lost outright in Chaos invasions. The population shrank, runes faded, and engineering blueprints were burned battle by battle. By roughly 2500 IC, the number of karaks still in Dwarf hands was painfully small.

But the Dwarfs never ceased striving to reclaim what was lost. The Engineers Guild, in the crisis, produced Gyrocopters and torpedo boats, enabling the Dwarfs to strike back handsomely even from a position of disadvantage.

### The Alliance with the Empire

The alliance between Dwarfs and Men is one of the most durable political structures in the Old World — spanning over two thousand years, it has never truly broken.

It began with Sigmar himself. In 15 years before the Imperial Calendar, the Dwarf High King Kurgan Ironbeard was taken captive by Greenskins. The young Sigmar infiltrated the enemy camp alone and rescued him. In return, Kurgan had the Runesmith Alaric forge Ghal Maraz for Sigmar and swore the Eternal Oath — that Man and Dwarf would never forsake one another.

After that, the Dwarfs taught Men engineering, firearms, and metallurgy. The Empire's gunpowder, handguns, and cannon all trace their lineage to Dwarf engineers. In return, the Empire received Dwarf heavy infantry and artillery at every existential crisis. At Black Fire Pass, it was Dwarf ironbreakers fighting alongside Sigmar's coalition that shattered the Greenskin host.

Nothing embodies this alliance better than Gotrek Gurnisson and Felix Jaeger — a Dwarf Slayer seeking atonement in death, and a human poet. The two walked the length of the Old World together — fought Dragons, slew Skaven, ventured deep into the Chaos Wastes. Felix recorded every tale; Gotrek tried in every battle to find a worthy doom (but he was too strong to die). They represent the highest form of the Dwarf-Man alliance: not interest, but mutual recognition.

## Fate & Present Status

In the End Times, there were no miracles for the Dwarfs. The Chaos legions flooded down from the north; the Skaven launched a full offensive underground. Karak after karak fell. High King Thorgrim Grudgebearer held Karaz-a-Karak (the Everpeak) to the last, and was consumed along with his throne hall by Chaos.

Gotrek Gurnisson — the Slayer who had sought death his entire life — finally got his wish. In the End Times, he took a rune-axe into the heart of a Daemon host and died fighting. Felix fell beside him. The last epic of the Dwarfs was not a story of survival — it was a story of dying on one's feet.

After the destruction of the Old World, the Dwarf legacy divided into two branches. The Kharadron Overlords — sky-Dwarfs, sailing armada-vessels through the skies of the Realm of Metal, worshipping profit above all. The Fyreslayers — volcano-Dwarfs, inheritors of Grimnir's bloodline, forever seeking the scattered gold fragments of their ancestor god in the heart of volcanoes. Both carry the old Dwarf temper: stubbornness, expertise, and a heart that never stops burning.

## Verdict

The Dwarfs are one of the most tragic races in Warhammer Fantasy. It is not that they never knew glory — they knew it too early, and then watched it all collapse with their eyes open. Greenskins, Skaven, Chaos, earthquakes — each time they rose and fought again, and each time they lost something that could never be recovered. Yet Dwarfs never indulge in self-pity, never negotiate, never retreat except tactically. They are the most reliable allies in the Old World and the most terrifying enemies — terrifying because their memory of grievance is not personal but species-wide.

Viewed through the grand logic of the Warhammer universe, the Dwarfs represent "Order" in its most extreme form. No compromise, no forgiveness, no forgetting — these qualities come off as pig-headed obstinacy in peacetime. In the age of apocalypse, they are the spine. The Dwarfs did not fall because they were weak. They fell because they were still standing there when everyone else had stepped back.
