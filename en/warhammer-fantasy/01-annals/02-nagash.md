# 02 · Nagash, the Great Necromancer

## Overview

Nagash, called the Great Necromancer, known to the world as the Lord of Undeath.

> Nagash was, Nagash is, Nagash shall be.

This was Nagash's self-proclaimed tripartite declaration — a mortal announcing to the cosmos that he was worthy of the title of death-god. He was a native of Khemri, the great city of ancient Nehekhara, first serving as High Priest of the Mortuary Cult, later usurping the throne to become King of Khemri. Endowed with prodigious gifts, his mastery of magic reached heights no mortal had attained. He invented necromancy itself, opened the path to raising the dead, brewed the Elixir of Life, and indirectly gave rise to the vampire race. His existence was one of repeated ruin and inevitable return — every defeat merely set the stage for the next resurrection. At the End Times, he devoured the Wind of Death and ascended to true godhood. In the Age of Sigmar, Nagash rules the Realm of Shyish as master of the Grand Alliance of Death, alternately allied with and estranged from Sigmar, locked in eternal contest with the Chaos Gods for the souls of the world.

## Origin

Nagash was the firstborn son of Khetep, King of Khemri in the ancient empire of Nehekhara. His mother died in childbirth, and the people regarded him as an ill omen. His father Khetep despised him for it, reserving all affection for his younger son, Thutep.

From his earliest years, Nagash stood apart from others — silent, withdrawn, fixated with an unnatural intensity on the mysteries of life and death. As he grew, he immersed himself in the Dark Arts, spending his days and nights in worship of the god of death, probing the fate of souls within the funeral rites. Khetep perceived his nature. Although Nagash was the firstborn, Khetep refused him the throne and instead appointed him High Priest of the Mortuary Cult. When Khetep died, Thutep became king. Nagash chafed within the temple precincts, his resentment immense — but he swallowed it. He did not act.

Later, Nagash led an expeditionary fleet across the sea, hoping to win fame through foreign conquest. The fleet was intercepted by a Dark Elf armada. Every man aboard was taken captive and shipped to the slave-pits beneath Naggaroth. The mines were lightless, airless, a place where mortal men were sent to die. But Nagash's extraordinary gifts drew the attention of three Dark Elf sorceresses. They saw the rare quality of his bones, and in secret they taught him the arts of Dark Magic — the technique of gathering the impurities within the Eight Winds, twisting the very essence of magic itself. Its power was immense, its instability lethal; few among the Elves dared study it. Nagash drank it all in. When civil strife erupted among the Dark Elves, he escaped in the chaos and crossed the sea back to his homeland, carrying those forbidden arts in his skull.

Once back in Khemri, Nagash shed all pretence. He used Dark Magic to murder his brother Thutep, publicly declaring the death a sudden illness, and then proclaimed himself King of Khemri. With the throne secured, he bent the full resources of the kingdom to a single labour: fusing Dark Magic with the ancient funeral rites of Nehekhara. The result was a force the world had never known — necromancy. A forbidden art that could command the souls of the dead and compel the corpses of the departed, it struck terror into the living and denied rest to the dead.

Nagash then raised the Black Pyramid at the heart of Khemri. A colossal ziggurat of obsidian, its interior laced with magical matrices, it was designed to draw in the energies of the dead and concentrate the power of death itself. On the day the Pyramid was completed, the sky howled, the ghosts wailed, and every priest in Nehekhara felt a chill rising from the deep earth.

## Deeds

### First Empire and First Fall

Once Nagash had mastered necromancy and obtained the secret of the Elixir of Life — a compound of human blood and exotic herbs that required perpetual consumption to sustain immortality — his ambition swelled without limit. He began to conquer with armies of the dead. The neighbouring kings were horrified. A coalition of seven city-states — led by the kings of Lahmia, Ashur, Zandri, Quatar, and others — joined forces and besieged Khemri.

The war lasted years. Bloodshed and horror flooded Nehekhara. Nagash summoned tens of thousands of dead to fight for him, but the weight of numbers was against him. On the final day, the coalition breached the walls of Khemri and the assembled kings destroyed Nagash's mortal body. But his soul was not extinguished. Long before, at the Black Pyramid's completion, he had woven a passage of escape into its matrix. His body died; his soul fled into the Pyramid and lay dormant.

### Nagashizzar and the Second Rising

For centuries, Nagash's soul drifted across the cursed lands beyond the World's Edge Mountains, drawing strength back to itself. There, among the peaks, he raised a bleak fortress and named it Nagashizzar. Under an eternal ceiling of black cloud, legions of the dead toiled day and night, mining warpstone to feed Nagash an unending stream of magical power.

With his strength restored, Nagash launched his second invasion of Nehekhara. His undead host now dwarfed anything he had commanded before. City after city crumbled under the tide of walking corpses.

It was in this period that Nagash undertook the largest magical ritual in the history of the Warhammer world — the Great Ritual of Awakening. Using the Black Pyramid as the fulcrum of a continent-spanning spell, he intended to awaken every soul that had ever died in Nehekhara and bind them into his undead legion. The ritual was initiated, but its power far exceeded his control. Torrents of death-energy rampaged across the land, and the dead within countless tombs began to stir.

What he did not anticipate was that, at the critical moment — while Nagash poured his entire being into controlling the ritual — Alcadizaar the Conqueror, the last living king of Nehekhara, launched a counterattack that was in every practical sense a suicide charge. Alcadizaar rode alone into the depths of the Black Pyramid, gripping a blade forged from meteoric iron, and drove it through Nagash's heart as the necromancer's attention was split.

Nagash's body collapsed once more. His second empire ended. But the aftershock of the Great Ritual did not fade. Over the centuries that followed, the dead of Nehekhara rose from their tombs — and they did so with their memories, personalities, and ambitions intact, becoming undead with conscious will. This was the origin of the Tomb Kings. In a sense, Nagash lost, but his spell reshaped the entire trajectory of Nehekharan history.

### The Nine Books of Nagash

The most famous legacy Nagash left the world is the nine volumes of magical lore he composed over a lifetime, known to scholars as the *Nine Books of Nagash*. Spanning the full corpus of Dark Magic and necromancy, from first principles to the highest forbidden rites, each volume was bound in human skin and inked in human blood. The pages thrum with fragments of Nagash's own will. Any mortal who reads them is gradually hollowed out and remade as an instrument of his purpose.

The Nine Books were scattered across the world during Nagash's successive falls, and through the ages they became the most coveted treasures of every necromancer. A single book could make a warlord; several could shake the world. The origin of the vampire race, the awakening of the Tomb Kings, the great undead wars that plagued the Empire — trace any of them back, and you find the Nine Books.

### Third Rising and the War with the Skaven

Early in the Imperial Calendar, Nagash's soul coalesced once more. He had failed repeatedly, yet his ambition for godhood never dimmed. He struck a pact with the Council of Thirteen of the Under-Empire of the Skaven, promising enormous quantities of warpstone in exchange for their aid in rebuilding Nagashizzar.

But Nagash's nature admitted no lasting alliance. The moment the Skaven fulfilled their end, he tore up the pact and slaughtered their emissaries inside the fortress. The outrage convulsed the entire Under-Empire. The Council of Thirteen dispatched a vast Skaven army under Warlord Kritislik Fang-Edge to assault Nagashizzar.

What followed was one of the most savage wars ever fought beneath the earth. The two forces battled across the mountain range for nearly two thousand years, the fortress changing hands again and again, the dead piling mountain-high. In 1111 IC, Kritislik broke into the depths of Nagashizzar wielding the Skaven artefact known as the Fellblade — a sword forged and saturated with warpstone, whose venom can rot divinity itself — and struck Nagash a crippling blow. Nagash sank into silence, and for over a thousand years there was no trace of him.

### The Origin of Vampires

It is worth noting that although Nagash did not personally create vampires, he became the progenitor of their race by indirect means. At the height of his power, he had shared the formula of the Elixir of Life with Neferata, Queen of Lahmia. Neferata's gifts were no less than Nagash's own. She refined his formula and created the more potent Elixir of Blood. When she and her followers drank from it, they attained true immortality and superhuman power — and with it, the eternal curse of blood-thirst.

This was the origin of the vampire race. Every one of the six great vampire bloodlines — von Carstein, Blood Dragon, Lahmian, Necrarch, Strigoi, and Jade-Blooded — descends from Neferata's work, and tracing further back, from the evolution of Nagash's own sorcery. In this light, although Nagash regarded himself as the master of all souls, the vampire children he inadvertently sired have always harboured their own ambitions, and have never truly submitted to him.

## Fate & Present Status

### The End Times and Ascension

In 2526 IC, the End Times descended upon the Old World. Arkhan the Black, Nagash's most loyal servant, aided by the vampire Mannfred von Carstein, gathered Nagash's scattered relics and implements of power and conducted the rite of resurrection upon the ruins of Nagashizzar.

This time, Nagash not only returned to the world — he seized the heaven-sent opportunity of the End Times, when the Winds of Magic blew in unprecedented chaos. With necromantic mastery beyond all rivals, he consumed the entire Wind of Death, fusing his being with the essence of death itself. He succeeded — not reborn as a mortal, but as a god.

Yet fate played one last joke at his expense. In the very moment of his apotheosis, Ptra, the sun-god of Nehekhara, joined by the full pantheon of the Nehekharan gods, dragged Nagash into the depths of the Realm of Chaos and imprisoned him within the Realm of Shyish. He had become a god — trapped in a god's prison.

### The Age of Sigmar

In the Age of Sigmar, Nagash at last broke free of his bonds and established himself as the supreme deity of the Realm of Shyish. He rules over the final destination of all mortal souls in the Mortal Realms, presides over the Grand Alliance of Death, and stands as counterweight to Sigmar's Pantheon of Order.

Sigmar's creation of the Stormcast Eternals — recycling the souls of fallen heroes into immortal warriors — was, in Nagash's view, nothing less than stealing souls from his plate. The two have clashed repeatedly, sometimes in open warfare, sometimes in shadowed manoeuvre. Simultaneously, Nagash is locked in an eternal contest with the Chaos Gods. Whether a soul passes into death, into Chaos, or is taken by Sigmar — that is the most fundamental wager of the Mortal Realms.

## Verdict

Nagash's life is a specimen of ambition and will entwined to the ultimate limit. Born a prince who was shoved into the priesthood, taught Dark Magic as a slave in a foreign mine, rising and falling again and again from the ruin of his own body, finally ascending to godhood and dominion over the realm of death — no other figure in the history of the Old World has traced such a trajectory.

But examine his methods, and every foundation reduces to a single word: self. Murdering his brother for the throne — that is without virtue. Betraying his allies — that is without honour. Treating all living creatures as chaff — that is without mercy. Using the dead as weapons — that is without reverence. He trusts no one — not the living, not the servants to whom he entrusts his survival, not even the children his own magic brought into being. That he ascended in the End Times at all is owed entirely to Arkhan's thousands of years of faithful labour. But given Nagash's nature, to that loyalty he has never shown a trace of gratitude.

Nagash's deepest contradiction is this: he is the incarnation of death, yet he fears his own ending more than anyone. Every defeat deepened his obsession with immortality; every resurrection intensified his terror of death. That terror drives him forward on the path to godhood and will not let him turn, and it ensures that even as a deity, his heart will never know peace.

Compare him with Sigmar: Sigmar's path from man to god was one of sublimation — won through sacrifice, deeds, and the willing following of the people. Nagash's path from man to monster to god was a perversion — won through betrayal, slaughter, and bartering the souls of the world. Both started as mortals; they walked opposite roads to divinity.

Later chroniclers often ask: Is Nagash an irredeemable evil, or an excessively honest worshipper of power? The answer may lie in the fact that he does not measure himself by good or evil at all. In his eyes, there are only two kinds of beings in the world: masters and slaves. And he chose to become the master of everything, even if the price is sitting alone forever on a throne built of bones.
