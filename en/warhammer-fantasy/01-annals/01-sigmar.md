# 01 · Sigmar, Founder of the Empire

## Overview

Sigmar Heldenhammer — Sigmar the Hammer-Bearer — founder of the Empire of Man in the Old World, born among the Unberogen tribe. As a mortal, he united the twelve tribes, forged the institutions of the Empire, expanded its frontiers, and repelled its enemies. After a fifty-year reign, he abdicated and walked east into the mountains, never to be seen again in the flesh. The Empire he built endured for over two thousand years; human civilisation survived on the foundations he laid, and never broke. In the End Times, Sigmar ascended as the God-King of Azyr, commanding the Stormcast Eternals in eternal war against the Chaos Gods. Of all the emperors the Old World ever knew, none surpassed him in the labour of founding.

## Origin

Sigmar was born around 30 years before the Imperial Calendar. His father, Björn, was chieftain of the Unberogen tribe. His mother was attacked by a Greenskin raiding party on the eve of labour; the twins were delivered with the umbilical cord wrapped around their necks, which the tribe took as an ill omen. Björn did not abandon the child, and raised him with the full honours due a chieftain's son.

As a youth, Sigmar killed a giant boar with his bare hands, earning the name "Hammer-Bearer." Even before reaching manhood, he displayed martial prowess beyond anything his people had seen.

Around 15 years before the Imperial Calendar, an Orc warband under the Warboss "Gnash-Tooth" swept into Unberogen lands. Björn fell in battle. Sigmar, consumed by grief and fury, charged alone into the enemy line. Wielding his father's longsword and a great axe torn from an Orc's grip, he carved a path from one end of the battle line to the other, and the Greenskin host broke. The Unberogen tribe acclaimed him king. Sigmar was barely into his teens.

That same year, the Dwarf High King Kurgan Ironbeard was taken captive by Greenskins. Sigmar infiltrated the enemy camp alone and rescued Kurgan — an act of courage for which the Dwarf race has never ceased to be grateful. Kurgan bestowed upon him the legendary rune-warhammer *Ghal Maraz*, meaning "Skull-Splitter." Forged by the master runesmith Alaric the Mad, this hammer became the singular symbol of Imperial sovereignty. The Eternal Alliance between Man and Dwarf began in that moment.

## Deeds

### Uniting the Twelve Tribes

Working outward from the Unberogen heartland, Sigmar pursued a three-pronged strategy — marriage, alliance, and conquest — to bring the twelve great human tribes of the central Old World under one banner.

The Teutogen chieftain Ottag was won through a champion's duel. The Asoborns were bound by marriage: their war-queen Freya wed Sigmar, and the two peoples merged. The Brigundians, Merogens, and Talabeclanders were drawn in through diplomacy. The Thuringians joined after the two tribes fought side by side against a Nurgle-corrupted warband. The Jutones were bound by shared maritime interest; the Udoses and Endals by the common threat of Chaos; the Cherusens were brought to heel by force of arms. By the eve of the Imperial Calendar, all twelve tribes stood beneath his standard.

The craft displayed was remarkable — iron resolve where it was needed, conciliation where it served better — and it belonged to a chieftain barely into adulthood.

### The Battle of Black Fire Pass

In the first year of the Imperial Calendar, scarcely had the unification been completed when Sigmar faced the greatest military trial of his life.

A Greenskin WAAAGH! of unprecedented scale surged through from the World's Edge Mountains. The Orc warboss "Bone-Crusher" Urgok commanded the horde. Sigmar led the combined host of the twelve tribes to meet them head-on at Black Fire Pass. The battle raged for days. At the decisive hour, Sigmar spurred his horse into the Greenskin core and slew Urgok with *Ghal Maraz* in his own hand.

> By Ghal Maraz!

That Dwarf-forged hammer, in Sigmar's grip, announced its weight to the entire Old World for the first time. The great Greenskin standard fell, and the WAAAGH! collapsed.

After this battle, the main Greenskin military power in the region was shattered, and the Empire would know no large-scale Greenskin incursion from the south for a thousand years. Black Fire Pass is reckoned the true birth of the Empire — it proclaimed to the Old World that the twelve tribes were no longer a scattering of fragments but a nation that could fight a hard war together.

That same year, the Ar-Ulric, high priest of the White Wolf, crowned Sigmar Emperor in the great cathedral that would bear his name. The Imperial Calendar was inaugurated from that day.

### Fifty Years of Imperial Rule

After his coronation, Sigmar reigned for a full fifty years. For a man who rose through war, holding the realm demanded more patience than seizing it.

He laid several foundational stones:

**The Elector Count system.** The twelve tribal chieftains were transformed into twelve Elector Counts — granting the old leaders honourable station while binding their power within an institutional framework. This system would carry the political life of the Empire for over two thousand years.

**Codified law.** Sigmar promulgated the first Imperial legal code. The chaotic patchwork of tribal customary law was given a unified written standard for the first time.

**Infrastructure.** He built the Imperial road network, stitching the twelve provinces together with stone and engineering. Military logistics and commerce both depended on this web.

**Religion.** He established the twin-pillar framework of the Cult of Sigmar alongside the Cult of Ulric. Rather than using state cult power to forcibly swallow the old faith, he spared the Empire centuries of religious civil war.

**Defence.** A chain of fortresses and border keeps was raised along the World's Edge Mountains, forming the skeleton of the Empire's eastern defence line for the next millennium.

**The Dwarf Oath.** He and High King Kurgan sealed the Eternal Alliance: "Man and Dwarf shall never forsake one another." Though political reality would later produce occasional friction, the alliance has never been formally broken.

**Suppression of unsanctioned magic.** In an age when the Winds of Magic were growing ever stronger, Sigmar relentlessly hunted unauthorised spellcasters. This policy has been debated by later generations, but in his era, tolerating rogue wizards meant tolerating Chaos infiltration. He made the only rational choice available.

### Eastward Departure

In the fiftieth year of his reign, Sigmar's most trusted cousin and chief advisor, Gerreon, was discovered to be a secret devotee of Nurgle, conspiring to spread plague across the Empire. Sigmar slew the man he had regarded as family with his own hand. As Gerreon died, a Nurglite blade slipped from his grasp and pierced Sigmar's shoulder, leaving a corruption wound that would never heal.

The wound gnawed at his body and his mind without cease. Sigmar understood that Chaos had begun to unmake him from within — his Empire needed a leader free of the taint.

He summoned the Elector Counts to the fields outside Reikdorf and announced his abdication. He named no successor. That decision would become the structural root of the Empire's succession crises, civil wars, and Elector-count feuding for the next two thousand years — but it seemed, by then, beyond his concern.

He took only *Ghal Maraz* with him. Alone, he walked east, into the vast peaks of the World's Edge Mountains. At the last, he turned to look back once upon the nation he had built with his own hands, and then vanished into rock and snow.

No one ever saw his mortal body again.

## Fate & Present Status

### Deification and Return

After Sigmar's disappearance, folk legend never died: that he still walked among the mountains, waiting to return in the Empire's darkest hour. Over the centuries, veneration of Sigmar crystallised into an organised religion — the Cult of Sigmar — which became the dominant state faith of the Empire.

In 2522 IC, the Storm of Chaos swept across the Old World, and Archaon the Everchosen brought his host to the walls of Middenheim. Sigmar's divine power manifested on the battlefield through an avatar named Valten — a blacksmith's son who bore the twin-tailed comet branded upon his chest — who met Archaon's daemon-blade and held the line. The forces of Sigmar and the Chaos Gods clashed directly through their mortal champions.

In the End Times, Sigmar's true nature was revealed: a god trapped within the Vortex of the Celestial Realm. At the final moment, when the Old World collapsed, he broke free of his bonds and ascended to the Mortal Realms with the full godhead of Azyr, lord of the Wind of Heavens.

### The Age of Sigmar

Though the Old World perished, Sigmar's story did not end.

After the formation of the Mortal Realms, Sigmar became the central deity of the Grand Alliance of Order. He rules from Sigmaron, capital of the Celestial Realm of Azyr, commanding the Stormcast Eternals — immortal warriors forged from the souls of mortal heroes. Every Stormcast Eternal was, in life, the finest champion of his or her people, chosen by Sigmar at the moment of death, reforged in the forges of Azyr, and sent back into battle in armour and thunder.

He entered a brief pantheon alliance with Nagash, the god of death, which shattered over the question of who owns the souls of the dead. His war against Archaon stretches across the entire Age — one man risen to godhood, the other fallen from faithful knight to world-ending champion of darkness. Their struggle long ago transcended personal enmity; it has become the eternal symbol of Order against Chaos.

## Verdict

Sigmar's life, from chieftain's son to mortal emperor, from emperor to abdicate, from the vanished to the divine — every step answered a single, simple question: how far can a mortal go?

His methods for uniting the tribes were pragmatic. When his fist was hard enough, he used it; when it was not, he negotiated marriage and mutual interest. Not one of the twelve tribes was won by moral persuasion.

His institutional designs after founding the Empire were pragmatic. The Elector Count system preserved face for the old chieftains; the law code provided rules of governance; the road network delivered infrastructure; the twin-pillar cult framework avoided a war of legitimacy. That a tribal confederation freshly emerged from the Bronze Age could function for fifty years without tearing itself apart owed little to genius and much to institutions doing the work.

His manner of leaving was pragmatic too — the moment he recognised corruption eating him from within, he surrendered power without hesitation, rather than drag the Empire down with him.

But pragmatism exacts its own price. He named no successor — this reads less like oversight than a failure of judgement. He may have believed the Elector Count system would naturally produce a new emperor, but he underestimated the destructive force of a power vacuum against a backdrop of tribal culture. Every succession crisis, every civil war, every bout of Elector-count separatism across the next two millennia traces back to this root. Later historians, almost without exception, regard it as the single and fatal error of Sigmar's career.

The legacy he left the Old World was not an impregnable capital nor an invincible artefact. It was a framework — rough-edged, full of compromises, riddled with flaws — that nonetheless held twelve disparate tribes together and kept them running. That it lasted over two thousand years, for a civilisation barely into the Iron Age, is something close to a miracle.

As for his eventual apotheosis, it was less the work of destiny than the completion of a symbol. The mortals of the Old World needed to believe that the ancestor who had carved open the calendar was still watching over them. And so Sigmar ceased to be merely a man, and became a flag. Mortals need flags.
