# 07 · Mannfred von Carstein

## Overview

Mannfred von Carstein, the most treacherous of the von Carstein bloodline. If Vlad was the sovereign among vampires, and Konrad the madman, then Mannfred was the schemer — you can never be sure whether his next move will help you or knife you. He was the single most unpredictable variable of the Old World's End Times. One man, one mind, a bottomless well of malice, turned the entire trajectory of the End Times upside down. Half the blame for the collapse of the Old World's defence lines can be laid at his feet.

In intelligence, Mannfred had no rival within the von Carstein line. In loyalty — likewise, he had none whatsoever.

## Origin

What manner of man Mannfred was before undeath, he himself preferred not to discuss, and so the historical record breaks off. What is certain is that he was taken in by Vlad von Carstein in Sylvania, made into a vampire and apprenticed. Among Vlad's get and followers, Mannfred was neither the best fighter — that was Konrad — nor the favourite — that was Isabella. But he had one thing no one else possessed: a brain.

Under Vlad's tutelage, Mannfred almost never engaged in direct violence. His interest lay in books, in the deep places of necromancy. He spent decades poring over the dark texts Nagash had left behind, travelling to every corner of the Old World in search of forgotten magical knowledge. Vlad wanted The Empire. Mannfred wanted something far larger — he wanted to be greater than his master.

While Vlad lived, Mannfred was his most able apprentice. But Vlad almost certainly knew this student could not be trusted.

When Vlad was struck down before the walls of Altdorf by Ghal Maraz, his ring stolen, never to rise again — where was Mannfred? He was not on the battlefield. He was observing from a safe distance. Some say he even had a hand in the theft of the ring. There is no proof. But if you were to rank every character in the Warhammer universe by likelihood of having done it, Mannfred would be in the top three without question.

## Deeds

### The Third Vampire War

After Vlad's death, Konrad was the first to leap into action, launching the Second Vampire War. That campaign had no structure — pure madness and violence — and Konrad ended with his head taken by a combined army of Dwarfs and Imperials. Mannfred never lifted a finger to help Konrad. He was waiting. Waiting for Konrad to get himself killed. Waiting for Sylvania's vampire power structure to need a new master. Only then did he step forward.

The Third Vampire War, fought from 2122 to 2145 IC, was Mannfred's stage. His approach bore superficial resemblance to Vlad's — he used his brain, he strategised, he avoided indiscriminate slaughter. But he lacked Vlad's sovereign scope. Mannfred did not want The Empire. He wanted to prove he was greater than Vlad. His armies pushed hard and at one point came close to Altdorf. But one problem persisted from beginning to end: no one could trust him. Vlad could win over human nobles because Vlad, for all his cruelty, had integrity. Mannfred had none. He promised a town safety if it surrendered, and turned its people into skeletons the next day. In the end, even his own undead generals hesitated at critical moments, unsure whether to obey his orders.

Mannfred was defeated in the end, but he did not die. He retreated into Sylvania's shadows and waited another four hundred years.

For those four centuries he did many things, but the core was one: studying Nagash. Mannfred became one of the foremost experts on Nagash in the entire Old World. He did not worship Nagash — he worshipped the kind of power Nagash possessed. He believed that if he could resurrect Nagash and stand beside him, a share of the spoils would be his. He was wrong, of course. Nagash does not share. Nagash consumes.

### The End Times

When the End Times came and the power of Chaos blanketed the Old World, Mannfred smelled opportunity. Together with Arkhan the Black — Nagash's most loyal undead servant — he began the work of resurrecting Nagash.

That Mannfred should be the one to do this is a fine irony. Nagash's most faithful servant was Settra (though Settra would never have admitted the word "servant"), and his most reckless was Arkhan. Mannfred was neither faithful nor reckless. He was after a transaction. He would help resurrect Nagash; Nagash would then give him due reward. In Mannfred's mind, this logic was sound.

He also did something that perfectly demonstrates Mannfred's way of operating: he resurrected Vlad.

Resurrecting Vlad was not nostalgia. He did not even return the Carstein Ring to Vlad. He needed Vlad — Vlad's combat power and command authority were things Mannfred could never marshal on his own — but it had to be a Vlad without the ring. Useful, but never stronger than himself. That is Mannfred in essence.

Nagash was successfully resurrected and completed his apotheosis, becoming the God of Death. Mannfred believed his reward was coming. But Nagash made him understand one thing: in the eyes of the Death God, Mannfred was not even a pawn. He was a tool. Once used, disposable.

This utterly enraged Mannfred. No — it did not enrage him. It lit the fuse that no one could ever predict.

### The Final Betrayal

The Old World's last battle centred on Middenheim. Archaon's Chaos legions pressed down from the north. The remnants of The Empire's army, the Dwarfs, the united elven hosts, Vlad's undead forces — every force of Order that could still fight was crammed into that battle line. This was a battle where everyone had staked everything, and everyone understood that losing meant the world's end.

Mannfred also stood on this side of the line. He was waiting for his moment to move.

When Vlad finally chose to die for Isabella. When the Order alliance's battle line was frayed to the last thread but somehow still holding. When victory and defeat hung on a single hair — Mannfred struck.

He betrayed everyone.

Not to Chaos. He would never go over to Archaon — Chaos could not give him what he wanted. His target was Nagash's power itself. While everyone else was fighting to the death for their races and their world, Mannfred was deep beneath Middenheim, trying to seize the Wind of Death, trying to make himself into a new god of death.

His actions tore a fatal gap in the battle line. The Order alliance's front was already on its last breath. Mannfred's move fractured the defence from within. Archaon's armies poured through the breach. From that moment, the fate of the Old World was sealed. Archaon reached the geomantic nexus deep beneath Middenheim and personally severed the world's weave of magic.

Mannfred did not intend to destroy the Old World. He never thought that far. He merely chose, at the most critical moment, the only thing he had ever been loyal to: his own ambition. The consequences did not concern him. In his view, if the Old World collapsed, so be it — so long as he could scramble to higher ground before being buried.

The result: he never got to higher ground. Nagash did not give him the chance. In the chaos of the Old World's collapse, Mannfred was directly consumed by Nagash's will — his soul ripped out, sealed away in the depths of the Realm of Death as a failed specimen. A lifetime of scheming, and his final reward was to be tossed into a corner by the very power he had tried to climb.

## Fate & Present Status

Mannfred's soul was devoured by Nagash at the close of the End Times and sealed in the depths of the Realm of Death — an afterthought, discarded by the god he had schemed to usurp. He does not rule. He is not feared. He is simply forgotten.

## Verdict

The von Carstein line produced three figures worthy of Warhammer's canonical record. Vlad represents order — tyrannical order, but order nonetheless. Konrad represents chaos — pure violence and madness. Mannfred represents the inability to stay in any camp for more than three seconds.

He is the purest schemer the Old World ever produced. People say Nagash is disloyal — Nagash is at least loyal to his ultimate goal. People say Skaven are disloyal — Skaven are at least loyal to betrayal itself. Mannfred is not even loyal to betrayal. Today's ally is tomorrow's offering. Every partnership of his is on a timer; when the clock runs out, the knife comes out.

Mannfred's tragedy is that he never recognised that his greatest strength — his intelligence — was also his greatest weakness. He could design schemes no one else could conceive, but he could never calculate one thing: no one walks to the end with a man who changes sides faster than he blinks. He helped resurrect Nagash; Nagash used and discarded him. He resurrected Vlad; Vlad preferred to stand with the humans. He betrayed the Order alliance at Middenheim; Chaos gave him nothing. His whole life was spent searching for the most advantageous position, but every position he took left him more isolated than the last.

That is Mannfred. Half the tonal shift of the End Times was his doing, the apex of three thousand years of conspiracy, one of the most direct architects of the Old World's collapse — and his ultimate fate is to be sealed away in a corner where no one needs to care about him, no one needs to guard against him, and no scheme can ever be set in motion again.

That is, perhaps, the cruellest punishment a schemer can receive — not death, but irrelevance.
