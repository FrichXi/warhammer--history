# 03 · Vlad von Carstein

## Overview

Vlad von Carstein, Elector Count of Sylvania, progenitor of the von Carstein vampire bloodline, one of the most dangerous undead sovereigns in Old World history. He launched three Vampire Wars, besieged Altdorf twice, and came within a hair's breadth of sitting on The Empire's throne. Among the six great vampire bloodlines, the von Carstein line is the most reminiscent of human aristocracy — and that aristocratic bearing was shaped by Vlad's own hand.

To call him "aristocratic" is not to call him merciful. He ruled Sylvania with an iron fist, never flinching from cruelty when it served his ends. But he possessed something other vampires lacked: a sovereign's sense of proportion. He knew ruling the living was more profitable than exterminating them. He knew the difference between what belonged in the Book of Grudges and what did not. And he knew — when Chaos sought to devour the whole world — that even a vampire should stand with the living.

## Origin

Vlad was born Vashanesh, a prince of ancient Nehekhara. The exact city and lineage have been buried in the desert beyond recovery, but one fact is certain: he was among the first to drink from the Elixir of Blood.

The Elixir was the creation of Queen Neferata of Lahmia. Under Nagash's indirect guidance, she had reworked Nagash's original immortality elixir into a more potent — and more cursed — formula. Those who drank it lived forever, but with an unquenchable thirst for blood. Vashanesh was among those who raised the cup at Neferata's banquet. With him were Abhorash, W'soran, and others who would later become the founders of the bloodlines.

Neferata's vampire dynasty did not last. The Priest Kings of Nehekhara united and marched on Lahmia. The vampires scattered. Vashanesh fled north, crossed the World's Edge Mountains, and eventually settled in a cold, damp backwater on the edge of The Empire — Sylvania.

Sylvania had always been the least-loved corner of The Empire. Barren soil, sunless forests, a population of gaunt and deeply superstitious peasants. Its one virtue: no one cared what happened there. Vashanesh took a human name — Vlad von Carstein — and married Isabella, daughter of the local count, Otto von Drak.

This marriage was the most important turning point in Vlad's existence, more than drinking the Elixir ever was. Isabella was already broken — locked in a tower by her father, her mind unravelled. Vlad did not marry her for her dowry. He genuinely loved her. He turned her into a vampire, and from then on the two ruled Sylvania together. In the annals of vampire history, the relationship between Vlad and Isabella is one of the vanishingly few things you can actually call love. The tyrant's one soft spot, from beginning to end, was his wife.

When Count Otto died, Vlad inherited Sylvania as a matter of course and became a lawfully recognised Elector Count — on paper, at least. From Castle Drakenhof, he spent centuries turning the province into a realm of the undead. The living still farmed, paid taxes, raised children. Their lord was simply a monster who never died. In a sense, Sylvania under Vlad was more stable than it had ever been — Vlad wanted order, not pure terror.

## Deeds

### The Carstein Ring and the First Vampire War

Vlad possessed a ring — the Carstein Ring. Its origins are disputed: some say Nagash forged it himself; others claim Vlad wrought it with his own necromancy. Whatever the provenance, the ring's power was certain: so long as it remained on his finger, Vlad could not truly die. Kill him once, kill him a dozen times, and he would rise from the blood-soaked ground on the next night.

With this ring, Vlad launched the First Vampire War.

In 1797 IC, Vlad's undead legions poured out of Sylvania. The imperial counts barely took it seriously at first — what could that poor scrap of Sylvania muster? By the time the deathless host had crossed the border and swallowed province after province, they realised they were facing not a local rebellion but a strategist with infinite patience waging an infinitely patient conquest.

Vlad's approach was meticulous. He did not slaughter indiscriminately — he wanted The Empire, not a charred wasteland. Towns that surrendered carried on as before; only those that resisted became new recruits for the undead host. He even submitted a formal claim to the imperial throne in his capacity as Elector Count — he was Otto von Drak's son-in-law, the lawful ruler of Sylvania, his bloodline purer than some of the sitting Elector Counts'. The legal argument might not have held up in court, but politically it was devastating: it split the imperial coalition. Some thought they could negotiate with Vlad; others insisted on fighting to the death.

The war lasted thirteen years. Vlad pushed all the way to the walls of Altdorf and laid siege. For the first time in its history, The Empire faced the prospect of being ruled by a vampire.

The man who stood at the breach was not the Emperor. It was Grand Theogonist Wilhelm III. Wielding Ghal Maraz — Sigmar's own warhammer, the sacred relic of The Empire — he met Vlad face to face on the battlements. The hammer struck the vampire. Vlad fell.

The morale of the undead host collapsed instantly. Without their master, the uncontrolled dead scattered and routed. The Empire had won.

But this should not have been Vlad's end. He had the ring. He should have risen. The problem was that someone tampered with his body in the chaos. A thief in imperial pay stole the Carstein Ring before Vlad's corpse could be returned to Drakenhof. Without it, Vlad could not rise again.

The ending carried its own black joke: the invincible vampire lord was not undone by a hero's hammer. He was undone by a nameless cutpurse who stole his chance to resurrect.

### Heirs

Sylvania did not know peace after Vlad's death. He left behind a brood of get and apprentices. The two most consequential were Konrad and Mannfred.

Konrad von Carstein launched the Second Vampire War. But he was nothing like Vlad — Vlad was a statesman; Konrad was a madman. His campaign had no strategy whatsoever, just one blood-soaked massacre after another. From 2097 to 2122 IC, Konrad ravaged Sylvania and the imperial borderlands until a combined army of Dwarfs and Imperials cornered him. His death lacked drama — someone lopped off his head with an axe. A vampire without the ring dies when killed.

Mannfred von Carstein was the third. He was Vlad's cleverest pupil, and the least trustworthy. He launched the Third Vampire War (2122–2145 IC) with more method than Konrad, but still lacked Vlad's sovereign scope. He was ultimately defeated but did not die. He retreated into the shadows and waited.

### The End Times

When the End Times arrived, Mannfred did one thing: he resurrected Vlad.

The motive was not a shred of noble. Mannfred needed Vlad's help resurrecting Nagash — he was using his old master as a tool. But when Vlad awoke, he made a choice no one expected: instead of following Mannfred into Nagash's service, he walked straight toward The Empire's camp.

Vlad found the retreating imperial army and said something that very nearly made every human present raise their sword: I am here to help you fight Chaos.

The imperial commanders — mostly staff officers under Karl Franz — did not believe him. Vlad von Carstein, instigator of the Vampire Wars, tyrant of Sylvania, now offering alliance? But Vlad's logic was simple: if Chaos destroyed the world, he would have nothing left to rule. He wanted The Empire — not a scorched husk divided between Khorne and Nurgle.

Karl Franz — or, by then, Karl Franz possessed by Sigmar — made a staggeringly bold decision: he accepted Vlad's aid.

And so a strange tableau appeared: undead legions fighting alongside The Empire's army, vampire knights and Sigmarite templars holding the same line against the Chaos Warriors. Vlad himself fought on the front repeatedly. He did not have the ring — Mannfred had not returned it after the resurrection. He knew that if he died this time, it would be the true death.

Vlad's end did not come from a Chaos warrior's blade. He died for Isabella.

Isabella had been corrupted by Nurgle during the chaos of the End Times and become a vessel of plague. Vlad found her, ravaged by disease and decay beyond recognition. He did not try to "save" her — Nurgle's corruption is irreversible. He did one thing: he drove a stake through his own heart and died with her. The tyrant who had ruled Sylvania for centuries, in his final choice, did not choose the throne. He did not choose victory. He chose his wife.

## Fate & Present Status

Vlad chose final death alongside Isabella von Carstein during the End Times, piercing his own heart rather than abandon her to Nurgle's corruption. He died without the Carstein Ring — a true death, and the one he chose freely.

## Verdict

Vlad von Carstein is a villain so complex it is difficult to hate him simply.

In ambition, he was one of the non-human forces who came closest to conquering The Empire. Twice he besieged Altdorf; one step more and he would have been Emperor. In method, he seized power through marriage, ruled through terror, and crushed all resistance under undead legions. He was a tyrant in the fullest sense.

But he had limits. His way of ruling Sylvania was not pure horror — he taxed, legislated, and maintained order. Under him, the living did not live happily, but they lived with structure. Among the six great bloodlines — Neferata wielding seduction and intrigue, the Blood Dragons deluding themselves with a knightly code, the Strigoi mere beasts — the von Carstein line is the one most like a state. This was not because Vlad was merciful. It was because he understood the simplest of truths: a heap of ruins has no value. A functioning empire does.

His love for Isabella is an exceedingly rare thing across the entire Warhammer narrative. Vampire stories are usually about corruption, betrayal, and the endless thirst for power, but Vlad's story has one thread that never snapped, from beginning to end: his love for this madwoman. His first death came because the ring was stolen. His final death was chosen — with Isabella. You can call him a monster. But this monster knew what love was.

His choice to stand on The Empire's side in the End Times was the ultimate expression of his political instinct. Vlad did not suddenly become a good man. He made a ruthlessly rational calculation: Chaos wanted total annihilation; The Empire's survival was better for him. But whatever the motive, his choice objectively bought The Empire's defence line a few more days, and let more people live to the end.

A sovereign among vampires — ambitious, and not without limits. That is Vlad von Carstein.
