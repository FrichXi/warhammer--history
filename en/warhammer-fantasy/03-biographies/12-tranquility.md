# 12 · Thanquol

## Overview

Thanquol, chief Grey Seer under the Great Horned Rat, the most prodigiously scheming and perversely successful bureaucrat in the under-empire of the Skaven. Every grand plan that passed through his claws ended in failure — yet after each catastrophe, he found a scapegoat and climbed higher on the bones of his colleagues. His life is the perfect Skaven fable: pure genius and pure avarice wrapped into one creature, proving that in the world of the Council of Thirteen, failure does not mean death — so long as you can shift the blame fast enough.

## Origin

Thanquol's exact year of birth is beyond recovery. Skaven society keeps no record of whelping; from the moment they emerge, ratlings tear at one another in the brood-nest, and less than a third survive to weaning. The young Thanquol early exhibited two traits that set him apart: a sensitivity to the energies of warpstone far beyond his peers, and an even keener instinct for when to bow before the powerful.

He was sent to the Temple of the Horned Rat to undergo the training of a Grey Seer. The selection was brutal — apprentices were fed undiluted warpstone dust; those who survived the ravages and mastered sorcery earned the title of Grey Seer. Those who went mad or exploded made up the majority. Thanquol not only survived; he greedily consumed many times the safe dosage and has been hopelessly addicted ever since. His command of magic is fathomless. His snout is perpetually crusted with the green glow of warpstone residue. Every decision he makes is simultaneously the product of meticulous calculation and drug-fuelled paranoid hallucination.

A Grey Seer does not rise by merit. He rises by killing the one above him. In this, Thanquol displayed the core talent that would define his entire life: he never needed to do the killing himself. His master perished during a diplomatic mission to Clan Moulder, "accidentally" devoured by a mutated rat-ogre — no one could ever prove the deed was connected to a certain tampering with warpstone potions, but immediately afterward, Thanquol inherited every resource and position his master had held.

## Deeds

### The Nemesis: Gotrek and Felix

If Thanquol has one insurmountable obstacle in his life, it is the dwarf Slayer Gotrek Gurnisson and his rememberer, Felix Jaeger. The existence of these two constitutes the longest-running humiliation of Thanquol's career.

Their first encounter unfolded in the sewers deep beneath Altdorf. It was somewhere around the year 2500 by the Imperial calendar. Thanquol had engineered what he considered the most exquisite conspiracy of his life: using warpstone dust to formulate an airborne plague specifically calibrated for humans, to be disseminated through the labyrinthine sewer network beneath the Imperial capital. Once triggered, Altdorf would become a city of the dead within three days. His site selection was perfect. His formula was unassailable. His Skaven troop strength was overwhelming. Then Gotrek stormed into his incubation chamber with Felix in tow, hacked every plague-vat to pieces, left the Skaven garrison strewn through the waterways in pieces, and nearly took Thanquol's head off — half his whiskers shorn away by a sweep of the dwarf's axe before a desperate teleportation spell carried him clear.

It was the first catastrophic defeat of his life. It was far from the last.

Over the next twenty years and more, Thanquol hatched no fewer than ten elaborate revenge plots against the pair. He used warpstone crystals as bait to draw them into a Chaos rift deep in the earth. He secretly funded a Chaos cult, arranging for a mutated daemon-engine to ambush them on a road they were bound to take. He even pursued them halfway across the world to the deserts of Araby, commissioning a master assassin of Clan Eshin to poison their drinking water. Every time, he came within a whisker of success. When the daemon of the rift pinned Gotrek to the ground, Thanquol's exultation vibrated all the way to the tip of his tail. When Felix collapsed from the poison, Thanquol was already drafting the language of his victory report to the Council of Thirteen. But every time, fate mocked him: Felix found the antidote herb at the last possible second; Gotrek rose from the rubble in a killing frenzy and split the daemon in half; then the two of them came back together and blew Thanquol's entire plan sky-high.

The most absurd incident took place in the Chaos Wastes. Thanquol, through a warpstone apparatus that drained a massive share of Skaven resources, summoned an ancient Greater Daemon out of the Warp and into the material world. The daemon swatted Gotrek dozens of yards through the air and shattered Felix's sword. Thanquol stood shrieking with laughter behind the daemon, delivering an extended victory oration. The speech was not yet finished when Gotrek hauled himself out of the rubble, walked up carrying his axe, and said one sentence: *"Rat, you talk too much."* The daemon died by the axe. Thanquol teleported away once more — this time with a section of his tail burned off.

Ironically, these repeated catastrophes made Thanquol a fixed character in the legend of Gotrek and Felix. In dwarf taverns and Imperial coaching inns, people told tales of how the Slayer sent "that mad rat" fleeing for his life, over and over. And in the Skaven under-empire, jokes about "the Grey Seer who can't handle one dwarf" circulated in whispers for decades.

### The Art of Climbing Through Failure

By the logic of any normal Skaven career, enduring this degree of consecutive failure should have left a corpse fed to the rat-ogres long ago. But Thanquol not only survived — he obtained higher station after every disaster. This was not luck. It was the most outstanding ability he possessed within Skaven society: the art of blame-shifting and political neutralisation.

His method was stable and ruthlessly efficient. After every mission failed, a detailed report reached the Council of Thirteen before any living witnesses could. In that report, the narrative had been carefully restructured: the plague had failed because Clan Moulder's mutant rats were substandard; the ambush was broken because Clan Eshin's assassin deserted at the critical moment; the daemon ritual spiralled out of control because Clan Skryre's warpstone was riddled with impurities. In every report Thanquol's pen produced, Thanquol was always the hero — the one who alone perceived his allies' incompetence and struggled single-handedly to salvage the situation. And those Skaven who knew the truth — subordinates who had witnessed his flight — tended to meet with "accidents" on the journey home: either dissolved into jelly by warpstone radiation, or devoured alive by a rat swarm that materialised from nowhere in a tunnel.

The other members of the Council of Thirteen were not fools, of course. But they were locked in their own schemes — thirteen rivals, each whittling away at the others. Thanquol's failure reports conveniently furnished them with ammunition for their private wars: the Skryre warlord seized upon "impure warpstone" to attack his own rivals; the Eshin assassin lord used "incompetent killers" to purge the opposition within his clan. Thanquol alive was useful to them. A Grey Seer too strong to need allies would, by contrast, be a threat to everyone. A rat who failed perpetually but always provided political weapons was safer than a rat who actually succeeded.

Thus, in the twisted logic of the Skaven empire, Thanquol's career did not sink — it rose. From a regional Grey Seer, he climbed to the top tier just below the Council of Thirteen itself, commanding his own warband, his own personal bodyguard (the Bonecrusher — a colossal rat-ogre construct modified by warpstone and sorcery, periodically blown to pieces every few generations so Thanquol could build a fresh one), and a veto over the military planning of the Skaven empire. If the Old World knew of this, it would likely sigh: that is very Skaven.

### The End Times — His One Truly Successful Plan

When the End Times descended and Chaos swept into the Old World with irresistible momentum, the surface nations fell one after another. To the Skaven, this was not an apocalypse. It was an opportunity — with the surface-dwellers slaughtering one another to exhaustion, the time had come for the under-empire to reap the world.

The Council of Thirteen ordered Thanquol to preside over an unprecedented grand ritual: using the window of the End Times, when the Winds of Magic howled at their wildest convergence, to pull the Great Horned Rat directly out of the Realm of Chaos and into the material world.

This time, Thanquol did not screw it up. Perhaps because every one of his enemies was too busy fighting Chaos to trip him. Perhaps because the Great Horned Rat Himself personally guided him — the Skaven god had always been a wild god denied a seat before the four Ruinous Powers, and the End Times was His only chance to turn the table. Thanquol, at the very peak of the magical mastery his life had accrued, completed the summoning. The titanic shadow of the Great Horned Rat vaulted into the material world.

Morrslieb — that second moon of solid warpstone that had hung in the skies over the Old World for countless centuries — began to fall toward the earth, dragged down by the Great Horned Rat's arrival. The warpstone shockwave released by the impact ripped the surface apart. The armies of Chaos and the forces of Order alike were buried beneath the storm. And the Skaven — the Skaven, already adapted to warpstone radiation — burst from the ruins and began reaping every survivor.

This single stroke reshaped the architecture of the gods. The Great Horned Rat was no longer a wild god barred from the table of the Four. He had personally dragged down a moon. He had destroyed a world. His sheer magnitude of power could no longer be ignored. At the endpoint of the End Times, the Chaos Gods acknowledged Him — the Great Horned Rat became the Fifth Chaos God, and the Skaven, from vermin on the margins of Chaos, became a force that the grand Chaos alliance could no longer afford to dismiss. And the key to it all was held in the claws of Thanquol.

Ironically, even after executing the only grand plan of his life that truly succeeded, Thanquol's ending remains unsettled. Some accounts say the Great Horned Rat drew him into the Realm of Chaos and elevated him to a Verminlord. Others say he continued to live on as a Grey Seer into the age of the Mortal Realms. But given his talent for survival, whichever path it was, he is still alive somewhere on some plane, plotting his next inevitably-disastrous conspiracy — and already lining up the scapegoat for when it goes wrong.

## Fate & Present Status

Thanquol's ultimate fate is ambiguous across sources. He may have been raised to daemonhood as a Verminlord in the service of the Great Horned Rat. He may simply persist, as Skaven do, somewhere in the Mortal Realms. What is certain is that — in some form — he endures.

## Verdict

If you could distill the Skaven as a race into the body of a single rat, you would get Thanquol.

He is supremely intelligent. His mastery of Skaven sorcery ranks easily within the top three of the entire under-empire. Every conspiracy he ever designed was flawless on paper — plague dispersal models accurate to the block, ambush force deployments covering every variable, every line of diplomatic negotiation pre-calculating ten steps of the opponent's response. Judged purely by raw intellect, he is one of the most dangerous operators in the Old World.

And then he destroys everything himself.

His problem is not insufficient intelligence. It is that he carries a switch that can never be turned off: greed. The moment before a plan succeeds, he cannot resist grasping for just one more advantage. The enemy is already beaten, but he has to stand in front of them and deliver a lengthy speech, giving them time to get back up. He could already take the lion's share of credit and go home, but he has to claim *all* the credit and turn his allies into enemies. He could already pocket the prize and withdraw, but he has to snort warpstone dust past the overdose threshold and hallucinate a miscast spell at the critical instant. It is not that he does not understand restraint. He understands it too well. He simply cannot help himself. This is the deep-rooted nature of the Skaven race: selfishness and greed are not moral flaws — they are genetic. A rat reaching out his claw for one more scrap at the very moment he should pull back — that is instinct written into the bone.

The great irony of Thanquol's life is this: he achieved the Skaven's most momentous victory in the manner of a failure. Decades of meticulous plans had all blown up. The moon that truly descended upon the Old World, Morrslieb, was not even his calculated design — it was the momentum of the End Times carrying him along; he simply happened to be standing in the right place at the right time. But this, too, is the Skaven definition of competence: you do not need to win every time. You only need not to lose the one time that matters most.

Gotrek once said to Felix in the sewers beneath Altdorf, summing up Thanquol: *"That rat is less an enemy than a curse. You can't kill him, because his enemy was never us. It's himself."*

The Great Horned Rat ascended as the Fifth Chaos God. The Old World died beneath the warpstone moon. Thanquol is probably still alive in some corner. He will always be alive.
