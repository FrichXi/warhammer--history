# 05 · Gotrek & Felix

## Overview

Gotrek Gurnisson, Dwarf Slayer, white-bearded Dragonslayer, the most death-defying warrior in the Old World; Felix Jaeger, human poet, educated at the University of Altdorf, a man who held both sword and quill. One Dwarf, one man — bound by a drunken oath, journeying together for forty years, from the sands of Araby in the south to the Chaos Wastes in the north. They slew dragons, banished daemons, and thwarted the Grey Seer Thanquol's schemes time and time again. When the End Times came, Gotrek became the incarnation of Grimnir, the Dwarf ancestor-god of war; Felix stood at his side as he always had. Together they strode into the Realm of Chaos, completing the most legendary tale of companionship the Old World has ever known. When it comes to the word "partners," there is no equal in the Old World.

## Origin

Gotrek was born in the Dwarf hold of Karak Kadrin, a prodigy of engineering and smithing in his early years. He designed fortifications for Dwarf strongholds and forged battle-axes that ranked among the finest in the kingdom. Dwarf society is built on honour, especially for an engineer — your walls must not crumble, your valves must not leak, your calculations must not err, because the enemies of the Dwarfs are always at the gate, and a single fissure could doom an entire hold.

During a Chaos incursion, Gotrek committed the one error an engineer can never atone for: a gate he had designed buckled prematurely under the blow of a Chaos ram. The invaders flooded the hold and killed his wife, his daughter, his kin. For a Dwarf, such shame cannot be settled by any court of law. There is only one path to redemption: the Slayer's Oath. He shaved his scalp, dyed his beard orange, and from that day forward became a Slayer — stripped of all honourable standing in Dwarf society, his sole remaining purpose to seek the most glorious death he could find and wash his sin clean in blood.

Felix's beginning could scarcely have been more different. He was the son of a wealthy merchant in Altdorf. He studied law and rhetoric at the city's finest institution, the University of Altdorf, wrote elegant verse, and spoke with reckless abandon. Around 2502 IC, he was rescued by Gotrek during a drunken street brawl — a drunk Felix had drawn his blade against a company of imperial soldiers, and Gotrek casually knocked the soldiers aside. Under the twin influence of alcohol and gratitude, Felix swore a public oath: he would follow this Dwarf until the day he fell in battle, and then write his life into an epic for generations to sing. The next morning, sober, he found Gotrek already waiting at the door, gripping a battle-axe nearly as tall as his own shoulder. "Come, manling," said the Dwarf. "You swore an oath."

## Deeds

Felix could not have imagined that a drunken oath would consume the rest of his life. But he kept his word.

Their first adventures unfolded within The Empire's borders. Gotrek dragged him into a fortress overrun by Orcs, down tunnels chewed by Skaven, through the shadowed alleys of Middenheim hunting remnants of a Chaos cult. Felix, at the outset, could do nothing but hold a pen. Thrown in front of monsters by Gotrek, he shook on every leg — yet each time he survived. Gotrek's assessment was pure Dwarf: "You can't die. You haven't finished my epic."

They soon earned a name. Bounties began circulating from the Karaz Ankor: hunts for Chaos Giants, mutated Cave Trolls, and ancient drakes nesting in abandoned Dwarf mines. Gotrek went gladly — the mightier the foe, the worthier his death. But every single time, he won. Wielding a rune-axe, channelling the Slayer's fury and the innate stubbornness of the Dwarf race, he cleaved creature after creature — beasts that by all reason should not be killable by mortal hands — in half. Felix stood beside him, sword in hand, finishing off stragglers, then afterward sat down, wrote the account into verse, and collected drinking money from the locals.

It was also during these years that they met the most tenacious nemesis of their lives: Grey Seer Thanquol. The most notorious member of the Skaven Council of Thirteen, Thanquol had engineered a plot in the sewers beneath Altdorf to destroy the whole of The Empire with a warpstone plague. Gotrek and Felix stumbled into his scheme and cut his ratmen to pieces. Thanquol fled in disgrace. Over the next two decades, he attempted revenge no fewer than ten times — poison, ambush, summoning Chaos daemons, even luring the pair into the Chaos Wastes. Every single time, the two overturned his plans. Thanquol became the least-respected Grey Seer in Skavendom, having wasted the Under-Empire's incalculable resources and troops on Gotrek — and never succeeding once.

The pair's footsteps grew ever farther. They crossed the deserts of Araby, battling undead legions in lost tombs; they went north to Kislev, chasing Khorne's Skullcrusher cavalry through blizzards; they crossed the sea to Albion and faced an avatar of the Dark Gods among ancient megaliths. They even briefly reached the continent of Lustria, witnessing the power of the elder races among the temple-pyramids of the Lizardmen.

Their most famous battle took place at the edge of the Chaos Wastes. A Chaos Dragon, its true name long eroded from memory, had laired in an ancestral Dwarf fortress. Its scales were crystallised with warpstone deposits; its breath exuded the plague-reek of living corruption. It was a favoured beast of Nurgle, and in centuries none had walked away alive from before it. Gotrek climbed the dragon's neck one-handed, his axe gripped in the other, and as the dragon's breath half-melted the armour from his body, he split its skull open with one stroke. Felix wrote in his memoirs: "He stood atop the dead dragon, smoke rising from every wound, and the look on his face was not triumph — it was disappointment. Because he had discovered this dragon was still not worthy of killing him."

In forty years, the two men walked every corner of the Old World, bore witness to The Empire's waxing and waning, the Dwarf realms' decline, the rhythmic rising and receding of Chaos. They met with the Emperor of The Empire; they were received by the High King of the Dwarfs (Gotrek refused all honours, for a Slayer is unworthy). They were denounced by elves as trespassers and then acknowledged by elves as allies. Felix transformed from a trembling merchant's son into a warrior whom Dwarf clans recognised — one Dwarf even told him to his face: "You are more worthy of a beard than most Dwarfs." This is the highest compliment a Dwarf can give a human.

When the End Times descended, the Chaos hosts flooded in at a scale the Old World had never witnessed. The elves united, the Dwarf holds fought a final desperate resistance, The Empire's Middenheim line collapsed, and Archaon struck down humanity's last hopes. The Old World was on the edge of annihilation.

In this sky-falling catastrophe, Gotrek finally found what he had sought his entire life. The daemon legions of the four Chaos Gods poured into the world. The geomantic energies of the World's Edge Mountains fractured under the pressure. And the power of Grimnir, the Dwarf ancestor-god of battle, surged from the void and found the one Dwarf in the world closest to his essence — a Slayer who had not taken a single backward step in forty years of combat. Gotrek was suffused with Grimnir's divinity, his mortal body transmuted into living rune-fire, his axe a god-slaying instrument. He plunged into the heart of the Chaos host as the avatar of the Dwarf war-god, buying the Old World its last breath. Felix stood behind him, sword in hand — as he had for every moment of the previous forty years.

The palaces of The Empire collapsed. The mountains of the Dwarf holds split open. The earth dissolved piece by piece under Chaos's jaws. But no one knows the true end of Gotrek — only that, with Felix beside him, he strode deep into the Realm of Chaos. No one saw him fall. And for a Slayer who wanted nothing else his whole life, that was probably exactly enough.

## Fate & Present Status

Gotrek was suffused with the divine power of Grimnir during the End Times and strode into the Realm of Chaos with Felix at his side. No one saw him fall. A Slayer who wanted a worthy death his entire life may have finally found it — or may have fought beyond even that. Felix's ultimate fate is equally unwritten; his chronicle of Gotrek, if it survived, is lost to the Old World's destruction.

## Verdict

Gotrek and Felix were neither emperors nor generals. They held no authority, commanded no armies. Yet they solved something every emperor and general in The Empire failed to: with the strength of just two men, they turned the alliance between men and Dwarfs from a parchment document into a living proof of blood and bone.

Gotrek sought death his entire life, and fate denied him. This is not luck. It is more like the Old World's joke on him: the more you want to die, the less you can, because you are too good at killing. Forty years of battles where he thought "this time, surely" — and every one was hacked into victory by that great axe. In the end, death had ceased to be a means of atonement and had become an irrelevant formality. A Slayer's honour, long ago, stopped being about whether he died. It was about what he had killed.

But the tragic core carries a deeper irony: he spent his whole life seeking death, never knowing that beside him, there was always someone writing the story of him alive. Felix's quill was the other reason Gotrek never got his honourable death — every page fought against forgetting.

From the narrative-historical perspective of the Warhammer universe, Felix is a figure of particular significance. He represents the people of the Old World who were never supposed to be part of "epic-scale events" — scholars, scribes, ordinary people who had never seen a real battle. He was in dragon-lairs and daemon-pits only because of an oath sworn while drunk. And then he honoured it, seriously, for forty years. To walk alongside myth in the weakness of mortal flesh — that weighs more than being born a god to slay gods.

The strongest alliance between Dwarfs and Men is not in council charters, not in diplomatic pleasantries. It is in the forty-year path walked by two men, footstep by footstep. When all order turned to ash in the End Times, Grimnir's power chose Gotrek — and this was not a divine gift. It was a confirmation. He was not made into a war-god because he was chosen. He had been one for a long time already.
