# 14 · Magnus the Pious

## Overview

Magnus the Pious, Emperor of the Empire, crowned in the Imperial year 2304. He is the greatest mortal ruler the Empire has known since Sigmar himself. In the Empire's darkest hour — fractured, divided, teetering on the brink of collapse — he walked alone from province to province, persuaded rival lords to set aside blood-feuds, and reunited a nation on the verge of dissolution. He led the combined armies of mankind against the great Chaos incursion on the snowfields of Kislev and broke it. And after the war, in defiance of a millennium-old ecclesiastical ban, he established the Imperial Colleges of Magic with the aid of the High Elves, transforming sorcery from a capital crime into an institution of the Empire. That the Empire stood firm for the two centuries that followed is, in half its foundations, his doing.

## Origin

Magnus was born into a noble family of Nuln. The exact year is lost. His house had venerated Sigmar for generations, and from his youth he was known for his piety — whence he would later earn the name "the Pious." His early life held no remarkable feats. In an age thick with nobles, he was merely one more unremarkable provincial aristocrat. But life in Nuln gave him a firsthand view of the suffering the Empire's division had brought: the elector counts at each other's throats, the roads abandoned, trade choked off, villages and towns left undefended to be savaged again and again by beastmen and greenskins. These years planted in him the seed that would one day flower into the salvation of the Empire.

## Deeds

### The Empire's Darkest Hour

To grasp the scale of Magnus's achievement, one must first see the Empire as he inherited it. In the two thousand years since Sigmar's disappearance, the Empire had suffered through prolonged cycles of civil war. By the eve of Magnus's time, the Empire had descended into the "Age of Three Emperors" — multiple elector counts proclaimed themselves emperor, the provinces refused to recognise one another and warred without end, and the electoral system was a dead letter. Central authority had utterly collapsed. The Imperial Palace in Altdorf had stood empty for generations. Even the Cult of Sigmar was riven by schism, the Grand Theogonist and the provincial arch-lectors locked in mutual denunciation. The Empire at this moment was less a nation than a loose confederation of feuding warlords rubbing a handful of sand together.

And from the north, a still more terrible threat. In the Imperial year 2301, a Chaos warhost of unprecedented scale boiled out of the deep Chaos Wastes. Kislev's eastern marches fell first. Praag was besieged. If Kislev broke, Chaos would drive straight into the Empire's heartland.

### The Pilgrimage and the Unification

While the elector counts still watched one another, each refusing to commit troops first, Magnus made a decision that defied every logic of the age: he left Nuln alone and began walking. On foot, he circled the entire Empire. He had no army, no treasury, no electoral title — he had only his voice and his faith in Sigmar.

He walked the Reikland, Talabecland, Middenland, Ostland, Nordland... Wherever he stopped — a town square, a village shrine, even the court of an elector count — he spoke the same words to all who would listen: *Chaos is coming. If mankind keeps fighting itself, everyone dies.* He favoured no faction. He sought no advantage for himself. He asked only that the rival lords call a temporary truce and march north together.

If you read the chronicles of the time, in an age soaked through with political calculation, his words were simple to the point of naivety. But it was precisely this simplicity that moved hearts. Common townsfolk, rural farmers, junior officers, village priests — these people stood behind him before the elector counts ever stirred. First a bodyguard of a few dozen, then hundreds, then thousands. By the time the elector counts took notice, Magnus commanded a volunteer army drawn from commoners and minor gentry — and its morale was terrifyingly high, because these people were not fighting for the advantage of any lord. They were fighting for the survival of their own homes.

Political pressure at last forced the elector counts' hands. In the Imperial year 2304, an Electoral Council convened at Nuln. Without any dispute, the assembled elector counts unanimously acclaimed Magnus as Emperor — an almost unheard-of unanimity in Imperial history. He had neither imperial blood nor a great army. The sole reason he was chosen was that everyone could see: this man, and this man alone, could pull the Empire back from the cliff's edge.

### The Great War Against Chaos

Magnus did not linger one extra day in Nuln after his coronation. He immediately integrated the armies of the various provinces with his own volunteer force, forged them into a single imperial host, and marched north by starlight.

By this time, Kislev was on the verge of collapse. Praag had been under siege for months; famine gnawed through the city. The city of Kislev itself swayed under the warhost's blade. The Everchosen — the supreme champion of Chaos — pressed closer with every hour.

When Magnus reached the walls of Kislev, the battlefield situation was brutally grim. The Chaos host vastly outnumbered the allied army, and a single Chaos Warrior was worth several ordinary human soldiers in close combat. Magnus did two things to turn the tide. First, he led from the front — not directing from the rear but riding his warhorse into the thickest press of the enemy. When the Imperial soldiery saw the Emperor himself charging ahead of them, their will to fight caught fire. Second, he accepted a choice that, in those times, was tantamount to heresy: he allied with the High Elves.

The High Elf archmage Teclis arrived at Magnus's camp, accompanied by two elven mages. Teclis told Magnus plainly: mankind cannot defeat Chaos with steel alone — you *need* magic. Magnus, at the risk of being condemned by his own church, accepted the elves' aid. In the decisive battle beneath the walls of Kislev, Teclis's sorcery and the human charge fused into a lethal combination. The Chaos warhost, crushed from two sides, at last broke. The Everchosen withdrew. Kislev was saved. The Empire was spared annihilation.

### The Colleges of Magic — A Legacy Greater Than Victory

If Magnus had been merely an emperor who could fight battles, he might rank somewhere in the top five of Imperial history. What makes him the foremost ruler beneath Sigmar alone is what he built after the war.

Before the war, magic was an absolute prohibition within the Empire. For over a thousand years, the Cult of Sigmar had equated all spellcasters with worshippers of Chaos; anyone caught was burned. But the field beneath Kislev had demonstrated an undeniable fact: magic was not merely usable — it was an indispensable weapon in mankind's fight against Chaos.

Magnus faced an immense political problem. The Cult would never accept the legalisation of magic, but an Empire without magic was doomed to perish in the next Chaos incursion. His solution was a stroke of political genius: he did not attempt to forcibly overturn the church's ban. Instead, he invited Teclis to demonstrate to the Cult the difference between *order magic* and *chaos magic*. The Cult of Sigmar was ultimately persuaded — or rather, the lesson of Kislev had been too searing for even the church to deny.

The Imperial Colleges of Magic were thus founded, in Altdorf. Teclis helped design the system: the Winds of Magic were divided into eight, each College dedicated to a single Wind, crossing forbidden. The Light Order (Hysh), the Bright Order (Aqshy), the Gold Order (Chamon), the Jade Order (Ghyran), the Celestial Order (Azyr), the Grey Order (Ulgu), the Amber Order (Ghur), and the Amethyst Order (Shyish) — eight Colleges, each independent, each a check on the others. Every wizard must be trained within the Colleges, licensed to practise, and bound by Imperial law. This gave the Empire magical strength while also locking magic inside an institutional cage.

This system would, over the following two centuries, become one of the firmest pillars of the Empire. Without the Colleges of Magic, the Empire would not have survived the first wave of the Chaos storms and the End Times.

## Fate & Present Status

Magnus lived out his reign in quiet service. He never sought a shred of personal gain. He lived simply throughout his time as Emperor, dwelling in his old house in Nuln rather than the Imperial Palace at Altdorf. He died peacefully in some unremarkable year of the mid-Imperial calendar — cause unknown, no world-shaking legend attending his end. After his death, the Empire slipped back into electoral politicking and power games. But the Colleges of Magic he had founded, and the sense of Imperial unity forged in the Great War Against Chaos, ensured that the Empire never again returned to the abyss of the Age of Three Emperors.

## Verdict

Over two and a half thousand years of Imperial history, there have been many great emperors. Sigmar founded the Empire. Mandred Skavenslayer ended the Skaven Wars of plague. Karl Franz went down with the Empire in the End Times. But if the question is which single mortal most profoundly shaped the *institutional survival* of the Empire, none can be ranked before Magnus.

Sigmar gave the Empire a name and a banner, but Sigmar left no mechanism for succession — which is the structural root of the two thousand years of civil war that followed. Magnus filled the greatest gap Sigmar left behind: he gave the Empire a set of institutions capable of sustaining themselves. The Colleges of Magic were not his only reform — during his reign he also rebuilt the credibility of the electoral system, restored the Imperial road network, and reorganised the state's standing forces — but the Colleges were the decisive one, because they gave the Empire something it had never possessed before: a capacity to generate defensive strength at scale, continuously, without depending on any individual hero.

That almost-naive faith he carried with him as he walked the whole country — he never lost it after becoming emperor. He never took a coin of private profit, lived simply his entire reign, and always stayed in his old house in Nuln rather than the Imperial Palace in Altdorf.

If Sigmar is the Empire's father, Magnus is the older brother who — after the father vanished — pulled the siblings, who had been beating one another bloody, back to the same table, and handed each of them a weapon with which to defend themselves. He does not manufacture legends. He manufactures the conditions in which legends can happen.
