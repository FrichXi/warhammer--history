# 13 · Arkhan the Black

## Overview

Arkhan, called Arkhan the Black, foremost of Nagash's Nine Dark Lords and steward of the realm of Death in the domain of the Great Necromancer. In the entire Warhammer cosmos, he is the single being who has never, from beginning to end, betrayed Nagash. In life, he was his ally. In undeath, his foremost general. In the End Times, the mastermind who resurrected Nagash. In the Age of Sigmar, he still carries out the will of the same master.

Nagash has been betrayed more times than anyone could count: his brother turned against him, the kings of Nehekhara waged war on him, the Skaven broke faith with him again and again, and Mannfred von Carstein — ever outwardly obedient — forever schemed behind his back. Settra the Imperishable bent the knee only under duress, his mind fixed on when to bite back. Arkhan alone, from the age of living Nehekhara to the destruction of the Old World, to the new epoch of the Mortal Realms — whatever Nagash asked of him, he did. No question of right or wrong. No calculation of cost. The loyalty of the undead is far more reliable than that of the living.

## Origin

In life, Arkhan was a pharaoh of Nehekhara, ruler of a city-state whose name is now difficult to verify. In the era he lived, Nagash was not yet the Lord of Undeath — he was merely a Khemrian king who had returned from the Dark Elves with a store of dark sorcery, murdered his own brother, and usurped a throne.

At the time, the city-states of Nehekhara were split into two camps. Most kings regarded Nagash as a monster, a traitor, and a heretic who had desecrated the mortuary traditions of Nehekhara. But a handful of rulers saw the potential of the dark magic in Nagash's hands — or perhaps they saw the sheer unstoppable force of Nagash's will — and they threw their lot in with him. Arkhan was the firmest among them.

The exact reasons can only be guessed at. Perhaps political advantage. Perhaps a hunger for forbidden knowledge. Perhaps he was simply shaken by the sheer audacity of Nagash's vision — *I will conquer death itself.* Whatever the cause, Arkhan placed his wager on Nagash. The decision cost him his life, and yet — thousands of years later — it made him one of the most powerful undead in the Old World.

## Deeds

### In Life: Nagash's Ally

As Nagash expanded his power in Khemri, built the Black Pyramid, and invented the art of necromancy, Arkhan remained his most reliable outside supporter. Troops, materiel, intelligence — Arkhan's city-state furnished Nagash with resources no other king was willing to provide.

The patience of the other kings had limits. When Nagash's necromancy grew ever more abominable and the threat to the traditions of Nehekhara became unmistakable, seven kings — including the rulers of Lahmia, Zandri, and other major city-states — formed a coalition and marched on Khemri. In a decisive battle, Nagash's forces were broken. His body was destroyed. His soul fled into the depths of the Black Pyramid and fell into a long slumber.

Arkhan did not escape. The attention of the seven kings was not confined to Nagash alone. They purged Nagash's allies. Arkhan was taken. The record of his execution is sparse, but the outcome is clear: he was killed, his corpse given preliminary mummification after the custom of Nehekhara, interred in the desert sand, and left — like every dead pharaoh — to wait for an afterlife that would never come.

What came for him was not the afterlife. It was Nagash.

### Resurrection: From Pharaoh to Undead General

Over the centuries that followed, Nagash's soul slowly gathered strength in the Cursed Pit. He raised the fortress of Nagashizzar east of the World's Edge Mountains and began assembling his undead legions on a grand scale. In the course of this second rise, Nagash did one thing in particular: he returned to the deserts of Nehekhara, sought out the tombs of those who had once been loyal to him, and dragged their souls back from the Realm of Death.

Arkhan was among them.

Nagash did not simply make him another mindless zombie. Arkhan was restored with full consciousness, full memory, full will — and an unshakeable obedience to the master he had served in life. Nagash clad him in a new skeleton, reinforced with dark sorcery until it was all but indestructible. Thus was born Arkhan the Black: a skeletal general armoured in black plate, his arm bones and ribcage engraved with runes branded by Nagash's own hand, mounted on a spectral steed conjured from bone and dark magic.

From the moment of his resurrection, Arkhan ceased to be the Nehekharan pharaoh he once was. He became an extension of Nagash's will, the commander of the armies of death — a perfect instrument that needed no food, no rest, and no contemplation of questions like "who am I?"

### A Millennium of War

Arkhan carried out too many tasks on Nagash's behalf to be counted. Most of the time, Nagash was either slumbering, or recovering his soul after being slain again, or imprisoned in some extra-dimensional void. During these prolonged interregnums, it was Arkhan who kept Nagashizzar running. Arkhan who ensured the undead legions did not crumble. Arkhan who roamed the world collecting Nagash's lost grimoires, relics, and fragments of the Black Pyramid.

The war between Nagash and the Skaven dragged on for nearly two thousand years. In the siege of Nagashizzar, Arkhan commanded the undead phalanxes, throwing back Skaven tunnel-assaults again and again. He voiced no complaint, showed no hesitation — nor even any sign of *weariness* at the span of the struggle. He was already dead, and the dead do not grow tired.

What is notable is that, over all those centuries, every other "ally" and "servant" Nagash possessed betrayed him or tried to slip the leash at some point. The Skaven dealt with Nagash only as temporary convenience. Mannfred von Carstein considered himself Nagash's loyal servant, but his "loyalty" was always centred upon himself — whenever Nagash's commands ran counter to his own interests, he could conjure a thousand reasons to comply in letter and defy in spirit. Settra bent the knee in the End Times but never accepted it in his heart. Even the vampires Nagash had personally created — Neferata, Vlad, Abhorash — scattered after the fall of Lahmia, none of them returning to their creator.

Arkhan did. For a thousand years as for a single day, Arkhan stood at Nagash's side.

### The End Times: Architect of Nagash's Return

By the coming of the End Times, Nagash had been silent for too long. He had been stricken into the void in the year 1111 of the Imperial calendar by the Skaven's Fellblade, his soul-fragments scattered across the world. To resurrect him required an extraordinarily complex procedure: gathering relics, locating a suitable ritual site, and wielding magic immense enough to stitch the soul-fragments back together.

Mannfred von Carstein wanted to do this — but because resurrecting Nagash served *his own* interests. Vlad von Carstein was resurrected by Mannfred to assist, but Vlad wanted only to fight Chaos; serving as Nagash's lackey held no appeal.

The one who truly drove all of it from behind, who placed every piece on the board in its proper position, was Arkhan.

Arkhan had spent centuries gathering the relics of Nagash — the Crown of Alakanash, the Nine Books of Nagash, the core fragments of the Black Pyramid. He threaded through crypts and abandoned fortresses across the world, dealing and warring and stealing from every kind of power — he minded no ally and minded no murder, so long as he obtained what Nagash needed.

Under Arkhan's orchestration, Mannfred and Vlad were drawn, one after another, into the project of Nagash's resurrection — sometimes as willing collaborators, sometimes as pieces moved against their will. While the elves, the Empire, and the dwarfs were consumed with fighting Chaos and the Skaven, Arkhan worked in shadow to bring about the ultimate return of Nagash.

The ritual succeeded. Nagash achieved apotheosis in the End Times, becoming the true god of death. Though he was immediately afterwards dragged into the depths of the Realm of Death by the sun-god Ptra, Nagash had at last crossed the threshold he had been chasing for five millennia. And Arkhan, from beginning to end, was the chief executor of this feat. When Nagash became Death itself, Arkhan stood beside him — exactly as he had five thousand years before.

### The Age of Sigmar

The Old World was destroyed. The Mortal Realms took form. In the Age of Sigmar, Nagash is the supreme deity of Shyish, the Realm of Death, sovereign over all the kingdoms of the dead. Arkhan's station rose accordingly: he is Nagash's steward across the Realm of Death, managing the day-to-day affairs of the Great Necromancer's domain. When Nagash must weigh diplomatic relations with Sigmar, contend with the Chaos Gods for souls, or manage the internecine wars of the death factions, Arkhan is the executor he trusts above all others.

In the Grand Alliance of Death of the Age of Sigmar, Arkhan's profile is what it has always been — low, yet indispensable. He does not thrust himself into the spotlight like Mannfred. He does not cultivate his own petty kingdom like Neferata. His existence means one thing and one thing only: to execute the will of Nagash. Five thousand years have passed. This has not changed.

## Fate & Present Status

Arkhan the Black serves as the Mortarch of Sacrament, Nagash's foremost lieutenant and steward of the Realm of Death. He endures, as he has always endured, as the instrument of his master's design.

## Verdict

Arkhan occupies a unique place in the Warhammer cosmos. He is not the most powerful character. Not the most dramatically compelling. Not even the most popular. But he is the most *stable*. In a universe that runs on betrayal, corruption, schism, and backstabbing, Arkhan is an iron bar that has never bent.

What kind of being is Nagash? A creature whose ambition is so vast he means to swallow death itself into his belly. A creature who cares for no one, who will betray any ally at any moment, who treats every other life as a tool. A creature like that should, by all logic, have no loyal servant at all. But Arkhan's very existence provides the most powerful counterpoint in the whole narrative of Nagash: you are the purest egotist, and yet you have one follower who is purely faithful.

Some would argue Arkhan's loyalty is not voluntary — that he is controlled by Nagash's necromancy, his devotion merely the product of a spell. This argument is simple but insufficient. Nagash controls countless undead; zombies and skeletons obey by compulsion. But Arkhan is different. He retains full consciousness and independent judgment. What Nagash gave him was not a dog's leash but something rarer: trust that Nagash has never considered withdrawing. Nagash trusts no one — but he trusts Arkhan. The weight of that trust is heavier than any compulsion magic could ever be.

Another thing worth dwelling on: Arkhan's loyalty does not depend on Nagash's success or failure. When Nagash fell the first time, Arkhan had already been executed. When Nagash was slain by Alcadizaar the second time, Arkhan was prosecuting military operations far away. When Nagash was grievously wounded by the Skaven the third time, Arkhan held Nagashizzar. When Nagash sank into silence, Arkhan did not — as everyone else did — seize the moment to build his own power base. He gathered relics in silence, preserved order, and waited for his master's return. This kind of loyalty scarcely exists in the world of the living. It can only be found among the dead.

The loyalty of the undead is more precious than that of the living — not because dead men are more trustworthy (most undead have no concept of "loyalty" at all), but because Arkhan proved, over five thousand years, that there is no calculation in it. No transaction. No waiting for Nagash to fall so he can take his place. He simply executes the same order, day after day.

Nagash's greatest general — and the only one he never needs to watch his back against.
