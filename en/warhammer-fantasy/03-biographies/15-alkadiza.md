# 15 · Alcadizaar the Conqueror

## Overview

Alcadizaar the Conqueror, last living pharaoh of the human civilisation of Nehekhara, King of Alidia, bearer of the title "the Conqueror." In the annals of all the Old World's legends, he belongs to that tiny company of figures of whom it can be said: *this man killed a god with his own hands.* With a blade forged from meteoric iron, he drove his sword through the undying Lord of Necromancy in the depths of the Black Pyramid of Nagashizzar. Without Alcadizaar, Settra and the Tomb Kings would never have awakened, and Nagash might have swallowed all Nehekhara into his dominion of the dead. But the story of Alcadizaar is also one of the Old World's cruellest chains of cost: he killed Nagash — and then Nehekhara died with him. Or worse than died: the entire civilisation was trapped in some place no one wants to be, between life and death. Alcadizaar himself did not even find rest in death. His corpse was stolen by the Skaven and turned into a component in a conspiracy spanning a thousand years.

One man killed a god. The cost was the immortality of an entire civilisation — an undying immortality. Alcadizaar is, in all likelihood, the only Nehekharan who wanted to die and could never quite manage it.

## Origin

By the time the Tomb Kings awakened, no one could speak with certainty of Alcadizaar the Conqueror's origins. The living civilisation of Nehekhara was destroyed too completely. Every living chronicler, priest, and scribe was turned to bone in the sandstorm kicked up by Nagash's Great Ritual of Awakening — and then yanked upright by that same power and made to stand again as something no longer human. The palace Alcadizaar once dwelt in. His inscriptions. The conquests he carved into obelisks. All buried.

What can be established is not much, but every piece is as hard as granite: he was originally the king of a Nehekharan city-state. The city was called Alidia — not the largest on the map of Nehekhara, perhaps, but in Alcadizaar's hands it became the final bastion of living Nehekharan civilisation. He was called "the Conqueror" — whether the title was earned in life or added in death, the record does not say — but judging by his final battle, if anyone ever deserved the name, it was him.

Alcadizaar came to the throne in an age when Nehekhara had already half-rotted under Nagash's iron heel. Nagash had regathered his strength from the Cursed Pit, raised the fortress of Nagashizzar east of the World's Edge Mountains, and loosed his undead legions westward like a flood. The last time, the city-states of Nehekhara could still scrape together the armies of seven kings to drive Nagash away. This time, they could not — Nagash was mightier by orders of magnitude than he had been centuries before. City after city was crushed. King after king died, or suffered fates worse than death. Alcadizaar was the last king who could still muster an army, the last who could still make the living believe that fighting on was not hopeless.

He was not chosen by destiny. He was not the saviour of any prophecy. He was simply the one left standing at the end — and the one left standing has no choice.

## Deeds

### The Second Rise of Nagash

Nagash's second rising is the darkest page in the history of Nehekhara. He had slumbered in the Cursed Pit for centuries, his soul patiently piecing itself back together in the deeps. When he stood on the surface once more, what he wanted was no longer a kingdom. He wanted the souls of an entire species. He launched a total invasion of Nehekhara. The undead legions poured out of Nagashizzar on a scale that dwarfed his first usurpation. Khemri, Lahmia, Numas, Quatar — city-states that had once blazed with glory — were washed away like sandcastles before a tide.

It was from this extremity that Alcadizaar gathered the last handful of the living who could still hold a sword, out of the ruins of Alidia. His army was not a coalition — a coalition required other kings to still be breathing. He had the remnant troops of a single city-state, a few priests who had not yet surrendered, and commoners who would rather die fighting than be turned into Nagash's skeletons. With this host, he marched east from Nehekhara's western border, across battlefields already turned into graveyards, across wastes rendered sterile by necromantic corruption, toward Nagashizzar.

Then Nagash threw his deadliest fuel onto the fire — the Great Ritual of Awakening.

It was the grandest and most deranged working in the history of necromancy. Nagash intended to wake every corpse buried in the sands of Nehekhara, all at once, and turn them into his undead legion. The energy of the ritual was so colossal that beings in distant Naggaroth could feel the black vapour churning over Nehekhara. The core of the working was the apex of the Black Pyramid — that colossal structure Nagash had raised to absorb the energy of death. Nagash stood at the summit, poured the entirety of his will into the earth, and gave the command. Across the desert, every pharaoh, priest, general, and commoner who had been interred in the sands over thousands of years began clawing their way out of the ground.

If he succeeded, his undead army would outnumber all the living beings in the Old World combined.

But the Great Ritual had one fatal interval: in the brief moment when the dead were stirring but not yet fully bound to his command, Nagash's attention was wholly tethered to the ritual — and his physical body was vulnerable. The interval was very short — perhaps a quarter of an hour. Nagash knew this, of course; the defences of Nagashizzar were arrayed like an iron drum. Undead guardians layered upon each other, necromantic wards impermeable — not a living creature, not even a sand scorpion, should have been able to penetrate the Black Pyramid.

Alcadizaar did not go through the front gate. No one knows how he infiltrated the pyramid's heart. The battle records of Nagashizzar's assault are lost in the subsequent two-thousand-year war between Nagash and the Skaven. Only one fact is certain: he got in. He stood before Nagash's physical body, a sword of meteoric iron in his hands, and — before the Lord of Undeath could pull his consciousness back out of the ritual — drove the blade through him.

Nagash's body fell. The Great Ritual, robbed of its controlling mind, burst apart like a snapped steering column; the ritual's energy spilled out in all directions. The dead continued to rise from the sand, but no one told them what to do. They simply stood beside their own tombs, staring blankly at a world they no longer recognised. Nagash was defeated once more, his soul fleeing into deeper darkness.

Alcadizaar stood beside Nagash's corpse. He had won.

But winning and losing are the same thing in Nehekhara.

### The Price of Victory

Nagash was dead, but the residual effects of the Great Ritual seeped into every inch of Nehekhara's soil like venom. The dead pharaohs did not return to their tombs — they were awake, and Nagash's death would not put them back to sleep. Settra awakened and began reconquering his empire. Neferata awakened, rebuilding her power in the ruins of Lahmia with her bloodline progeny. The other kings awakened too, each claiming his own domain. Nehekhara did not perish — it simply transformed from a land of the living into a land of the dead. The Tomb Kings dynasty was born.

And the living — the army of living men Alcadizaar had led into Nagashizzar — none of them walked out alive. Alcadizaar himself likely died on that same battlefield, consumed by Nagash's death-curse, or drained dry by the backlash of his own meteoric blade. He was the last living pharaoh of Nehekhara. After him, no living ruler ever again sat upon a Nehekharan throne.

But Alcadizaar did not rest.

The Skaven, in the chaos following Nagash's second downfall, swiftly turned their attention to Nagashizzar. The fortress was a military treasure to them — it sat atop subterranean rivers and tunnel networks beneath the World's Edge Mountains, in a position that commanded the throat of the Skaven under-empire's sphere of influence. More importantly, Nagashizzar contained vast caches of warpstone and necromantic relics — including the meteoric blade that had killed Nagash.

Alcadizaar's corpse was secretly smuggled out of Nagashizzar by Skaven agents. The Council of Thirteen treated the body as both war trophy and research material — a mortal who had slain the Lord of the Dead with his own hands; his bones, his blood, the sword in his grip were strategic resources beyond price. It is said that Grey Seers spent centuries dissecting and studying Alcadizaar's remains, trying to crack the secret of "how a mortal kills the undying." Those studies eventually became one of the key technical foundations for the forging of the Fellblade — the Skaven weapon that would later grievously wound Nagash.

From a pharaoh, to a godslayer, to a research specimen disassembled piece by piece in Skaven hands. The last living pharaoh of Nehekhara — his final resting place was a pile of splintered bones in the under-nests of the Skaven.

## Fate & Present Status

Alcadizaar's ultimate fate after the Skaven took his remains is unknown. His body was studied and dismantled. The Fellblade was forged in part from the knowledge gained. What remains of the Conqueror, if anything, is lost to the deep dark.

## Verdict

Across the biographies of all Warhammer's antagonists, Nagash possesses one distinctive property: every time he is beaten down, someone must pay a price so ruinous it is grotesquely disproportionate to the victory. The first defeat — the seven kings' coalition triumphed, but Nagash died without dying; his soul fled into the Black Pyramid. The second defeat was Alcadizaar's doing — and the price was that all Nehekhara died but could not rest, the Tomb Kings immortal forevermore, yet their immortality more agonising than any living fate. The third was the Skaven's Fellblade — and the price was Nagashizzar becoming Skaven territory, the war beneath the earth grinding on for two thousand years.

Alcadizaar did what everyone considered impossible: as a mortal of flesh and bone, he faced the most powerful undead being in the Old World, and killed him. On that single thrust of the sword, his courage yields nothing to Sigmar's — Sigmar fought greenskins and Chaos, things that could at least be *killed*. Nagash was necromancy itself, a being who had grown stronger after being slain once before. When Alcadizaar killed him, it was not a fight. It was a straight-up collision with death itself.

But he could not save Nehekhara. The Great Ritual had already been triggered. Nagash's curse had, long before that ritual, already saturated the earth of Nehekhara through the Black Pyramid. The blade Alcadizaar drove home kept Nagash from seizing control of the undead horde, kept Settra and the Tomb Kings independent in their wills rather than turned into Nagash's puppets — by the outcome, he at least won the dead of Nehekhara their freedom. But freedom and *being alive* are not the same thing. Settra's first thought upon waking was not to thank Alcadizaar; it was to begin reconquering his empire, bone against bone. Every civil war of the Tomb Kings' dynasty, every undead campaign abroad, rests upon a single fact: Nagash's Great Ritual had turned them all into monsters. Alcadizaar could kill the monster-maker. He could not revoke the spell that had already turned everyone into monsters.

This is the deepest part of Alcadizaar's tragedy. He is not a tragic hero — he is a *victor*. He achieved the impossible; his blade pierced the heart of the god of death. But the victory meant nothing. His homeland turned to ash behind him. His people became the walking dead. His own corpse was dragged into a rat-warren and turned into a lab specimen. The entire history of Nehekharan civilisation after Alcadizaar is a political history of the dead — Settra's empire, Neferata's intrigues, Arkhan the Black's unyielding loyalty. All of it is dead men dealing with dead men. The living no longer have any part in the story.

The limit of mortal power is shown with perfect clarity in Alcadizaar. You can raise your sword and kill the greatest monster before you. But you cannot kill the wound that monster has already carved into the world. Nagash's curse is heavier than Nagash himself.

In the long life that followed, Nagash almost never spoke of Alcadizaar. Not from forgetfulness — Nagash forgets none of the enemies who have brought him low. It was because Alcadizaar's existence proved one thing: the greatest of the undying, at the very instant of completing his ritual and reaching his apex, could be stabbed to death by a creature of flesh and blood. Nagash cannot accept this fact, so he never thinks about it.

Alcadizaar does not need to be remembered. His sword has already spoken for him.
