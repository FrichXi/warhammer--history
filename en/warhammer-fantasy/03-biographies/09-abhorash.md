# 09 · Abhorash

## Overview

Abhorash, founder of the Blood Dragon bloodline — the most singular of the six great vampire bloodlines. In the history of vampirism, he is the first, and the only one, who truly broke free from the bloodthirst. He substituted the blood of a dragon for the blood of men, subdued the instincts of the undead with the code of the warrior, and wrenched a race fundamentally defined by corruption and predation onto a path that could, against all odds, be called "honourable."

The other vampire progenitors — Vlad wanted to be emperor, Neferata wanted to build an empire, Mannfred wanted to play the grand game of intrigue. Abhorash wanted something none of them did. It wasn't that he ceased to thirst. It was that he found the idea of living forever by skulking up to living throats utterly pathetic. So he did something about it: he slew a dragon, drank its blood dry, and never touched human blood again. The Blood Dragon order he later founded has one rule — seek ever-stronger opponents, temper yourself through martial mastery, until the day you are worthy of the word "knight."

In a race composed entirely of monsters, Abhorash is the one figure you cannot reduce to that word. He is not a good man. He may not be a bad one either. He is simply a man who, trapped by a curse, chose to break it himself.

## Origin

Abhorash's early life is rendered in the vaguest strokes by both chronicle and legend. No city-state, no noble house, no record of his mortal name — in the annals of Nehekhara, he first appears already standing at the side of his queen. What is certain is that he was the captain of the royal guard of Lahmia under Queen Neferata, and acknowledged across all Nehekhara as the greatest warrior alive.

This was no empty title. In Nehekhara's golden age, inter-city tournaments and duels were commonplace. Champions of rival kings traded victories; no one could claim absolute supremacy. Abhorash was the exception — those who fought him never wanted a second bout. He possessed an almost inhuman focus, an ability to commit no error in battle. In modern terms, he was a born martial artist, not forged by training but carrying it in his very bones.

Neferata chose him as her guard captain not only for his strength. More importantly, Abhorash possessed a quality vanishingly rare among Nehekharan nobility: he had no appetite for power. He wanted no fief, no title, no part in court intrigue. Every day he did the same things — train, stand guard, and occasionally ride out to cut down whatever fool had drawn the queen's displeasure. To Neferata, this pure, undiluted loyalty was priceless — especially once her grander ambitions had begun to take shape.

But loyalty has a price. When Neferata perfected the formula of the Elixir of Life and began secretly building her vampire dynasty within the palace, Abhorash was the first she sought to convert. Not because he begged for it — because she knew he would drink without question. He did. Among the ranks of the first vampires, Abhorash's name was listed beside Vlad's, second only to the queen herself.

## Deeds

### The Shame of the Thirst

The first years as a vampire were wretched.

It wasn't a question of power. A vampire's strength far exceeds the mortal — speed, endurance, senses, regeneration, every attribute magnified geometrically. His martial skill actually sharpened after the transformation, for he no longer tired, no longer aged, and could push his body to any extreme he willed. The problem was the bloodthirst.

A vampire's bloodthirst is not ordinary hunger. An empty stomach can be endured. Thirst can be tolerated. But the vampire's craving for blood is a curse that operates directly on the will — it seeps into every thread of thought, so that when you look at a living person you no longer see what they are wearing or what they are saying, but first the tracery of veins beneath the skin of the neck. Abhorash discovered he could not control it. He would bite prisoners to death without conscious thought. He would walk alone into the city at night and return with blood at the corner of his mouth. In a routine sparring match, catching the scent of his opponent's wound, he would simply tear the man apart.

To Vlad or Neferata, this was no problem — they had never intended to restrain themselves. To Abhorash, every day was humiliation. He was the greatest warrior in Nehekhara, yet before his own desire he was an infant learning to walk. He once told Neferata that if this was what immortality meant, he would rather not have it.

Neferata dismissed it. In her view, the bloodthirst was simply the cost of being a vampire, as natural as mortals needing to eat. She told Abhorash: get used to it.

He didn't. After the fall of Lahmia — when the armies of the rival kings descended and the vampires scattered — he walked west, alone. No destination, no plan, only a single vague thought: find a way to master the thirst, or die trying on the road.

### The Dragon-Slaying

For centuries thereafter, Abhorash wandered the wilds of the Old World alone. He crossed the World's Edge Mountains, sheltering in abandoned dwarf holds. He killed Greenskin warbands — not out of hatred, but because Greenskins had no blood worth drinking; their green ichor stirred no thirst. He challenged Chaos Warriors. He roamed the borderlands of Bretonnia and the Empire, watching human civilisation claw its way back from ruin.

But the thirst never left him. At intervals, he still lost control, still found himself with a mouthful of blood when awareness returned. He tried everything — self-isolation, prolonged fasting, drowning the craving in pain — none of it worked. A vampire's curse is etched into the soul; willpower alone cannot muscle through it.

Then he heard of the dragon.

Legends of dragons threaded through every land of the Old World — ancient lairs deep in the World's Edge Mountains, fire-drakes in the forests of Bretonnia, ice-dragons at the edge of the Chaos Wastes. Abhorash's reasoning was simple: if the essence of the bloodthirst was dependence on human blood, then a stronger blood — a blood that transcended the mortal — might serve as a substitute. There was no precedent for this theory. It was purely a warrior's intuition: a stronger foe, a stronger blood, fewer chains.

It took him a long time to find the right dragon. Not every drake was worth the challenge — a juvenile's blood might not be potent enough, a sickly wyrm's blood might be poison. Only a true primeval creature in its full vigour was worthy of the experiment. At last, deep in a rift valley of the World's Edge Mountains, he found a red dragon.

No one witnessed that battle. He never afterward spoke of it in detail. The only account that survives says the duel lasted an entire night and half a day. Abhorash used his vampire speed to evade dragonfire and claw, spent centuries of honed swordsmanship carving through scale inch by inch, and finally drove his blade upward through the jaw into the brain. When the dragon fell, the whole ravine shook.

He crawled onto it and drank the blood dry.

## Deeds (continued)

### The Way of the Blood Dragon

The instant dragon's blood entered his body, Abhorash knew the gamble had paid off. The agitation of the thirst did not vanish — but it changed. No longer was it an uncontrollable hunger zeroing in on the veins of every living throat. It became a force he could ride. He still needed blood, but he no longer needed to kill the innocent to get it. His body had accepted dragon's blood as a substitute. For the first time since becoming a vampire, he felt truly lucid.

There was a side effect he had not anticipated: dragon's blood tasted so exquisite that he never wanted to touch human blood again.

Purified by the dragon, Abhorash did not return to vampire society. Neferata's Silver Pinnacle, the von Carstein stronghold of Drakenhof, Mannfred's circles of intrigue — none of these held the slightest interest. He did the one thing only he would do: he began taking disciples.

These disciples became the Blood Dragons. Abhorash's criterion for students was brutally simple: you must be a warrior, and you must want to be a better, stronger, cleaner warrior. Man, dwarf, elf, fighters of any other race — if you were willing to let your sword speak, you were welcome. He would turn these warriors into vampires — using his own dragon-tainted blood rather than the standard sanguine conversion — and then tell them one thing: if you cannot bear the thirst, slay a dragon. If you cannot slay a dragon, then train forever, until you can.

Thus was the creed of the Blood Dragon order established. This brotherhood differs from every other vampire bloodline: they hold no territory, nurse no political ambitions, cultivate no spy networks, and study no necromancy. They do one thing — seek out the mightiest opponents and fight them. Greenskin warbosses, Chaos champions, dwarf slayers, elven swordmasters — if they are strong enough, a Blood Dragon will show up and issue a challenge. Win, and you move on to the next. Lose, and you are simply dead — for an immortal vampire, perhaps that is also a kind of solution.

The creed was eventually distilled into the core tenet of the Blood Dragon way: master your thirst, let martial prowess speak in place of intrigue, and refine yourself by challenging ever-stronger foes. If you fail the first — if you cannot restrain the bloodthirst — you are unworthy to call yourself a Blood Dragon.

In a sense, what Abhorash gave his disciples went far beyond swordsmanship. He gave them a road: even after becoming a monster that survives on blood, you can choose not to be a monster. This is not self-deception — dragon's blood is a real, proven substitute, and the self-discipline that follows is a real, viable way of life. He proved one thing: a vampire's curse does not equal despair.

## Fate & Present Status

On Abhorash's ultimate fate, both canon and legend fall silent. The most widely accepted account is that he roams the Old World still, never ceasing his search for worthier opponents. Some say he crossed into the Chaos Wastes to test himself against the champions of Khorne. Some say he returned to the deeps of the World's Edge Mountains and sleeps in a dragon's lair. Still others say he has no "final fate" at all — how can an immortal who does not age, cannot die, and no longer needs human blood possibly have an ending?

The Blood Dragons hold as an article of faith that their founder still lives, merely journeying farther than any of them have yet gone. The belief itself is part of the Blood Dragon way: the greatest warrior is always already on the road to the next fight.

## Verdict

The vampire race is, at root, a tragedy. All bloodlines — however different their ambitions, their methods, their character — share a single point of origin: a handful of mortals drank a terrible elixir in pursuit of eternal life, and the immortality they received was not a gift but a curse. You live, but you are no longer you. Every act is driven by the instinct of the thirst; your reason is merely its servant. Vlad's imperial dream, Mannfred's genius for conspiracy, Neferata's web of seduction — at bottom, they are all just different ways of thrashing inside a cage called hunger.

Abhorash is the only one who broke the cage open.

He did not do it through sorcery, or a divine relic, or the favour of a god. He did it with a sword, four hundred years of wandering, and the blood of one dragon. The experiment he ran was, in theory, almost absurdly simple: the curse says you must drink blood — what if he found a higher-grade source? If it worked, it worked. If not, he would die — which was no worse than his current state. This pragmatic, almost cold-blooded trial-and-error spirit has no parallel in vampire history.

The Blood Dragon order he founded is likewise the most alien among the six bloodlines. Other vampires rule the living through terror; the Blood Dragons only need the living to stay alive — so they may one day be challenged. Other vampires manipulate history through conspiracy; the Blood Dragons have no interest in history. Other bloodline founders sought to carve their empires into the Old World; Abhorash left behind a code: restraint, refinement, respect for the opponent. This may be the closest thing to "morality" that a vampire has ever produced.

Of course, one might say the Blood Dragon way is another form of self-deception. Dragon's blood may have extinguished the craving for human blood, but the Blood Dragons remain undead, still live on blood, still are not truly "alive." And their obsession with "challenging the strong" is itself a new kind of bloodthirst — the impulse to drink replaced by the impulse to fight, not so different in essence. But the Blood Dragons are untroubled by such critiques. They do not care about philosophical debate. They care about the next fight, the next dragon, the next stage of martial cultivation.

The most important thing Abhorash ever said was not spoken to anyone else. It was what he said to himself after slaying the dragon. No complete record survives in later oral tradition, but the meaning passed down through the Blood Dragon lineage runs roughly thus: *I am no longer a slave.*

In an immortality whose essence is a curse, he found his own honour.
