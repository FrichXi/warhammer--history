# 01 · Tyrion & Teclis

## Overview

Tyrion and Teclis — the most celebrated twins ever born on Ulthuan. Both were descendants of Aenarion, the first Phoenix King, yet they inherited wholly different gifts: Tyrion became the Defender of Ulthuan, the greatest elven warrior of the age, donning the Dragon Armour of his forefather; Teclis became the most powerful High Mage the Old World has ever seen, the man who systematised magic and brought it to The Empire. When the End Times came, Tyrion walked Aenarion's path — drawing the cursed Sword of Khaine, the Widowmaker, only to become Khaine's puppet. Teclis, desperate to stop his brother from annihilating everything, did the unthinkable: he resurrected Malekith, five-thousand-year nemesis of the Asur. The twins, inseparable from birth, were split apart by the Widowmaker into opposing poles. After the Old World fell, Teclis ascended as god of the Realm of Light, while Tyrion was dragged into the Age of Sigmar along with the curse of Khaine.

## Origin

Tyrion and Teclis were born on Ulthuan, scions of Aenarion's bloodline. From the moment of birth they presented a contrast that seemed almost fated: Tyrion was robust, flawless, every inch a warrior in the making; Teclis was frail and sickly, barely able to walk without a staff, so thin a strong wind might knock him over.

But elves are not men. The sickly child proved to possess a magical gift so rare it could be counted on one hand across the entire elven race — he could sense all eight Winds of Magic simultaneously and weave them into High Magic. The strong one excelled at swordsmanship, riding, and tactics, distinguishing himself in the White Lions' guard while still a youth.

The brothers were inseparable from childhood. Tyrion shielded Teclis from every sneer and slight; Teclis used his intellect to see through every battlefield variable on his brother's behalf. A saying circulated among the elven nobility: put Tyrion and Teclis together, and you have the sharpest sword and the keenest eye in a single breath.

As they aged, each brother's abilities reached their zenith. Tyrion donned the Dragon Armour of Aenarion, mounted his steed Malhandir, and became the acknowledged "Defender" of Ulthuan — not a title, but what the entire Asur race called him by default. Teclis entered the White Tower of Hoeth to study High Magic and surpassed every tutor in astonishingly little time, becoming the youngest High Loremaster in elven history. His power so unnerved the Council of Mages that some whispered: had the child somehow siphoned away Tyrion's half of their shared vitality?

## Deeds

### The War of the Twins

The brothers' first major joint campaign came in 2301 IC, during the third great Chaos incursion. Chaos hosts broke through the Kislev defence line and drove straight for the heart of the Old World. Emperor Magnus the Pious of The Empire sent pleas to allies across the world. Teclis led a contingent of elven mages across the ocean to the Old World.

This was no mere military aid. On the battlefield, Teclis saw human spellcasters dying by the score — either from uncontrolled magic or burned as witches by their own people. They had talent but no system. After the Chaos incursion was repelled, Teclis did something that altered human history: he stayed in The Empire and helped Magnus found the Imperial Colleges of Magic, separating the eight Winds into eight independent orders and dragging human magic out of chaotic wilderness into a codified discipline, using the theoretical framework of elven High Magic. The Empire now had a legal, organised corps of mages. It remains one of the greatest gifts the elves ever gave the men of the Old World.

Meanwhile, Tyrion fought his own wars on Ulthuan. Every few centuries, the Witch King Malekith of the Dark Elves would launch a massive invasion of Ulthuan. Every time, Tyrion was on the front lines. The sight of him charging on his white steed turned back armies several times the size of his own. Among the Dark Elves a saying spread: "See gold armour and a white horse, prepare to die." The High Elves called him the Light of the Seas, convinced that as long as Tyrion lived, Ulthuan would never fall.

### The Widowmaker

When the End Times began, everything accelerated into ruin.

Chaos pressed down from the north, Skaven boiled up from the deeps, undead rose across the globe. The Dark Elves seized their chance and invaded Ulthuan. The High Elves were beaten back province by province. Phoenix King Finubar died in the chaos — the throne stood empty. The weight pressing on Tyrion's shoulders exceeded anything a mortal could bear. He was the Defender, but now there was nothing left to defend.

At this moment, the Sword of Khaine — the Widowmaker — reappeared on Ulthuan. This was the cursed blade Aenarion had drawn from the Shrine of Asuryan in another desperate age: it granted its wielder invincible power while devouring their soul, slowly transforming them into the incarnation of Khaine, God of Murder. Aenarion had used it to repel the first Chaos invasion, at the cost of an indelible curse upon himself and all his descendants. After his death, the sword was sealed away in the north of Ulthuan. Generation after generation of elves walked around it. No one dared touch it.

Tyrion walked up to the blade.

His logic was irrefutable: Ulthuan was being crushed between Chaos and the Dark Elves. Convention could no longer turn the tide. Aenarion, too, had drawn the Widowmaker only when facing annihilation — and Tyrion was Aenarion's blood. If he drew the sword, perhaps he could do what his ancestor had done.

He drew the Widowmaker.

The moment the sword's power flooded his body, he was invincible. Every enemy assaulting Ulthuan shattered against him. But simultaneously, the blade began to consume him. His eyes turned blood-red. His voice no longer sounded like his own. With every kill, Khaine's will claimed another sliver of him. The Dark Elf sorceresses were delighted — Khaine was exactly whom they worshipped.

### Teclis's Betrayal and the Truth of Malekith

Teclis watched it all happen.

Of everyone in the elven race, he knew Tyrion best. He understood that his brother had drawn the blade to protect, but he could also see where that path ended: Tyrion would be wholly devoured and become a second Aenarion — and Aenarion's ending was to immolate himself, thrusting the Widowmaker back into the sacred flame, sealing it with his own death. Teclis did not want his brother dead. Nor did he want Ulthuan to perish at Khaine's hand.

He made a decision no one could have foreseen: he resurrected Malekith.

The Witch King, the traitor the High Elves hated most, the tyrant of the Dark Elves, Ulthuan's arch-enemy for five millennia. Teclis found Malekith's soul, brought him back to Ulthuan, and persuaded him to walk once more into the Flame of Asuryan.

This was Teclis's calculation: Asuryan's flame had burned Malekith but had not killed him — he was the only being ever to enter the flame and survive. What if the fire had not rejected him, but tested him? What if five thousand years of dark exile were the god's own forging?

Malekith entered the flame. The fire healed every burn, shed his cursed armour, and revealed a whole human face beneath. The truth was laid bare: he had always been Asuryan's true chosen Phoenix King. The flame had not accepted him the first time because Asuryan did not want a proud heir — he wanted a king who had endured five thousand years of suffering and failure before he was worthy of the throne.

Then Phoenix King Malekith faced Khaine's avatar, Tyrion, head-on.

Brother became enemy. Wielding the power of Khaine, Tyrion slew Malekith's dragon and gravely wounded the Witch King himself. At the close of this battle that was tearing the elven race apart, Asuryan's power seeped through and drove Khaine's will from Tyrion's body. Tyrion, in a fleeting moment of clarity, died on the battlefield.

Teclis stood by through all of it. He could not stop his brother dying. Everything he had done — resurrecting Malekith, revealing the true Phoenix King, uniting the three elven peoples — was fundamentally to keep Tyrion from dying under Khaine's control, to keep Ulthuan from being dragged down with the twins' tragedy. He saved the elves. He could not save his brother.

After Tyrion, Malekith united the High Elves, Dark Elves, and Wood Elves into a combined elven host that held the line until the Old World's final moment.

### Age of Sigmar

After the Old World collapsed, the Mortal Realms precipitated out of the chaos. Teclis entered the Realm of Light, Hysh, and ascended to godhood. He is the central architect of the Lumineth Realm-Lords — a new race carrying forward the magical traditions of the High Elves, a continuation of Ulthuan's legacy. He also stands as one of Sigmar's key allies in the Pantheon of Order.

Tyrion's fate is more obscure. Legends say his soul was dragged into some state beyond mortal existence — neither death nor full divinity — bound together with the curse of the Widowmaker. The shadow that clings to Aenarion's bloodline, from life to death, from the Old World to the new epoch, has never truly lifted.

## Fate & Present Status

Teclis: ascended god of Hysh, architect of the Lumineth Realm-Lords, ally of Sigmar. Tyrion: lost to the Widowmaker's curse, his soul suspended between states — neither dead nor divine, the Aenarion shadow unbroken across eons.

## Verdict

The story of Tyrion and Teclis is, at its heart, a fable of two halves of one whole.

They were twins who divided Aenarion's legacy between them: one took the gift of the body, the other the frontier of the mind. For centuries this division worked flawlessly — Tyrion fought, Teclis schemed; one kindled hope at the front, the other built systems in the rear. The High Elves did not fall for millennia largely because these twins held the two flanks upright.

But the Widowmaker shattered that precision structure.

The moment Tyrion drew the sword, Teclis faced one of the cruellest dilemmas in the Warhammer cosmos: save your brother, at the cost of the elven race's extinction; save the elves, at the cost of consigning your brother to death. There was no third path. He chose to resurrect Malekith, to pit five thousand years of enemy truth against his brother's fall. In outcome, the choice worked: the elves were not exterminated, Khaine did not win. But in process, Teclis personally pushed his twin onto a foreordained death.

Tyrion's tragedy cuts even deeper than Aenarion's, for this reason: he knew the sword would consume him. He had read his ancestor's war histories. Since childhood he had been warned countless times never to touch that blade. Yet at Ulthuan's direst hour, he chose the exact path his ancestor had taken — because he had no other choice, because he was Aenarion's heir. He inherited the ancestor's strength. He inherited the ancestor's curse. The sword was the knot baked into his bloodline from the beginning.

In the Age of Sigmar, Teclis became a god. By any measure, the highest achievement an elf can reach. But he is a god of light split in half — his twin is gone, taken by the very blade Teclis had foreseen would consume his brother centuries earlier. A god who vanquished Chaos, founded the Realm of Light, inherited all of Ulthuan's magical legacy — and could not save one man.
