# 08 · Neferata

## Overview

Neferata, Queen of Lahmia in ancient Nehekhara, the first vampire of the Old World, progenitor of the Lahmian bloodline. With mortal hands, she refined Nagash's elixir of immortality and birthed the vampire race; with millennia of patience, she wove a web of covert power spanning the entire Old World; with beauty beyond mortal reckoning and cunning beyond mortal measure, she bent kings and nobles to her will without their ever knowing. Undeath was never her curse. It was the sceptre in her hand.

## Origin

### The Queen in the Desert

Neferata was born to the royal house of the city-state of Lahmia in the ancient empire of Nehekhara. It was an age of warring cities. In Khemri, Nagash had already usurped the throne and raised his Black Pyramid. The shadow of necromancy was spreading across the deserts. Into this turmoil, Neferata ascended the throne of Lahmia to become the first female pharaoh in Nehekharan history.

Nagash needed allies. He extended an olive branch to Lahmia, bestowing his secret elixir of longevity — a blend of human blood and rare herbs that slowed ageing and preserved vitality. Neferata accepted the gift. She did not, as Nagash expected, become his vassal.

Her ambition had never been smaller than Nagash's. It was merely wrapped more exquisitely.

In the depths of the Lahmian court, Neferata worked in secret with her closest attendants — her bodyguard captain Abhorash, the noble prince Vashanesh, and others — to refine Nagash's formula. She purged the original recipe of the component that withered the flesh into skeletal gauntness, creating a purer and far more perilous draught: the Elixir of Blood. Around 1350 years before the Imperial Calendar, she and her most trusted followers raised the cup and drank.

Immortality descended. Strength beyond mortal limits, speed, and a deep affinity for magic flooded their veins. But a price came with it — a curse absent from Nagash's original formula: the blood-thirst. From that moment, they could sustain their existence only by drinking from the living. Lest their power wane and their reason unravel, degenerating into mindless, blood-crazed beasts.

Every vampire in the world traces back to that first cup of blood.

## Deeds

### The Lahmian Court — Golden Age of the Undying Aristocracy

The first vampires did not rush to conquer the world. They built a sumptuous court in Lahmia — a pleasure-city of immortals. Music and feasting day after day, blood-wine flowing night after night. Neferata sat at the centre of her throne, ruling her new race with a queen's majesty and a vampire's mesmerism. Her court was the pinnacle of beauty and the pinnacle of cruelty — every mortal who set foot in the palace of Lahmia might become the vintage at that night's banquet.

This was the closest the vampire race ever came to a golden age. The bloodlines had not yet split. Neferata's authority went unchallenged. Lahmia glittered under the moon like a jewel set in the desert.

But secrets cannot hide forever. Servants vanishing from the palace, the queen's face unchanged by decades, human screams piercing the night — the neighbouring kings began to take note. The truth was uncovered faster than Neferata had anticipated. The city-states of Nehekhara swiftly formed a coalition and laid siege to Lahmia. These desert kings might have been enemies to one another, but on the matter of "exterminate the undead abominations," there was no dissent.

Lahmia became a ruin in fire and war cries. The first generation of vampires fled in every direction — Abhorash went north in search of mastery, Vashanesh slipped east into Sylvania, and the rest scattered like seeds across the Old World. History calls it the First Diaspora.

Neferata had lost everything. But unlike some fugitives who sank into despair or madness, she simply withdrew — temporarily.

### The Silver Pinnacle — Weaver of a Thousand-Year Web

Neferata retreated north with a handful of retainers and, deep in the World's Edge Mountains, found an abandoned Dwarf outpost. She spent centuries reshaping it into a hidden palace: the Silver Pinnacle. From outside, it looked like a shard of rock among the peaks. Step inside and corridors led to banquet halls, banquet halls led to secret chambers, and secret chambers led down into the heart of the mountain that no outsider had ever seen.

This was not a refuge. It was a command centre.

Neferata had learned the lesson of Lahmia's fall. She would never again attempt to build an open vampire kingdom. She chose a more covert route, more enduring and more chilling: infiltration.

She founded the Lahmian Sisterhood — a clandestine order composed entirely of female vampires. These women wielded beauty, mesmerism, and immortality as their weapons, infiltrating the courts, temples, noble households, and merchant guilds of every nation in the Old World. They became kings' mistresses, high priests' advisors, duchesses' confidantes, wealthy merchants' private secretaries. They never openly seized power. They let the powerful believe the power was their own — while in reality, every key decision had a silk-gloved hand moving the pieces from behind.

The influence radiating from the Silver Pinnacle was vast, yet few knew it existed. A duke of Bretonnia found himself drowning in a border war without ever realising the war had been instigated by his mistress — a Lahmian agent — for the sole purpose of placing a more pliable heir on the throne. An Elector Count of The Empire suddenly changed the laws of succession because his new wife had whispered in his ear for three years.

Neferata herself seldom left the Silver Pinnacle. She sat in the highest chamber of the mountain palace, like an old woman spinning thread in the dark — the other end of each thread tied to half the power centres of the Old World. She did not need to act personally. She had already woven a net large enough.

This net was the only power base among the five bloodline founders that did not rely on military force. Vlad relied on armies, Abhorash on martial perfection, the Strigoi on brute ferocity, the Necrarchs on sorcery — Neferata relied on patience and information. Her enemies were often deposed by their own court allies before they had even raised a sword.

She despised open conflict, not from cowardice, but because open conflict was too ugly, too primitive, too much like Nagash's way of doing things. Her entire life was a demonstration of one point: she was not Nagash's creation. She was Nagash's surpasser.

### A Thousand Years of Evading Nagash

Every time Nagash rose, he summoned his "children" back to his banner. Every time, Neferata pretended not to hear.

She understood Nagash too well. He did not recognise heirs, only tools. Vampires, in his eyes, were improved elixir containers. Neferata had spent fifteen hundred years building her network of power. She was not about to hand everything over at Nagash's first beckoning. She sealed herself in the Silver Pinnacle, shut the passageways, and used the Sisterhood to spread false intelligence — making Nagash believe she had died in some internecine struggle. Nagash fell and rose again, fell and rose again; his undying will was like an underground river beneath the desert sands, drying up in one era only to surface through an unknown crevice in the next. Neferata feared this, and could not help but admire it.

### The End Times — A Queen Recalled

The twilight of the Old World descended. Chaos crept down from the north, the dead rose from their graves, and even the Death God Nagash himself reassembled his shattered soul. This time, Nagash completed the work four millennia in the making — he achieved true apotheosis, becoming the incarnation of death itself, lord of the Realm of Death.

Then he glanced back at his "children."

Neferata could no longer hide. In the deluge of the End Times, the Silver Pinnacle could not stand alone. The hosts of Chaos were closing in, and Nagash's summons was deafening. Neferata faced the question she had evaded for over a thousand years: face Chaos alone, gambling that the Silver Pinnacle could survive the world's ending; or return to Nagash's fold, becoming a piece on the board of the Realm of Death.

She chose the latter. Not from loyalty — from realism. Nagash was a true god now. Resistance was futile. Better to serve as a Mortarch beneath the throne of Death than to be ground into nameless bone under the hooves of Chaos.

Nagash granted her one of the nine seats of the Mortarchs. Her Lahmian spy network transformed in the grand alliance of Death into a new function — no longer manipulating the power of the living, but infiltrating enemy intelligence lines, spreading false battle reports, and sowing discord among Chaos's tribal alliances. The thing she had always done best, transplanted to a new battlefield, remained her home ground.

The Silver Pinnacle was eventually reduced to rubble under Chaos's advance. But Neferata herself did not perish with her tower. She survived, and continues to survive. To a vampire who has lived four thousand years, losing a palace is nothing. She has patience enough to build another.

## Fate & Present Status

Neferata survived the End Times and entered the Age of Sigmar as one of Nagash's Mortarchs, the queen of shadows and whispers. The Silver Pinnacle is gone, but the Lahmian Sisterhood's methods — infiltration, seduction, manipulation — remain as effective in the Mortal Realms as they ever were in the Old World. Where there is power, there is a place for Neferata.

## Verdict

Neferata is the true mother of the vampire race. Nagash gave them the curse in their blood; Neferata gave them their self-awareness as a species. In Nagash's blueprint, vampires were supposed to be an obedient undead sub-species. In Neferata's actual execution, they became an independent, proud race with their own aesthetic and will to power. One cup of blood in the palace of Lahmia opened the history of vampires — not as Nagash's appendages, but as the hidden rulers of the Old World.

Her philosophy of survival is cold rationality at its most extreme. Nagash charged headlong, Vlad fought in the open, Mannfred flip-flopped endlessly, Abhorash sought spiritual purification — all her contemporaries among the bloodline founders wrestled with the curse in violent ways. Only Neferata chose not to wrestle at all. She made the curse into daily routine, turned blood-thirst into social etiquette, and wielded immortality as political capital. She was the quickest of the five founders to accept her monstrous nature, and because she accepted it so calmly, she lived the longest.

But her flaws are equally visible. Her entire power rested on indirect control — she had no army she could field for a stand-up war. Once the web was torn, her hand's worth of power evaporated rapidly. Her return to Nagash's fold in the End Times was, in essence, the bankruptcy of fifteen hundred years of independent operation. Against true god-level power, all shadow operations amount to nothing.

And yet, on a grander scale, it may not be failure at all. Nagash's empire was built on undead legions — it has been overthrown countless times across history. Neferata's network was built on the soft underbelly of human society. So long as there are living people, and power, and desire — her playbook will never go out of date. After the Old World's destruction, her methods continued to work in the shadow wars of the Realm of Death. To find one's ecological niche in any era — that itself is a kind of millennial wisdom.

Nagash created the undying body. Neferata created the undying way of life. Nagash's victory is called godhood. Neferata's victory is called this: she has never admitted defeat, and she has never been truly beaten. For a woman who has lived four thousand years and is still smiling somewhere in the dark — that is, perhaps, the best ending there is.
