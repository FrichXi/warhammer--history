# 02 · Karl Franz

## Overview

Karl Franz — the greatest Emperor of The Empire's twilight years, Elector Count of Reikland, wielder of Ghal Maraz. He ascended the imperial throne in The Empire's most precarious hour and, by sheer force of will, re-forged the squabbling Elector Counts into a whole. He won battles on the most impossible of fields, and when the End Times came, he became the vessel of Sigmar's divine body — burning himself out, and with him, the last era in which The Empire still had light.

He was not a god. He was a mortal emperor who brought an army, a warhammer, and a griffon, and stood before the apocalypse without running. For that alone, the people of The Empire called him "Sigmar Reborn" — a title shouted by soldiers on the battlefield, not conferred by the church.

## Origin

Karl Franz was born into Reikland's ruling house — the Franz family. His father, Luitpold, was Emperor. He was no commoner who clawed his way up; he was an imperial prince. But The Empire's throne is not hereditary. The Emperor is elected by the Elector Counts — if you're mediocre, your father's blood means nothing.

In 2502 IC, Luitpold died. The fifteen Elector Counts convened in Altdorf for the election. Karl Franz, at the time, was merely the Elector Count of Reikland — young, untested, hardly the frontrunner. Boris Todbringer, Elector Count of Middenland, outstripped him in both strength and renown.

But Karl did something rare in imperial politics: he neither threatened with force nor bought votes with marriage pacts. He relied purely on his tongue and his mind, persuading enough Elector Counts to vote for him. His promise was not a share of the spoils — it was "to make The Empire an empire again." The Elector Counts cast their votes, half-expecting a joke.

They did not get a joke. They got an emperor.

When Karl Franz knelt in the Great Cathedral of Sigmar and reached for Ghal Maraz — the legendary warhammer that no man had lifted since Sigmar himself — a mortal calmly raised it above his head. The Grand Theogonist proclaimed then and there that he was Sigmar's chosen. Believe in gods or not, the sight brought the whole of Altdorf to its knees.

## Deeds

### Uniting the Empire

Karl inherited a wreck: Elector Counts suspicious of each other and acting independently, the cults locked in internecine strife, border towns sacked on rotation by Greenskins and Beastmen, the imperial treasury so barren it could not pay the soldiery. Call it a "loose confederation" if you were being polite — in truth, it was waiting to be destroyed.

The first thing he did was not to march an army against anyone. He wrote letters. A personal letter to every Elector Count — earnest but not grovelling, neither commanding nor begging. He proposed a grand council in Altdorf, not to quarrel but to divide tasks: who holds which border, who pays, who provides troops. A batch of Elector Counts watched coldly from the sidelines, but several key provinces — Talabecland, Stirland — were moved by him. The gears of The Empire began to turn again after that.

Within a few years, he had downgraded the hostility between Elector Counts from "wishing the other dead" to "I still don't like you, but let's kill the Greenskins first." In the context of The Empire's two-thousand-year history of infighting, this verged on miracle.

### The Warrior Emperor

Karl was not an emperor who sat in a palace studying maps. He rode an Imperial Griffon — Deathclaw — and personally led armies into the field. The Empire's long history had no shortage of campaigning emperors, but those who, like Karl Franz, appeared at the very front of nearly every major battle could be counted on one hand.

Wielding Ghal Maraz, he personally repelled an enormous Greenskin WAAAGH! at the walls of Nuln; he carved his way through more than one undead host along the borders of Sylvania; he drove off Norscan raiding fleets on the shores of Nordland. He was not a commander waving a pennant from the rear — he was the man swinging a warhammer into the enemy's skull. When soldiers saw their Emperor standing in the front rank, retreat ceased to be an option.

Ghal Maraz in his hands was more than a symbol of authority — that thing killed, and the killing was done by him.

### The Alliance with Bretonnia

Bretonnia and The Empire were the Old World's two greatest human powers, but for two thousand years their relationship had been complicated — occasional allies, more often mutual disdain. Imperials thought Bretonnian knights were posturing fools; Bretonnians thought the imperial army was a collection of honourless mercenaries.

Karl changed this. He forged a personal friendship with King Louen Leoncoeur of Bretonnia — both rode monstrous steeds (a griffon, a hippogryph), both fought with their own hands, both were idealists. The Treaty of the Griffon they negotiated was the first formal military alliance between the two great human nations. Its significance rivaled that of Sigmar's eternal pact with the Dwarfs — at the hour of greatest threat, humanity finally stopped fighting itself.

### The Storm of Chaos

In 2522 IC, Archaon the Everchosen led the hosts of Chaos south. The Storm of Chaos engulfed the north. Kislev fell; Praag was reduced to rubble under siege. The Chaos vanguard aimed straight for Middenheim — The Empire's northern capital.

Karl Franz led the imperial main army north. In the great battle for Middenheim, the combined army of The Empire and the Dwarfs held the walls to the last. Valten, the incarnation of Sigmar, faced Archaon in single combat beneath the city. Karl Franz himself did not square off one-on-one with the Everchosen, but he directed the entire campaign, beating back each of Archaon's divided hosts in turn. The Storm of Chaos ultimately failed to take Middenheim. Archaon was forced to retreat — The Empire survived.

For a nation widely regarded as "about to collapse," this battle proved Karl was not flailing in a death spiral. He was seriously winning.

### The End Times — Sigmar Reborn

The true test came three years later. In 2525 IC, the End Times began. This was not another Storm of Chaos — not a "massive invasion." This was the end of the Old World itself. Nagash was resurrected. Skaven boiled from the deeps. Undead blanketed every battlefield. Archaon descended with the full might of Chaos — tenfold stronger than before.

Province by province, The Empire fell. Altdorf was besieged. Ostland was butchered. Middenheim hung on by its nails under the Chaos siege. Every army that could be mobilised had been committed; every stockpile that could be burned was ash. By the final phase of the End Times, The Empire no longer existed in any practical sense — only a scattered remnant of people still fighting.

Karl Franz did not surrender. He gathered the relics of the Sigmarite cult and the remnants of the imperial army and fought on, everywhere there was still ground to stand on. At some decisive junction of the End Times — the exact details now lost with the world's destruction — Karl Franz was fully possessed by Sigmar's divine power. His mortal flesh became the vessel of Sigmar walking the earth. Ghal Maraz in his hand was no longer a human weapon. It was a god's hammer.

He fought to the very end in Sigmar's body. By the accounts of those who might have witnessed it, the power he unleashed in that final battle made even the slaves of the Chaos Gods feel fear. He died. The world died. The death of Karl Franz was the moment The Empire ended.

## Fate & Present Status

Karl Franz died in the End Times, his body consumed as the vessel of Sigmar incarnate. His death marked the final extinction of The Empire — a mortal who rose to godhood for a single apocalyptic battle, then burned out with the world he had spent his reign trying to save.

## Verdict

Karl Franz was not a founding god-emperor like Sigmar. He could not forge tribes into a nation or win a single battle that secured a thousand-year foundation. He inherited an empire already sliding downward — politics rotten, armies exhausted, faith shaken, enemies pressing from every side. Everything he did was slamming the brakes at the edge of a cliff.

But his greatness lies precisely in this: in an age where the known outcome was ruin, he lived as a man who still believed he could win. He did not despair. He did not betray. He did not give up. He picked up the shattered pieces of The Empire from the ground and fitted them back together and led everyone through the last war. He was Emperor for decades, and every day The Empire looked ready to collapse tomorrow — but on the day it actually did collapse, not a single soul felt the Emperor had let them down.

The people of The Empire called him "Sigmar Reborn." The church later made it official doctrine. But the soldiers on the field did not follow him because of that. They followed him because the Emperor walked in front.

From mortal Elector Count to divine avatar, from the Great Cathedral of Altdorf to the ashes of the End Times — this man's life was The Empire's last time lifting its head.
