# 08 · Dwarf Runesmithing & Engineering

## Overview

Dwarfs do not cast spells. Not that they cannot—it is physically impossible. The Eight Winds of Magic blow down from the Chaos Wastes; they seep into humans, they flow through the elves, but when they hit a dwarf, they bounce off like wind against a stone wall. This is not a gap in training—it is essence. Dwarfs are innately immune to all magic in fluid form.

But no magic does not mean no power. The dwarfs found an entirely different path: they do not ride the wind—they hammer it into things.

---

## I. The Guilds: A Kingdom in the Mountain

Dwarf society is not a city-state, not a kingdom, not an empire. It is the Guilds.

Every dwarf, from the day they can grip a tool, belongs to a guild. The Miners' Guild bores through the mountain; the Smiths' Guild turns stone into steel; the Weaponsmiths' Guild turns steel into instruments of killing; the Brewers' Guild turns grain into whatever fermentable liquid dwarfs can find within arm's reach. No guild, no identity; no identity, no name; no name, you are not a dwarf.

At the apex of the pyramid sit two behemoths: the Engineers Guild, which commands steam, blackpowder, alloy ratios, and everything capable of producing a loud noise on the battlefield; and the Runesmiths' Guild, which commands runes—the only "magic" the dwarfs acknowledge, the first hammer-stroke taught by the Ancestor God Grungni himself.

The two guilds cannot stand the sight of each other. Not the kind of hostility that manifests in muttered mockery behind closed doors—the kind where they pass each other in a hold corridor and do not nod. The feud has lasted millennia and has never once erupted into civil war. Dwarf stubbornness makes them incapable of reconciliation; the same stubbornness makes them incapable of betraying their own race. When the great enemy is at the gates, neither guild's firepower is ever absent.

## II. Runes: Will Forged Into Iron

The foundational principle of rune magic is a single word: *forge*.

Human mages chant incantations; elven mages meditate; Chaos sorcerers sacrifice—all of them are "calling upon" magic. Dwarfs call upon nothing. A Runesmith spends decades, sometimes centuries, hammering a lifetime's will and the memory of the ancestors into metal, one blow at a time. This does not resemble spellcasting; it resembles engraving an eternal command into iron.

Once a rune is struck, the binding is permanent. It does not fade; it does not backlash. A warding rune forged into a shield makes that shield five times harder than ordinary steel—no conditions attached. A burning rune struck into an axe-blade sets fire to whatever it cuts—no argument.

The power-source of runes is not the Realm of Chaos; it is the vestigial will of the dwarf Ancestor Gods. Every rune is a soul-imprint of an ancestor—not metaphor; the dwarfs believe this literally. Grungni forged the first rune; Grimnir carved runes into his war-axe and walked into the Chaos Wastes; Valaya buried protection-runes into the foundations of the first dwarfhold. Every Runesmith since is merely repeating what the ancestors already did—not creating something new, but executing the old thing more precisely, making it more like what the ancestor's own hand would have done.

The mightiest runes can carry three functions simultaneously. Ghal Maraz, forged by Alaric the Mad, is the archetype—it ignites whatever it strikes, reflects psychic assault, and grants the wielder perception of malice in those nearby. Still greater works by Alaric himself are said to have performed seven functions in a single blow, but most of those have been lost along with the Runesmith himself.

The fatal limitation of rune magic: runes *deplete*. Every rune slowly consumes its ancestral charge through use—less with each activation, dimmer with each passing century. The Masters capable of forging new runes grow ever fewer, not for lack of genius, but because the techniques of a thousand years ago were never passed down. Every Runesmith who dies takes with him runes that will never be struck again. This is not an academic gap—it is a race losing its own memory in slow motion.

## III. Ghal Maraz

Skull-Splitter. The Empire's foremost warhammer, the namesake weapon of Sigmar Heldenhammer's title.

It is the ultimate expression of runic artifice. Alaric poured a lifetime's mastery into it—this Runesmith, nicknamed "the Mad," is said to have spent a hundred days without leaving the forge during the hammer's making, until the weapon was complete and he himself collapsed unconscious beside the anvil.

Ghal Maraz's core effect: it grants its wielder the ability to shatter any target with precision measured in microns. Not brute force—precision. Every blow lands exactly upon the target's point of weakness and delivers precisely the force required to destroy it. One swing, and dragon-scale powders; a daemon's skull shatters like a clay pot; Chaos plate bursts open like paper. But there is a detail: Ghal Maraz cannot be wielded by just anyone. Its runes carry a condition—only a wielder "acknowledged" by the warhammer itself can activate its full power. In the hands of one unacknowledged, it remains a fine hammer, but only a fine hammer, and it will not produce the blaze of golden light that lit half the sky above Black Fire Pass.

The hammer was later bestowed upon Sigmar by the Dwarf High King Kurgan Ironbeard in person (see the Life of Sigmar for full details). The highest craft of Karaz-a-Karak passed into the hands of a human—not as a trade, but as the price the dwarfs set for a life-debt, paid with the most precious thing they owned.

## IV. Blackpowder and Steam

The Engineers Guild's philosophy is the exact inverse of the Runesmiths': the Rune-folk hold that "good things take time"; the Engineers hold that "good things need to be faster and more numerous."

The dwarfs possess the most advanced firearms technology in the Old World—not the most advanced among several, *the* most advanced. The Imperial Gunnery School of Nuln has studied for centuries and still produces cannons a full grade inferior to the dwarfs'. This is not a technological gap; it is a philosophical one. A human engineer designs a cannon and considers it a success if it fires; a dwarf engineer designs a cannon and demands that under the most extreme conditions—thunderstorm, forty-below-zero, stomped on by a Troll—it must not, under any circumstances, explode in the barrel. After the cannon comes the Organ Gun, multi-barrelled volley fire whose saturation can make an entire charging rank of Orcs topple backward simultaneously. Then comes the Flame Cannon—not solid shot, but jets of fire, purpose-built against the rat-tides boiling up from below. Then the Gyrocopter takes to the sky—a steam-driven single-rotor craft from which dwarfs rain lead. The Gyrobomber follows, larger rotors, more bombs. At the infantry level, the killing piece is the Drakegun—a single tongue of flame burning through an entire tunnel.

Behind all these weapons stands a sentence dwarfs never speak aloud but inscribe in the margin of every blueprint: absolutely reliable, tenfold safety redundancy, better three tonnes overweight than a barrel-burst. No dwarf cannon has ever gone to the field without testing—more shells are spent in proofing than will ever be fired in battle. This is not caution; it is obsession. A dwarf engineer inspecting his product is not checking a machine—he is showing the ancestors that he has not shamed them.

## V. The Eternal Debate

The conflict between the Runesmiths' Guild and the Engineers Guild is no secret—every hold can hear it.

The Runesmiths' logic is simple: you play with blackpowder; a cannon rusts in three years. A rune is still glowing after three thousand. You call this progress; we call it desecration—the desecration of the principle that a weapon should be hammered into being with reverence, not welded in batches.

The Engineers' logic is equally simple: one rune of yours takes ten years to strike; the skaven do not wait, the greenskins do not wait. By the time your rune is hammered out, the hold's gates have already been battered open three times.

In peacetime, the two factions occupy separate sectors of the hold, each with their own voting rights, oil and water. Between the Rune-forge quarter and the Engineers' bearing-workshops sits a full level of abandoned mine-tunnels, to prevent either side from hearing the other's hammer-fall and taking offence. The High King presides above, favouring neither—and able to favour neither, since whichever side he leans toward, the other will enter it into the Book of Grudges.

In wartime, everything changes. The two faction-commanders stand together in the war-room, and every council of war is an unending debate. Should the Runesmiths be brought to the front lines to perform field-enchantment? The Engineers object—that would clog the logistics corridors. Should the Gyrobombers be scrambled to destroy the siege towers? The Runesmiths have an opinion—the fuel might detonate over the hold's water source. The two factions argue perpetually, and perpetually, while they argue—the cannons fire, the runes glow, and the enemy falls before the walls. Here lies the dwarf paradox: they do not get along, but they never undercut each other.

## VI. The Book of Grudges

If the guild system is the dwarf skeleton, the Book of Grudges is the dwarf bloodstream.

In the dwarf tongue, it is called the *Dammaz Kron*—the Book of Grudges, the Ledger of Wrongs. The physical form of the book is irrelevant (every hold has at least one, guarded personally by its Grudge-Scribe); what matters is the operational logic. Every wrong ever committed against the dwarfs—from a defaulted debt to the murder of a king—must be recorded. Every grudge bears a date of occurrence, the persons involved, the compensation amount (if any), and the complete ancestral genealogy of all aggrieved parties. Not simple grudge-holding—grudge-bookkeeping.

The dwarf logic is not "revenge is sweet." Their logic is far colder: one life equals one life. A dwarf's death must be levelled by at least the death of the one who caused it. If the other party cannot pay—greenskins have no diplomatic envoys, for example—then payment is collected on the battlefield by axe. If that still does not suffice—if the offending party is already extinct—that entry in the Book receives a "Pending" stamp and transfers to the hereditary archive, waiting for the day some descendant encounters the right opportunity for commensurate settlement.

This is how the War of the Beard began. The elves shaved a dwarf emissary's beard. That single act sparked a war that lasted over four hundred years and killed nearly the entire upper echelons of both sides. To outsiders, this is the height of absurdity. To dwarfs, it is the fundamental operating principle of civilisation—to forget an insult is not tolerance but degeneracy.

The bookkeeping technology of the Book of Grudges is highly advanced. Dwarfs invented double-entry accounting not for coin but for grudges. They possess a device called the "Grudge-Settlement Ledger," purpose-built to process cross-generational grievances—lives owed by ancestors, wounds repaid by descendants, all aligned on a single spreadsheet. A human chronicler would see himself doing petty arithmetic; a dwarf Grudge-Scribe would see the base-code of how the world works.

## VII. The Doom of Decline

All of the above—the glory, the obstinacy, the brilliance, the stubbornness—rests on a foundation that is silently crumbling: the dwarfs are going extinct.

Not being exterminated—slowly vanishing. The birthrate of dwarf women is so low that a single hold might see a mere handful of newborns in a century. Hold after hold has collapsed soundlessly under successive assaults by greenskins and skaven—no reinforcements to call, no recruits to muster; every dwarf warrior who falls is a name erased outright from the clan-tree. Karak Eight Peaks was lost. Karak Drazh fell. Karak Kadrin was swallowed by Chaos. The subterranean highway network that once threaded the entire length of the Worlds Edge Mountains—the arterial system of the dwarf Golden Age—is now, across the vast majority of its length, either collapsed or occupied by skaven.

The cruelest part is this: every dwarf knows it. This is not panic—it is a daily weight on the shoulder. A dwarf smith at his forge knows he may be the last living member of his family line. A dwarf warrior on the wall knows that if the gate behind him breaks, his hold's name will never again be spoken on the next North Wind's Day. But dwarfs never admit it. They do not discuss extinction, the way they do not discuss defeat. They write every new entry into the Book of Grudges, adjust every screw on the engineering blueprint, strike every hammer-blow on the runes—as though their mountain-realm could live another ten thousand years.

This is not denial of reality; this is dwarf dignity. Grimnir himself walked into a doom from which there was no return and did not look back. The dwarfs learned this lesson: falling is not to be feared; what is to be feared is not being in the middle of doing something when the last moment comes.

## VIII. Epilogue

Dwarf craftsmanship and rune magic are, in essence, a story about time. The Runesmith turns a hundred years into a groove that glows forever; the Engineer turns a thousand tests into a cannon that never bursts; the Book of Grudges turns every second of fury into an heirloom passable to a great-grandson. Everything they do is a war against the one variable the dwarfs can never control: time itself.

And time, in the end, won. Not by force of arms (the dwarfs fought for ten thousand years and were never conquered in open battle), but by attrition—a war the birthrate could not win, the holds' survival could not win, the lost rune-lore could not win. But the dwarfs delivered their answer: Ghal Maraz, the greatest weapon of the Old World, born on a dwarf anvil and raised into the sky by a human emperor. That hammer proves where the highest dwarf craft was sent—not kept in their own hands to die with the species, but given to one who was willing to stand alongside them in battle. The dwarfs were stubborn for ten thousand years, but on the matter of trust, they never miscalculated.
