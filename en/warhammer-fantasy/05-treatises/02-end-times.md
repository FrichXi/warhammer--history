# 02 · The Book of the End Times

## Overview

The End Times was the final settlement of every hatred, every ambition, and every unsettled debt in the Old World.

No one knows its precise starting point. Perhaps it was ordained the moment the Old Ones built the polar gates; perhaps it began brewing when Nagash unleashed his first necromantic ritual in Nehekhara; perhaps, from the day Sigmar walked into the World's Edge Mountains without a backward glance, the ending was already written in the shadows. But none would deny it: around 2525 IC, the Old World began, in an irreversible fashion, to move toward its end.

The name of this ending is the End Times.

---

## I. Prelude: Portents in the Sky

The first sign was not blades but the sky.

The Winds of Magic had always moved each in its own course, maintaining a delicate equilibrium in the skies above the Old World. By the eve of the End Times, this balance had utterly broken. The boundaries among the Eight Winds blurred, overlapped, and fused; magical energies surged across the surface in uncontrolled fashion. Necromancers everywhere found their powers waxing; the Chaos Wastes expanded southward at an unprecedented rate; even humble farmers saw violet lightning splitting the northern sky at night.

At the same time, the Chaos moon Morrslieb — that green orb composed of warpstone — grew larger and brighter, as if slowly descending toward the Old World. The Skaven launched simultaneous offensives through their global under-empire; every one of the Empire's borders was screaming for help. And in the northern Chaos Wastes, a host mustered from the farthest reaches of the ice was breaking camp — Archaon, the Everchosen, was mounting his last great southward march.

The picture was clear: this was not a localised war, nor merely another Chaos incursion, nor a second Storm of Chaos. What the Old World faced this time was a wave of annihilation from every direction at once.

---

## II. The Return of the God of Death

In the chaos of the End Times, the first to fulfil his ultimate destiny was not Chaos, not Man, not the Elves — but Nagash.

Nagash had lain dormant for over a thousand years. His spirit drifted somewhere between death and nothingness, his power faint and near-forgotten. But his most loyal servant — Arkhan the Black, one of the Nine Mortarchs — never abandoned the plan of resurrection. Arkhan allied with the most cunning of vampires, Mannfred von Carstein, to gather Nagash's scattered relics: his ashes, his crown, the scrolls in which he had sealed his dark magic. Mannfred even temporarily resurrected Vlad von Carstein, bringing him back into the world to serve Nagash's return.

The resurrection ritual was completed in the deepest crypt beneath the Old World. The instant Nagash opened his eyes anew, all the necromantic energy in the world trembled — the First Prince of Nehekhara, the inventor of necromancy, the supreme lord of the undead legions, had at last fulfilled the ambition of millennia: he had become the true God of Death.

He had no time to issue commands, however, before the sun god Ptra and the last vestiges of Nehekhara's pantheon dragged him into the depths of the Realm of Chaos, imprisoning him in what would later be called Shyish, the Realm of Death. Nagash missed the final battle of the Old World — but he survived, and he was ready for the new epoch. More importantly, his resurrection signalled the unification of the undead: the Tomb Kings, the Vampire Counts, and the undead legions were all drawn into the nascent framework of what would become the Grand Alliance of Death. Even Settra — that undying Pharaoh who "serves no one" — was forced, before Chaos's total onslaught, to momentarily swallow his pride and acknowledge Nagash's authority.

---

## III. The Truth of the Phoenix King

Almost simultaneously with Nagash's resurrection, an event unfolded on Ulthuan that would change the fate of the Elves.

Taking advantage of the chaos of the roiling Winds and the wider Chaos invasion, the Dark Elves launched their last grand assault on Ulthuan. The High Elves were caught between two pincers: the Druchii forces on one side, the Chaos legions sweeping down from the north on the other. In this hour of existential peril, Ulthuan's general Tyrion — descendant of Aenarion, the greatest warrior of the High Elves — made a terrible decision: he walked into the shrine of Khaine and drew the cursed blade, the Sword of Khaine.

The Sword of Khaine was the most powerful weapon in Elven legend — and the most deeply cursed. Aenarion had used it to drive back the first Chaos incursion, at the price of an eternal curse upon himself and his line. Tyrion's brother Teclis — a supreme master of High Magic — knew exactly what would happen: the Sword of Khaine devours the soul of its wielder. Tyrion was marching toward self-destruction.

Teclis made the boldest decision in the entire history of the Elves: he secretly resurrected Malekith.

Malekith — the one who had triggered the Sundering, who had ruled the Dark Elves of Naggaroth as Witch King for over five thousand years. In all common reckoning, he was the traitor, the condemned, the mortal enemy beyond all forgiveness for the Phoenix Throne. But Teclis saw a deeper truth: the ordeal of the Sacred Flame of Asuryan had never been about selecting the perfect candidate. It was about selecting the true heir. Malekith had been burned by the Flame a thousand years ago not because he was unworthy of the Phoenix King, but because he needed a millennium of ordeal and suffering to become one who *was* worthy.

Malekith returned to Ulthuan and walked into the Flame of Asuryan, which had now split into two halves — light and darkness. Within the fire, his burns healed. Asuryan's will was revealed: Malekith was the true Phoenix King. He always had been.

The three Elven kindreds — High Elves, Dark Elves, and Wood Elves — merged into a single unified host in the final hours of the End Times. Though Ulthuan ultimately fell before Chaos's onslaught, the Elves as a race achieved, in the Old World's last moments, their final reconciliation.

---

## IV. The Empire's Last Stand

The Empire of Man, in the End Times, faced a noose tightening from every side.

Archaon's Chaos host descended from the north with a scale that dwarfed every legion of the Storm of Chaos era — it contained every champion of all four Chaos Gods, daemonic legions, the daemon-engines of the Chaos Dwarfs, and the entire martial strength of the northern tribes. The Empire's borderlands collapsed one by one: Kislev fell, Ostland was reduced to cinders, Middenheim was besieged. From the south, the Skaven armies boiled out of every subterranean exit. From the east, Greenskins and Ogres fought their own wars amid the chaos. From the west, Beastmen roared out of every forest.

Karl Franz — the Empire's last great Emperor — made the decision only an Emperor could make: he surrendered his body wholly to Sigmar. Sigmar's divine power descended from the heavens, taking Karl Franz's body as an avatar to manifest upon the battlefields of the Old World. Ghal Maraz — Sigmar's legendary warhammer — blazed with light in the Emperor's hands such as had not been seen since Black Fire Pass.

But it was not enough. Sigmar's true form was trapped in the vortex of the Realm of Azyr and could not fully project its deity. The avatar's strength, mighty as it was, could not stem the full tide of Chaos. After Altdorf fell, the Imperial army fought a retreating action; the Dwarf holds went dark one after another under Chaos siege; the combined Elven fleet lost all supporting capability after Ulthuan's fall.

And then the Skaven delivered the killing stroke for all of them.

Grey Seer Thanquol presided over the largest ritual in Skaven history — the summoning of the Great Horned Rat into the material world. The shockwave of the ritual knocked the Chaos moon Morrslieb out of its orbit. That green moon began to swell in the sky, plummeting straight toward the Old World.

---

## V. The Collapse of the World

The end occurred at Middenheim.

Middenheim was not only the Empire's northern capital — it was the core node of the Old World's magical circulatory system. Part of the magic sustaining the world itself was stabilised through the node beneath Middenheim. Archaon knew this better than anyone. When he realised that a frontal siege could not conclude before the moon struck, he chose a more direct path: he personally led a force infiltrating from below, through the Skaven tunnel network, straight for the magical foundation beneath the city.

In the deepest reaches beneath Middenheim, Archaon raised the Slayer of Kings and with his own hand severed the magical tether that held the Old World together.

The Old World collapsed with a roar.

The Empire, Bretonnia, Ulthuan, Naggaroth, Nehekhara, Karaz Ankor, Athel Loren — every kingdom, every civilisation, every hatred and every glory — all zeroed out in that moment. This was not the end of a war; it was the ending of a world. Morrslieb smashed down; the continents shattered; the oceans boiled; all borders dissolved; all histories burned.

But the ending was not the final point.

In the Old World's last instants of dissolution, the ancient Slaan of the Lizardmen activated the final protocol left by the Old Ones — the mightiest among them sacrificed themselves, using their dying magical strength to send the souls of the Lizardmen into the stars, transforming them into what would later be called Seraphon. And there was a deeper significance: the Slaan's magic gave form to the debris of the Old World.

Eight entirely new realms condensed out of the Realm of Chaos — each corresponding to one of the Winds of Magic: Azyr the Realm of Heavens, Aqshy the Realm of Fire, Chamon the Realm of Metal, Ghyran the Realm of Life, Hysh the Realm of Light, Ghur the Realm of Beasts, Ulgu the Realm of Shadow, and Shyish the Realm of Death. These were the Mortal Realms.

The Old World was destroyed, but its soul did not die.

---

## VI. Afterword: the Watershed

The End Times is the watershed between the old world and the new, the turning point of every character's destiny.

Sigmar broke free of the Realm of Azyr, becoming the supreme God-King ruling the heavens. He formed the Pantheon of Order and created the Stormcast Eternals to strike back against Chaos. Archaon was no longer merely a destroyer — he became the supreme military commander of the Grand Alliance of Chaos, continuing his eternal war across the Mortal Realms. Nagash took root in the Realm of Death, shifting from member of the Pantheon to independent third force, seeking to claim every dead soul under his dominion. Malekith was reborn as Malerion, ruling the Realm of Shadow, continuing to walk the line between darkness and light. And Settra — he who "serves no one" — is said to have found, somewhere in the Realm of Death, a throne that only he can sit upon.

The old world died. The old people remained. They carried their old hatreds and obsessions, defeats and victories, curses and contracts into the new epoch. The End Times ended everything, yet ended not one soul's story — it merely turned the page.

---

## VII. The Decisive Duel: Archaon vs Sigmar

The final battle of the Old World raged on nearly every front simultaneously — Tyrion and Malekith fought brother against brother before the Shrine of Khaine, Nagash and Settra wrestled in an undead civil war within Nehekhara, and the Skaven Verminlords purged each other in a bloody power struggle beneath the Chaos moon — but the ultimate confrontation that decided the Old World's fate occurred in the storm above Middenheim.

It was the last day of the Imperial Calendar — or rather, the moment when the calendar itself had ceased to have meaning.

Archaon stood upon the crumbling walls of Middenheim, beneath him the burning northern capital of the Empire, above him Morrslieb hurtling toward impact. The Slayer of Kings laughed softly in his grip — this daemon-blade, sealed with the blessings of all four Chaos Gods, had drunk deeply of the Old World's essence, but it still lacked one thing: the blood of a God-King.

Sigmar descended from the sky.

Using Karl Franz's body as his vessel and his own divine power as his sail, he blazed with golden lightning. The arcs drawn by Ghal Maraz in the air illuminated the ruin for miles around. No words passed between the two ancient enemies — ten thousand years of hatred, the cumulative fury of a thousand battlefields, the collision of two cosmic wills, all detonated in the first instant of blade meeting blade.

Every stroke of Archaon's sword bore the marks of the four Chaos Gods: Khorne granted the power to annihilate all things, Tzeentch made each blow repeat thrice in the fissures of time, Nurgle caused wounds to fester and spread instantly, Slaanesh gave the attacks a serpent's fatal precision. Sigmar's riposte was far simpler — the barbarian chieftain turned war-god, every hammer-blow meant for one thing alone: to smash Chaos out of this world.

Their duel was not merely on the plane of strength. Archaon knew the world's magical tether was already severed; if he could prolong the fight, the Old World's own collapse would finish what Chaos had started. Sigmar knew this too — but he had no alternative. He had to fell Archaon here, even if this was the last swing of his warhammer.

The decisive moment came suddenly. Sigmar hurled Ghal Maraz — the warhammer tumbled through the air, carrying the whole of his divine might — but Archaon did not evade. The Everchosen opened his palm and the black light of the Slayer of Kings met the golden lightning of Ghal Maraz in mid-air. The shockwave hurled both bodies in opposite directions.

In that instant, the Old World fully shattered.

The earth split open; the sky burned; the torrent of the Eight Winds seized both souls, dragging them separately into the depths of the Realm of Chaos. Archaon plunged into the very heart of Chaos, reborn upon the throne of the Inevitable Citadel as the supreme commander of the Grand Alliance of Chaos; Sigmar was pulled into the vortex of the Realm of Azyr, where he endured long and solitary ages before the day came to reassemble the Pantheon and forge the Stormcast Eternals.

This battle had no victor — or rather, both were victors. Both survived into the next epoch. And the Old World, in the after-shock of two gods' final exchange of blows, became the soil of the Mortal Realms.

---

## VIII. The Fates of the Powers After the Old World's Destruction

The Old World's destruction was not a return to zero. Eight Mortal Realms condensed from the wreckage, and every force from the old world found its place on this new board — some continuing, some transforming, some utterly metamorphosing.

**Sigmar and the Pantheon of Order.** After awakening in Azyr, Sigmar did not counterattack immediately. He spent long ages assembling the Pantheon of Order. He sought out former allies and adversaries — the Dwarf ancestor-gods Grungni and Grimnir, the Elven brothers Tyrion and Teclis, the Wood Elf life-goddess Alarielle, and Malerion of the Realm of Shadow (the former Malekith). The Pantheon acted in unity for a time, pushing Chaos to the fringes of the Eight Realms. But the Pantheon eventually fractured — Nagash pursued an absolute monopoly over souls, Malerion pursued sovereign rule over shadow, and Gorkamorka (the fusion of the Greenskin deities Gork and Mork) tired of Order and chose to return to pure war. In isolation, Sigmar created the Stormcast Eternals — immortal warriors whose raw material was the souls of mortal heroes and whose bodies were celestial lightning and star-iron. They became his keenest blade in the new epoch.

**The Dwarfs.** The Dwarf holds of the Old World were almost entirely annihilated in the End Times. But Dwarf civilisation did not perish. The ancestor-gods Grungni and Grimnir each left legacies in the Mortal Realms. Fragments of Grimnir scattered as ur-gold, spawning the Fyreslayers — fanatical mercenaries who fight to collect these shards of their god. Grungni inspired a faction of Dwarfs who mastered the technology of sky-sailing — the Kharadron Overlords — who ply the Realm of Metal in sky-fleets, trading by contract and coin rather than by the ancient codes of oath and grudge. A portion of Dwarfs remained behind in Sigmar's Cities of Order — the Dispossessed — the last Dwarfs who still remember the glory of the Old World.

**Lizardmen → Seraphon.** The Lizardmen completed the Old Ones' final directive before the Old World's destruction. The Slaan expended the last of their magical strength to send the souls of their entire race into the stars, transforming them into eternal beings composed of celestial magic — the Seraphon. No longer creatures of flesh and blood, they became the embodied will of light and order. The Slaan rebuilt the Old Ones' celestial temples and from there look down upon the Chaos wars of the Mortal Realms, launching strikes from the starlight.

**Greenskins → the Grand Alliance of Destruction.** The Greenskin deities Gork and Mork merged into a single two-headed god during the Old World's collapse: Gorkamorka. Gorkamorka joined Sigmar's Pantheon of Order for a time — whether out of allied goodwill or simply because it meant more fighting is hard to say — but ultimately Gorkamorka could not tolerate the constraints of the word "order" and left Sigmar. In the new epoch, the Greenskins re-manifested as Orruks and Grots, forming an independent Grand Alliance of Destruction. Their sole credo: fight; fight harder; fight until the whole world remembers your name.

**Skaven and the Great Horned Rat.** The Skaven were among the greatest winners of the Old World's destruction. The Great Horned Rat successfully entered the upper echelons of the Grand Alliance of Chaos in the End Times, becoming the fifth officially recognised Chaos deity. The Skaven's reach not only did not contract — it exploded amid the chaos of the Mortal Realms. Every Realm has traces of Skaven cities in its deepest places. The Grey Seer theocracy further consolidated; the Verminlord caste stabilised. If in the Old World the Skaven were merely a subterranean threat underestimated by the forces of Order, in the new epoch they are an apocalyptic force personally crowned by a Chaos god.

**The Undead.** Nagash established his absolute rule in Shyish, the Realm of Death. He was not the only god of death in that Realm — but he was the first to act by devouring every other death-god. In the process, he absorbed the power of all deaths that had ever existed, becoming Death itself. Under his rule, the Grand Alliance of Death took clear shape: Vampire lords and Tomb Kings were reorganised into sub-legions such as the Legion of Sacrament; those undead who refused to submit were simply erased. Nagash's ultimate goal was unchanging: to bring every soul — living, dead, and yet to be born — under his sole dominion.

**The Vanished.** Not everyone survived the End Times. Bretonnia as a kingdom was near-entirely annihilated — the compact between the Lady of the Lake (Lileath) and mortal Bretonnia ended with the Old World's collapse, and the souls of Bretonnian knights were said to have been taken by Lileath into her own hidden realm to become its final guardians. Kislev, Araby, Nippon, and Ind — these human civilisations were utterly erased from the map by the apocalypse. The Empire, though it survived in spirit, did not survive as a political entity — the ruins of old Altdorf became mythological sites in the Mortal Realms, not a home one could return to.

---

## IX. Conclusion

The End Times was not a war that could be won. It was the physical collapse of the Old World as a stage — when the earth that bore civilisation shattered, the swords of the righteous and the blades of the wicked could only fall into the same abyss.

But it was in this very abyss that the Old World achieved its most astonishing metamorphosis: it became myth.

Sigmar went from barbarian chieftain to the master of the heavens. Nagash went from the First Prince of Nehekhara to the essence of Death itself. Malekith went from the un-blessed victim to the legitimate ruler of the Realm of Shadow. Archaon went from an orphan sacrificed to Chaos to the absolute military leader of an entire cosmic force. Every survivor of the End Times who endured it was no longer a lord of a single nation, a general of a single army, or the chief of a single race — they became gods.

This is perhaps the greatest paradox of the End Times: it killed the Old World, yet granted its greatest sons and daughters immortality. It ground history into dust, yet sent the figures in that dust ascending into the galaxy. The legends that lived for millennia in the Old World became, in the instant of its destruction, eternal. The Mortal Realms are not a sequel to the Old World — they are the God-Wars of the Old World's survivors, and this war has only just begun.
