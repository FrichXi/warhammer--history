# 05 · The Skaven Under-Empire

## Overview

Spread out every map of every human empire in the Old World, trace a dense web of lines across them, then turn the page over—the lines have punched through the paper and continue their course across the underside of the desk, branching, merging, threading through one another with a layer of wood between them and the cartography no one ever bothered to draw. That is the Skaven Under-Empire.

---

## I. The Shape of the Under-Empire

It has no borders. The Empire ends at Black Fire Pass; Bretonnia's marches halt west of the Grey Mountains. The Under-Empire acknowledges none of these things. Its "territory" is the tunnel network itself—a colossal subterranean arterial system stretching from the frozen north to the jungles of the Southlands, from the Worlds Edge Mountains to the seabed beneath Ulthuan. Beneath every human city there are skaven-tunnels. Beneath the roots of every dwarfhold, rat-claws have scraped. The sewer network of Altdorf was designed on paper as a drainage system; in skaven eyes it is a transit hub in the belly of the Empire.

The Under-Empire is not a state. It has no unified tax code, no posting-stations, no administrative boundaries you can sign onto parchment. It is a living web—today this tunnel belongs to Clan Moulder's beast-runs, tomorrow Clan Skryre's drills might collapse it, the day after it falls wholesale into the hands of Eshin assassins. But one thing is certain: whatever king claims the surface, whatever nation raises its banners, the skaven got there first beneath their feet.

## II. The Council of Thirteen

The Council of Thirteen is the supreme ruling body of the skaven—twelve occupied seats, and a thirteenth that stands forever empty.

The vacant seat is reserved for the Horned Rat himself. The practical effect is as brutal as it is simple: any warlord who attempts to seize absolute power by taking that seat will find the remaining eleven tearing him apart in unison within moments. This "nobody wins" mechanism has functioned for millennia and is more effective than any constitution. The twelve Lords of Decay each represent the interests of different clans and warlords, and the hatred between them often runs deeper than their hatred of outside races.

What keeps this apparatus from collapsing outright is the Grey Seers. Grey Seers belong to no clan, owing allegiance in theory only to the Horned Rat. White fur and black horns are their birthmark—the skaven believe albino rats are the Horned Rat's chosen. Grey Seers monopolise skaven sorcery, channelling the winds of dark magic through raw warpstone with prodigious power and equally prodigious mortality rates. They frequently die from magical backlash—detonation, spontaneous combustion, dissolving on the spot into green vapour—but none flinch, because losing favour in the Council is worse than being blown apart by sorcery.

The game between Grey Seers and the Lords of Decay is the central tension of skaven politics: the lords have armies and wealth, the Grey Seers have divine mandate and magic. Both sides sing each other's praises in public while hiring Clan Eshin assassins to take each other's lives behind the scenes. The game has no winners—the Council of Thirteen is itself a system designed to ensure that no one side ever wins.

## III. The Four Great Clans at Work

The Four Great Clans are the true muscle of the skaven.

**Clan Skryre** — war-engineers. Core philosophy: warpstone plus technology equals death; whose death is irrelevant. The product line spans Warp-lightning Cannons, Warpfire Throwers, poison-wind globes, and the Screaming Bell—a mobile weapons-platform mounted on a colossal wheel, whose tolling across the battlefield drives allies into frenzy and enemies into rout, with roughly a one-in-three chance of first pulverising the skaven pushing it into paste. The common trait of all Skryre weaponry: destructive yield scales in direct proportion to self-destruction probability. One rat explodes—there are three more litters behind it.

**Clan Pestilens** — plague monks. They do not use machinery; they use contagion. Their plague rituals stand only a membrane's thickness from Nurgle corruption. In 1111 IC, the Black Plague released by Pestilens erased half the Empire's population; their armies boiled up from the sewers and swept through the Empire's heartland until Emperor Mandred personally cut down the skaven warlord and drove the survivors back underground. To Clan Pestilens, that was not a failure—it was test data for next time. The plague monks cultivate diseases in their own bodies, using their rotting flesh as a weapon. They may not win the battle, but they win the epidemic.

**Clan Eshin** — shadow assassins. They do not rely on numbers; they rely on waiting. They travelled far into the Far East and learned three arts from the human assassin-cults: stealth, assassination, and betrayal. Returning home, they redefined each in skaven terms: stealth is hiding in the direction you never look down; assassination is in your food, under your bedroll, in the moment your lieutenant changes sides; betrayal is not a moral question, it is the business model. No skaven warlord goes to sleep without setting three layers of traps—you never know if a political rival has already made Eshin's down payment.

**Clan Moulder** — mutation masters. They do not invent; they stitch. Core technique: place living creatures into a surgical cage with warpstone, seal it for a few months, and open it—what comes out no longer has a name. Rat Ogres are the fusion of man-shape, rat-nature, and raw Chaos in one body. Hell Pit Abominations are ever-regenerating masses of living flesh, assembled from a dozen rats. Moulder creations do not distinguish friend from foe; on the battlefield, a Rat Ogre is as likely to turn and crush its own side as the enemy's. Moulder's attitude: they're all rats anyway, no loss.

Each of the Four Great Clans has mastered a distinct survival logic—explosion, plague, silent killing, mutation. The four logics are mutually incompatible but none can annihilate the others. Skaven society runs on a balance of terror: Skryre needs Moulder's war-beasts as cannon fodder; Pestilens needs Skryre's weapons for pest control; Eshin collects payment from everyone; Moulder needs everyone else to stay alive so the experiments can continue. It is not cooperation—it is that none can afford to lose the others.

## IV. Warpstone

Warpstone—technical name: crystallised Chaos energy; skaven name: "the Horned Rat's dung"—is a mineral ranging from dark green to near-black, the solidified form of Realm of Chaos energy in the physical world. On the surface it is an extreme hazardous substance; any Imperial citizen caught hoarding a sliver of it is strapped to a pyre by the Witch Hunters. In the Under-Empire, it enters every link of skaven civilisation.

Ground to powder it becomes "warpstone snuff," capable of driving an ordinary clanrat into an instant frenzy—muscles swell, pain perception vanishes, it bites anything in reach. It lasts a few minutes, then the rat dies. But the one that drops is immediately replaced by the one behind it, and from a tactical standpoint the effect is roughly indistinguishable from immortality. Warpstone snuff is standard-issue consumable for skaven charges.

Grey Seers use raw warpstone to cast directly—they swallow the fragments, using the Chaos energy within their own bodies to forcibly interface with the Winds of Magic. Efficiency is absurdly high; side effects are equally absurd: spontaneous combustion on the spot, joints bending backwards, a third eye sprouting mid-spell—an eye which usually burns itself out instantly.

Clan Skryre has developed warpstone to an industrial scale: liquefied as fuel, pulverised as poison gas, compressed into crystalline warheads, focused into beams as ersatz lasers. In the underground factories of Skavenblight, hundreds of skavenslaves mutate into Chaos-spawn chunks from warpstone leakage every day—those chunks are shovelled straight into the smelting vats as raw material for the next batch of weapons.

Without warpstone, the skaven would be nothing more than rats that learned to walk upright. It was the moment, unknown millennia ago, when the first rat gnawed its first mouthful of warpstone that gave this race the ability to stand, to think, to suspect one another. Warpstone gave them their strength and locked in their ceiling: a civilisation built entirely on the waste-products of Chaos will never achieve "stability"—but it is precisely this instability that makes them impossible to truly exterminate.

## V. Skaven Battlefield Doctrine

Skaven tactical philosophy in one line: never fight fair if you can fight from underneath.

A skaven army is the least army-like army in the Old World—no formations, no drill, no concept of discipline. A typical offensive runs like this: sewer grates explode open, green vapour billows forth, and the shrieking scrape of thousands of claws on stone floods up from below. The first wave is skavenslaves—undersized, in chains, clutching rusted knives, spending their lives to clog the enemy line. They do not fear death, because the clanrats behind them will threaten them with a worse one.

The second wave is clanrats with blades and armour, and the tactic is pure mass: boil out, overrun, keep coming. If the first two waves fail to break through, Skryre's weaponry enters the equation—Warp-lightning Cannons, Warpfire Throwers, poison-wind globes. These weapons distinguish neither friend nor foe, but in skaven parlance this is called "collateral culling": three rats for one Imperial soldier is a profit.

Moulder monstrosities come in from the flank, the rear, or straight up through the ground. Eshin assassins do not participate in the assault—they entered the city before the battle even began, tasked with ensuring that the garrison commander dies suddenly, the granary catches fire, and the messenger's plea for reinforcements never reaches its destination.

The fatal weakness is morale. A skaven offensive flows like a tide, but if a single chokepoint holds firm—or a Skryre weapon blows up more of their own side than the enemy's, or a Moulder brute turns and tramples swathes of clanrats—every skaven on the field will, in the same instant, arrive at the same thought: *today, leave.* The moment the first rat turns to run, the entire army dissolves within seconds into a scattering torrent of fleeing rat-shapes. A rout is not a retreat—it is a melt. The rats vanish into the cracks of the earth; what remains on the ground is corpses and warpstone residue.

The foundation of anti-skaven doctrine: do not flee when the skaven charge, because you cannot outrun them; hold the line after the first wave is checked, and wait for them to break themselves.

## VI. Faith in the Horned Rat

The Horned Rat is not one of the Four Chaos Gods—at least, the Four themselves say so. Khorne despises him for his lack of forthright slaughter; Tzeentch finds his schemes laughably crude; Nurgle tolerates him because skaven do not belong to the cycle of decay and rebirth; Slaanesh has never bothered to notice him at all.

But the Horned Rat does not care. He has squatted for millennia in the shadows at the periphery of the Realm of Chaos, attending no divine councils, demanding no recognition. He needs only one thing: for the skaven to survive. Every heartbeat powered by fear, greed, hunger, and mutual betrayal—every one of them—is his altar.

Thirteen is the sacred number of the Horned Rat. The thirteenth empty seat on the Council is empty and not-empty for him; the Grey Seers' prophecies are believed to be thirteen lies wrapped around one truth—and the truth is never labelled. The skaven social structure is the Horned Rat's dogma made flesh: betrayal is not sin, it is virtue; the only way upward is to climb over the heads of others while making certain no one can reach your neck.

The End Times were the Horned Rat's longest gamble. While Chaos and Order clashed across the surface, the Horned Rat's command was brutally simple: dig deeper, ignore what is happening above. As the Old World collapsed, the skaven burrowed further into the depths. When the Winds of Magic ballooned into the independent Mortal Realms, they surfaced from the deep places to discover—the world had turned, but the tunnels had already punched through the crust of the new one. The circumstances of the Horned Rat's ascension to the fifth Chaos God are detailed in the Book of the Old World Pantheon. The rituals conducted by Grey Seer Thanquol under the malign moon are recorded in the Biographies.

## VII. In Sum

The skaven are the most chronically underestimated force in the Old World. Humanity spent millennia laughing at rats who could not build a civilisation; the dwarfs only remembered what was beneath their feet when skaven war-fires licked at the doors of their great halls; the elves never deigned to look downward at all.

And then came the End Times, and the force that truly tipped every theatre of war was not the charge of Imperial knights, nor the high magic of the elves, nor the thunder of dwarf artillery—it was the skaven, boiling up simultaneously from every sewer, every mine, every chasm, appearing at the worst possible moment behind every battlefield. In numbers, they exceed the greenskins; in technology, they rival the dwarfs; in infiltration, they put every spy in history to shame; in sorcery, Grey Seers armed with warpstone punch holes through the laws of physics.

The skaven are the most unpredictable force in the entire galaxy. Not because they are strong—one clanrat, an Empire spearman can kill ten of them. It is because their very weaknesses are the source of their unpredictability: having no loyalty, they cannot be subverted; having no plan, they cannot collapse in the execution; having no bottom line, they will dare any weapon. You cannot predict what the skaven will do next—the skaven themselves do not know.

They are not hiding in the shadows of the Old World. They are claiming territory inside them. And by the time the surface finally realised that these shadows had spread beneath the feet of every civilisation, it was too late.
