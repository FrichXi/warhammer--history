# 03 · The Book of the Gods of the Old World

## Overview

The gods of the Old World do not sit behind clouds. They reach out, and they withdraw. They bestow blessings, and they curse. The devotion of mortals toward the gods is, at bottom, born of fear — fear of war, and one worships the war-god; fear of death, and one worships the death-god; fear of plague, and one prostrates oneself before a festering effigy. Only the Dwarfs venerate their ancestor-gods out of gratitude: Grimnir forged them; Grungni taught them to work stone; Valaya gave them hearth and home.

And all gods — of whatever race, whatever name — come from a single place: the Realm of Chaos.

The polar gates the Old Ones bored through the north and south poles admitted more than the Winds of Magic; they also admitted something even more primal — the energy of emotion. The Realm of Chaos is, in essence, the projection into the immaterial of every soul's desires and fears. When these emotions gather in sufficient mass and concentration, they acquire consciousness, and a name. That name is what mortals call "a god."

## I. The Gods of the Empire of Man

The gods worshipped by the men of the Old World have the most mixed pantheon, and the most pragmatic. A farmer praying simultaneously to Sigmar, Ulric, and Morr sees no contradiction — in war, Sigmar; in winter, Ulric; when dead, Morr.

**Sigmar the Hammer-Bearer** is one of the few confirmed cases in the Old World of "mortal ascending to godhood." When he returned from the blood-soaked field of Black Fire Pass in 1 IC and was crowned founding Emperor, he was still a mortal. Fifty years on the throne, he laid the institutional foundations of the Empire, was wounded by a Nurglite cursed blade, and walked alone into the World's Edge Mountains, never again appearing in flesh. Over the next two thousand years, his worship evolved from folk memory into an established state church — the Cult of Sigmar, centred on the Grand Cathedral of Altdorf, with the Grand Theogonist holding ecclesiastical authority and a seat among the Elector Counts. Sigmar's power manifested through the avatar Valten during the Storm of Chaos, and in the End Times he possessed the body of Karl Franz. He is not an omnipotent deity; he presides only over war and the survival of the Empire. His response comes in only one form: when a mortal warrior lifts the hammer, the hammer grows heavier.

**Ulric** is the god of the White Wolf, the god of winter — older than Sigmar. When Sigmar was still a tribal youth, the Teutogens of the north were already worshipping him. Ulric has no concern for civilisation or order; he cares only for the pure will to survive — when the snows seal the mountain passes, only the most ferocious will live. The seat of the Cult of Ulric is Middenheim, where the Ar-Ulric sits beside the hearth in the Temple of the White Wolf, living white wolves always at his side. The Empire's deep political structure springs from these two gods: the south believes in Sigmar; the north, in Ulric. The rivalry between Middenheim and Altdorf is not merely political — it is fundamentally a religious question of whose heaven is harder.

**Morr** is the God of Death. His aspect is silence, not terror. Morr belongs neither to good nor evil — he is merely the inescapable terminus. His priests preside over funerals and the keeping of graves. During the Black Death and the Vampire Wars, the Cult of Morr was the only Imperial institution that never crumbled — they had always stood on the threshold of death. Morr's relationship with Nagash is a critical ledger entry: Nagash did not replace Morr; he usurped death's authority from another direction. Morr receives souls; Nagash hijacks them. Two understandings of death are locked in eternal struggle.

## II. The Elven Pantheon

The gods of the Elves were not made later. In the age of the first Chaos incursion, they already had form and body standing upon the soil of Ulthuan — at least, so the Elves themselves record.

**Asuryan**, King of the Gods, the Phoenix God. He represents the fundamental principle of light and order. The Sacred Flame of Asuryan burns perpetually; every Phoenix King must walk into it and pass the ordeal — those not accepted never reach the other side of the flame. When Malekith was burned by the Flame and turned to rebellion, superficially he was contesting the throne; in essence, he was challenging Asuryan's judgement. And when the Flame ultimately chose him in the End Times, it proved that Asuryan's time-scale is not comprehensible to mortals: a test can last a thousand years.

**Khaine**, the Bloody-Handed God, Lord of Murder and War. He has no daily priesthood, no fixed temple. The moment one Elf takes up a weapon intending to kill another Elf, Khaine is present. The most dangerous — and the most real — of the entire Elven pantheon is he. The Sword of Khaine runs through the whole of Elven history: Aenarion used it to beat back Chaos; Tyrion died because of it. The curse has not diluted in a thousand years.

**Lileath**, Goddess of the Moon and Dreams, forms the obverse of Khaine's bloodiness. It is said she turned her tears into moonlight to wash the blood-stained night sky. The Lady of the Lake worshipped by the humans of Bretonnia is almost certainly an avatar of Lileath — Elven moonlight falling upon human lands became the reflection on the lake's surface.

Beyond these three core deities, the Elven pantheon also includes: **Isha** (the Earth Mother), **Vaul** (the Lame Smith-God), **Kurnous** (the Hunter God), **Hoeth** (God of Wisdom and Magic), and **Loec** (God of Shadow and Dance), among others. The Elven gods are entangled in webs of love and grievance — their internal conflicts are themselves a mirror of Elven history.

## III. The Dwarf Ancestor-Gods

Dwarfs kneel to nothing. Their relationship with their ancestor-gods is not worship — it is *remembering*. Remembering what their ancestors did. Remembering what their ancestors sacrificed for them.

**Grimnir**, the Ancestor War-God. He was not worshipped into godhood — he walked to the end of the Chaos Wastes to guard the gate, and never returned. He embodies the most fundamental tenet of the Dwarfs: some things must be done by someone. No hope of survival. No hope of remembrance.

**Grungni**, the Ancestor God of Forging and Veins. He is the founder of Dwarf civilisation — identifying ore, excavating mountainsides, building subterranean strongholds: all of it was taught by his own hand. Grungni and Grimnir are brothers: one governs how to live; the other, how to die.

**Valaya**, the Founding Ancestress-Goddess. She presides over the hearth, ale, healing, and protection. Without her, Grungni's mine-tunnels would be mere holes, and Grimnir's axes would be mere iron. She turned the mountain into a home. Every Dwarf hold has a foundation-stone carved with Valaya's rune — the first stone laid, the last stone taken out.

The three Ancestors form a complete trinity: external combat, internal construction, and the protection of the hearth-fire. There is no hierarchy among them — a Dwarf does not think the war-god outranks the hearth-goddess.

## IV. The Four Chaos Gods in the Old World

The four Chaos Gods — Khorne, Tzeentch, Nurgle, and Slaanesh — are the same entities in the Old World as they are in the far dark future. The Realm of Chaos exists outside time. What differs is the form of their manifestation in this age.

**Khorne** in the Old World is not the remote and unassailable figure seated upon the Brass Citadel; he is the invisible hound squatting on the shoulder of every northern warrior. The northern tribes cry his name when they swear oaths upon their blades; the Greenskins, charging bloodied into battle, unwittingly offer worship to him as well. His response has no oracle — the killer of the most men upon the field is his eye.

**Tzeentch** in the Old World manifests principally as *change* rather than *scheming*. The mutations of the Chaos Wastes are not random genetic variation — they are his tendrils reaching directly into flesh and rearranging it. Every unforeseeable deflection of the Winds of Magic points at root toward his calculations. The hardest curriculum for a human magister in his lifetime is not mastering a single Wind — it is resisting Tzeentch's temptation to master all eight.

**Nurgle** in the Old World appears in the image of "Grandfather." His followers call him Grandfather Nurgle. When the Black Death ravaged the Empire, the Skaven had sown the poison — but the speed of the plague's spread and the depth of its corruption exceeded the Skaven's control. Nurgle merely took the seeds the Skaven had scattered and grew them into his own garden.

**Slaanesh** is the most recent of the Chaos Gods to enter the world — because it was the Elves' ultimate depravity that finally summoned him into full being. It was not that Slaanesh existed first and then tempted the Elves; rather, the Elves' excess and dark magic coalesced into the very consciousness of Slaanesh. The "God of Pleasure" worshipped by Morathi's secret cult had no name in the earlier age.

A feature unique to this setting: the common folk of the Old World do not directly perceive the Chaos Gods. An Imperial farmer would never say "I believe in Khorne," but he might on the battlefield hack and butcher in mad frenzy — at that moment, Khorne is using his body.

## V. The Rise of the Great Horned Rat

Outside the spectrum of the four Chaos Gods, one being has long been excluded: the Great Horned Rat.

He is the racial god of the Skaven. Though the Chaos Dwarfs, Beastmen, and northern tribes each follow their own Chaos gods, all have coordinates on the board. Only the Skaven do not — the Great Horned Rat is not acknowledged by the four Chaos Gods. He is not the legitimate "thirteenth seat" in the Chaos pantheon; this argument has never been conclusively settled.

But over the long history of the Old World, the Great Horned Rat has been steadily accumulating power in the shadows. The very existence of the Skaven is his altar: every rat's fear, greed, hunger, and betrayal; every warpstone's green glow; every inter-clan plot and massacre — all are offerings to him. He does not demand open martial glory like Khorne, he does not thrill to strategy like Tzeentch, he does not care for cycles like Nurgle, he does not pursue the extreme like Slaanesh. The Great Horned Rat simply wants more rats, more warpstone, more gnawing at the surface world.

The End Times was the Great Horned Rat's greatest wager. Grey Seer Thanquol presided over a summoning of unprecedented scale beneath Morrslieb — pulling the Great Horned Rat directly from the Realm of Chaos into the material world. The ritual succeeded; the Chaos moon plummeted toward the Old World. Thereafter, in the Age of Sigmar, he passed from "the unacknowledged one" to the undeniable fifth Chaos God. Khorne hates him, Tzeentch calculates him, Nurgle tolerates him, Slaanesh once disdained him — but none of them could push him back into the void.

The rise of the Great Horned Rat proves this: one does not need to be recognised by the orthodox pantheon. If a race is sufficiently numerous, sufficiently dense, living its own self-contained existence in the deep places of the world, its collective consciousness can gestate a true god in a corner of the Realm of Chaos.

## VI. Summary

The forms and rites of the Old World's gods vary wildly, but all obey the same iron law: born of the Realm of Chaos, their power arises from the projection and accumulation of mortal emotion. Mortals shape the gods, and the gods shape mortals.

The difference lies only in the path. Sigmar ascended from mortal to god: memory transformed history into faith, and faith condensed into divine power. The Elven gods were already self-existent divine entities when Chaos first descended in the age of myth. The Dwarf ancestor-gods are the inverse — they did not ascend from mortality; rather, Dwarfs were created with the ancestors as "templates" already present; Dwarfs inherit them, they do not worship them into being. The four Chaos Gods are the primordial forces of the cosmos — not created, but the foundational laws of the Realm of Chaos crowned with names. The Great Horned Rat is a later arrival — condensed from the specific racial consciousness of one species, proving that the generation of gods is not exclusive to the ancient age.

At root: whether one prays before Sigmar's hammer or swears an oath on Khorne's skull, the call reaches the same immaterial domain. The only difference is which current one stirs, and what emotion one pours into it. Every prayer, every battle fought, every dying curse of a mortal reshapes the face of the gods. Gods make men? Men make gods. This is perhaps the deepest truth resting on every altar in the Old World.
