# 01 · The Book of the Winds of Magic

## Overview

Magic is not native to this world. Long before Mankind learned to strike fire, the Old Ones built the polar gates at the northern and southern extremes of the globe — two fissures opening directly into the Realm of Chaos, designed to draw Chaos energy to power their works of creation. The gates later collapsed, letting Chaos flood in — but alongside it came something more primal and more pure: the Winds of Magic.

The essence of magic is raw energy flowing from the Realm of Chaos into the material world. It is most volatile in the Chaos Wastes of the far north, and grows thinner and more tractable as one travels south. It behaves like a torrent cascading from north to south — near its source, no one can keep their footing; downstream, one can build dams and channel the flow.

Elven scholars of antiquity were the first to discover that this energy is not a uniform mass but can be stratified — by frequency and affinity, magical energy naturally divides into eight colours. The Elves named these the Eight Winds. This discovery would become the foundation of all magical theory in the Old World.

---

## I. The Eight Winds in Detail

**Hysh — White, Warding**

Hysh is the light of sun and moon, truth and revelation. Those who wield Hysh may banish illusions, purify corruption, and raise shields of protective energy. Hysh is the purest of the Winds, philosophically the most opposed to Chaos. The spiritual discipline of Light mages borders on the ascetic, demanding absolute clarity of mind. In the Colleges of Magic of the Empire, the Light Order's magisters wear white robes and carry staves; they are known for exorcism and warding. Its corresponding realm is Hysh, the Realm of Light — Teclis's ultimate domain.

**Shyish — Purple, Death**

Shyish is the sigh of the departed, the terminal point of time. It settles upon graveyards, ancient battlefields, and forgotten places. Necromancy draws its power from this Wind — but the orthodox user of Shyish devotes himself to understanding the rhythm of death and the passage of time, not to the forcible manipulation of the dead. The purple-robed Amethyst Order is the most sombre and feared of the eight human colleges. After Nagash attained godhood, the ultimate form of Shyish became the Realm of Death, the foundation of the Grand Alliance of Death.

**Aqshy — Red, Fire**

Aqshy is flame, passion, courage, and wrath. It is active wherever intensity burns — volcanic vents, deserts, battlefields. Fire mages are quick-tempered and direct, the most destructive spellcasters in combat. The red-robed Bright Order is famed for its battle magisters; Imperial Fire wizards on the field can conjure fireballs from thin air or ignite enemy formations. After the Realm of Fire was born in the Age of Sigmar, it became one of the most fiercely contested battlefields between Chaos and Order.

**Ghyran — Green, Life**

Ghyran is the spring rain, the sprouting seed, the power of healing. It is the force of forests, rivers, farmland, and all growth. Ghyran magisters are healers, and they can also cause thorns to erupt from the earth and bid trees to obey their call. The green-robed Jade Order shares a natural spiritual kinship with the Wood Elves. The Wind of Ghyran birthed the Realm of Life, over which Alarielle, queen of the sylvaneth, presides.

**Ulgu — Grey, Shadow**

Ulgu is mist, deception, trickery, and concealment. It gathers in deep ravines, narrow alleyways, and the fears that lurk in the hearts of men. Ulgu magisters excel at illusion, invisibility, and mental manipulation. The grey-robed Grey Order serves as the Empire's intelligence and spy network; their members never reveal their true identities and distrust even their own colleagues. When Malekith became Malerion and assumed dominion over the Realm of Shadow, it was the apotheosis of Ulgu's power.

**Ghur — Brown, Beasts**

Ghur is the call of the wilderness, the instinct of the predator. It surges through untrodden forests and mountain ranges. Ghur magisters can summon beasts, shapeshift into animal forms, or awaken a primal battle fury in warriors. The Amber Order's magisters tend to live in seclusion, spending most of their time in the wild, keeping their distance from the civilisation of the Imperial cities. The Realm of Beasts is the source-realm of this power.

**Azyr — Blue, Celestial**

Azyr is the stars, the lightning, and prophecy. It drifts in the upper air, entangled with fate and the future. Azyr magisters can summon lightning, foretell coming events, and read the star charts. The blue-robed Celestial Order serves the Empire as astrologers and seers. In the Age of Sigmar, Sigmar became the God-King of the Realm of Heavens; his Stormcast Eternals are immortal warriors forged from the Wind of Azyr itself.

**Chamon — Gold, Transformation**

Chamon is the rationality of metal, the transmutation of alchemy. It clings to veins of ore, to forges and laboratories. Chamon magisters can manipulate metal — dulling enemy blades, hardening their own armour — brew arcane elixirs, and even attempt the transmutation of lead into gold. The gold-robed Gold Order consists largely of alchemists with close ties to the Dwarfs and the Imperial Engineers' College. The Realm of Metal would later become the mercantile empire of the sky-Dwarfs, the Kharadron Overlords.

## II. High Magic and Dark Magic

The Eight Winds are independent, but they can be united. There are two diametrically opposite ways of doing so.

**High Magic (Qhaysh)** is the harmonious unity of the Eight Winds. The caster does not suppress any single Wind but allows them all to express themselves like the different sections of a symphony orchestra, united into a single whole. This is not addition but synthesis — all Eight Winds deployed simultaneously yield a power far exceeding that of any single Wind. Only the Elves have truly mastered High Magic, for their lifespans are long enough and their minds steady enough to maintain balance within the storm. No human lives long enough to learn it.

**Dark Magic (Dhar)** is the forcible unification of the Eight Winds. There is no harmonising — all Winds are seized and crushed together. Dhar is extremely unstable and corrupts the caster, but it is the fastest and most direct path to power. Both the Dark Elves and the Skaven Grey Seers are principal users of Dhar. Nagash invented necromancy precisely because he learned the fundamental principles of Dhar from the Dark Elves.

The difference between Qhaysh and Dhar is not one of magnitude but of attitude. The former is dialogue; the latter is enslavement.

## III. The Imperial Colleges of Magic

The Empire's attitude toward magic underwent a dramatic reversal — from persecution to incorporation. During Sigmar's reign, all spellcasters were treated as agents of Chaos and killed on sight. For the next two thousand years, unsanctioned spellcasting remained a capital crime in most Imperial provinces.

The turning point came in 2304 IC. The third great Chaos incursion nearly destroyed the Old World, and in their desperation, men realised that without magisters, sword and gunpowder alone could not hold back Chaos. Magnus the Pious, who rose to prominence in this war and was crowned Emperor, did something none of his predecessors would have dared: he legalised magic.

He invited the High Elf Archmage Teclis to Altdorf. Working from Elven magical theory, Teclis designed a system tailored for humans: magic was to be rigidly divided into eight Colleges, with each student permitted to study only one Wind. Mono-Wind discipline was the only way humans could safely control magic.

The eight Colleges sit in Altdorf, each operating independently, with strict internal hierarchies and training regimens. The senior magisters of the Colleges form the Magisterial Council, answerable directly to the Emperor. This system functions to this day. Though the common people of the Empire have never entirely lost their fear of magisters, the Colleges of Magic have become the Empire's most indispensable shield against the darkness.

## IV. Dwarf Rune Magic

Dwarfs do not cast spells. Not for lack of wanting to, but because their very nature is inherently resistant to the flow of magic — a Dwarf's body is like a stone standing in the wind: the wind blows past, but it cannot enter. This is a physical property of Dwarfs, not the result of any discipline.

But the Dwarfs found an entirely different path: Rune Magic. They do not ride the Wind; they hammer its energy *into* objects — like a smith beating white-hot metal into a mould. Every rune is a successful capture, fixing flowing magic into the substance of matter.

Runesmiths are the most revered stratum of Dwarf society, their tradition strictly hereditary and their techniques guarded in absolute secrecy. The greatest Dwarf runesmith, Alaric, forged Ghal Maraz — Sigmar's warhammer. The hammer bears no visible aura of active magic, but every strike carries the locked-in might of runes, enough to shatter dragon scale and daemon skull alike.

Dwarf rune magic has one fatal limitation: runes are consumable. Each rune exhausts a portion of its stored power each time it is used, until it becomes nothing more than a shallow groove. And in the latter ages, as the Dwarfs declined, fewer and fewer masters remained capable of forging new runes. They were not learning new things — they were losing old ones.

---

## V. Elven Magic

The Elves are the true masters of magic in the Old World. Their bodies are innately sensitive to the Winds of Magic — not stones that repel the wind like the Dwarfs, but broad conduits capable of channelling all Eight Winds at once.

The magical tradition of the High Elves has continued for over seven thousand years, long before Mankind learned to write. The Great Vortex at the centre of Ulthuan is a colossal magical mechanism built by the Elves in the aftermath of the first Chaos incursion; its function is to channel and purify the Chaos energy flooding in from the polar gates, preventing the world from being drowned beneath a magical tide. This Vortex still turns today, and is the fundamental guarantee of magical stability in the Old World.

Elven casters can wield all Eight Winds simultaneously — High Magic, Qhaysh. They do not limit themselves to a single Wind; a competent Elven mage is proficient in coordinating at least three Winds in combination. The greatest among them, such as Teclis, can command all Eight with the ease of conducting an octet.

The Dark Elves took a different road: Dhar. In the caverns beneath Naggaroth, Dark Elf sorceresses fused Dhar with the rites of the Slaaneshi cults, developing an unspeakable tradition of dark sorcery. Dark Elf sorcerers are adept in both Qhaysh and Dhar — a duality they regard as a higher form of power, but at the cost of spiritual and physical corruption alike.

The Wood Elves share a unique bond with the Wind of Ghyran. They do not fight the Eight Winds or seek to control them; instead they use the forests of Athel Loren as a medium — channelling Ghyran through the forest's spiritual network to strengthen defences, heal wounds, and spin mazes of living thorns.

At root, the three Elven races embody three distinct attitudes toward magic: High Elves pursue harmonious mastery, Dark Elves pursue coercive domination, and Wood Elves pursue symbiotic allegiance.

## VI. Summary

The theory of the Eight Winds is the cornerstone of the entire magical system of the Warhammer world. It defines the source of power (energy flowing in from the Realm of Chaos), the classification of power (the eight-coloured Winds), the modes of its use (single Wind, combined, or forged), and the differentiated magical paths of the races — humans study one Wind in strict isolation, Dwarfs seal magic into runes, and Elves stand at the centre of the Eight Winds commanding the whole.

At the End Times, the boundaries between the Eight Winds collapsed. After the Old World's destruction, the Eight Winds each expanded into eight independent Mortal Realms — Light, Death, Fire, Life, Shadow, Beasts, Heavens, and Metal — these eight worlds constitute the entire stage of the Age of Sigmar. The Winds of Magic are no longer invisible energies: they have become worlds themselves.

## VII. The Eight Winds and Chaos

The Eight Winds are not the opposite of Chaos. They are Chaos energy — the "controlled version," after being diverted, filtered, and slowed. Every apprentice who has trained in the Imperial Colleges of Magic is told this on their very first day: the light at your fingertips, and the fire breathed by a daemon upon the Wastes, come from the same source.

In the Chaos Wastes of the far north, magical energy arrives unprocessed, mingled with the will of the Chaos Gods and a pure impulse toward entropy. An untrained spellcaster attempting any incantation there will end up either possessed by a daemon or suffering instant physical mutation. Chaos is indiscriminate — it devours anyone who tries to harness it.

This is the true significance of the Eight Winds theory: it is not a method of keeping Chaos out, but of taking Chaos apart to use. The essence of mono-Wind practice is to select the narrowest possible path, exposing the magister to only a single affinity, thereby reducing the probability of corruption to a tolerable margin. But "tolerable" is not "zero." Beneath every one of the Empire's Colleges of Magic lie the mutated remains of apprentices — those who failed to survive the trial and were devoured by the Wind they chose. Even a mage cultivating the pure Wind of Hysh, should their concentration falter and allow fear or desire to penetrate, will find the warding light turned inward — the shelter becoming a judgement that sears the soul.

The risk cannot be eliminated, only managed. To use magic is to use the filtered residue of Chaos; every casting is a contract signed without a guarantee clause.

## VIII. The Great Vortex of Caledor

The Great Vortex sits at the heart of the Isle of the Dead off the coast of Ulthuan — an eternally rotating magical abyss. Light of eight colours streams in from across the sky, converging into a vortex whose depths no eye can see. It is the fundamental stabilisation mechanism for magic in the Old World, and the precondition for mortal beings ever being able to wield magic at all.

Its principle can be crudely compared to a hydrodynamic siphon: the magical tide pouring in from the Chaos Wastes is a flood; the Vortex draws this flood into a closed circuit, using centrifugal force to separate the blended energies by their "specific gravity" — the lightest, highest-frequency energy of Hysh is ejected first into the outermost ring, while the heaviest, deepest-settled Shyish is discharged last. Once separated, the Eight Winds flow along fixed "channels" to different parts of the world. The Elves call them the "Eight-Wind Arteries" — without the Vortex as the sorting station, these arteries would not exist.

At the centre of the Vortex slumbers Caledor Dragontamer — the Elven mage who forged this mechanism after the first Chaos incursion. He and his followers sealed themselves within a time-stilled barrier, their souls perpetually active to sustain the Vortex's rotation. For over ten thousand years they have neither aged, nor had consciousness, nor known release. They are no longer living beings — yet if they ever wake, the Vortex will stop, and the world will be drowned in raw Chaos within hours.

The Vortex is not invulnerable. In every major Chaos incursion — Asavar Kul's westward march, Archaon's War of the Everchosen — Chaos sorcerers have concentrated their assaults on the Vortex, trying to halt or reverse its spin. Whenever the Vortex's core wavers for even a second, every magic user on the planet simultaneously feels their spells destabilise and daemons slip through the cracks. Imperial magisters call this experience "Vortex shock."

## IX. Notorious Magical Disasters

There is a proverb in the Old World: "A spell that can't go wrong isn't a spell." In thousands of years of magical history, disasters are beyond counting, but a few have been written into the compulsory curriculum for fresh apprentices in all eight Colleges:

**The Great Necromantic Ritual of Nagash.** Nagash originally studied Dhar under the Dark Elves, but he was not content. He forcibly blended Shyish and Dhar, inventing an entirely new mode of casting — necromancy. This was not a new spell; it was an amendment to natural law. Eventually he unleashed the Great Awakening, meant to transform every deceased being in Nehekhara into undead. The aftermath of this spell polluted the entire land, creating an undead wasteland that has not dissipated in a thousand years. Necromancy remains the most intractable form of magical corruption — and its source was a single act of unchecked hubris.

**The Fall of Egel Styrn.** In 1850 IC, a Celestial magister named Egel Styrn secretly studied a second Wind, attempting to combine the prophetic power of Azyr with the transmutation arts of Chamon, convinced he was the "exception of genius." His laboratory beneath Altdorf detonated in a crater that blew half the Celestial College into the sky. No trace of his body was ever found — the Magisterial Council's post-catastrophe investigation detected only the residue of two Winds simultaneously present, and a claw-mark matching no known living creature.

**The Skaven Warpstone Catastrophes.** Skaven regard warpstone as a gift from the Horned Rat and never consider safety precautions. The written record of the city of Skavenblight contains at least three documented "city-wide catastrophic chain reactions" triggered by warpstone, each of which flattened an entire under-district. The Skaven who survived merely shrugged that their comrades had poor luck, and carried on chewing on the glowing stones.

**The Curse of Be'lakor.** Be'lakor, the first mortal to be cursed with eternal daemonhood by Chaos, was once a mortal warrior who attempted to channel all Eight Winds through sorcery. In his era, this meant trying to seize every current simultaneously in the Chaos torrent — the result: he was bodily warped into a Daemon Prince beneath a colourless sky, his flesh torn and contested by the four gods, ultimately becoming a being that belonged to no single god, cursed forever.

---

## X. Conclusion

The Eight Winds system is not perfect. It was merely the optimal solution the Old World devised for the fundamental paradox of magic: survival demands magic, and magic itself can kill you at any moment. Humans reduce the risk through mono-Wind isolation; Elves spread the risk thin with long life and High Magic; Dwarfs bow out of the race entirely, hammering magic dead into runes once and for all.

But whichever path one walks, the base layer is the same: fight Chaos with Chaos. It is a cycle with no end. At the End Times, the cycle broke — the Eight Winds each expanded into eight complete Mortal Realms, and the Old World crumbled to ash. The Winds of Magic did not vanish; they simply became a new stage, waiting for the next round of users to walk in. Magic never ends. It only changes its scenery.
