# 06 · Lizardmen & The Great Plan

## Overview

Of all the civilisations of the Old World, there is only one race that has never asked the question: *Why are we here?* Not because they dare not—because at the genetic level, they never needed to. The Lizardmen are the last batch of direct creations the Old Ones left behind in the material universe. Humans are not direct creations; the elves are not; the dwarfs are not. Only the Lizardmen carry the Old Ones' engineering serial number on every chromosome, and every individual that crawls out of a spawning pool already knows which position it is meant to occupy.

---

## I. The Sole Standing Force of the Old Ones

The Old Ones vanished more than ten thousand years ago. In those ten thousand years, the elves mythologised the Old Ones into legend, the dwarfs carved the Ancestor Gods into cuneiform on stone tablets, and humanity does not even know the name *Old Ones*. Only the Lizardmen are still executing the orders they left behind—the Great Plan.

The Great Plan is not written on paper. It is inscribed upon the golden plaques buried deep within every pyramid-temple of Lustria, connected above to star charts and below to the geomantic ley-lines. The text on the plaques does not read like an edict; it reads like an infinitely nested set of conditional instructions: if the stars are in a certain alignment, execute a certain action; if a city is contaminated by Chaos, initiate purification protocol; if skaven activity exceeds a certain threshold, deploy the corresponding number of Saurus cohorts into the tunnels to cleanse. Everything is an "if...then..." structure; there is no paragraph that explains "why."

The Slann are responsible for interpreting these plaques. Seated in their meditation chambers atop the pyramids, they use astral cognition to scan every symbol on the golden plaques, cross-reference stellar shifts, geomantic tremors, and the flow of the Winds of Magic, and ultimately produce a single directive—it may be a cohort of Saurus marching seven days east to eliminate intruders; it may be every Skink mobilised to reinforce a section of wall; it may also be: observe, do not move, await the next signal.

This system has run for over ten thousand years without malfunction. The Lizardmen do not question. The Old Ones do not reply.

## II. The Pyramid-Temples and the Geomantic Web

To understand how the Lizardmen wage war, one must first understand how their cities are built. The temple-cities were not sited at random. Each one rests upon the convergence nodes of Lustria's geomantic web—the meridian network along which magical energy naturally flows through the planetary crust. At the centre of every city stands a step-pyramid; at its apex is the Slann meditation chamber; within its body are buried golden plaque arrays and celestial positioning instruments. Radiating outward from the pyramid in fixed geometric patterns lie the spawning pools, armouries, beast-pens, and Skink workshops. The entire layout is not a question of architecture—it is the blueprint on the Old Ones' plaques. The Slann merely build to specification.

The pyramid itself is more than a building. It is a node in the geomantic web and an amplifier-antenna for magical energy. When a Slann casts from the top of the pyramid, the structure magnifies his psychic power dozens of times and transmits it along the ley-lines to the other cities. If Lord Mazdamundi of Hexoatl initiates a geological displacement during meditation, the defensive wards of Xhotl hundreds of miles away strengthen in synchrony. The geomantic web has transformed the entirety of Lustria into an integrated magical machine; every pyramid is one gear in the mechanism.

The Old Ones' design logic was clear: the city-template is fixed, because when the Old Ones return, they must not need to re-adapt. They come back, walk straight into the pyramid, access the plaque records, review all execution logs for the past ten thousand years, and then resume advancing the next phase of the Great Plan. The Lizardmen have accepted this logic in its entirety and have never once considered that the Old Ones may not be coming back.

The spawning pools deserve a word of their own. They are not ovaries; they are biochemical production facilities. Before departing, the Old Ones set every breeding parameter; the Lizardmen are only responsible for maintaining the pools' operation. Every new batch of Saurus, Skinks, or Terradons is an exact duplicate of the pre-set genetic template. Output volume and type are scheduled by the Slann according to current combat losses and the needs of the Great Plan: more soldiers needed—raise the Saurus production ratio; fortifications to build—spawn more Skinks. The "population" of the entire race has never been the result of natural reproduction; it is a production schedule.

## III. Flesh-Forged Arsenal — The Four Subspecies and the Great Beasts

A Lizardmen army requires no training. They are born knowing how to fight, the way a cheetah is born knowing how to hunt. This is not racial pride; it is the factory setting the Old Ones installed.

### The Slann

The Slann are not generals in Lizardmen society, but they determine the strategic direction of the entire race. A single Slann can sit in meditation atop a pyramid for five hundred years continuously, during which his body barely metabolises—heartbeat drops to a few times a day, breathing slows until it is almost imperceptible. His soul drifts upon the Winds of Magic, one eye fixed on the star charts, one eye scanning the geomantic web, processing an information load exceeding all the mages of every Imperial College of Magic combined.

Slann do not command troops. They relay directives through Skink Priests. The archetypal scene: a Slann briefly surfaces from meditation, speaks a single sentence to the Skink Priest kneeling before him—"The fifth bridge to the north, three days hence, the Saurus cohort crosses"—and then closes his eyes again. The Skink Priest breaks that one sentence into a march route, supply schedule, and force composition, and passes it down the chain. The Slann only produces strategic judgement; he touches no detail of execution.

Most renowned is Lord Mazdamundi of Hexoatl, the de facto supreme leader of Lustria. He is awake more than most Slann, but even that amounts to only a few months every fifty years. The directives he issues in those few months suffice to keep the Skinks busy for five hundred.

### The Saurus

A Saurus emerges from the spawning pool already at adult size, scales as hard as iron plate, a mouth fully lined with teeth capable of crushing bone. They require no childhood, no learning, no "growth process" of any kind. From the first breath, the body already knows how to grip a spear, how to stand in formation, how to identify an enemy.

Saurus do not speak. Not because they cannot—because they do not need to. Their information-exchange methods are radically compressed: on the battlefield, a single syllable, the angle at which a crest of scales is raised, suffice to convey a complete tactical intent. A human scout hiding in the jungle eavesdropping on a Saurus camp will hear only insect-song and the sound of water, and have no idea that several hundred Saurus have just completed a full cycle of relief, repositioning, and ambush coordination.

Temple Guard are the elite of the Saurus. Every Slann has his own contingent of Temple Guard, numbering from a few dozen to several hundred. This unit exists for one purpose alone: protect the Slann. The Slann slumbers—they stand motionless before the pyramid door. They stand for years, decades, centuries—no food, no water, no sleep, no movement. Until someone intrudes, or the Slann awakens with a new directive. This is not loyalty; it is the absence from their factory settings of any option labelled "abandon post."

### The Skinks

Skinks are the only group in the Lizardmen military that can be "communicated with." Not that they will chat with you, but at least they can understand the "slightly more flexible" parts of an order. Saurus only walk in straight lines; Skinks know when to go around. Saurus only hack and kill; Skinks know when to scout, relay messages, repair roads, tend the great beasts.

The Skinks' military role is auxiliary. They are small, fast, and more agile moving through water and canopy than Saurus on the ground. Their equipment is javelins and blowpipes—the weapons themselves are not heavy, but the tips are coated with neurotoxin refined from the poison-dart frogs of the Lustrian rainforests. A Skink javelin may not penetrate dwarf plate, but if it breaks skin, the venom paralyses within three breaths and kills within ten.

Chameleon Skinks are the invisible arm of the Skink kindred. Their skin can match ambient colour and texture with the speed of living pixels; when motionless, the naked eye cannot distinguish them from a stone. Battlefield application: infiltrate the enemy camp before the battle to assassinate the commander, or during the melee, slip behind the enemy mage and end her. A Chameleon Skink lying in jungle ambush can change position three times in the ten seconds you spend not looking at it.

Skink Priests are even more important. Though their magical power falls far short of the Slann, they are responsible for something the Slann cannot do: resurrection. Lizardmen do not breed naturally. After a battle, as long as a corpse remains broadly intact, the Priests can reanimate it using the resurrection rituals left behind by the Old Ones. A Saurus cohort wiped out to the last warrior—as long as the Priest survives and the bodies survive, the unit returns to the field weeks later. This is not like necromancy driving corpses—it is rebooting a machine's backup power. In the Old Ones' design, Lizardmen were built as durable goods, not expendables.

### Terradons and Great Beasts

The Lizardmen's air power is carried on Terradons—winged reptiles tamed by Skinks from the moment they hatch, bearing Skink skirmishers into the attack from above. Terradons are not particularly intelligent, but their coordination with Skinks is innate: the Skink on its back blows a bone whistle at a specific frequency and the Terradon knows whether to climb or dive. Ripperdactyls are larger-framed and more aggressive, used for directly mauling enemy monsters or aerial units.

The ground beasts are the Lizardmen's industrial-grade firepower. Bastiladons carry the Solar Engine on their backs—a colossal magical crystalline array whose activated beam of searing light can vaporise an entire rank of heavy infantry in a single sweep. A Stegadon's charge is the equivalent of a horned battering ram the size of a building; it hits a stone wall and a hole opens. The Carnosaur is the purest killing machine: an adult Carnosaur can bite through a man-at-arms' full plate armour in one snap—including the man-at-arms.

Troglodons are the only great-beast type capable of surviving in subterranean caverns; the Lizardmen use them to track skaven tunnels. A Troglodon's venom-spit can corrode even the surface of warpstone—a strategic-level hard counter against the skaven.

The daily husbandry of these beasts is the Skinks' work: feeding, cleaning, healing, training. A Skink in the beast-pens spends half its lifetime on the back of a great beast—or inside its jaws.

## IV. Psionic Operation — The Slann Way of War

Slann spellcasting is nothing like a human mage chanting incantations and tracing rune-sigils. A Slann builds complete multi-layered arcane matrices directly inside his brain, then releases them simultaneously. A human mage can only handle one Wind of Magic at a time; an elf can use High Magic to weave all eight together—but a Slann goes further. He does not "weave" the eight winds; he operates multiple winds simultaneously and independently, each a fully realised spell, none interfering with the others.

An adult Slann on a battlefield can simultaneously execute the following operations in full: make the undergrowth erupt to a height of three metres in three seconds, entangling the enemy vanguard; summon a meteor wreathed in Azyr lightning from on high to strike the enemy general precisely on the skull; rip a forty-metre chasm directly beneath the enemy formation's centre; and raise a transparent protective dome around himself drawn from the energy of Hysh. These four spells belong respectively to Ghur, Azyr, Ghyran, and Hysh—four Winds in parallel, zero interference.

But the cost is immense. A Slann is not a god; his body is merely biological substrate modified by the Old Ones, and the burden of psychic energy flowing through his nervous system during casting is equivalent to a mortal seizing a high-voltage cable with bare hands. After a single large-scale casting, a Slann may sleep for centuries—this is literal. A human mage depleted of power collapses for a few days; if a Slann exerts himself at full capacity, the next time he opens his eyes the human Empire may have cycled through two dynasties.

Consequently, the Slann deploy their psychic power with extreme parsimony. Not because they cannot do more, but because once they have done it, they take themselves offline for several hundred years. The entire operational paradigm of the Lizardmen military is therefore constrained by one fundamental rule: a Slann holds back as long as possible. Send in the Saurus legions first, let the Skink Priests hold the line with ritual magic, let the great beasts flatten whatever can be flattened. Only at the last extremity—when a Greater Daemon of Chaos has set foot on the pyramid steps—does a Slann open his eyes.

## V. The Blind Spot — A Race That Only Reads Plaques

The Lizardmen have no diplomatic tradition. Not that they cannot—they have no concept of it. The plaques of the Great Plan cover every scenario for "what to do about outsiders," and the taxonomy is brutally simple: this area is a Forbidden Zone designated by the Great Plan—intruders, exterminate; non-Forbidden Zone intruders—first assess threat, then decide between purge or expulsion.

They do not negotiate. A human explorer attempting to explain that he is here to study ancient ruins equates, in the Lizardmen cognitive framework, to: an insect crawling across the pyramid steps is emitting some indeterminate sound-wave. A Saurus will pick it up and toss it into the jungle—if it does not resist. If it does, crush it.

They do not ally. In the entire history of the Old World, the Lizardmen have never proactively signed a single treaty with any other race. The High Elves once attempted communication. The Slann read the elven emissary's letter and fell silent for a very long time—not a refusal; the concepts in that letter had no corresponding code in his cognitive system. Treaty? Non-aggression? Trade agreement? These things have no names on the golden plaques.

This produces an eternal paradox: the Lizardmen are one of the most efficient forces against Chaos in existence, yet they are utterly incapable of integrating into any collective defence framework. When Sigmar convened the pantheon to form the Grand Alliance of Order, the Slann did not attend—not out of opposition, but because they could not, in any literal sense, understand what "joining an alliance" meant. In the end, they did only one thing: silently dispatched the Stellar Host to continue fighting Chaos. Not out of support for the Pantheon—the Great Plan says to eliminate Chaos, and whoever happens to be in charge of the elimination effort is irrelevant.

## VI. The End Times' Final Gambit

When the End Times arrived, the Lizardmen did not panic. The Great Plan contains a dedicated chapter whose title translates roughly as: *System Collapse — Ultimate Protocol*.

Archaon's Everchosen host drove south from the north; the skaven launched total subterranean invasion of Lustria; the malign moon Morrslieb descended toward the earth; warpstone rain saturated the jungles. Temple-cities fell under siege one by one. The Saurus cohorts fought battle after battle at near-zero loss ratios—they are a species built for attrition warfare, die and revive, revive and die—but the skaven's numbers were generated by limitless warpstone, so many that they dammed rivers and kept coming. Pahuax fell; Xhotl fell. Every city lost meant one more node severed from the geomantic web, and the total psychic power available to the Slann kept draining away.

At the critical threshold, Mazdamundi initiated the Ultimate Protocol. This was not a retreat in defeat—it was the contingency plan the Old Ones wrote at the bottom of the plaques: when physical-world defence is no longer tenable, compress and package all Great Plan-relevant data—the souls of living Lizardmen, the complete memory-banks of the Slann, the entire plaque information library—and transmit it via the pyramids' inbuilt translocation mechanisms to celestial space.

The execution worked as follows: every surviving Slann entered deep meditation simultaneously, channelling the entire geomantic energy of their respective pyramids into the apex in a single surge. The energy formed a trans-dimensional portal—with the temple-ships as hulls and souls as payload. Mazdamundi and the majority of the Slann expended their last shreds of vitality in the casting; their physical bodies turned to ash on the spot, their souls merging into the portal itself to stabilise the passage.

Kroak—that legendary Slann who had perished in the first Chaos invasion and had persisted for ten thousand years in mummified form—fought the final rearguard at Itza. As the daemons of Chaos breached the pyramid gates, he released the full psychic force he had held in check for ten millennia. A shaft of pure white light erupted from the apex, burning every daemon for kilometres around straight back into the Realm of Chaos. He bought the critical window the temple-ships needed to ascend, then sank with Itza into the earth.

With the temple-ships' launch, the Lizardmen's material civilisation in the Old World drew to a close. But their souls did not dissipate. In the Age of the Mortal Realms that followed, these star-borne Lizardmen souls evolved into the Seraphon—celestial beings who continue to execute the Great Plan in astral form.

The Old Ones did not return. But the Lizardmen changed venues and are executing still.

## VII. In Sum

The Lizardmen were designed by the Old Ones as a sword that would never need to know what it was doing. A sword need not understand war, need not pity the enemy, need not burn a victory dispatch after the battle. A sword need only confirm that it is still in its wielder's hand—and the hand that held them disappeared ten thousand years ago.

And yet they keep swinging.

Interpreting the plaques, maintaining the geomantic web, spawning new soldiers, purging intruders—at no step have they asked "why." Not because they dare not, but because they do not need to. When the Old Ones made them, they simply removed that neural circuit—the one that asks "why." In exchange they received ten millennia of uninterrupted operational efficiency, and a loyalty that will never receive a reply.

To every other race, this is horror. To the Lizardmen, this is normal. Between the two positions, no dialogue is possible.
