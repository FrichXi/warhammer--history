# 07 · Warriors & Daemons of Chaos

## Overview

The far north of the Old World has no frontier. Three hundred leagues north of the Empire's Kislevite watchtowers, the map turns white—cartographers mark the Chaos Wastes as blank space, not because no one has been, but because those who went saw something that is no longer the same land. Day one inside the Wastes: the sky is the wrong colour—not overcast, the air itself is refracting. Day three: the guide begins to laugh—an unprovoked, unstoppable laugh. Day seven: time stops moving, or moves too fast, or moves backward. Only then do you understand: you have not walked into a frozen wasteland; you have walked into a nightmare mid-formation.

---

## I. The Northern Chaos Wastes

The Chaos Wastes are not a geographic concept; they are a condition. They are the zone where the Realm of Chaos overlaps with the material world—the suture between two mutually incompatible realities. The laws of physics turn like rusted gears, sometimes catching, sometimes spinning in place. A mountain due north today may appear in the west tomorrow, and the day after may not be a mountain at all, but a crouching, breathing carcass of a behemoth.

People live in the Wastes. Norsca sits at the southernmost rim, near the Great Ocean, notorious for longship raids—the Chaos-adjacent race the Old World's humanity knows best. Further north are the Kurgan, nomadic horsemen tribes in the interior, deeper into Chaos and deeper in unpredictability. Further north still, the clean lines between "tribes" dissolve—only warbands of Chaos Warriors drift across the land, like stones tumbling without pattern down a river.

Life in the Wastes is itself a continuous Chaos-sentience test. Norscans must sacrifice before putting to sea—literally cut a living thing and hang it from the prow, otherwise the crew goes mad one by one. A Kurgan on the march who finds a glowing stone will not pause to consider whether it is a magical artefact—he will eat it, press it into a wound, or offer it to the gods; any of the three may cause explosion or mutation or sudden elevation to Champion. But no one walks around it. In the Wastes, caution is cowardice, and cowardice is death. Chaos does not spare the hesitant.

The defining feature of the Wastes is not cold and not monsters—it is erosion. It erodes the land: the forests of the Empire's northern marches recede league by league each year, replaced by mutated briars and carnivorous vines. It erodes time: a blizzard may last three days or three centuries; the Wastes' inhabitants cannot tell. It erodes the mind: stay long enough and you will remember no face from the south; even your own name will gradually go strange. In the end, you remember only one thing: there are four voices speaking to you from the inside of your skull.

## II. The Ascent of the Chaos Warrior

Every life in the Wastes is a wager. The Four Chaos Gods charge no fee—but they are always watching, always deciding who is worth promoting.

At the bottom is the Northman marauder, a mortal clutching an axe and nothing more. He bears no god-mark, no title. Chaos to him is only a vague premonition. Every day he lives is an attempt to prove himself: has he killed enough? Brutal enough? Mad enough? If the answer is sufficient, one of the gods—at a moment he can never precisely recall—grants him his first Mark.

A Mark is not a blessing. It is a god's brand seared into living flesh. But the moment the Mark is received, the Northman is no longer mortal—he has become a Chaos Warrior. He is formally acknowledged as a servant of a god. From this moment, his life no longer belongs to him: he dons Chaos Armour—not forged by any smith, but condensed from the Mark upon his body as a second skeleton fused to his skin, never to be removed.

Above the Chaos Warrior stands the Chaos Champion. A Champion is the leader of a warband, reaching this station through the twin accumulation of martial prowess and divine favour. A Champion must not only fight but draw followers—in the Wastes, no one follows an inadequate leader, because the price of following the wrong man is to be eliminated by Chaos without distinction. The Champion's Marks are thicker and deeper than the Warrior's: there is not a sliver of original skin left untransformed on his body.

Above the Champion stands the Chaos Lord. A Lord rules a Wastes fortress, a tribe, even an entire region. His Marks have accumulated to the point where he barely resembles the species he once was—a man walks in, a walking altar walks out. A Lord need not personally raid or hunt; his enemies are other Lords, and the occasional Daemon Prince sent to test him.

And the terminal point of mortal ascension is the Daemon Prince. When a mortal's accumulated Marks reach critical mass—when at least one of the Four judges that his soul is worth more than an army—he sheds his flesh entirely and emerges as a creature of pure Chaos will. A Daemon Prince no longer ages, no longer dies, and is no longer the person he was. His memories remain, but their emotional roots have been pulled out. He stands at the junction of the Realm of Chaos and the material world, belonging to neither side.

Every step of ascent requires new Marks—and every Mark is a physical mutation. The Mark of Khorne: an internal conflagration, the skin splitting into searing, brutal plates, blood seeping from the seams—not a wound, Khorne checking the body temperature. The Mark of Tzeentch: extra eyes sprouting, extra fingers, extra tongues, extra tendrils—Tzeentch's mutations are always mathematical, symmetrical, recursive; you look in the mirror and see an eye inside which another eye is growing. The Mark of Nurgle: rot commencing from the internal organs outward, wounds breeding maggots, skin swollen and sheathed in pus—but you feel no more pain; the pain-nerves have sloughed off with the decaying flesh; you feel only warmth, the warmth of being cradled in the depths of the swamp by Grandfather's embrace. The Mark of Slaanesh: a deformity that grows ever more beautiful—the physique approaching a divinely sculpted perfection of proportion, but the limbs have gained an extra joint, the tongue is forked in three, one eye has become the compound eye of a beetle. Beauty and horror are wholly fused in a single form.

## III. The Six Treasures and the Everchosen

The Chaos Gods do not cooperate, but occasionally they require a unified leader.

The reason is simple: single-god invasions are insufficient. If Khorne marches south alone, Tzeentch will plot behind his back. If Nurgle launches an independent plague-war, Slaanesh will use the opportunity to steal his worshippers. Chaos can suppress its internal wars in only one circumstance—when all Chaos requires an Everchosen.

The Everchosen is not elected; he is fought into existence. To become Everchosen, a mortal must gather the Six Treasures of Chaos—six artefacts guarded by the four Chaos Gods, each corresponding to a trial, a conquest, and a fresh wave of mutation. The names and order of the Six Treasures shift slightly from era to era, but their nature never changes: they are the seals by which each Chaos God locks away a portion of his power. He who gathers all six proves himself worthy of the unified recognition of all Four.

Each Treasure, as it is claimed, grants its bearer new armies. Because a Treasure does not merely sit there to be seized—it naturally attracts warbands and daemons sworn to the corresponding god. When you seize the first Treasure, you lead a single warband; by the third, you command a Chaos legion; by the sixth—behind you stands every living thing in the Northern Wastes capable of holding a weapon.

The one who claims all Six Treasures is the Everchosen. He is Chaos's supreme executive in the material world—not the gods' proxy, but the recipient of a war-commission jointly signed by the Four, authorising him to execute Chaos's will upon the physical plane. The Everchosen commands no longer tribes or warbands but the entire massed host of the North: Norscan longships, Kurgan riders, Chaos Warriors, Chaos Champions, Daemon Princes—and daemons themselves, pouring directly into reality through the rents he tears in the boundary of the Realm of Chaos.

The most famous Everchosen is Archaon—the host he led in the End Times was the single largest Chaos armed force in Old World history, and it brought the Old World to its end. But Archaon was not the first Everchosen, nor the only one. Before him came Asavar Kul; before Asavar Kul came countless whose names have been buried utterly by the blizzards of the Wastes—the position of Everchosen has never lacked competitors. For full details, see the Life of Archaon.

## IV. The Daemon Legions

Daemons are not Chaos Warriors. Chaos Warriors are men—men transformed by Chaos, but ultimately their first cry was heard in the material world. Daemons are not. Daemons are the fractal emanations of Chaos will, independent mappings of every facet of the Four's essence within the immaterial realm.

To pull a daemon from the Realm of Chaos into the material world requires ritual—bloody, repetitive ritual lasting days, sometimes months. At sites thick with Chaos energy (the core Wastes, or battlefields saturated with mass slaughter and sorcerous contamination), the summoner carves runes into the earth, drenches them in blood, and chants profane litanies in endless repetition, drawing the attention of Chaos towards a single gap. Daemons do not squeeze in on their own—they must be called by name, must be invited, the way water does not flow into a sealed bottle of its own accord.

Each of the Four commands its own daemon species:

**Bloodletters** — Khorne's infantry. Taller than a man by a head, copper-red hide smooth as fired clay, the spine curving in a reversed arc, swept-back horns cresting the skull. In their hands they always carry a Hellblade—an extension of the soul; if broken, it grows back. Bloodletters do not speak; they count: how many killed today.

**Horrors** — Tzeentch's living spells. Seething masses of pink-blue flesh without fixed form: head becomes arm, eyes sprout from fingertips, mouth spews glowing sorcerous fluid. They are always laughing—not joy, but because Tzeentch compels them to see all possible futures, and none of them is not funny. Kill one and it splits into two; kill two and they split into four, until the smallest wink out in a harmless puff of coloured vapour.

**Nurglings and Plaguebearers**. Nurglings resemble drowned infants stuffed into a rotting pumpkin—round, slick, giggling and crawling across fungal carpets; not lethal but morale-destroying. Plaguebearers are the opposite—tall, gaunt, cyclopean humanoids bearing colossal rusted blades, dragging trails of slime behind their steps. They do not pursue; they only walk slowly forward, venting plague from within. No one can stay near them long enough to remain uninfected.

**Daemonettes** — Slaanesh's dancers. Neither male nor female, their frames slender as insects', crab-claw arms paired with scorpion-limb extremities. They advance in dance-steps—the nerves of those who watch are sliced: the more beautiful, the more the brain hurts. Their voices are screaming and whispering simultaneously; left ear seduction, right ear terror.

## V. The Four-Way Civil War

Chaos has never been a unified faction. Khorne, Tzeentch, Nurgle, Slaanesh—four mutually exclusive models of reality. Chaos is not a four-headed monster; it is four monsters locked in the same cage.

Khorne hates Tzeentch—not dislike, but hatred at the existential level. Khorne is direct martial valour: blood spilled, skulls stacked, everything settled in one stroke. Tzeentch is everything that cannot be settled in one stroke: conspiracy, sorcery, deception, replacing one objective with another. To Khorne, Tzeentch's existence itself is an insult to the sincerity of war.

Tzeentch, conversely, considers Khorne a fool—possessing nearly unlimited military force, yet unable to conceive of anything beyond relentless frontal assault. Tzeentch will never attack directly; he will wait until Khorne's army has marched to a critical stretch of terrain and change the weather, or slip a cultist into Khorne's fortress to engineer a mutiny from within.

Nurgle thinks the other three are all too impatient. He particularly disdains Slaanesh—the excessive pursuit of ultimate sensation betrays a total failure to grasp the wisdom of the cycle. Nurgle sees the world as rot-to-rebirth-to-rot, an unceasing cycle. Slaanesh sees it as sensation-to-stronger-sensation-to-ultimate-sensation, a never-ending ascent.

Slaanesh thinks Nurgle is too slow, so slow as to barely be alive. Slaanesh's trick is to steal the followers and champions of others—his daemons never challenge Khorne directly, but when a Khorne Champion has taken a thousand skulls, Slaanesh breathes into the champion's ear: *wouldn't you like to try something even sweeter than decapitation?*

The Four Gods do not ally. The unity of Chaos is always a temporary state—the Everchosen suppresses the Four's civil war through sheer military authority. Archaon could field Khorne's Bloodletters and Tzeentch's Horrors in the same legion, not because he made the two daemon-breeds "reconcile," but because he was Everchosen: beneath the Everchosen's banner, all Chaos forces must drive east and south, or else defy a war-pact signed jointly by the Four, which no single god can unilaterally tear up.

But the moment the Everchosen dies, restraint evaporates. Within months the Northern Wastes return to factional hunting-season—Khorne's warbands hunting Slaanesh's cults, Tzeentch's sorcerers secretly unravelling Nurgle's tribes, and fresh Everchosen contenders crawling out of the kill-heaps to begin the new Six Treasure collection. This is Chaos's default state: not war against the outside, but reckoning within. The invasions of the Old World are only interludes between sessions.

## VI. In Sum

Chaos is a thought experiment, and the subject is every living thing.

Khorne represents the most primal rage—killing needs no reason; killing is the reason. Tzeentch represents the infinite thirst for the unknown—to know every possibility, one will overturn every certainty. Nurgle represents acceptance within despair—already rotted this far, might as well find the advantages of rot. Slaanesh represents transcendence—every sensation has a limit, and beyond the limit lies more extreme sensation. Every living being carries these four drives; the only question is where one stops.

The Chaos Warrior's ascent is the process of these four drives being progressively amplified within a mortal: rage becomes a mission, curiosity becomes mad omniscience, endurance becomes happiness, pursuit becomes disease. With every Mark received, you carve off a piece of your humanity in exchange for an answer. But by the time you reach Daemon Prince, you find you have answered every question—and so you become immeasurably powerful and immeasurably dull, because there are no new questions left to ask.

This is the double face of Chaos: it is freedom's highest form—a mortal can hack his own path to becoming an eternal being—and it is also freedom's ultimate curse, because at the end of the road, freedom no longer points anywhere, and only the four hungers remain, rotating their shifts inside your skull.

In the era of Everchosen Archaon, Chaos demonstrated for the first time the terrifying efficiency of combined-arms warfare. But even then, Chaos's unity was only a state of extreme pressure—every ounce of the Four Gods' fury compressed into a single direction, the vessel bound to burst. After the Old World's destruction, the Grand Alliance of Chaos persisted into the Mortal Realms: Archaon was reborn as the Supreme Commander of Chaos, the Four's legions waging war by the realm under his command. But that belongs to the next epic. On the scale of the Old World, Chaos was always this: four mutually loathing patrons, turning the entire north into an eternal wrestling-ground, and then occasionally—only occasionally—joining hands to paint the south red.
