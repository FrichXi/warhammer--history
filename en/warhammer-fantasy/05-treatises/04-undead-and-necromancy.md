# 04 · The Book of the Undead and Necromancy

## Overview

Death is not an endpoint — in the Old World, this is a statement of physical fact. Every undead being — vampire, Tomb King, masterless skeleton on the wastes — traces back to a single point: Nagash's Great Ritual punched a hole in the rules of death. From that moment onward, death was no longer irreversible annihilation; it became a state one could regulate.

---

## I. The Source of Undeath — the Curse of Nagash

What Nagash (see the corresponding biography in this chronicle) did in the deserts of Nehekhara can be reduced to a single phrase: he reverse-engineered death. Back when the Empire was still millennia from existing, he learned Dark Magic (Dhar) from the Dark Elves, fused it with the ritual traditions of Nehekharan funerary rites, and brute-forced into existence a mode of casting that had never existed — necromancy. This was not a new spell; it was an amendment to natural law.

The core mechanism of necromancy is crude and simple: intercept souls that should depart the material world, stuff them back into corpses, and give orders. Zombies and skeletons are the assembly-line products of this procedure. But Nagash took it to its extreme: one night around −1600 IC, within the Black Pyramid, he unleashed the Great Ritual — its objective: to awaken every single deceased being in all of Nehekhara. The ritual was interrupted halfway through by Alcadizaar, yet its residue never dissipated. Over the centuries that followed, from the greatest Pharaoh to the lowliest menial, the dead of Nehekhara clawed their way out of the yellow sand one by one.

Nagash did not conquer them — but he turned death into a contagion. Every subsequent undead war in the Old World, every tyrant unwilling to stay dead, every curse upon the vampire bloodlines — trace them all to their origin, and the trail ends on that single night.

## II. Vampires — the Civilised Undead

Nagash invented necromancy, but his Elixir of Life was not yet "perfect." The one who truly pushed immortality to its extreme was Queen Neferata of Lahmia (see the corresponding biography in this chronicle). She refined the formula and created the Cup of Blood — those who drank gained immortality and supernal power, at the price of eternal blood-thirst. The first generation of vampires was born. After Lahmia's fall, these aristocrats split into six bloodlines, each going its own way.

**The von Carsteins**, progenitor Vlad (see the corresponding biography). Among all bloodlines, the one closest to true kingship. Vlad did not skulk or assassinate — he marched out of Sylvania with his army and openly contested the crown with the Elector Counts of the Empire. Three Vampire Wars overturned half of the eastern Empire; twice he reached the walls of Altdorf. The von Carstein philosophy: a living empire is more worth ruling than a wasteland — under Vlad's governance, living subjects in Sylvania farmed and paid taxes as normal, with rates even lower than neighbouring provinces. This was not mercy; it was a cold calculation.

**The Blood Dragons**, progenitor Abhorash (see the corresponding biography). Captain of the Lahmian royal guard. Upon becoming a vampire, he could not bear the truth that his existence now required human blood, and spent his life seeking a path of purification. He ultimately slew an ancient red dragon and drank its blood — dragon's blood washed away the thirst for human gore. From this he established the Path of the Blood Dragon: subjugate the curse through the martial way, prove one is still "human" through combat. Blood Dragon knights build no kingdoms and take no part in politics. They roam the Old World year after year, seeking only an opponent worthy of their full strength.

**The Lahmians**, infiltrators named for the ancient city of Lahmia. They have no armies, no territory — only a hidden network spanning the entire Old World: the Lahmian Sisterhood. A noble's mistress, a temple priestess, an Elector Count's distant cousin — the tendrils reaching out from the Silver Pinnacle are buried in the highest circles of every realm. They do not contest thrones; they control those who sit on them.

**The Neferata Bloodline**, with Neferata herself as progenitor. The most ancient of all bloodlines, the purest direct descent. Unlike the Lahmian sisterhood with its sprawling network, Neferata occupied the Silver Pinnacle deep in the World's Edge Mountains and built a highly stratified vampire court. Layers of blood-rank, exacting rituals of the Blood Kiss, absolute matriarchal rule — Neferata's court is the ultimate blueprint of vampire society, at least in her own eyes.

**The Necrarchs**, progenitor W'soran. The scholars among vampires. Their sole obsession: to plumb the ultimate depths of necromancy, to surpass Nagash. The Necrarchs care nothing for appearance — the more withered the body, the purer the mental focus. They dwell in seclusion in remote mountain towers and subterranean tombs, four millennia of uninterrupted research, experiment, and writing, seeking to prove that Nagash was merely a first draft lucky enough to arrive four thousand years early. Four millennia have passed. Not one has come close.

**The Strigoi**, the most tragic branch. Prolonged isolation from civilisation, inbreeding, and the compounding of the curse have gradually regressed these vampires into half-human, half-beast existences. Their strength ranks near the top of the six bloodlines; their intelligence lies at the bottom. They were jointly purged by the other bloodlines — not for being evil, but for being "an embarrassment." The other bloodlines seem to have collectively chosen to forget: the Strigoi did not choose to become beasts.

## III. Vampire Military — the Hierarchy of the Dead

A vampire's army is not a disorderly heap of skeletons. It has a clear pyramid structure.

At the base are **Zombies** — slow-moving meat-shields, recently dead, effective only in mass. Above them are **Skeleton Warriors**, the standard undead infantry — bones are swifter than rotting flesh and can maintain basic formation. Above these are **Grave Guard**, elite warriors in life, now clad in black-iron heavy armour — the iron core of any vampire battle-line.

The middle tier sheds the purely physical: **Wraiths** are condensed resentment, undead who have lost their physical bodies; ordinary steel blades are nearly useless against them — they require magic or holy water to banish.

At the apex sit the vampires themselves in the field. **Blood Knights** are the fusion of heavy-armoured skeletal warriors with the Path of the Blood Dragon — fully encased in black plate, mounted on skeletal steeds, their shock impact far exceeds any living knightly order. Above them, **Varghulfs** are vampires who have regressed into bestial monstrosities, charging on four limbs, tearing through plate as if it were parchment. **Terrorgheists** are giant undead bats whose shrieks alone can shatter enemy morale. At the very peak of the pyramid sits the **Zombie Dragon** — a dragon's corpse infused with the Wind of Death, slaveable only by the most powerful vampire lords.

The system's advantage lies not in individual combat power but in regeneration: skeletons shattered on the field can reassemble and rise again. Only by destroying the summoner can an undead army truly be driven back into the grave.

## IV. The Necromantic Magic System

Necromancy is the only magic unrecognised by the eight Colleges — not because it is weak, but because it is too powerful and too unclean. Orthodox magisters study Wind by Wind; necromancers directly blend Shyish (the Wind of Death) with Dark Magic (Dhar). This mixed-Wind technique would be a death sentence in any College of Magic — but necromancers are not of the Imperial college system to begin with; they do not care.

The core ability of necromancers is area-effect summoning: a sufficiently powerful necromancer can cause every enemy fallen on the field to rise on the spot as an ally. This snowballing mechanism lets undead armies grow stronger as the battle lengthens, the opponent's forces thinning continuously. In the end, victory depends not on who has more troops, but on who can eliminate the opposing necromancer first.

The supreme scripture of necromancy is the **Nine Books of Nagash**. These nine volumes cover the full spectrum of necromantic knowledge, from the most basic skeleton summoning to the replacement of godhood with soul-dominance. Each volume is written on human skin in human blood, and each bears a fragment of Nagash's will — an ordinary person who reads the first page will begin to hear Nagash's whispers inside their own skull. The Nine Books were scattered during Nagash's successive defeats and became the ultimate relics that every necromancer since has chased. Possession of a single volume can make one a regional power; possession of several can convulse the world. The birth of the vampire bloodlines, the awakening of the Tomb Kings, the three Vampire Wars of the Empire — trace the source, and every one of these events is directly or indirectly linked to the circulation of some volume of the Nine Books.

## V. The Tomb Kings — a Different Species of Undeath

Tomb Kings and vampires may both look like "moving skeletons," but their natures are fundamentally distinct. Vampires chose undeath voluntarily — they drank the Cup of Blood and willingly abandoned death, accepting the curse. The Tomb Kings were resurrected without their consent — Nagash's Great Ritual was a magical tsunami that tore an entire continent's dead from their rest without warning.

The more crucial difference lies in consciousness. Vampires retain the intelligence of life and *enhance* it — they are cleverer and more manipulative than they were when alive. The Tomb Kings retain all the memories, personalities, and desires of life. Settra's first action upon waking was not to marvel at how the world had changed; he looked around and saw that the vassal kings he had conquered in life had also awakened, and each was ruling independently. His immediate response was to launch a war of reunification. This is not undead instinct — this is pharaonic inertia.

The Tomb Kings do not hate the fact that they became undead. They hate *who brought them back*. In their view, had Nagash never existed, Nehekhara's living civilisation could have continued to flourish, and the Pharaohs could have enjoyed true immortality in the realms of the gods, rather than being trapped in dry bone wandering for eternity. This fury at the "violation of their rest" defines four thousand years of Tomb King politics: toward any external invader (Imperials, Greenskins, Bretonnian knights), they are merciless; but at the mere mention of Nagash, they are berserk.

## VI. The Armies of the Tomb Kings — the Death-Host of an Ancient Civilisation

The defining feature that distinguishes the armies of the Tomb Kings from those of the vampires is discipline. Tomb King hosts are ancient battle-formations that have retained the tactical memory of life.

The base consists of **Skeleton Warrior Phalanxes** — armed with bronze-corroded spears and curved khopesh blades, advancing in close-order formation. Ordinary undead skeletons are a loose scrum; Tomb King skeletons can hold formation for four thousand years. Above the infantry are **Skeleton Chariots** — the signature of a Tomb King siege assault. Light chariots drawn by skeletal steeds hurtle into the flanks of enemy formations at high speed. Settra himself (see the corresponding biography) favoured no form of war more than personally driving a chariot over the enemy host.

The middle tier leaves basic humanoid form. **Ushabti** are the supreme masterpieces of the Nehekharan mortuary cult — semi-magical constructs carved from great stone in the images of the gods, infused with the soul-fragments of fallen warriors. **Sepulchral Scarab Swarms** are a moving devouring-storm — tens of thousands of magically-driven metallic scarabs sweeping through, leaving behind only polished bone.

The apex consists of colossal constructs. **Warsphinxes**, crafted from stone, bone, and incantation, can mount entire squads of archers on their backs. **Bone Giants**, assembled from the skeletons of multiple warriors, wield greatbows, each arrow capable of piercing a rank. At the vertex of the pyramid is the **Necrosphinx** — the ultimate decapitation construct. It was designed for a single purpose: to lock onto an enemy commander on the field of battle, decapitate him in a single stroke, and end the engagement in a single blow. This is not a weapon; it is an instrument of verdict.

The fatal weakness of Tomb King armies is the inverse of the vampires': they have no regenerative capability. A shattered skeleton stays shattered; a destroyed construct is a ruin. This is because Tomb King undead are not necromantically driven but are the passive residue of Nagash's ritual still in effect — their existence is at once singular and finite; they cannot, like vampire armies, rise again on the spot.

## VII. The Essence of Their Two Immortalities

Vampires and Tomb Kings share the same raw material and the same original architect, yet represent two opposite philosophies of immortality.

The immortality of the vampire is contractual: you drink this cup of blood of your own will, in exchange for eternal life, at the price of eternal thirst. It is a transaction — a bad one, but one entered into voluntarily. The greatest psychological burden of the vampire is not the blood-thirst, but the fact that they must live their entire existence facing one truth: the path they chose is, at its core, a path of irreversible predation. Abhorash slew a dragon and drank its blood — what looked like purification was, in truth, the swapping of one addiction for another, rarer addiction.

The immortality of the Tomb King is catastrophic: you had no choice, no warning; you simply opened your eyes inside your own tomb to find the world no longer knew you. Settra has not for a single moment in four thousand years accepted the fact that he is trapped in bone. The inscription on his throne reads: "Behold my works, ye mighty, and despair." This is not the conquest of death; it is a refusal to submit to death.

The two converge at the same destination: both dwell beneath Nagash's shadow. The six vampire bloodlines each walk their own road — some want a throne, some want honour, some want knowledge, some want control — but none can truly escape Nagash's system. The Tomb Kings have spent the entire strength of an ancient civilisation hunting Nagash for four thousand years, and still cannot escape the fundamental definition of "who it was that woke you."

In the End Times, the Old World collapsed; Nagash attained godhood. The destinies of all undead were drawn together once more — within the Realm of Death, Nagash became the nominal god of all the undead. Vlad died with his wife; Settra withdrew his forces; Mannfred engineered his own destruction in betrayal. This entire kindred, which had made freedom and immortality its ultimate pursuit, discovered at the last: of these two things, some might perhaps attain one — but both together, no one ever has.

Those undead who still roam the shadowed corners of the Old World — be they vampire princes, Tomb Kings pharaohs, or nameless necromancers — know in their bones one truth: Nagash never disappeared, and death never truly let anyone go. Every day the undead do not die is further evidence that Nagash lives.
