## I. What is Warhammer?

Warhammer is a tabletop wargame setting created by the British company Games Workshop in 1983. Over four decades, it grew from tin soldiers into a sprawling universe spanning novels, video games, and animation. You may never have seen the miniatures, but you've probably encountered their shadow — the Terran Marines and Zerg of *StarCraft*, the oversized shoulder pauldrons on power armour in countless games, the "FOR THE EMPEROR" meme. All of it traces back here.

Newcomers frequently stumble on the same pitfall: **Warhammer is actually two completely separate universes.** They are not different eras of the same story.

**Warhammer 40,000 (40K)** — a gothic science-fantasy set in the 41st Millennium. Space Marines wear massive power armour. Fleets tear through the Warp to cross the galaxy. The entire Milky Way simmers in a never‑ending stew of war.

**Warhammer Fantasy** (the Old World) — sword‑and‑sorcery, set on a continent with the geography and texture of Renaissance Europe. Humans, Elves, Dwarfs, Undead, Orcs, and the tribes of Chaos slaughter each other across a world that feels like history gone mad.

These two worlds are independent. You don't need to finish 40K to understand Fantasy, or vice versa. The only element they truly share is the **Chaos Gods** — Khorne, Tzeentch, Nurgle, and Slaanesh — who exist in both settings as the ultimate existential threat, similar in form but unconnected in causality. Crude rule of thumb: **if it has a space marine helmet, it's 40K; if it has a horse and bow, it's Fantasy.**

The Old World's timeline ended in 2015 with the **End Times** — Chaos overran the world and it collapsed. GW then launched **Age of Sigmar**, a new setting closer to Norse mythology, composed of eight Mortal Realms. Sigmar, the mortal Emperor of Man in the Old World, ascended to godhood in this new epoch. This book covers the Old World primarily, with Age of Sigmar as an epilogue.

## II. Warhammer 40,000: the Galactic Epic of the 41st Millennium

If there were an award for "most comprehensively ruined galaxy in science fiction," 40K would win without contest.

### The Setting

The year is roughly 40,000 AD. Humanity once possessed a galaxy‑spanning interstellar civilization — but that is now a half‑remembered myth. The present‑day **Imperium of Man** is a decaying totalitarian machine: a theocratic fascist state that worships the Emperor as a god, where the **Adeptus Mechanicus** chants prayers while tightening bolts, technology is so lost they can no longer build or repair half of it, and the official creed is "Suffer not the alien (xenos), the mutant, the heretic." This is not *Star Trek*. There is no optimism, no exploration — only the desperate struggle to survive one more day in the dark.

### The Core Concept: the Warp and Psykers

The single most important concept for understanding 40K is **the Warp** — an immaterial dimension that mirrors the emotions of all sentient life. Every flicker of rage, desire, terror, and despair across the galaxy condenses, accumulates, and ferments in the Warp. A rare minority of humans are born with the ability to perceive and channel Warp energy: these are **psykers**.

Psykers are simultaneously the Imperium's most vital strategic resource — the **Astronomican**, the beacon that guides all Imperial fleets through the Warp, burns through the souls of thousands of psykers every single day — and its most dangerous liability. When a psyker loses control, the Warp notices, and things on the other side start crawling through the crack. Every day, psykers become living doors into hell without knowing it.

### The Emperor and the Space Marines

The Imperium's heart — literally — is a corpse on a throne: **the Emperor of Mankind**.

He was born around 8,000 BC in Anatolia, a fusion of the souls of countless ancient shamans who sacrificed themselves in a single ritual to create humanity's greatest psyker — immortal, a Perpetual, and the most powerful psychic being in the species' history. Across millennia he moved through history in disguise, nudging civilization forward from the shadows. In the 30th Millennium he revealed himself, unified Terra from its nuclear‑wasted techno‑barbarian tribes, and launched the **Great Crusade** — the largest military campaign in human history — to reclaim every lost human world scattered across the galaxy.

His greatest weapons were the **Primarchs** — twenty demigod generals crafted from his own genetic template, each the ultimate expression of a human archetype — and the **Space Marine Legions**, each commanded by a Primarch. Space Marines are genetically engineered super‑soldiers with two hearts, three lungs, the ability to eat an enemy's brain to read its memories, clad in near‑tonne ceramite power armour. They are the Emperor's sharpest blade.

### The Horus Heresy: the Original Sin

All of 40K's drama flows from a single civil war in the 31st Millennium: the **Horus Heresy**.

The Emperor's most beloved son, **Horus Lupercal**, elevated to **Warmaster** and given command of the entire Crusade while the Emperor returned to Terra for a secret project, was corrupted by the Chaos Gods through a poisoned blade and the whispers of traitors. He turned, splitting the twenty Legions into loyalist and traitor camps — nine Legions followed him into damnation, nine held the line, and two were purged from all records in a fog of redaction. This galaxy‑wide fratricide left wounds that have not healed in ten thousand years.

The war ended above Terra in a final duel between the Emperor and Horus. The Emperor unleashed the full force of his psychic might and obliterated Horus from every level of existence — not merely killed, but annihilated so completely his soul can never return. But the Emperor himself was shattered beyond repair. He was interred on the **Golden Throne**, a life‑support device originally built for an entirely different purpose, and has "ruled" in this half‑dead state for ten thousand years.

### Ten Thousand Years of Darkness

The millennia the Emperor has spent trapped on the Throne are the most suffocating part of the setting. The **Imperial Truth** he once preached — there are no gods, believe in science and reason — was twisted by his own Imperium into the very fanaticism he despised. Humanity went from "we don't need gods" to "the Emperor is the one true god and we will burn heretics to prove it."

In these ten thousand years the Imperium grew too vast for any central command to actually govern. The **Astra Militarum** (Imperial Guard) throws trillions of ordinary humans with lasguns and bayonets at the galaxy's most horrifying monsters. The **Inquisition** hunts traitors within its own borders. Xenos invasions, rebellions, and psychic catastrophes erupt simultaneously everywhere, all the time. The operational slogan of this civilization is: *do not think, obey orders, die for the Emperor.*

### The Era Indomitus: a New Rift

At the end of the 41st Millennium and the dawn of the 42nd, the plot lurched forward dramatically.

One of the loyalist Primarchs, **Roboute Guilliman** of the Ultramarines — a man who spent nearly ten thousand years in stasis after a poisoned blade laid him low — was resurrected by the combined efforts of Imperial and Aeldari allies. He awoke to find himself Regent of an Imperium that had become a religious, bureaucratic, technologically‑regressed nightmare. When he went to the Golden Throne to see his father, he came out shaken: the rational, empiricist being he remembered had become something cold, alien, almost godlike.

The bigger problem was the **Great Rift**. During the 13th Black Crusade, Chaos tore the galaxy open. The Warp surged out from the Eye of Terror and split the Milky Way in two — a bleeding scar across the stars. The half still connected to Terra is called **Imperium Sanctus**; the dark half, almost entirely cut off, is **Imperium Nihilus**. Soon after, another Primarch — **Lion El'Jonson** of the Dark Angels — also awoke. Two sons of the Emperor now struggle to hold together an Imperium that is literally coming apart.

### Enemies of the Imperium

The Imperium's enemies require a catalogue:

- **Chaos** — the nine Traitor Legions and their splinter warbands, plus the daemonic legions of the Chaos Gods. The eternal primary threat. The **Black Legion**, led by **Abaddon the Despoiler**, is the most dangerous concentration of Chaos Space Marines.
- **Aeldari (Eldar)** — the ancient race that once ruled the galaxy, who partied so hard they accidentally gave birth to Slaanesh. Now scattered into Craftworlders, Drukhari (Dark Eldar), Harlequins, and the Ynnari, surviving on scraps of their former glory.
- **Orks** — half‑fungal, half‑flesh biological weapons that reproduce via spores and can never be fully exterminated. War is literally everything to them: economy, entertainment, culture, all of it *is* fighting. Enough Orks together generate **WAAAGH!**, a massive collective psychic field.
- **Necrons** — an ancient race that traded their souls to the star‑gods (the C'tan) for immortal living‑metal bodies, only to discover they got ripped off. They slept under the surface of planets for sixty million years. They are waking up.
- **Tyranids** — an extra‑galactic organic swarm directed by a single **Hive Mind**. They do not negotiate, occupy, or rule. They eat.
- **T'au Empire** — the youngest race on the block, idealistic, expansionist, and almost entirely ignorant of the Warp. They preach the "Greater Good" and genuinely believe they can unify the galaxy. They have no idea what they're dealing with.

### Core Themes

Warhammer 40K's visual and narrative DNA comes from *Dune*, *Alien*, *2001: A Space Odyssey*, heavy metal, and European gothic tradition. Its foundational spirit can be summed up in one sentence: **in the darkest galaxy imaginable, the bravest people are doing something that almost certainly won't work.**

This is not a story about victory. It's about the cost — what the Emperor sacrificed for unity, what the Primarchs gave up for loyalty, how ordinary humans choose not to run when everything in front of them is nightmare. 40K has no clean answers. But in a world where everyone is practically waiting to die, you can always find the ones who chose to die standing.

## III. Warhammer Fantasy: Swords and Sorcery in the Old World

If 40K is despair on a galactic scale, Fantasy is two thousand years of slaughter on a single continent.

### The Old World

Warhammer Fantasy unfolds on a world that feels like a distorted mirror of Earth. The central stage is the **Old World**, whose geography is thick with European echoes — the most powerful human nation, **the Empire of Man**, is the Holy Roman Empire with griffons; the chivalric kingdom of **Bretonnia** is Arthurian France/England blended together; and frozen **Kislev** is tsarist Russia/Poland‑Lithuania under siege from the north. To the distant south lies Araby (a fantasy analogue of the medieval Islamic world).

The Empire was founded by the legendary hero **Sigmar**, son of a tribal chief. In 15 IC (Imperial Calendar, counting backward), his father was killed in a Greenskin raid. The young Sigmar charged alone into the enemy line, carved through it entirely, and was acclaimed king on the spot. Wielding **Ghal Maraz** ("Skull‑splitter" in Khazalid, the Dwarf language) — a rune‑hammer gifted by the Dwarf High King — he united the twelve tribes of the central Old World and shattered a Greenskin horde of unprecedented scale at Black Fire Pass. In 1 IC, Sigmar was crowned the first Emperor.

He established the Electoral system, the Imperial legal code, the road network, and an eternal alliance with the Dwarfs. After fifty years of rule, wounded by a Nurgle‑corrupted blade whose injury would never heal, Sigmar gathered the Elector Counts, abdicated, and walked alone into the World's Edge Mountains carrying Ghal Maraz. He was never seen again. For the next 2,500 years, the Empire lurched through innumerable civil wars, Chaos incursions, Vampire Wars, and Greenskin WAAAGHs!, staggering toward its fate at the End Times.

### The Chaos Gods: the Common Nightmare

The Chaos Gods are the strongest link between Fantasy and 40K. You only need to know four names:

- **Khorne** — the Blood God, lord of war and slaughter. Seated on a throne of brass. He respects martial honour and despises sorcery.
- **Tzeentch** — the Changer of Ways, god of schemes, mutation, fate, and magic. Every grand plan might be a piece on his board.
- **Nurgle** — the Plague Lord. Also, paradoxically, "Grandfather" — a twisted fertility deity who sees disease and decay as part of life's cycle, with a grotesque, paternal affection for his followers.
- **Slaanesh** — the Prince of Excess, god of indulgence and perfectionism. The youngest of the four, born from the Fall of the Aeldari — the galaxy's most ancient civilization partying so hard it literally gave birth to a god.

These four gods exist in both worlds in parallel. The northern **Chaos Wastes** — a frozen hellscape where reality dissolves — are the primary breach through which Chaos enters the Old World. The barbarian tribes of the north are its mortal agents.

### A Brief History of the Old World

Long before humanity, the **Old Ones** — a hyper‑advanced precursor race — arrived and built polar warp‑gates. They created the **Lizardmen** as biological servants. The gates collapsed, Chaos flooded in, and the Old Ones vanished. The Elven hero **Aenarion** drew the cursed Sword of Khaine and sacrificed himself to drive back the first Chaos invasion — but his bloodline was cursed forever.

Later came the **Sundering** — a civil war in which Aenarion's son **Malekith**, denied the Phoenix Throne, turned against his own people. The Elves split permanently into **High Elves (Asur)**, **Dark Elves (Druchii)**, and **Wood Elves (Asrai)**. Then the Elves and Dwarfs fought the **War of the Beard** — triggered by a diplomatic insult in which Dwarf ambassadors had their beards shaved by Elves, an act the Dwarfs considered tantamount to a declaration of war. The conflict was so devastating it gutted the leadership of both civilizations.

Meanwhile, to the south, in **Nehekhara** — a fantasy analogue of ancient Egypt — the most infamous necromancer in all Warhammer lore was born: **Nagash the Great Necromancer**. A prince of Khemri, he invented **necromancy**, built the Black Pyramid, and swept across Nehekhara with legions of the dead. He was defeated countless times and returned every single time, ultimately achieving godhood during the End Times. His Great Ritual reanimated the corpses of every Nehekharan king, creating another iconic faction — the **Tomb Kings**, undead monarchs who retain their memories and personalities. The proudest among them is **Settra the Imperishable**, whose declaration "Settra does not serve" is one of the hardest lines in the entire Warhammer canon.

Vampirism traces back here too. Queen **Neferata** of Lahmia, tutored by Nagash, refined the elixir of immortality and created the Blood Chalice. From her came the six vampire bloodlines that spread across the Old World. The most famous is the von Carstein line — **Vlad von Carstein** nearly conquered the Empire.

The Empire's 2,500‑year history is a running tally of invasions: Chaos incursions, Greenskin WAAAGHs!, the Vampire Wars, and the Skaven — a subterranean empire of intelligent ratmen whose plagues and assassinations gnaw at civilization from below. In 2522 IC, **Archaon the Everchosen** led the Storm of Chaos, which was repelled at Middenheim, but the seeds of the End Times were already planted.

### The End Times and Age of Sigmar

The Old World's story concludes in the **End Times**. Between 2525 and 2528 IC, Chaos invaded in full force. The three Elven races finally united in desperation (Malekith, in a final twist, walked into the sacred Flame of Asuryan and proved he had been the true Phoenix King all along). Nagash was resurrected and ascended to godhood. The Skaven pulled a moon out of the sky — literally. But in the end, Archaon reached the core beneath Middenheim and personally severed the magical nodes holding the world together. The Old World collapsed in his hands.

From the ruins, the eight **Mortal Realms** crystallized out of the void: Azyr (Heavens), Aqshy (Fire), Chamon (Metal), Ghyran (Life), Hysh (Light), Ghur (Beasts), Ulgu (Shadow), and Shyish (Death). Sigmar ascended to godhood and formed the **Pantheon of Order** alongside the Elven gods, the Dwarf ancestor‑gods, Nagash, and others. Chaos counter‑attacked and overran nearly every Realm — until Sigmar forged the **Stormcast Eternals**, immortal warriors reforged from the souls of mortal heroes. That counter‑offensive is the **Age of Sigmar**, still ongoing.

The End Times is a tragedy. But it opens into a stranger, more mythic stage. You don't need to know the Old World to understand Age of Sigmar, but if you've read the biographies of Sigmar, Nagash, Archaon, Settra, and Malekith first, you'll see the same characters continuing their eternal natures on a cosmic scale: Sigmar is still the hammer‑bearer, Nagash's ambition survived the death of a world, and Archaon graduated from destroyer of the Old World to conqueror of the new one.

## IV. How to Read This Chronicle History

This project organizes Warhammer using the **jìzhuàntǐ (纪传体)** system created by the Han‑dynasty historian Sima Qian in 91 BC. Rather than writing history as a chronological stream, Sima Qian told it through **people** — every chapter a biography, letting the events of an age emerge from the lives of those who shaped it. This is, coincidentally, the perfect format for Warhammer. What makes Warhammer compelling is never the setting alone — it's the people inside it: the Emperor's loneliness, Guilliman's suffocation when he awakens to find his Imperium turned into a church, Angron going to his death still raging that the Emperor robbed him of the right to choose it, Konrad Curze walking into a death he foresaw and refusing to dodge.

### The Five Sections

The work is divided into five sections:

- **Annals** (本纪, běnjì) — the lives of sovereigns, the figures whose personal fates *are* the skeleton of history. In 40K: the Emperor and the Primarchs. In Fantasy: Sigmar, Nagash, Archaon, Settra, Malekith.
- **Houses** (世家, shìjiā) — the great hereditary powers, the major factions. Each article explains a faction's origin, defining traits, and key historical moments.
- **Biographies** (列传, lièzhuàn) — exemplary lives beyond the main stage: Malcador the Sigilite, Abaddon the Despoiler, Saint Celestine, Commissar Yarrick, and dozens more from both settings.
- **Tables** (表, biǎo) — genealogies and timelines. In a story spanning tens of thousands of years, a good chronology is a lifeline.
- **Treatises** (书, shū) — topical monographs. What is the Warp? How does psychic power work? What is the Wind of Magic? How does the Mechanicus's tech‑religion actually function? Subjects that shouldn't be re‑explained in every biography get their own deep‑dive "book."

### Reading Suggestions

1. Start with this overview to build the mental framework.
2. Pick a character or faction that interests you. Want to understand why the Emperor is so tragic? Flip to the 40K Annals. Curious where vampires come from? Read the Fantasy Houses or Biographies.
3. Every article is self‑contained. No order required.
4. Terminology is consistent throughout; when in doubt, check the glossary in the appendix.
5. **40K and Fantasy are completely separate.** They share the Chaos Gods (parallel existence, similar forms, fully independent stories and causality). Beyond that, they have no relationship. You can skip an entire half and only read the one you care about.

This book exists to lower the barrier of entry. You don't need to read three hundred English novels and rulebooks. You just need to turn the pages, chapter by chapter, like reading a history — and before long, you'll find that those figures in colossal armour, with wings and rune‑hammers, have already taken up residence in your head.
