# 07 · Magnus the Red

## Overview

Magnus the Red, Primarch of the Fifteenth Legion — the Thousand Sons. The crimson cyclops, the most powerful psyker among all the Emperor's sons. A born scholar and seeker of knowledge, with boundless curiosity for the mysteries of the Warp. It was precisely this curiosity, and this goodwill, that drove him into the deepest tragedy of the galaxy — he who most wanted to protect the Imperium of Man became the one who single-handedly destroyed the Emperor's greatest endeavor, and in the end fell as a Daemon Prince of Tzeentch.

## Origin

When the infant Primarchs were snatched from the Terran laboratories by the Chaos Gods and hurled to the far corners of the galaxy, Magnus landed on Prospero — a world of quiet scholarship. There were no wilds and beasts here, no gladiator pits, only ancient libraries, contemplative cloisters, and communities of scholars devoted to plumbing the mysteries of the cosmos. He was adopted by a scholarly fellowship and raised amidst scrolls and sorcery.

Magnus's psychic gifts were almost innate. He could move objects with his mind in the cradle; by adolescence he was already conversing with entities in the Warp. His skin gradually turned a deep crimson — at once a physical manifestation of immense psychic power, and the origin of his title "the Red." Most striking of all was the single great eye centered on his face: Magnus had no pair of eyes, only this vast cyclopean orb, said to perceive not merely the material realm but the very essence of the Warp directly.

When the Emperor of Mankind recovered Magnus, father and son recognized each other at once. Of all the Primarchs, Magnus was among the few who could truly converse with the Emperor on the subject of the Warp. The Emperor revealed to him the Warp's dangers and the potential of psychic power, hoping that this, his most brilliant son, would explore with restraint and wield with caution. Magnus's response was to throw himself into learning and collection with fervor — he established on Prospero the greatest repository of knowledge in the galaxy, and the Thousand Sons became, as naturally as water flowing downhill, the only Space Marine Legion in which every warrior possessed psychic ability.

## Deeds

### The Flesh Change and the First Bargain

The gene-seed of the Thousand Sons carried a fatal flaw — a genetic curse known as the Flesh Change, which would randomly twist and mutate a warrior's body into unrecognizable, shapeless masses of flesh. Hundreds upon hundreds of Thousand Sons died in agony through these mutations, and the Legion teetered on the brink of extinction.

Watching his sons consumed one after another by the curse, Magnus made a decision: he ventured deep into the Warp and struck a bargain with a certain entity. This entity showed him how to halt the Flesh Change. The price — at the time, Magnus believed the price he was paying was manageable, or at least something his own intellect could master. The Flesh Change stopped. The Thousand Sons were saved. And the entity with whom he had made that bargain was Tzeentch, the Changer of Ways, the Chaos God supreme in stratagem and deception. Tzeentch had long had his eye on this most brilliant of psychic fruits. He was prepared to wait.

### The Council of Nikaea

As the Great Crusade advanced, more and more voices grew uneasy at the Thousand Sons' open use of psychic power. Leman Russ, Primarch of the Space Wolves — a warrior raised in the savage ice of Fenris — harbored a deep cultural loathing for Magnus's "sorcery." Mortarion of the Death Guard, whose homeworld's bitter memory of psyker oppression left him equally hostile to all witchcraft. Under pressure from many quarters, the Emperor convened the Council of Nikaea.

The outcome was catastrophic: the Emperor banned the Space Marine Legions from using psychic powers. The Thousand Sons were ordered to disband their core psychic organizations, seal all their sorcerous texts, and return to "pure" martial practice. Magnus argued his case forcefully at the Council, but ultimately submitted to his father's edict. Submission, however, did not mean surrender — back on Prospero, he and his sons went underground, continuing their psychic studies in secret. He believed that once he could prove the value of psychic power, his father would eventually change his mind.

### The Breaking of the Webway

On the eve of the Horus Heresy, Magnus, in his Warp explorations, saw a vision of the future so horrifying it froze the blood: his most respected elder brother, Warmaster Horus Lupercal, would be corrupted by Chaos and ignite a civil war that would engulf the entire galaxy. The vision was so clear, so terrible, that Magnus knew he must warn the Emperor at once.

But Terra was far, and conventional communications were too slow. The Edict of Nikaea meant he could not use psychic communication — to do so was tantamount to rebellion. Magnus made an astonishing gamble: he gathered the mightiest psykers of the Thousand Sons and conducted a grand ritual the like of which had never been attempted. He tore through the barrier between the Warp and realspace, projected his soul across dimensions, and cast himself directly into the Throne Room on Terra, seeking to tell his father: Horus has betrayed us.

What he did not know was that the Emperor was, in that very moment, deep beneath the Imperial Palace, undertaking the ultimate project for humanity's fate — the Webway Project. The Emperor's goal was to conquer the Webway, freeing humanity from its dependence on Warp travel and severing Chaos's access to the species at the root. Magnus's psychic lance struck exactly — and shattered — the seals on the Webway gate.

Warp entities flooded in like a tide. The Adeptus Custodes and the Mechanicum tech-priests fell in endless waves to the daemon onslaught. The Emperor's life's work collapsed, irreversibly, in a single instant. Magnus finally understood what he had done — his warning had succeeded, the Emperor now knew of Horus's betrayal — but the price was so immense that this "success" was the most terrible failure imaginable.

### The Razing of Prospero

The Emperor's fury can be imagined. He commanded Leman Russ to take the Space Wolves to Prospero and "bring Magnus back to Terra to face judgment." But the order passed through Horus's hands, and Horus, long since fallen, artfully altered it to "exterminate." Leman Russ, already filled with enmity toward Magnus, received this command and executed it without a moment's doubt.

The Space Wolves fleet darkened the sky of Prospero. The Thousand Sons transformed from scholars into warriors, resisting with sorcery and psychic might against overwhelming force. But the golden armor of the Custodes and the null-aura of the Sisters of Silence suppressed the psychic abilities the Thousand Sons relied on most, and the berserk charges of the Space Wolves tore through defense line after defense line. Prospero's cities crumpled in flames. Libraries burned to ash. Thousands of the Thousand Sons warriors died in droves.

Throughout this battle, Magnus never unleashed his full power. He was torn by guilt, fury, and contradiction: he had made a mistake, his sons should not have to pay the price — but what gave the Emperor the right to send savages to destroy his world? Only at the very end, when everything was irretrievable, did he at last reach out to the being that had been waiting: Tzeentch. The shattered remnants of the Thousand Sons were dragged into a Warp vortex and cast onto the Planet of the Sorcerers within Tzeentch's domain.

### The Fall

Once Magnus arrived on the Planet of the Sorcerers, there was no road back. His body had been torn apart and reassembled in the transit; his loyal shards of soul were scattered across the galaxy, and Tzeentch personally pieced back together the central fragment — along with a binding contract and chains. Magnus ascended, becoming a Daemon Prince of Tzeentch, gifted with limitless psychic power and immortal life, but also made forever a piece on the Changer of Ways' game board.

He had believed he was using Tzeentch — that the exchange was mutual, that he could master Chaos while retaining his own will. This, precisely, was Tzeentch's most brilliant trick: to let the prey believe it is the hunter. Magnus, to this day, does not consider himself Tzeentch's slave. This very refusal to admit it is the deepest prison of all.

## Fate & Present Status

Ten thousand years have passed, and Magnus still rules the Thousand Sons as a Daemon Prince. In M41, he led the Thousand Sons in an invasion of Fenris, repaying the old debt of Prospero's burning. He has also attempted to strike at Terra, but these campaigns have been thwarted. His psychic power exceeds that of nearly all beings; his knowledge of ancient secrets would leave any scholar in despair; he can raise terror-storms across the galaxy — but all of it operates entirely within Tzeentch's framework.

The Thousand Sons today are a strange and sorcerous force led by psychic sorcerers, with most common warriors cursed into the mindless Rubric Marines — souls of dust sealed within armor, the product of yet another failed attempt by Magnus to save them. The sorcerers still study, still gather knowledge, still weave endless intrigues, just as their Primarch does.

## Verdict

Of all the Primarch tragedies, the story of Magnus is the most heartbreaking. He is not Angron, driven by hatred; not Lorgar, blinded by faith; not Fulgrim, corrupted by a daemon blade. The cause of his fall is the ultimate version of "good intentions paving the road to hell": he meant to warn his father and destroyed his father's most important project; he tried to save his sons and sold them to Tzeentch; he believed he controlled everything and was played from beginning to end.

Magnus's hubris was his greatest enemy. He always believed that his intelligence and psychic power could overcome any risk, that he could "manage" a transaction with Chaos without being devoured in return. But Chaos never makes an equal exchange. Tzeentch never loses. Magnus lost — not because his abilities were insufficient, but because in his own eyes he was the only player at the board, and he never truly acknowledged that there was another, far older player, laying his pieces before Magnus was even born.

The Emperor once said: "The deepest betrayal often comes from the deepest love." Magnus loved his father, loved his sons, loved knowledge, loved truth. He wanted to save everything and in the end destroyed everything. This is the tragedy of the Crimson King — he was the one who most wanted to protect the Imperium, and the one who, with his own hands, carved the deepest wound into the Imperium's heart.
