# 15 · Alpharius / Omegon

## Overview

Primarch of the XX Legion — the Alpha Legion. Or rather, one of its Primarchs. Among all twenty brothers, Alpharius was the last to be recovered, and the one who baffled the others most. He was not the strongest warrior, nor the sharpest scholar, nor the most charismatic leader. He was a shadow. A man you had met but could not remember. A presence that left you wondering: *Was that really him I spoke to?* And Alpharius's greatest secret — one none of his brothers learned before his death — was that he was never alone. He was a twin. Omegon, his silent counterpart, shared the same soul, the same fate, the same face, and the same labyrinth of stratagems that no one ever untangled.

The Alpha Legion was the most unsettling military force in the Imperium. They fought no pitched battles. They sought no glory. They infiltrated, and infiltrated, and infiltrated again — worming into enemy chains of command, falsifying intelligence, sowing mutual suspicion, causing hostile forces to collapse before a single shot was fired. And what chilled the blood most: because every Alpha Legionnaire shared Alpharius's gene-seed, they all looked almost identical. Any Alpha Legionary could claim to be Alpharius. You never knew whether the figure standing before you was the Primarch himself or merely a rank-and-file legionary. And in the Horus Heresy, this Legion chose a path even they may never have walked to its end — betraying the Imperium in order to save it.

## Origin

Concerning Alpharius's origins — to be precise — no one knows for certain.

Every other Primarch's story has some traceable thread: the world they landed on, who raised them, how they grew, how the Emperor found them. Alpharius has none of this. His landing site is unknown. His upbringing is a void. His childhood is a sealed vault. The only certainty is that he was the last of all the Primarchs to be formally recorded by the Imperium. By the time the Emperor located him, the Great Crusade was already drawing to a close.

Omegon is stranger still. If Alpharius's dossier is merely incomplete, Omegon's simply does not exist in any Imperial record. Beyond Alpharius himself, no one knows when Omegon appeared, where he came from, whether he was swept away by Chaos alongside his twin or fissioned into being later. Certain veiled hints gesture toward a possibility: these twins were one from the beginning — a single soul divided into two bodies, like the two faces of a coin, one stamped Alpha and the other Omega.

Alpharius's stature was perhaps the least conspicuous among the Primarchs. His brothers were giants among giants — Sanguinius towered on angelic wings, Vulkan's frame was a mountain of flesh. But Alpharius stood shorter than them all, his build closer to an exceptionally tall Space Marine than to an unmistakable Primarch. This "ordinariness" was not a flaw; it was a weapon. Alpharius could shed his artificer armour, don the plate of a common legionary, and vanish into the ranks of his own gene-sons — and no one could tell. Not even his own warriors. He could serve anonymously in his Legion for decades, observing, commanding, infiltrating as "some soldier," without a single soul knowing their Primarch walked among them. This concealment was not the pinnacle of disguise — it was genetic destiny. The XX Legion's ultimate gift was to disappear.

## Deeds

### The XX Legion: Serpent and Web

The Alpha Legion was an anomaly among anomalies. Other Legions pursued tactical excellence, the glory of assault, the splendour of battlefield honours. Not the Alpha Legion. Their tactical manual contained a single line: *Make the enemy destroy itself.*

A typical Alpha Legion assault unfolded like this: the ruling class of a target world suddenly descended into mutual paranoia; military commanders received two sets of encrypted orders that contradicted each other; resistance leaders acquired a shipment of high-grade weapons from an untraceable source at a critical juncture; the planetary governor's personal guard dissolved in the night because someone told them the other hemisphere had already fallen — when in fact nothing had happened at all. By the time the Alpha Legion fleet actually made planetfall, the enemy was exhausted, fractured, and defeated. The war had been won before it began. The Alpha Legion's presence was never defined by the thunder of battle, but by every forged signature on midnight documents, every intercepted and doctored transmission, every informant who had been bought without ever knowing they had been bought.

The Emperor designed them as the Imperium's secret weapon — the most extreme tool, reserved for problems that could not be solved in the open. Their icon was the Hydra — cut off one head, two grow in its place. And their Legion motto was a single phrase: *"For the Emperor."* Uttered aloud, it sounded indistinguishable from any Loyalist Legion's creed — but from Alpha Legion lips, no one could say for certain which Emperor, which Imperium, that phrase was meant for.

### The Twin-Secret

The relationship between Alpharius and Omegon is one of the most impenetrable riddles in the 40K universe.

In all public settings, only Alpharius existed. Omegon never appeared before his brothers as a "Primarch." When Alpharius addressed the Warmaster's council, Omegon might have been commanding a covert operation a thousand light-years away — or he might have been standing at Alpharius's feet, clad in the armour of a common legionary, utterly silent. The twins commanded the Legion together, but only one face ever showed. This arrangement granted them an immense strategic advantage: the enemy believed they were contending with one Primarch, when in truth they were contending with an omnipresent web.

But were the twins always of one mind? This is a deeper question. Obscure threads suggest that, late in the Horus Heresy, Alpharius and Omegon may have diverged. One held to the choice they had made together at the outset; the other began to doubt. If they were indeed two bodies housing a single soul — what happens when that soul begins to lie to itself?

### The Cabal's Prophecy

On the eve of the Horus Heresy, the Alpha Legion encountered something that changed everything. A clandestine organisation composed of disparate xenos species — the "Cabal" — found them.

The Cabal were seers drawn from ancient races across the galaxy, possessing some ability to peer into future timelines. They presented Alpharius and Omegon with two possible paths. The first: the Emperor wins, Horus is destroyed, the Imperium decays over tens of millennia under Chaos's relentless corrosion, and ultimately Chaos consumes the entire galaxy — no one survives. The second: Horus wins, Chaos erupts across the galaxy in explosive expansion — but internal strife among the Chaos Gods and the fundamental inability of mortals to withstand Chaotic energies will cause Chaos to self-destruct within two or three generations. Human civilisation will perish, but the galaxy itself will be freed of Chaos's grip. Other species will live. Other species will thrive.

The Cabal's logic was cold to the point of obscenity: *Let Horus win.* Purchase a galaxy free of Chaos at the price of human extinction.

Faced with these two choices, Alpharius and Omegon made a decision none could comprehend: they joined Horus's rebellion. Not because they believed in Chaos. Not because they hated the Emperor. Not because they were corrupted. Because they had calculated, and chosen the path they believed would *save the galaxy.* To achieve ultimate victory, everything must first be betrayed.

### The Battle of Pluto and Death

Throughout the Horus Heresy, the Alpha Legion's visible role was that of traitor forces. They moved in shadow — passing intelligence, severing supply lines, sowing chaos. But what was stranger: abundant evidence suggests they were simultaneously sabotaging the traitor war effort. The intelligence they passed was sometimes genuine, sometimes false. Planets they conquered were sometimes razed, sometimes mysteriously spared. Loyalists marked for assassination sometimes died by accident, sometimes escaped unscathed. No outsider — quite possibly not even their own junior commanders — knew whether any given Alpha Legion operation was helping one side or the other.

Then came the Battle of Pluto. And everything stopped.

Alpharius attempted to infiltrate Terra. He orchestrated an audacious operation aimed at piercing the Plutonian defence cordon to reach the heart of the Sol System. But standing in his way was Rogal Dorn — Primarch of the Imperial Fists, Praetorian of Terra, a warrior defined by absolute logic and a loyalty that brooked no argument.

Alpharius did not hide among his legionaries. He stepped forward. He tried to explain everything to Dorn — *everything I have done has been for the Emperor.* His schemes, his treachery, his reasons for joining Horus — all of it was to secure the Imperium's ultimate victory. But Dorn did not listen. Or rather, Dorn heard and could not accept. In Dorn's lexicon, there was no entry for "betrayal in service of loyalty." You stood on one side or the other. There was no middle ground.

The Battle of Pluto ended like this: Dorn drove his great chainfist through Alpharius's chest. Alpharius died. All the grand designs — betrayal for ultimate salvation — collapsed in that moment into the body of a dead Primarch on the ground. No one knows what Alpharius thought in his final instant. Perhaps he thought Dorn would never understand. Perhaps he wondered whether Omegon could complete their plan alone. Perhaps he wondered whether he had been wrong from the very beginning.

### Omegon and the Infinite Fragments

Omegon survived. But what happened after — no one can say with certainty.

Some accounts hold that he inherited Alpharius's will and continued to steer the Alpha Legion from the shadows. Others claim he received a signal after Pluto — perhaps Alpharius's last command, perhaps his own conscience speaking — and proceeded to covertly sabotage multiple key traitor operations. Still others say he simply left, alone, vanishing into the deeps of the galaxy, never to be seen again.

The most famous legend takes place shortly after Pluto. It is said that Omegon — in the identity of Alpharius — secretly infiltrated an Ultramarines intelligence station and, without anyone recognising him as a Primarch, transmitted a single, minutely small piece of information: it may have been a set of coordinates, a warning, a command. Then he left. Then everything shifted. If he truly did this, then the entire course of the Horus Heresy may have been altered by this one man, this one ghostlike act. But we will never know — because this is how the Alpha Legion operates: you never know what they did. You only know that the outcome has changed.

## Fate & Present Status

Ten thousand years have passed. The Alpha Legion endures — or rather, countless entities calling themselves the Alpha Legion endure.

In every corner of the galaxy, in every star system, in every crevice of the Imperial bureaucracy, an Alpha Legion cell may lie dormant. It may consist of three warriors, or thirty thousand. It may be plotting the overthrow of a hive world's ruling dynasty, or simply watching, doing nothing at all. And what is most lethal: these cells may be unaware of each other's existence — may actively work against each other. Some cells believe they are operating in deep cover to avenge the Imperium. Some believe they are executing the ultimate plan Alpharius laid down ten millennia ago. Some have been fully corrupted by Chaos, genuine renegades in every sense. And some — no one knows what they are doing. Perhaps they do not know themselves. The commander of every cell can declare: *I am Alpharius.* Just as their Primarch once did — don the helmet, wear the common plate, vanish into the crowd.

The Inquisition officially designates the Alpha Legion as traitors — which, on its face, is correct: they did, after all, fight on Horus's side. Yet the Inquisition's treatment of the Alpha Legion has always differed from its treatment of the other Traitor Legions. Certain senior Inquisitors privately concede that the Alpha Legion case is too complex to be categorised as simple "loyalty" or "treachery." They wage no open war. They slaughter no civilians. They worship no Chaos. But they create disorder. Unending, pervasive disorder. One can never tell whether this disorder is corroding the Imperium or purging its corruption — just as one can never tell whether their first Primarch was a traitor or a martyr.

## Verdict

Alpharius is the most extreme tragedy among the twenty brothers. He was not corrupted — he was convinced. He did not turn in rage, or sink into desire, or rampage in hatred — he calculated, coldly, rationally, step by step, and chose a path that was unforgivable from every angle.

The core of this tragedy lies in intellectual overreach. Alpharius (and Omegon) may have been the most intelligent of all the Primarchs — but they were also the most distrustful of "simplicity." Confronted with the Cabal's prophecy, any ordinary human commander would likely have said, *This is xenos trickery — disregard.* But Alpharius did not. He had to see for himself, compute for himself, judge for himself. And when he perceived, across the two timelines, the possibility of Chaos's self-destruction, his genius mind derived a conclusion no other brother could reach: loyalty itself was the greatest obstacle to salvation. You must betray in order to save. You must become the villain in order to be the saviour. You must let the Emperor lose, so that the Emperor might truly win.

In the end, does this logic hold? Was the Cabal's prophecy accurate? Had Horus won, would Chaos truly have consumed itself within a generation or two? No one knows — because Horus died, the Emperor lost and won simultaneously, Chaos is still here, ten thousand years on. The hypothesis upon which Alpharius wagered everything has never been tested. And his entire Legion, his entire legacy, continues — on the foundation of this never-verified premise — an endless war with no goal, no endpoint, and arguably no commander.

*"For the Emperor"* — when those words leave Alpha Legion lips, every listener cannot help but ask: *Which Emperor do you mean?* The deathless, lifeless presence on the Golden Throne? The cold father in the Terran dungeon who told you, flatly, *"Your task is finished"*? Or the idealised Emperor of your own making — the one worthy of being saved through treachery? Perhaps the ultimate answer to this riddle is this: Alpharius fought for himself. Not for the Imperium, not for Chaos, not for rational logic — but for the compulsion itself: *I must conceive a plan more complex than anyone else's.*

The too-clever are the easiest to deceive themselves. Alpharius and Omegon were Primarchs who outsmarted their own souls. They wove a scheme of such exquisite intricacy — ten thousand nested lies, a hundred parallel, mutually camouflaging, self-contradicting lines of operation — that in the end, they no longer knew which side they were on. And when Dorn's fist punched through his chest, that moment may have been the closest Alpharius ever came, in a life glutted with stratagems, to simplicity. He fell. The Imperium's great traitor-saviour-enigma was ended. But his shadow remained. And the shadow does not know it is a shadow. The shadow continues to execute the order issued ten thousand years ago — an order whose issuer is long dead.

And so the Alpha Legion will exist forever. Because no one can prove they have already lost.
