# 10 · Mortarion

## Overview

Mortarion, Primarch of the XIV Legion, the Death Guard, and of all the Emperor's sons the most resilient and the most bitterly lived. He grew up in toxic fog that never lifted, learned to breathe air that could not be breathed, learned to survive in environments that could not be survived. He loathed all psychic power, sorcery, and invisible trickery—he believed a true warrior ought to win through physical endurance and force of will. The irony is that this man, who sneered at any form of "corruption," ultimately became a Daemon Prince beneath the plague god Nurgle. His fall had nothing to do with ambition, nothing to do with desire, not even hatred of the Emperor—he fell because he could not bear to watch his Legion waste away in torments from an inescapable plague. He surrendered so that his sons might stop suffering.

## Origin

Mortarion landed on a world called Barbarus. The skies of Barbarus were forever shrouded in yellow-green toxic fog, and its valleys were suffused with gases capable of dissolving human lungs in minutes. Only the highest mountain peaks offered air thin and clean enough to breathe.

This world was ruled by a race of xenos known as the Overlords. They breathed the crisp air of the summits while humanity eked out a wretched existence in the toxic valleys below, serving as their slaves. The most powerful of these Overlords was called Necare. It was Necare who found the infant fallen from the sky in a poison valley—Mortarion. He did not kill the child, but brought him back to his fortress.

Necare's calculation was simple: he would cultivate a perfect weapon. From his earliest years, Mortarion was placed in toxic environments that would kill any mortal. His body adapted to the poisons at an unthinkable speed; his lungs could work in air that was chemical-weapons-grade; his skin could resist corrosive acid mists. Necare taught him everything about war—but Mortarion's heart was never tamed. He saw the human beings in the valleys below, coughing blood in the poison fog, labouring, dying, while his adoptive father looked down upon them from the heights. When he was strong enough, he descended.

He led the humans of Barbarus in revolt. He taught them to fashion crude gas masks, to march through the fog, to launch night raids, to kill those arrogant xenos Overlords. Peak after peak fell. Overlord after Overlord collapsed before Mortarion's great scythe. At last, only the highest tower remained—Necare's final bastion. And here, Mortarion encountered an ironic dilemma: he had adapted so thoroughly to the toxic fog that he could not exert his full strength in the thin, pure air of the high altitudes. Every time he assaulted the peak, he was forced to retreat, gasping for breath. He gritted his teeth, stamped his feet, swore he would kill his adoptive father with his own hands—but he could not cross that threshold.

At this very juncture, the Emperor descended upon Barbarus.

The Emperor walked up that mountain in Mortarion's full view, entered Necare's tower with ease, and killed the xenos Overlord. Then he said to Mortarion: kneel, and acknowledge me as your father. Mortarion refused. The Emperor said: let us make a wager—if you can enter that tower alone and kill Necare, I will submit to you; if I kill him first, you will kneel.

The Emperor won. Mortarion knelt.

From that day forward, a splinter was lodged in Mortarion's heart that could never be removed. Among all the Primarchs, he was the only one who felt no glory whatsoever in the process of acknowledging his father. He did not feel reclaimed—he felt robbed. The Emperor had stolen his revenge, and then collected his knee with a wager. He did not say it aloud—but he remembered.

## Deeds

### The XIV Legion: Death Guard

After taking command of the XIV Legion, Mortarion swiftly moulded them into an army in his own image. The tactical philosophy of the Death Guard could be reduced to a single word: endure. No flashy raids, no flashy fortifications, not even flashy glory—they simply walked into battle through the most hostile environments imaginable, punched through the enemy by the most direct means possible, and then kept standing in places where even the enemy could not survive the poison gas, radiation, freezing cold, or vacuum.

The Death Guard favoured chemical weapons, phosphex bombs, and grinding heavy infantry advances. They wore unpainted grey-white ceramite—later corrupted by Nurgle into a sickly green—and marched through toxic mists and over corpses. Terrain that other Legions deemed "impassable"—poison swamps, radioactive wastes, viral contamination zones—the Death Guard crossed as though it were flat ground. They conquered not enemies, but "the environment itself that made enemies unable to survive."

In the eyes of the other Primarchs, Mortarion was an oddity. He was taciturn, unsociable, always sitting in the corner at his brothers' banquets. He was not antisocial—he genuinely did not care. Jaghatai Khan openly expressed his disdain, calling Mortarion a hypocrite—claiming to rely on nothing but willpower and nothing external, while his body was the most toxin-adapted of any Primarch. "You've simply grown accustomed to a different kind of dependency," the Khan said. Mortarion held the Khan in equal contempt, convinced that nomadic hit-and-run warfare was cheap opportunism.

But the thing Mortarion could not abide above all else was Magnus the Red and his Thousand Sons. Psychic power—in Mortarion's view—was the most shameful degeneracy in human history. A flash of light on the battlefield, a wave of the hand, a spoken incantation, and hundreds die? That was not combat. That was cheating. Psykers were warlocks, and warlocks were the root of all human suffering. The xenos Overlords of Barbarus had ruled the suffering people in the poison valleys with similar supernatural powers. He hated the psyker, purely and fiercely.

### Turning to Horus

When the Horus Heresy broke out, Mortarion's choice surprised no one. He hated the Emperor—not with the soul-scorching hatred of Angron, but with a colder, decades-accumulated resentment. The Emperor had stolen the one thing he most wanted to complete with his own hands, then bound him for a lifetime with orders and discipline. In Horus's rebellion, Mortarion saw an opportunity: overthrow this hypocritical tyrant, return the galaxy of warriors to the hands of warriors.

But he was no blind follower of Horus. He joined the rebellion with his own designs. He did not submit to Chaos; he looked down on the Word Bearers who worshipped the dark gods. He did not consider himself to be fighting for any deity. He simply stood on the opposite side of the Emperor, because that was the place he had always wanted to stand.

### Betrayal En Route to Terra

As the Death Guard fleet sailed towards Terra, the Warp suddenly descended into unprecedented chaos. Navigation systems failed entirely. Engines were seized by invisible hands. The entire fleet was dragged off its plotted course.

The architect of this was Mortarion's First Captain—Typhus. Typhus was Mortarion's foster-brother from Barbarus, the warrior he trusted most in the Death Guard. But Typhus had secretly pledged himself to Nurgle long ago. He had covertly altered the fleet's navigation data, steering the entire XIV Legion into the deepest reaches of the Warp—the Garden of Nurgle.

The fleet was trapped. No matter what they tried, they could not extract themselves from this Warp domain of endless rot and stench. Worse still: a plague began spreading among the warriors.

### The Garden of Nurgle

The Destroyer Plague—Nurgle's tailor-made gift for the Legion he most desired. This was no ordinary disease, no mere wound infection or bad air. It was a supernatural plague capable of taking root and multiplying simultaneously within the enhanced immune systems and genetically engineered organs of Space Marines. The Death Guard's bodies—conditioned over centuries by Mortarion to withstand any toxin, any radiation, any chemical weapon—became, in the face of this plague, the perfect growth medium.

How long the Legion suffered in Nurgle's Garden, no one can say. There is no linear time in the Warp, only endless cycles. Day after day—if "day" had meaning—Mortarion walked the decks of his flagship, the *Endurance*, passing row after row of warriors groaning on the deck plates. Their internal organs rotted inside their armour, their lungs filled with pus and maggot larvae, yet their gene-forged bodies would not let them die. They could only suffer, alive, forever, tormented endlessly by an incurable disease. No one groaned any less the next day. No one's wounds healed. Not a single son could close his eyes and find rest.

The most terrible thing was not the suffering itself, but that Mortarion could do nothing. He was the most resilient Primarch. His entire military philosophy was built on "endurance"—when the environment became too hostile to act, you clenched your jaw and pushed through; the enemy would break before you did. But now, there was no enemy. There was no freezing night or toxic fog to push through. There was only a disease that ignored all resistance, that offered no option to "push through." Endurance—everything he had taught his Legion—was meaningless in the face of this catastrophe.

Nurgle was waiting.

Nurgle is not Khorne, who forces submission through rage. Nor is he Tzeentch, who lures you into a trap with cunning. Nurgle's method is to embrace you. To give you "love." To let you know you have been accepted, whether you like it or not. "Grandfather Nurgle loves all his children"—this phrase, in the context of Chaos, sounds like mockery. But it is true. Nurgle really does love the Death Guard, in a certain way. He bestows "gifts" upon them—disease, pustules, decay—not as punishment, but because he considers these things beautiful. Only rot can bring new life. Only blight can bloom into a garden. You don't like your new body? No matter. Grandfather will help you get used to it.

Mortarion held on and held on. He hated the Emperor. Hated psykers. Hated Magnus. Hated everyone who won through external power rather than their own will. And now, collapsed at his feet, without even the strength to stand—were all of his sons. Surrender? Bow his head to this plague god who had used him as a game piece? That would be a negation of everything he had ever lived for. But not surrender? These children would rot before his eyes and never be able to die.

He surrendered. To make the pain stop.

### Ascension

The moment Mortarion bent his knee to Nurgle, the fate of the entire Death Guard Legion was sealed for eternity. The Destroyer Plague ceased its torments—but it did not vanish. It became part of them. Every Death Guard warrior was pulled back from the brink of death, but no one would ever call them "alive" again. Their bodies rotted. Their innards dripped pus. Flies nested in their hollow chest cavities. And they no longer felt pain. In its place was a gentle, dull, grandfatherly comfort—Nurgle's "love."

Mortarion himself ascended as a Daemon Prince of Nurgle. His wings were not angelic pinions, nor daemonic membrane—but pale, mould-flecked, spore-laced moth wings. Wherever he passed, pestilence spread automatically. The most venomous gift Nurgle gave him was the formula for Nurgle's Rot—the ultimate disease that slowly breaks down the living into pus-sludge, and the Death Guard embraced its dissemination as a sacred duty.

Mortarion hated all of it. He hated his own wings. Hated the stench that clung to him. Hated the eternally buzzing flies. Hated Typhus, who dared not admit but secretly regretted his betrayal. Hated Nurgle's cloying, inescapable "love." But he could not take it back. He had nowhere else to go. The Emperor would never reclaim a Daemon Prince. And his Legion—his children—were still "alive" within this rot. If this was the price of living, then so be it.

## Fate & Present Status

For ten thousand years, Mortarion has held court on the Plague Planet, commanding the Death Guard Legion in spreading pestilence in Nurgle's name. His fortress is a horror of living flesh fused with metal, its walls weeping pus, its floors breathing. From this world, the Death Guard fleets periodically surge across the galaxy—not to conquer, but to distribute. To distribute Grandfather Nurgle's gifts.

In the Era Indomitus of M42, Mortarion launched the Plague Wars, leading Nurgle's daemonic host in an invasion of Ultramar, the realm of Roboute Guilliman. He faced Guilliman directly on the battlefield and grievously wounded his loyal brother with Nurgle's accursed toxins. But at the last moment, the Emperor manifested on the battlefield as a Living Saint—a blast of pure psychic flame scorched Mortarion's daemonic form and drove him back. It was the first time in ten millennia that Mortarion had again confronted his once-father's will—and once again, he was repulsed.

## Verdict

Among the Primarchs' many tales of falling, Mortarion is the most reluctant traitor.

Fulgrim slid into Slaanesh's embrace chasing ultimate perfection. Angron plunged into Khorne's slaughter-circle through hatred of the Emperor. Magnus became Tzeentch's plaything through a well-intentioned mistake and a manipulated tragedy. But Mortarion—Mortarion never chased anything. From beginning to end, all he wanted was to fight alongside his men and win through his own strength. He hated the Emperor. Hated psykers. Hated cowards who gained power through cheap tricks. He spent his entire life trying to prove one thing: a true warrior needs no gods.

But this is the core of his tragedy. Faced with the unceasing plague in Nurgle's Garden, faced with the sight of his Legion writhing and wailing on the ground—dying but unable to die—he exhausted every last ounce of "willpower" and "endurance," the virtues upon which his entire existence was built, and discovered that these virtues were utterly useless against supernatural force. The Emperor could easily kill the xenos in that tower Mortarion could not climb; Nurgle could just as easily torment the warriors Mortarion had spent his life protecting. Before the truly vast powers of the universe, endurance? Endurance is nothing but a black joke.

He surrendered. For his sons. This may be, of all the traitor Primarchs' reasons for rebellion, the one closest to love. He was not seduced by power, not driven by ambition, not blinded by hatred—he was a father watching his children being tortured, powerless to help, and in the end he signed away his soul, trading his own eternal rot for the cessation of their pain.

And Nurgle, this plague god called "Grandfather," is thereby the most terrifying of all the Chaos Gods. Khorne wants your rage. Tzeentch wants your cunning. Slaanesh wants your desire—you at least know what they are taking. But Nurgle wants your love. When the thing you most want to protect is wrapped in disease and suffering, with no way out, Nurgle opens his rotting embrace and says: come. Let me bear your burden. You no longer have to carry everything alone. Give it all to me. And then you kneel—not because you were defeated, but because you were tired. This is Nurgle's deepest victory.

Ten thousand years have passed. The Death Guard still spread pestilence. Mortarion may hate Nurgle, may hate the Emperor, may hate Typhus—but the deepest hatred, he reserves for himself. A man said to be the most resilient was, in truth, trapped by his own resilience.
