# 06 · Sanguinius

## Overview

Sanguinius, Primarch of the Ninth Legion — the Blood Angels. The Emperor's most beloved son, and the one most deeply loved by all his Primarch brothers. He was the only Primarch to grow a great pair of white-feathered wings, radiant as an angel descending upon the battlefield. Horus, before his fall, spoke a sentence that eternity would never forget: "If Sanguinius had been made Warmaster, I would not have rebelled." He was slain by Horus's own hand aboard the Vengeful Spirit in the final battle of the Horus Heresy. After his death he was canonized as an Imperial Saint, and the Blood Angels to this day carry his blood and his name as their highest honor.

## Origin

Sanguinius landed on Baal — a dead world of irradiated wastes and mutant tribes. Baal's sky was a sickly yellow poison; its earth was cracked salt flats; its water carried radioactivity. The humans there had regressed to a tribal existence, slaughtering one another over every scrap of barely-habitable ground.

It was into this world that an infant with a pair of white-feathered wings fell from the heavens. The local tribe called the Blood took him in, regarding him as a god. On a land utterly without hope, the arrival of an angel was itself a miracle.

Sanguinius grew up in Baal's wastes and endured everything cruel the world could inflict upon a child. He learned to fight. He learned to survive in extremity. And he learned the quality that would define his entire existence: to be the light even in the darkest of places. By the time he reached adulthood, the wings at his back spread like clouds, pure and immaculate — the most absolute contrast to Baal's dead-gray earth.

When the Emperor came, Sanguinius did not resist — he had already seen this father in his dreams. He knelt before the Emperor, and the Emperor raised him up. It is said that the Emperor looked at his wings and was silent for a long time.

## Deeds

### The Legion and the Curse

The Ninth Legion had already won a fearsome reputation in the battles of the early Great Crusade — but signs of a dark unease had already begun to show. In the heat of combat, Blood Angels warriors would fall into an almost uncontrollable blood-frenzy, unable to stop killing once the slaughter had begun. This tendency would later be called the Red Thirst.

When Sanguinius took command of his Legion, he faced this curse directly. He did not deny its existence, did not try to conceal or suppress it. Instead, he took a different path: he refined the Ninth Legion's twin extremes of violence and beauty, fusing them into a single whole. Under his rule the Blood Angels were at once the most ferocious warriors and the most elegant artists. They meditated before battle; they sculpted, painted, and composed poetry afterwards. This was not a thin veneer of civilization draped over barbarism — it was a man who knew he could plunge into the abyss at any moment, clawing at the light with both hands.

But Sanguinius knew more. He possessed a natural gift of foresight — he could see visions of death and disaster, and in his dreams he had glimpsed the end of his own life. He had seen Horus's face. He had seen blood. He had seen his own feathers stained red. He never told any of his brothers the full truth.

### The Great Crusade

During the Great Crusade, Sanguinius and his Blood Angels piled up glorious campaigns. He did not command armies like Horus, did not subdue the foe with feral savagery like Leman Russ, did not lay the institutions of empire like Roboute Guilliman — he fought with something approaching perfection. He was one of the Emperor's most successful genetic creations: possessed of immense strength without losing agility, peerless in close combat, with a battlefield intuition that transcended all rational calculation. More critically, wherever he went and whatever enemy he faced, the sheer presence of Sanguinius made all who encountered him — common human soldiers and fellow Primarchs alike — willing to follow.

Rogal Dorn, the Emperor's most trusted master of defense, regarded him as his closest brother. Even Konrad Curze, the Night Haunter who walked alone in darkness, harbored toward Sanguinius a tangled emotion of both contempt and envy — Curze too could see the future, but what he saw was punishment and terror; Sanguinius saw sacrifice and hope. Two prophets, who chose opposite paths.

### The Council of Nikaea

At the Council of Nikaea, the Emperor issued an edict forbidding the Space Marine Legions from using psychic powers. Sanguinius obeyed — but in his heart he had his own view of the decision. He knew psychic power was poison, but he also knew that sometimes the only antidote is hidden at the bottom of a poison vial.

### Signus Prime

After the Horus Heresy broke out, Horus assigned Sanguinius a task dressed in apparent glory: take the Blood Angels to the Signus Cluster to put down a rebellion. It was, in truth, a trap. At Signus Prime, Sanguinius found that what awaited him was not rebel forces, but an entire star system overrun by Chaos daemons.

The Chaos Gods poured every effort into seducing him. The terms they offered were agonizingly tempting — if he would only fall, the entire Blood Angels Legion and he himself would be freed from the curse of the Red Thirst and granted unimaginable and perfect power. Through the extremes of torment and temptation, Sanguinius refused. He did not fall. He carved a path through seas of blood with his shattered forces and returned to the Loyalist camp.

But Signus left a scar — Sanguinius realized that Chaos's interest in him was no less intense than its interest in Horus. He knew he was marked, but did not know when the final blow would come.

### The Siege of Terra

The Siege of Terra was the final chapter of the Horus Heresy. The Traitor fleet encircled the cradle of mankind; Horus's host — nine Traitor Legions plus numberless daemons and mutants — launched the general assault on the Imperial Palace.

At the siege's most desperate hour, the Traitors broke through the outer defenses and surged toward the Eternity Gate — the last choke point before the inner sanctum of the Palace. If the Eternity Gate fell, Terra would be lost before the Emperor and Horus could ever face each other.

Sanguinius stood alone before the Eternity Gate.

He spread his wings and held back an entire army. The accounts of that stand have been retold, magnified, and beatified by countless generations — but the cross-referenced testimony of every survivor points to the same fact: he really did, single-handedly, hold back a tide of enemies. His wings burned in the fire. His sword danced among the daemons. And no one — not a single enemy — crossed the gate behind him.

### The Vengeful Spirit

In the siege's final hour, Horus lowered the Void Shields of his flagship, the Vengeful Spirit, issuing a silent challenge. The Emperor made his decision: he would board the ship personally and end it with Horus. Sanguinius and Dorn teleported aboard with him.

In the moment before the teleport, Sanguinius said something to Dorn. No one knows the exact words; only that Dorn spent the rest of his life carrying their weight.

Sanguinius knew he was going to his death. He had foreseen it. He could have stayed behind. He went.

In the throne room of the Vengeful Spirit, Sanguinius faced a Horus who was now infused with the combined power of the four Chaos Gods — a creature no longer a brother, more terrible than any entity of the Warp.

> I hear the voice of the Emperor calling me. I will answer.

Sanguinius launched his final charge. His sword shattered. His wings were torn. His blood stained the deck of the Vengeful Spirit.

Horus struck him dead with a single killing blow.

## Fate & Present Status

Sanguinius is dead. His body lay upon the deck of the Vengeful Spirit, his white-feathered wings soaked through with blood — exactly as he had seen them in his dreams a thousand times.

When the Emperor reached the scene, this was what he witnessed. He had hesitated before — Horus was, after all, his most beloved son, the first Primarch he had recovered, the child who had fought at his side longest through the Great Crusade. The Emperor had been waiting, waiting for one flicker of Horus's remaining humanity, one glimmer of the possibility of turning back.

Sanguinius's death shattered that possibility.

Looking upon Sanguinius's body, the Emperor finally accepted that Horus was beyond all redemption. He unleashed the full measure of his psychic power — not a warrior's fury, but a father's finality. He erased Horus from every level of existence: body, soul, memory, trace. Horus Lupercal was no more.

But the Emperor himself suffered mortal wounds in that act. His ruined body was carried back to the Golden Throne by Rogal Dorn, where it has burned in a half-dead state for ten thousand years.

Sanguinius's sacrifice was psychically imprinted forever into his sons' gene-seed. Every descendant warrior of the Blood Angels may one day suddenly "relive" their Primarch's final moments — seeing the deck of the Vengeful Spirit, seeing Horus's towering silhouette, feeling Sanguinius's last agony and fury. This is the Black Rage — at once a genetic curse and a genetic memory. Warriors who fall into the Black Rage lose their selves entirely, fighting to the death inside a mad hallucination.

## Verdict

Of all the Emperor's sons, Sanguinius was the one closest to the definition of "angel." Not because of his wings — but because of the decision to go, knowing that going meant death.

He could have stayed off the Vengeful Spirit. He had the gift of foresight; he had seen this moment years in advance. He could have found a thousand reasons to remain — the Emperor would not have blamed him, Dorn could have gone in his stead, any brother would have understood. But he went. He chose to walk into the most terrifying vision of his life, because it was what he ought to do.

This raises an unanswerable question: what kind of sons did the Emperor want? Warriors who obey commands without question, or saints who face fate with open eyes? Horus went from loving the Emperor to hating him. Guilliman discovered the Emperor saw him as a tool and fell into shock. But Sanguinius — Sanguinius never had a complaint to register with the Emperor. He proved with his death that loyalty needs no reason.

But is this what the Emperor desired? The most human of his sons — one who could fear, could struggle, could choose — ending in sacrifice. If this was what the Emperor hoped for from all his children, then the Emperor's Imperium itself is a temple built upon the corpses of his sons.

For ten thousand years, the warriors of the Blood Angels have inherited everything of their gene-father: his glory, his wings, his fury, his blood. In their veins flows Sanguinius's last memory. Every warrior who dies in the Black Rage is reenacting with his death their Primarch's final moment.

It is said that on Baal, Sanguinius's golden statue still stands, wings outspread, face turned toward the stars. Every Blood Angels neophyte, in the final rite of gene-seed implantation, kneels before it. They do not pray — Sanguinius in life never asked anyone to worship him. They do only one thing: silently make a promise.

The promise that there will come a time, when the Imperium most desperately needs light, when they too will spread their wings and walk toward that gate.
