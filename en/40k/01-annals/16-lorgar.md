# 16 · Lorgar Aurelian

## Overview

Primarch of the XVII Legion, gene-father of the Word Bearers. Among the Primarchs, he was the most fervent religious zealot — first worshipping the Emperor as a god, only to be publicly humiliated by the Emperor and have his holy city razed; then turning to seek a "true god," becoming the first to willingly embrace Chaos, and personally engineering the corruption of Horus. Lorgar himself rarely stood at the front line of battle, yet the entire Horus Heresy was, at its source, a script he wrote. The word *architect* — in the annals of the galactic civil war — belongs to him alone.

## Origin

Lorgar came to rest on Colchis, a world saturated through and through by religion. On Colchis, the divine was not an afterthought stitched onto society — it was the entire logic by which society functioned. Every class, every parcel of land, every war was explained and propelled by prophecy. Lorgar was taken in by the local priest Kor Phaeron. Kor Phaeron was no ordinary cleric; he was one of the most powerful high priests of Colchis's Old Faith, a master of doctrine, political manipulation, and the engineering of human souls.

From childhood, Lorgar displayed a disquieting gift for religion. His oratory, his instinct for the sacred, his capacity to stir the hearts of multitudes — all of these exceeded mortal bounds by an order of magnitude. Upon reaching adulthood, he did something extraordinarily rare on any galactic scale: he unified the faith of Colchis. Not through conquest. Not through slaughter. Through preaching. He walked into the heart of the Old Faith, reframed the fundamental meaning of the divine in his own words, and made everyone — including his foster father Kor Phaeron — acknowledge him as the Prophet. An infant cast from a laboratory onto a planet of zealots, raised by the most seasoned priest alive, ultimately returned to claim the souls of everyone on that world. This was Lorgar's gift, and this was the root of every tragedy that followed: he could not exist without a god.

## Deeds

### The Great Crusade: The Slowest Legion

After the Emperor recovered Lorgar, the performance of the XVII Legion — the Word Bearers — unsettled every one of his brothers. The objective of the Great Crusade was to unify the galaxy, rapidly reclaiming lost human worlds. Yet the first thing Lorgar did upon reaching a new planet was not deploy siege engines — it was build a cathedral.

The Word Bearers' rate of conquest was the slowest of any Legion. Lorgar spent several times longer pacifying a world than any of his brothers, because he was not fighting enemies — he was converting faith. He compelled every compliant world to confess the Emperor as God, to construct magnificent shrines to the Emperor, to compile scripture codifying the worship of the Emperor, turning the Imperial Truth — that official, godless, religionless doctrine of science and reason — into what his own mouth called the *Revelation of the God-Emperor*. In the process, Lorgar transformed the entire XVII Legion into an armed missionary order: every Space Marine was not only a soldier, but a preacher, an evangelist, an enforcer of faith. His Legion charged into battle singing hymns and bearing holy icons — an absurdity in the eyes of every other Legion, and the most natural thing in the universe in the eyes of Lorgar.

### Monarchia: Faith Crushed by the God It Served

Lorgar's conduct came into direct conflict with the Emperor's Imperial Truth. The Emperor insisted there were no gods in the cosmos; Lorgar insisted that the Emperor was the one and only God. This contradiction dragged on for a long time. The Emperor tolerated it — until he decided to tolerate it no longer.

The Emperor commanded the XIII Legion, the Ultramarines — the troops of Lorgar's brother Roboute Guilliman — to destroy Monarchia. That city was Lorgar's most cherished labour: he had spent years building it, shaping it into the holy city of the Emperor's faith, where every stone testified to the glory of the God-Emperor. The Ultramarines razed it to the ground. But this was not enough. The Emperor ordered the entire XVII Legion to assemble atop the ruins, forced Lorgar to kneel, and compelled him to confess, with his own lips, that he had been wrong.

This was not disciplining a subordinate who had made a mistake. This was burning the most sacred thing in a man's life to ash before the eyes of all his sons, then forcing him to kneel and kiss the hand that struck the match. The Emperor did not want repentance — he wanted obliteration. He wanted Lorgar to watch his God, with His own hands, destroy His own holy city, so that Lorgar would finally abandon the idea. The Emperor believed that a humiliated man would shrink back and correct his error. But he did not understand the logic of faith — and the logic of faith is this: when a believer is betrayed by the object of his belief, he does not stop believing. He finds a different god.

### The Pilgrimage: Opening the Door to Chaos

After the destruction of Monarchia, Lorgar did not rebel immediately. He first fell into a prolonged spiritual collapse — not fury, but *void*. The cause to which he had devoted half his life, the labour to which his entire Legion had consecrated itself — was negated in a single act by the god he himself had worshipped. It was as if a man had spent his life digging a well in the desert, only to discover there was no water beneath it, and that the one who had told him water was here was the very "god" he had found.

From that void, Lorgar forced an exit: if the Emperor was not God, then somewhere in the universe, true gods must exist.

> The difference between gods and daemons depends largely on where one stands.

This is a line later quoted from Lorgar again and again. The rubble of Monarchia taught him: the Emperor could be a god, and Chaos could be gods — the only difference lay in which altar you knelt before. The Emperor possessed the power of a god while denying the existence of gods; this in itself proved the Emperor was either lying or ignorant. Lorgar's logic was classically theological: first confirm that gods exist, then go and find them.

He dispatched expeditionary fleets to the galactic fringe and the Warp's fissures, led by his two closest aides: First Chaplain Erebus and his foster father Kor Phaeron. These men returned with an answer. The Chaos Gods were real. Khorne, Tzeentch, Nurgle, Slaanesh — not myth, not metaphor, but entities of limitless power that truly dwelt within the Warp. Lorgar had found his new gods, and this time, these gods would not burn his city. For in the doctrine of Chaos, faith itself *is* power — so long as one is sufficiently devout, any sacrifice is accepted.

### The Architect: Corrupting Horus

Once Lorgar had embraced Chaos, he began secretly spreading the Chaotic faith within the XVII Legion. The Word Bearers transformed from the Emperor's missionaries into Chaos's first evangelists. But Lorgar was clear-eyed about one thing: alone, with only himself and his Legion, he could not topple the Emperor and the entire Loyalist edifice of the Imperium. He needed a larger blade.

That blade was Horus — the Emperor's most beloved son, the Warmaster, supreme commander of all human armies across the galaxy.

Lorgar and Erebus together orchestrated the entire corruption of Horus. During the Great Crusade, Horus was wounded by the xenos anatheme blade, the *Anathame*. What appeared to be an accident was in truth arranged by Lorgar's hand. Erebus approached the dying Horus under the guise of healing, performed Chaotic rituals at his sickbed, and dragged the Warmaster's soul into a Warp-vision. There, the Chaos Gods presented a twisted prophecy of the Emperor's betrayal. The seeds of Horus's suspicion and ambition were planted by Chaos; when he awoke, the right hand that had once been the most loyal at the Emperor's side had quietly begun to clench a fist aimed at Terra.

Lorgar himself stood outside the spotlight throughout this drama. He remained in the shadows — composing scripture, codifying rituals, ensuring the Chaotic belief system took root within the traitor camp. He was not a general. He was a high priest. If Horus was the heart of the Chaos rebellion, Lorgar was its soul. Horus was the one *chosen* by Chaos to fight the war; Lorgar was the one who *chose*, of his own volition, to make the war happen. He did not merely embrace Chaos — he recruited new converts for Chaos. And not just any Space Marine. A Warmaster.

### The Ascension of Angron and Withdrawal

During the Horus Heresy, Lorgar attempted one more great work: the apotheosis of Angron. The Primarch of the XII Legion, Angron, had never been a worshipper of Chaos — he worshipped nothing at all. He wanted only to kill, to avenge, to destroy. Yet Lorgar perceived the agony within Angron's soul; he understood that rage born of being abandoned by the Emperor and stripped of all dignity. Lorgar translated Angron's pain into the language of faith, telling him that Chaos required no servitude — that the gods were simply the vessel for his rage, already there. Under Lorgar's guidance, Angron ultimately ascended as a Daemon Prince of Khorne.

But the rift between Lorgar and Horus was widening. Horus wanted power; Lorgar wanted faith. On the eve of the Siege of Terra, Horus judged that Lorgar's religious agenda was interfering with military operations and expelled him from the front. Lorgar withdrew the Word Bearers from the siege of Terra, returning to his homeworld of Colchis and later to his daemonic domain. This expulsion paradoxically spared him from the Loyalist reckoning at the battle for Terra — he survived, while Horus was utterly annihilated.

## Fate & Present Status

Lorgar ascended as a Daemon Prince, under the patronage of all four Chaos Gods — Chaos Undivided, sworn to no single power. He established his throne upon the daemon world of Sicarus, deep within the Eye of Terror. For ten millennia, he has scarcely ventured forth on campaign. He has sealed himself in Sicarus's innermost sanctum, meditating, writing, studying — attempting to penetrate the ultimate nature of Chaos itself. This has rendered him the "quietest" of the Traitor Primarchs.

Rumours persist that Corvus Corax — the Primarch of the XIX Legion, the shadow that entered the Eye of Terror to hunt traitors — now lurks in the vicinity of Sicarus, having come specifically for Lorgar. According to recent lore, Corax has manifested in some form beyond mortal Primarch flesh — a psychic shadow-presence — and directly engaged Lorgar upon Sicarus. Lorgar has been forced to lock himself within his sanctum to evade this brother from ten thousand years past. Hunter and prey, separated by a single door in the darkness of the Warp, have faced each other for an unknown span of time.

## Verdict

Lorgar is the most easily underestimated of all the Traitor Primarchs. He was a poor fighter — a fact he himself freely admitted. Yet from the perspective of history, the damage he caused exceeds that of any other Traitor Primarch, perhaps even that of Horus himself. Horus's war destroyed half the Imperium's military strength, but one book of Lorgar's — the Chaotic scripture he authored — altered the spiritual ecology of the Imperium for ten thousand years. Without Lorgar, Chaos would never have bloomed so profusely among mankind. Without Lorgar, Horus might have finished his battles on Davin and simply moved on — and nothing would have changed.

Lorgar's story is, at its essence, a parable about faith: what a man becomes when the object of his belief destroys him. The Emperor burned Monarchia, believing he was burning away an error — but what he truly burned was the last chain between Lorgar and himself. A fanatical believer who still believes in you is your most ferocious warrior; the moment you crush his faith before his own eyes, he will turn that same fanaticism toward finding the next god — to destroy you.

Lorgar was not born a villain. Among the Chaos Primarchs, Fulgrim was controlled by his sword, Magnus was cornered by history, Mortarion was broken and yielded after Nurgle imprisoned him, Angron was driven by agony from the moment of his birth. Lorgar is different. From beginning to end, he made his own choices — seeking a god, embracing a god, spreading the god's will. He merely switched the object once. This is more absolute than any passive fall. The Emperor himself cultivated this son's most fearsome capacity: the power to make people believe. And then the Emperor personally taught him that a god will betray its faithful. Lorgar took the logic the Emperor taught him and turned it against the Emperor's enemies.

He spent the second half of his life seeking the ultimate truth of the universe. Ten thousand years later, he is still searching, in that sealed chamber on Sicarus. The Chaos Gods have given him no answer, and perhaps never intended to. But Lorgar does not care. The one thing he learned amid the ruins of Monarchia is this: so long as you are still seeking, you have not yet despaired.
