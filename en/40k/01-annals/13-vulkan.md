# 13 · Vulkan

## Overview

Vulkan, Primarch of the XVIII Legion, the Salamanders, and the kindest of all twenty Primarchs. He was the greatest forge-master in the galaxy, blessed with the gift of the Perpetual—the ability to resurrect after being killed. Among all his Primarch brothers, he alone cared truly and unreservedly for the lives of ordinary humans, and the Salamanders remain famous to this day for the humanitarian traditions they established during the Great Crusade. He was captured at the Isstvan V Dropsite Massacre, repeatedly tortured to death and revived by Konrad Curze, the Night Haunter, becoming the darkest vessel of suffering in the Horus Heresy. After the war, during the War of the Beast in M32, he perished alongside a gargantuan Ork in mutual annihilation, leaving behind nine artefacts scattered across the galaxy and a prophecy that he would one day return.

## Origin

Vulkan landed on Nocturne, a death world ruled by periodic volcanic cataclysms. This world had no gentle seasons, only millennial cycles of the "Time of Trial": global volcanic eruptions occurring simultaneously, rivers of magma consuming continental shelves, the atmosphere choked with sulphur and ash. The humans of Nocturne had consequently evolved two traits: extreme resilience and an extreme spirit of communal mutual aid, because before a volcano, any individual heroism was utterly meaningless.

Vulkan was found and adopted by a smith named N'bel. An infant lying at the foot of an erupting volcano, with everything around him burned to glass by the lava—and he was completely unharmed. The smith carried him home and named him Vulkan, after the fire god in Nocturne's ancient legends.

He grew up beside the anvil. By day, he worked the bellows, swung the hammer, and quenched blades; by night, he listened to his foster father tell the folk-tales of Nocturne—stories in which no hero conquered nature, only humans helping each other survive one Night of Destruction after another. This childhood left a deep imprint on him: Vulkan never possessed a conqueror's mentality. He became a forge-master because he understood that a good hoe was every bit as important as a good sword.

When the Emperor came to reclaim him, there was no battle. Vulkan looked the Emperor in the eye, set down his hammer, and knelt. It is said he saw within the Emperor's eyes a fire he could not comprehend—not a fire of destruction, but something larger and more perilous being forged. He did not ask. He chose to trust.

## Deeds

### Fire and Humanity

During the Great Crusade, Vulkan's XVIII Legion distinguished itself from all others in two ways: flame-weapon forging and an intense care for civilians.

This was not a military regulation; it was Vulkan's personal character filtering downwards. Other Primarchs viewed planetary conquest as a military objective; Vulkan viewed it as "bringing humanity home." He required the Salamanders to prioritise civilian evacuation in every campaign, even when it increased the tactical cost. In the aftermath of battle, he personally helped with reconstruction in the ruins. His Legion did not requisition civilian housing in occupied zones—they pitched tents. This kind of behaviour was unique among all the Space Marines in the galaxy, dismissed by many of his brother Primarchs as "weakness," but Vulkan never cared.

The Salamanders' warriors inherited another gift from their Primarch: a natural mastery of flame and extreme heat, and an instinct-level talent for smelting and forging. The XVIII Legion always marched with portable forge-furnaces in their baggage train, and every warrior was required to forge a weapon or tool with his own hands between battles. This was not merely practical—it was a transmission of culture. Vulkan believed that a Legion that only tore things down without ever building anything would sooner or later forget what it was protecting.

### Brotherhood and the Anvil

Among his brothers, Vulkan was not the most dazzling. He did not inspire universal love like Sanguinius, did not command through sheer charisma like Horus, did not possess the political mind of Guilliman. But he had one thing even Horus had to concede: sincerity.

He was closest to Ferrus Manus. Two forge-masters, one in a volcano, the other on the scrap-iron world of Medusa—when they met, it was a competition to forge. Ferrus worshipped the absolute strength and hardness of metal; Vulkan valued its toughness and ductility. Their aesthetic sensibilities matched their personalities exactly. Ferrus forged a hammer so hard it was absolute; Vulkan forged a long blade that would not shatter under extreme heat. They gave their weapons to each other. It was the purest friendship between any of the Primarchs, completely free of political calculation.

His relationship with Konrad Curze was the most complex. Curze, Primarch of the Night Lords, was a mad prophet born with the ability to foresee disaster, who maintained order through terror in the darkness. Curze harboured a pathological fixation on Vulkan's goodness—he wanted to prove that kindness was meaningless in the universe, that Vulkan's humanitarianism was nothing but a paper mask before true suffering. He saved that fixation for Isstvan V.

### Isstvan V: Hell

After the Horus Heresy erupted, Vulkan led the Salamanders alongside the loyalist coalition forces to the Isstvan V system, their mission to suppress the rebel Legions under Horus. Vulkan, along with three other loyalist Primarchs—Ferrus Manus, Corvus Corax, and assorted additional forces—landed upon the black volcanic plains of Isstvan V.

What they did not know was that the four Legions originally supposed to reinforce them—the Emperor's Children, the Iron Warriors, the Night Lords, the Alpha Legion—had already moved behind their backs to the traitor side.

Vulkan watched from a lava plain as the traitors closed in from every direction. He was captured by Curze in the fighting. Ferrus, on another flank, was cut down by Fulgrim, his head presented to Horus. The entire landing zone became the burial ground of the loyalists. The Salamanders were nearly annihilated.

### The Cage and Immortality

Curze imprisoned Vulkan in an underground lava cavern on Nocturne—he deliberately chose Vulkan's home world as the execution site. In the days that followed, Curze inflicted upon Vulkan everything you can imagine: cutting, burning, electrocution, crushing, flaying alive. Again and again. Every time Vulkan died, his body reconstituted in flame—flesh regrew, breath restarted, eyes reopened. Perpetual. Immortal. This ability, absent in any other Primarch, became the cruelest curse upon Vulkan.

Curze tried frantically to kill Vulkan. He tried every method. Chainsword through the skull, bolter rounds through the heart, immersion in magma, Warp sorcery—all useless. Each time Vulkan died completely, his body temperature rose, his heart began to beat again, his eyes refocused, and he looked at Curze.

Curze could not understand one thing: Vulkan did not break under the endless torment. He did not beg. He did not curse. He did not fall. And this was precisely what shook the foundations of Curze's philosophy—if even the greatest pain cannot destroy goodness, then what, exactly, was his "terror is justice"?

Vulkan ultimately escaped. He seized a ship and returned to the loyalist camp. When he appeared before his brothers in the Imperial Palace on Terra, his entire body was scarred, but his expression was calm. The Emperor looked upon his son and, it is said, remained silent for a very long time.

### The Siege of Terra and After

During the Siege of Terra, Vulkan and the surviving Salamanders forces participated in the defence of the Imperial Palace. He did not stand alone at a gate and cut down a thousand foes like Sanguinius, nor did he coordinate the entire defence from the walls with iron will like Rogal Dorn. What Vulkan did was rush to the most critical breaches and fill them, again and again—just like the ordinary people in his childhood memories who quietly rebuilt their homes after the volcanoes had destroyed everything. He did not shine. He mended.

After the Emperor was interred upon the Golden Throne, Vulkan survived. He was one of the few loyalist Primarchs still active in the mortal realm after the war. But in his soul, he carried too much: he had lost Ferrus. He had endured Curze's cage. He had watched his Legion nearly wiped out, and had resurrected time and time again in the darkness.

### The War of the Beast

In M32, a crisis nearly forgotten by later ages erupted: colossal Orks—led by a Warboss known as the Beast—swept across the galaxy on a Waaagh! of unprecedented scale, directly threatening Terra.

Vulkan donned his armour once more. He knew he had to do this—not because the Emperor commanded it, but because if he did not go, countless civilian worlds would be ground to dust beneath the Orks.

In the final confrontation, Vulkan fought the Beast Ork to the death at the edge of the atmosphere. The battle between the volcano-smith and the greenskin war machine turned the sky red. In the end, he locked himself around the Ork leader and plunged towards a burning planet, both of them dissolving into light upon impact.

His body was never found. But everyone knows: he is a Perpetual. He died, yet he may not have died—somewhere, in the depths of magma, or on the surface of a planet not yet named, Vulkan's body may be reassembling itself. It may simply take ten thousand years this time.

Before leaving for the last time, Vulkan scattered nine artefacts forged by his own hands—the Artefacts of Vulkan—across the corners of the galaxy: the helm, the breastplate, the gauntlets, the pauldrons, the boots, the warhammer, the heart of the furnace, the eternal pulse, and the drake's horn. He left behind a single sentence: when the nine artefacts are gathered once more, Vulkan shall return.

## Fate & Present Status

Vulkan has been missing since M32 and has not yet returned. The Salamanders Chapter holds the pursuit of their Primarch's relics as one of its highest sacred duties. Over ten thousand years, they have recovered several of them. The prophecy says there are more remaining.

The Salamanders still maintain the smithing traditions of Nocturne to this day. After completing gene-seed implantation, every new recruit's first act is not to take up a gun, but to pick up a hammer and forge a piece of equipment with his own hands. Their habit of prioritising civilian protection in battle has also been preserved—in an Imperium growing ever darker, the Salamanders are the last Legion to write "protect" before "kill."

## Verdict

Among the twenty Primarchs, Vulkan is the one least like a "Space Marine Primarch." He is not fanatical, not obsessive, not self-destructive, not self-mythologising. He is more like an old man who would ladle you soup by the fire—provided, of course, that this old man stands three metres tall and can tear a Titan in half with his bare hands.

This is what makes Vulkan special. The other Primarchs are some extreme amplification of human nature—Horus is the limit of ambition, Sanguinius the limit of sacrifice, Curze the limit of fear, Angron the limit of rage. What is Vulkan? He is the limit of goodness. And this is precisely the hardest thing to understand: in a dark universe filled with Chaos, xenos, war, and tyranny, why was goodness not the first thing to be crushed?

The answer may lie in his immortality. Vulkan was killed and resurrected over and over, and every death taught him one thing: come back to life. This was not the triumph of a fighting spirit, but something more fundamental sustaining him—the fire in a smith's furnace, the hands of Nocturne's people rebuilding their homes in the volcanic ash, the stories his foster father N'bel told at the anvil about how humans helped each other to survive. Goodness is not about not getting hurt. It is about choosing, after being hurt, not to become the one who inflicts hurt.

When Curze tortured him, he could have chosen to hate. He did not. He understood Curze. In one of his later imprisonments, Vulkan even embraced the broken Night Haunter—he saw a soul in more pain than his own. This transcends morality. It even transcends logic. This is Vulkan.

In a dark universe, he is the warmest fire. But fire, too, can go out. Vulkan's tragedy is not that he died—it is that he believed too deeply that humanity was ultimately worth saving, and he did not know, at the time, that the Imperium would never become the vision he cherished.

Nocturne still erupts to this day. The forge-fires flicker in and out amid the volcanic dust. Every Salamander warrior, when forging his first piece of equipment, is told one thing: the hammer is heavier than the sword. Because building something is always, always harder than destroying it.
