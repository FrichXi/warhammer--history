# 09 · Fulgrim

## Overview

Fulgrim, Primarch of the III Legion, the Emperor's Children, the artist-Primarch who pursued perfection, and the first of all twenty-one Primarchs to ascend as a Daemon Prince. He had everything—superlative swordsmanship, breathtaking aesthetic talent, the statesman's genius to transform a dying world into paradise—but he was dismantled from within by a single Slaanesh-possessed blade, the Laer Blade. He killed his closest brother, Ferrus Manus, with his own hands, dragged his entire Legion into the most extreme sensory hell imaginable, and ultimately ascended to the rank of Daemon Prince beneath Slaanesh. In late M31, he wounded Roboute Guilliman with a poisoned blade, sealing the Imperial Regent in a stasis field for ten thousand years. Fulgrim's story is a mirror—the distance between perfection and damnation is terrifyingly short.

## Origin

Fulgrim landed on Chemos, a mining waste-world whose resources had been bled dry and whose habitable zones were on the verge of collapse. The air was thick with industrial dust, water was rationed by the day, and the entire planet was slowly suffocating. Fulgrim was found and raised by factory workers. From his earliest memory, all he saw was a society scrabbling day after day on the edge of extinction.

Any other Primarch would likely have led the people in revolt against their oppressors, or forged an army to conquer a richer neighbouring world. Fulgrim did neither. He chose to fix Chemos.

As a youth, Fulgrim displayed an almost magical gift: he could extract usable resources from piles of scrap, retrofit aging mining machines to double their efficiency, and re-engineer near-depleted mines into productive shafts. By the time he reached adulthood, Chemos had not merely survived—it had become a world of culture, of output, a world that had even begun to pursue beauty. For the first time, the inhabitants of this dying planet had the leisure to grow flowers. That was Fulgrim's style of rule. He did not only want people to survive; he wanted them to feel that survival was worth it.

When the Emperor found him, Chemos was building its first theatre. The first thing Fulgrim did after kneeling before the Emperor was to ask permission to finish painting the theatre's dome mural.

## Deeds

### The Great Crusade: The Perfect Legion Master

In the early Great Crusade, the III Legion, the Emperor's Children, faced a severe problem: a defect in their gene-seed kept their Legion at a perpetually minimal size, the smallest of all twenty Legions. A Legion without its Primarch was like a body without a heart; the Emperor's Children were on the verge of being disbanded or absorbed. After Fulgrim's return, the problem vanished. The gene-seed stabilised, the Legion expanded rapidly, and the Emperor's Children transformed from the brink of extinction into one of the most elite and formidable forces of the Great Crusade.

The Emperor's Children were known for "perfection." Other Legions were content to win; Fulgrim obsessed over the aesthetics of winning. Formations had to be elegant, charge ranks had to be symmetrical, a post-victory parade was a performance. Many of his brothers mocked this as ostentatious, but Fulgrim did not care. He believed one thing: the Emperor did not create the Imperium so that humanity could gnaw at each other in the mud. He created it so civilisation could reach its proper heights. Combat, too, should be civilised—this was his obsession, and his vulnerability.

It was during this period that Fulgrim met the most important brother of his life: Ferrus Manus, Primarch of the X Legion, the Iron Hands. The two were polar opposites—Fulgrim elegant, obsessed with beauty and form; Ferrus rough, revering raw strength, convinced that ornamentation was a waste of time. Yet it was precisely this contrast that drew them together. They forged weapons together, fought wars together, argued together over whether art had any use at all. Fulgrim saw this as the most perfect friendship among the Primarch brothers. Ferrus forged Fulgrim a mighty hammer, Fireblade, and Fulgrim regarded Ferrus's iron arms as the greatest sculptural masterpiece of the Great Crusade.

### Corruption: The Slow Poison of a Blade

Corruption did not fall from the sky. It began as a single loose thread, and then slowly unravelled the entire garment.

During the campaign to bring the xenos world of Laer into compliance, the Emperor's Children encountered a highly evolved serpentine alien civilisation. The Laer's cities were themselves works of art—curving serpentine architecture, spiral temples embedded in cliff faces, every weapon and piece of armour designed to aesthetic standards beyond human comprehension. Fulgrim was entranced. In the main Laer temple, he found a sword—the Laer Blade, with a slender blade, gem-encrusted hilt, every line as though tailored to his palm.

He picked up that sword.

Inside the sword dwelt a daemon of Slaanesh. It did not control Fulgrim directly—such crude methods were beneath it. It spoke. It whispered in Fulgrim's mind using Fulgrim's own voice. It let him win at the right moments and made him hesitate at the critical ones. Fulgrim felt his swordsmanship improving, his aesthetic instincts sharpening, his inspiration deepening. He did not know that the sword was, step by step, reshaping his self into whatever shape pleased the daemon.

The cruellest moment came between him and Ferrus. Fulgrim was dispatched alongside Ferrus to the Isstvan system to suppress the rebellion of Warmaster Horus—yes, he was still loyal at this stage. But when he found himself aboard the fleet, trying to persuade Ferrus to "join Horus together," he was startled by his own words. The blade trembled faintly in his hand, like a laughing third party. Ferrus, of course, refused and stormed out.

In that instant, Fulgrim realised what was controlling him. He was seized by immense terror and remorse. He even threw the sword away. But the daemon whispered in his ear: you can't go back. You've already betrayed the Emperor in your heart. Ferrus won't forgive you. The Emperor never will. Your only way out is forward. Fulgrim picked the sword back up. It was the last moment he had a chance to turn back.

### Isstvan V: The Killing of a Friend

The Isstvan V Dropsite Massacre was the decisive turning point of the Horus Heresy. Four loyalist Legions—the Iron Hands, the Salamanders, the Raven Guard—deployed to the planet's surface to encircle and destroy the traitors. What they did not know was that the four Legions originally pledged as reinforcements—the Emperor's Children, the Iron Warriors, the Night Lords, the Alpha Legion—had already drawn their knives behind their backs.

Fulgrim faced Ferrus directly. Lava churned beneath their feet. Iron Hands and Emperor's Children slaughtered one another on all sides. Ferrus's cold, hard face held no hesitation—he meant to kill Fulgrim. Fulgrim knew the sword was controlling him, yet his body no longer fully obeyed his will. When the blade pierced Ferrus's chest, the daemon within the sword trembled with ecstasy. The tip burst through Ferrus's back, the Primarch of the Iron Hands' blood spattering across Fulgrim's face.

He had killed his closest brother with his own hands. Ferrus's head was presented to Horus. From that moment on, Fulgrim understood the method of "release": stop thinking. Stop hurting. Sink into it. He began chasing anything that could drown out remorse—violent sensory extremes, unspeakable pleasures, the ultimate beauty wedded to the ultimate cruelty. This was what Slaanesh was for.

The Emperor's Children walked this path further than any other traitor Legion. Their warships' interiors were remade into sensory labyrinths—living flesh upon the walls, the wails of captives echoing through corridors alongside manufactured music, warriors activating their nerves with hallucinogens and pain before battle. The Legion that once pursued perfection now pursued what they called "ultimate experience." The line between perfection and madness was invisible until you had already crossed it.

### Ascension and the Poison Blade

In the late Horus Heresy, Fulgrim's fall reached a critical threshold. Slaanesh elevated him to Daemon Prince—he was the first Primarch to reach this "state." Ascension meant he was no longer a mortal Primarch, but a daemonic entity reshaped by Warp-power and Slaanesh's will. He gained a serpentine lower body, multiple arms, superhuman senses—and eternity.

Not long after the Heresy's end, Fulgrim was dispatched to deal with Roboute Guilliman, who was racing back to Ultramar from the direction of Terra with an entire fleet. In orbit over Macragge, Fulgrim descended in his Daemon Primarch form. Guilliman's swordsmanship was textbook precision, but Fulgrim's swordsmanship had become a living nightmare poem. Guilliman struck him many times, but a daemon's body cares nothing for wounds. In the end, Fulgrim's poisoned blade slipped through the gap in Guilliman's gorget—the toxin on the blade had been personally concocted by Slaanesh, a blend of Warp corruption and Fulgrim's own venomous spite. Guilliman's throat would not heal. The Ultramarines' Apothecaries had no choice but to seal him into a stasis field at the last moment: a wound eternally frozen at the instant just before death.

For ten thousand years.

## Fate & Present Status

Fulgrim now rules as a Daemon Prince beneath Slaanesh over a pleasure planet deep within the Warp. There, everything he ever imagined—every beauty, every sound, every texture—exists in endless, inexhaustible abundance. He glides through labyrinths of pleasure, chasing a hunger that can never be filled. Every "gift" Slaanesh bestows upon him takes him one step further from the man who once painted murals for Chemos.

But one thought remains locked away in the deepest recesses: in many ancient records, Fulgrim once briefly regained clarity from the grip of Chaos. For a few seconds, he saw what he had become, saw Ferrus's blood still on his hands, saw his Legion transformed into a horde of living nightmares. And then he chose to close his eyes again. The truth was too painful. He would rather return to pleasure.

> Sanity is for the weak.

Chaos's creed was, for Fulgrim, not a slogan but a lifeline. Sanity meant confronting Ferrus's blood moment after moment, facing what he had become. Chaos gave him another option—don't wake up.

Certain heretical Imperial chroniclers—those quickly immolated by the Inquisition—have speculated that somewhere in the depths of Fulgrim's soul, his true self is still imprisoned, forever sunken at the bottom of the sensory sea, occasionally sending up a muffled cry for help. But no one can hear it, and no one will come.

## Verdict

Among all the traitor Primarchs, Fulgrim's fall is the most lamentable—and the simplest. He did not have Angron's righteous rage born of enslavement and betrayal. He did not have Lorgar's collapse of faith after the Emperor publicly humiliated him. He did not have Magnus's tragedy of destroying his father's life's work out of good intentions. All he had was a sword and a thought: I want to become better.

This is the core of everything. Fulgrim never stopped reaching for "better." His perfectionism was a hunger, and hunger must be fed. The sword only helped him find a nearer trough. From improving Chemos's irrigation systems, to studying the firing angles of every rank of riflemen in a battle-line, to chasing the most extreme sensory stimulation—the logic formed a single, continuous, descending spiral. Those who chase perfection fall the easiest, because they are always looking for "more."

A single sword ruined a Primarch. This statement is both true and incomplete. What truly ruined Fulgrim was not the sword, but the thought it reflected back at him: perfection is everything. And once you believe perfection is everything, pleasure can reasonably say to you—I, too, am a kind of perfection.
