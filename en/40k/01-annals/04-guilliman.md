# 04 · Roboute Guilliman

## Overview

Roboute Guilliman, Primarch of the Thirteenth Legion — the Ultramarines, master of the Five Hundred Worlds of Ultramar, author of the Codex Astartes, and Lord Commander of the Imperium. Among the twenty Primarchs, he is the only Loyalist Primarch who was mortally wounded after the Horus Heresy and sealed in stasis for ten millennia — and the only one to be resurrected in the present age and retake the reins of the Imperium. If the Emperor is the soul of the Imperium, Guilliman is the last reason it can still hobble forward in the mortal realm.

## Origin

Guilliman landed on Macragge — a cold but orderly world on the eastern fringe of the Imperium. He was adopted by Consul Conor Guilliman; the name itself meant he was raised from childhood to be a ruler. Macragge's society was modeled on a Roman-style republic — consuls, a senate, a tradition of law. All of this deeply shaped Guilliman's mind.

He was not the sort of Primarch who grew up wrestling beasts in the wilderness. His childhood was paperwork, tactical simulations, and senatorial debate. This early political training gave him, even as a youth, an astonishing gift for governance. When a noble rebellion erupted on Macragge, the not-yet-adult Guilliman personally led troops to crush it, settled everything in a single battle, and immediately began extending Macragge's institutions to neighboring star systems as Conor's heir. This was the embryo of the Five Hundred Worlds.

When the Emperor found him, Guilliman was already a mature consul, not a lost boy waiting to be "discovered." This set the tone of their relationship: respect without intimacy, acknowledgment without fervor.

## Deeds

### The Great Crusade: The Imperium's Most Successful Conqueror

Of the twenty Space Marine Legions, the Ultramarines' record of conquest was the most impressive — not because they fought best, but because they governed best. Guilliman's principle was simple: conquer a world, then build an administrative apparatus on it, lay supply lines, train local militias, and only then move on to the next. Other Legions conquered a world and left; what they left behind was often rebellion or ruin. Worlds conquered by the Ultramarines would, within decades, begin supplying troops and materiel back to the Crusade.

By the time he took stock, the Ultramarines had conquered over five hundred worlds — a figure far surpassing any other Legion. Ultramar, the autonomous stellar domain of those five hundred worlds, was the one place in the Great Crusade where the "Imperial ideal" was genuinely realized: orderly, prosperous, literate, capable of infrastructure. The bitter irony is that, in the ten thousand years after the Horus Heresy, this "ideal Imperium" was the galaxy's exception, not its norm.

### The Horus Heresy: Loyalty Too Late

When the Horus Heresy broke out, Guilliman's fleets were far away on the Imperium's eastern frontier. The Warmaster Horus Lupercal knew full well how great a threat Guilliman posed — once the war machine of five hundred worlds got rolling, the strategic calculus of the entire rebellion would be overturned. So he did something highly effective: using Warp Storms and communication blackouts, he ensured Guilliman never received Terra's distress calls.

By the time Guilliman realized what was happening, assembled the Ultramarines fleet, and raced at full speed toward Terra, the final battle between the Emperor and Horus was already over. En route, he watched the distant shudder of the Astronomican. He did not make it.

This was the wound Guilliman's life could never close — a man for whom planning and order were the central creed, absent at the most important moment. He will forever ask himself: if I had arrived a day earlier, would Sanguinius not have died? Would the Emperor never have been placed upon the Golden Throne?

### The Codex Astartes: Dismantling His Own Empire

After the Horus Heresy, the Imperium was a ruin. The Emperor lay at death's door. The nine Loyalist Legions were decimated, and the remaining Traitor forces still lurked hungrily at the galaxy's edge. If another Legion-level rebellion broke out now, human civilization would be finished.

Guilliman wrote the Codex Astartes. It is not a tactical manual — it is a constitution. Its core provision: split all nine Loyalist Legions into a thousand independent Chapters in a Second Founding, each Chapter limited to a thousand Space Marines, with no unified chain of command between them. Any Chapter Master who turned renegade could only mobilize severely limited force and could never replicate a Horus-scale Legion rebellion.

This decision meant Guilliman personally dismantled the Legion-empire he had built with his own hands. He carved the Ultramarines from a super-Legion of a quarter-million warriors into dozens of Chapters with no hierarchy among them, retaining only the Ultramarines parent Chapter for himself. Of all the Primarchs, only he could have done this — because he genuinely understood what "checks on power" meant. No other Primarch had the mind for it, or the stomach.

Yet this same decision caused countless Chapter wars and warlord fiefdoms across ten millennia. The Codex is both shackle and fuse. Guilliman knew the cost. He chose to pay it.

### Wounded and Entombed

Not long after the Horus Heresy's end, Guilliman fought the Slaaneshi Daemon Primarch Fulgrim on his homeworld of Macragge. Fulgrim's poisoned blade pierced his throat — the toxin on that blade was a fusion of Warp corruption and Fulgrim's personal malevolence. The Apothecaries of the Ultramarines exhausted every means to neutralize it, and in the end could only seal him within a stasis field: the wound was frozen at the instant "just about to kill him," and so he hung between life and death forever.

For ten thousand years.

During this age, Guilliman's stasis casket became a sacred relic of the Ultramarines Chapter. Pilgrims, Chapter Masters, and Magi of the Adeptus Mechanicus came in endless procession to pay homage. Some believed he was slowly healing; others were certain he was already dead — the wound no longer worsened, but neither did it improve. The Codex contained no instructions for resurrecting a Primarch, so no one knew what to do.

### Resurrection and the Indomitus Crusade

At the end of M41, the galaxy transformed. Abaddon launched the Thirteenth Black Crusade, destroyed the Cadian Gate, and the Eye of Terror erupted into the Great Rift, tearing the Imperium in two. In the Imperium's most desperate hour, two unlikely powers joined forces: Yvraine, leader of the Aeldari Ynnari, and Archmagos Belisarius Cawl of the Adeptus Mechanicus.

What Yvraine brought was not simple goodwill but the Ynnari's own calculus of survival — the followers of the newly awakened Aeldari death god Ynnead needed a powerful ally against Chaos. What Cawl brought was the fruit of a secret project Guilliman himself had commissioned ten thousand years earlier: the Armour of Fate, capable of simultaneously suppressing the toxin and sustaining life, and the complete technology suite for a new generation of Primaris Space Marines. The two powers together finally succeeded in M42 — Guilliman was awakened from stasis.

The first thing the resurrected Guilliman did was not celebrate. Donning the Armour of Fate and wielding the Emperor's Sword, he launched the Indomitus Crusade — leading mass reinforcements of Primaris Space Marines to every corner of the galaxy, saving Baal from the Leviathan Hive Fleet of the Tyranids, rebuilding defensive lines in the Imperium Nihilus, and dragging the Imperium one step back from the brink of collapse.

Returning to Terra, he entered the Golden Throne chamber and held a "conversation" with the father who had sat there for ten thousand years — the content of which no one knows. He came out silent and cold. The Emperor was no longer the father who had pursued rationality and the Imperial Truth, but a cold psychic entity whom the Ecclesiarchy had worshipped as a god for ten millennia. He did not recognize Guilliman. Or he recognized him and did not care.

Guilliman took the mantle of Imperial Regent. He began cleaning the corruption out of the Imperial bureaucracy, fencing with Inquisitorial fanatics, waging a war of attrition with the Ecclesiarchy — while at the same time Mortarion led the hosts of Nurgle in an invasion of Ultramar, launching the Plague Wars. At the battle's critical moment, the Emperor's power descended through the form of a Living Saint, searing Mortarion and driving him back. Guilliman felt no gratitude. He only grew colder: the Emperor had the power, but chose to unleash it only at that moment.

## Fate & Present Status

Today Guilliman is one of two Lord Regents of the Imperium — the other being Lion El'Jonson, Primarch of the Dark Angels, awakened from his own ten-thousand-year slumber. The two govern the Imperium together, with distinct mandates: Guilliman handles administration and diplomacy; Lion oversees military reconstruction in the Imperium Nihilus.

His daily existence is a trudge through paperwork, intelligence reports, battlefield dispatches, religious disputes, and the power games of the High Lords. He tries to promote reason, standards, and rule of law — but the Imperium is a machine that has been rusting for ten thousand years. Mend one gear, and three other parts crack. He knows this with perfect clarity. He does not stop.

## Verdict

Of all the Primarchs, Guilliman is not the mightiest warrior, not the quickest swordsman, not the cleverest psyker, not the finest battlefield general. He is a "normal man" — and in a mad universe, being a normal man is the maddest persistence of all.

He believes institutions matter more than individuals. He believes calculation is more reliable than intuition. He believes five hundred orderly worlds will outlast the grace of a god. His tragedy is that he tried to constrain with institutions an Imperium that was itself built on sacrifice, superstition, and fear — a brutal machine. He is derided as the "bureaucrat Primarch," the "dull accountant." But every page of the Codex he wrote, every administrative order he signed, did more to keep the Imperium alive than the most brilliant military victory of any other Primarch.

He woke up. He found that the truth he believed in had become a religion, the institutions he built had become dogma, the humanity he protected had regressed into a species he no longer recognized. He did not flee. He did not kneel and pray. He picked up his pen and kept processing files.

> Victory needs no explanation. Defeat allows none.

Guilliman knows better than anyone: the Imperium has no time to discuss his personal feelings. The only metric that matters is who is still standing when it is all over.

This is Guilliman — the Imperium's last rational civil servant.
