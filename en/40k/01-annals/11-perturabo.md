# 11 · Perturabo

## Overview

Perturabo, Primarch of the IV Legion, the Iron Warriors, and of all the Emperor's sons the greatest siege engineer and the heaviest of heart. He was a genius architect and tactician, capable of seeing the weakest point in any structure—a fortress wall, an army formation, even a person's expression—in an instant. But this gift was also his curse: he was too good at seeing flaws, so he could never find anything that was truly perfect. He despised brothers of lesser ability receiving glory. He resented always being sent to do the dirtiest work. His loyalty to the Emperor ultimately crumbled to dust in the face of his own guilt over destroying his home world with his own hands. During the Horus Heresy, he was placed in command of the Siege of Terra, a siege that came within a hair's breadth of bringing the Imperium down. After the war, he became a Daemon Prince of Chaos Undivided, refusing to align with any one god, shutting himself away to continue building his fortress.

## Origin

Perturabo landed on a world called Olympia, a cold, hard world of power struggles among mountain city-states. He was adopted by the local tyrant, Dammekos.

From a very young age, he knew he was different. When other children looked at a castle, they saw stone and towers; he saw that the load-bearing wall between the third and fourth levels was angled wrong by three and a half degrees, and a single cannon shot to that point would bring the entire west wing down. He looked at a cliff and saw which fissure was the true structural weakness. He watched a man swing a blade and knew how much and for how long the elbow would be exposed on the next strike.

This gift—which he later called the "Eye of All Things"—made him the most formidable military mind and architect in Olympia's history. He built palaces for Dammekos that even the Emperor would admire, and designed Olympia's defence systems to be unassailable. But Olympia gave him no friends. His adoptive father, Dammekos, saw him as an asset, not a son; Olympia's nobles feared his abilities but shunned him for his grim disposition. No one ever praised him. No one ever loved him. He responded to everything with blueprints and data—because that was the only language he trusted.

On the day the Emperor descended upon Olympia, Perturabo knelt. But in that kneeling, he believed no more than he had before. He simply obeyed logic: the man before him was stronger, the galaxy needed unification, and an architect's level was determined by the scale of his projects. He served the Emperor as a project worthy of his talents—not because he accepted this man as his father.

## Deeds

### The IV Legion: Iron Warriors

After taking command of the IV Legion, Perturabo moulded the force into the coldest and most efficient war machine in the entire Adeptus Astartes. The Iron Warriors specialised in one kind of war: siege warfare. Head-on grinding advance, meat-grinder attrition, wave after wave of infantry crashing against the walls while artillery bombarded fortifications day and night without pause, until the enemy collapsed both psychologically and materially. The hardest nuts in the galaxy to crack—worlds other Legions could not take after months or even years of grinding—fell to the Iron Warriors like a badly designed tower being dismantled.

But this was precisely the problem. Siege warfare was the least glorious kind of victory in the Great Crusade. No glorious charges, no evenly matched duels, no heroic tales to be sung. Only weeks or months of attrition, long-range bombardment, whole brigades of infantry reduced to numbers beneath the walls. The Iron Warriors were the hardest hammer in the Imperium's arsenal, but no one sings ballads about a hammer.

This planted the first splinter in Perturabo's heart. He felt the Emperor was using him—not cultivating him. Other brothers won glorious conquests, earned titles, honours, the praise of the Imperium; he and his men were forever in some forgotten war zone, drowning equally forgotten enemies in blood and gunpowder. He watched Rogal Dorn—his mirror-image brother—summoned back to Terra by the Emperor to build the Imperial Palace. On what grounds? Dorn's entire method was making walls four times thicker and calling himself a master architect, while Perturabo could build things a thousand times more exquisite. But the Emperor never asked him.

Perhaps this was the one thing he truly wanted his entire life: to be asked, just once.

### The Olympian Rebellion

Late in the Great Crusade, a rebellion erupted on Perturabo's home world of Olympia. The news reached him while he was off-world, prosecuting a siege. His reaction was not concern, not rage—it was a kind of cold, dead calm. He turned the fleet around, returned to Olympia, and washed the entire planet off the map in blood.

The Emperor's most loyal siege engine committed the Emperor's least loyal act before the Horus Heresy even began. The destruction of Olympia was not a declaration of treason—it was a fracture. Perturabo had not premeditated betrayal. He had simply destroyed the world that raised him, and then found he had no road left. He had personally killed everything he had reason to protect. Report to the Emperor? What would the Emperor think of him—a Primarch who couldn't even control his own home world? A builder who had exposed himself as a destroyer?

Horus saw through this fracture. The Warmaster did not need to buy Perturabo; he only needed to say one sentence: come with me, and I will give you what you deserve. Perturabo joined the rebellion—not for Chaos, not out of hatred for the Emperor, but because he had already destroyed his only way back, and the traitor camp was the only door still open.

### The Siege of Terra

In the Horus Heresy, Perturabo was assigned the mission he had spent his entire life preparing for: command of the Siege of Terra. The Imperial Palace—built by his nemesis Rogal Dorn—was the mightiest defensive system in ten thousand years. And Perturabo was the galaxy's foremost expert on dismantling mighty defensive systems.

The siege was a slow, expressionless slaughter. Perturabo deployed his Eye of All Things to analyse every crack in Terra's defensive network, piece by piece, designing an assault plan precise to the hour. He calculated which sector's artillery support would run out of ammunition first, which section of wall had a supply-line junction that could be severed with ease, at which consecutive hour of which night the garrison of which tower would begin to exhibit delayed neurological response. Under siege, Terra's walls collapsed tier by tier. The blood of the Adeptus Custodes, the Imperial Fists, the entire loyalist host, soaked every tile outside the Imperial Palace.

He came so close to winning. The last gate of the Imperial Palace—the Eternity Gate—failed to fall only because Horus and other Primarchs interfered with his command. Perturabo would later insist, again and again: had he been left in full command throughout, Terra would have fallen. No one can prove him wrong.

### After the War

After Horus's death, Perturabo did not flee like the remnants of the other traitor Legions. During his withdrawal, he laid a trap—the Iron Cage. This was a labyrinthine fortress he had specially built, appearing to be a disorganised retreat but in truth an inescapable killing ground, with a single purpose: to lure Rogal Dorn and the Imperial Fists in, and then slowly kill them.

Dorn walked in. With that self-righteous conviction Perturabo loathed most of all. The Imperial Fists were eaten battalion by battalion inside the Iron Cage. Blood ran in rivers. But Dorn refused to withdraw—for many reasons, one of which was guilt. The death of the Emperor was a bone-gouging agony for every loyal Primarch, but Dorn carried it heaviest of all. The Iron Cage was not Dorn's revenge battle; it was his atonement suicide.

In the end, Roboute Guilliman arrived and forcibly dragged Dorn out.

Perturabo watched the remnants of the Imperial Fists withdraw, saying nothing. He had won—but it may have been the most joyless victory he ever won. What kind of victory is butchering a man who already wanted to die?

## Fate & Present Status

Perturabo ascended as a Daemon Prince within the Eye of Terror—but he refused to bend his knee to any of the four Chaos Gods. He knelt to neither Khorne, nor Tzeentch, nor Nurgle, nor Slaanesh. He found himself a planet—an iron fortress near Medusa, the home world of his dead brother Ferrus Manus—and remade it into an unmatched daemon-fortress. For ten thousand years, he has shut his gates and done what he has always done: design, build, refine. The interior complexity of that fortress is said to be such that any invading force would be trapped inside until they died of despair.

The Iron Warriors, across the long millennia, have maintained the cold efficiency he instilled in them. They are the most like a Legion of all the traitor Legions—no religious rites, no daemonic dances, no conspiratorial scheming. Haul the siege guns forward. Open fire. Log the casualties. Replace the numbers. Keep going. From the Great Crusade to the present day, the method has not changed. What has changed, perhaps, is Perturabo himself—after ten thousand years of solitude, remorse, resentment, and self-dissection, he is no longer the youth who stood disoriented among the ruins of Olympia. He has become a fortress. A cold, hard fortress without a door.

## Verdict

Perturabo may be the most misunderstood of all the traitor Primarchs.

Everyone says he was difficult to get along with. True—he was sensitive, arrogant, paranoid, incapable of expressing his needs, his face forever clouded. He was not charming like Fulgrim, could not smile like Horus, could not make everyone love him from the heart like Sanguinius. His brothers thought him an oddball, a resentful workaholic, and even the Chaos Gods, after his fall, couldn't be bothered to compete for him.

But who would want to be the one forever sent to fight the worst battles?

This is the cruelest contradiction in Perturabo: the thing he was best at was destruction. But destruction was the thing he hated doing most. He wanted to build—palaces, theatres, monuments, things that made people understand at a glance: this is a work of art. The Emperor turned him into the Imperium's sharpest battering ram, yet never let him design or build anything that was even symbolically a "beautiful thing." His hatred of Dorn was, at root, envy—Dorn was permitted to build, while he was forever nailed to the role of destroyer. Dorn built an eternal city. Perturabo tore the same city down. Who gets remembered?

The Imperium had no use for him after the Great Crusade ended. Ten thousand years later, he is still building that fortress of his—perhaps it is no longer just a fortress. Perhaps, to him, that fortress exists to prove one thing: even if the whole galaxy believes he can only tear things down, he can still build something. Even if no one ever comes to see it.
