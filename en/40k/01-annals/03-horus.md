# 03 · Horus Lupercal

## Overview

The Emperor's most favored son. Primarch of the Sixteenth Legion. Elevated to Warmaster, supreme commander of the whole Great Crusade. Corrupted by the Chaos Gods, he launched the bloodiest civil war the Imperium has ever known — the Horus Heresy — and was ultimately erased from all existence by the Emperor's own hand above Terra.

## Origin

Horus was one of the twenty Primarchs created by the Emperor. On the day the Chaos Gods snatched the infant Primarchs from the Terran laboratories and scattered them across the galaxy, Horus landed on Cthonia — a mining world closer to Terra than any other. Beneath Cthonia's surface, a violent gang culture ruled the mine-pits, and Horus grew up in this environment, rising from a street-gang child to a leader of men.

He was the first Primarch the Emperor recovered. That "first" meant everything: of all the brothers, Horus fought at the Emperor's side the longest. He followed the Emperor and learned war, politics, and statecraft, watching as the Emperor recovered the other Primarchs one by one, watching as the Imperium grew mightier day by day. From the mine-pits of Cthonia to the brightest star at the Emperor's side — it took Horus thirty years. The Emperor's trust in him was not given lightly. Horus was, in truth, the one most like him.

## Deeds

### The Great Crusade: The Luna Wolves

The Sixteenth Legion's early name was the Luna Wolves, earned for the honors it won storming Terra's moon Luna during the Unification Wars. After Horus rejoined them, the Legion rapidly became the most decorated fighting force of the Great Crusade. The Luna Wolves' style was ferocious and direct — decapitate the enemy's leadership with overwhelming shock assault, then use the Primarch's own extraordinary charisma to bring the remaining worlds to heel. Horus was no specialist genius in one type of warfare; he was a genius of integration — infantry, armor, fleet warfare, diplomacy, psychology — he was top-tier in all of them. On the map of the Great Crusade, no one had conquered more territory than the Luna Wolves.

The Emperor later renamed the Legion the Sons of Horus — an honor unique among all Legions. Most Legions bore symbolic names; only Horus's Legion was named for its Primarch himself.

### Warmaster: The Emperor's Proxy

In the closing phase of the Great Crusade, the Emperor resolved to return to Terra to conduct a secret project — what would later be understood as the Webway Project. He needed someone to continue commanding the entire Great Crusade in his stead: twenty Legions, billions of soldiers, a battlefront spanning half the galaxy. He chose Horus.

The Emperor held a grand triumph on Ullanor, and before the assembled hosts publicly bestowed the title of Warmaster upon Horus. From that moment, Horus was no longer simply the Primarch of the Sixteenth Legion — he was the Emperor's proxy, supreme commander of all human forces in the galaxy, the superior of the other Primarchs. The arrangement seemed reasonable at the time: Horus was the first brother recovered, the most decorated, closest to the Emperor, and everyone — at the time — respected him. It is said that Horus himself once remarked: "If Sanguinius had been made Warmaster, I would not have rebelled." That sentence would be quoted again and again through the ages, the most bitterly ironic footnote of the entire Heresy.

### Corruption: The Wound on Davin

The Horus Heresy did not begin on a battlefield. It began on a world called Davin.

During the campaign, Horus was wounded by the xenos-corrupted blade called the Anathame. The wound itself was not fatal, but the Chaotic essence on the blade began to spread through his blood. Erebus, First Chaplain of the Seventeenth Legion — the Word Bearers — and the hidden architect of the entire Horus Heresy, approached the dying Horus under the pretense of healing. Using the wound as a conduit, he drew Horus's soul into a Chaotic vision within the Warp.

In that vision, the Chaos Gods showed Horus a twisted vista of the future: the Emperor made divine, while the Primarchs were forgotten in the dust of history, none remembering their names or deeds. Chaos did not directly command him to rebel. It planted a seed — doubt. Why had the Emperor returned to Terra? What was he doing there? Had he never intended to let the Primarchs share the Imperium's future? Once these thoughts took root, they grew wild inside Horus. When he awoke, he was no longer the Horus who had fallen.

### The Horus Heresy: From Brother to Bitter Foe

Horus's first act of rebellion was ice-cold: he ordered the purge of every Loyalist in his own Legion on Isstvaan III. Virus-bombs incinerated the planet, and the Traitor fleet bombarded from orbit anyone who tried to escape. He fired the first shot against his own brothers — beginning by killing his own men, to leave no loose ends behind.

Then came the Drop Site Massacre at Isstvaan V. The Emperor dispatched seven Loyalist Legions to suppress the revolt, but Horus had already orchestrated his trap. The four Legions ordered to reinforce the Loyalists — the Emperor's Children, the Iron Warriors, the Night Lords, and the Alpha Legion — turned mid-battle and struck the Loyalist coalition from behind in a devastating ambush. Ferrus Manus, Primarch of the Iron Hands, died on the field; his severed head was presented to Horus. The Salamanders were nearly annihilated; the Raven Guard shattered and scattered. It was the most savage military betrayal in Imperial history — in a single stroke, three of the Loyalists' core fighting forces were cut down.

For seven years thereafter, Horus led the nine Traitor Legions driving toward Terra.

> Let the galaxy burn!

This was the signature war cry of Horus's rebellion, the ultimate sentence the forces of Chaos pronounced upon the Imperium. With every world he took, Chaos tightened its hold on him. The four Chaos Gods personally poured their power into him; his body swelled far beyond the stature of an ordinary Primarch, and the light in his eyes transformed from the warm gold of the old days into the eldritch flame of Chaos. He was no longer the boy who had grown up in Cthonia's mine-pits, no longer the general who had learned war at the Emperor's side. He was the Warmaster of Chaos, clad in twisted power armor, bearing a warhammer that could slay gods, and behind him the gathered malevolence of the entire Warp.

The Siege of Terra was the end of the Horus Heresy. Horus deliberately let the Emperor teleport aboard his flagship, the Vengeful Spirit. He wanted to end it face to face.

### The Final Battle: Father and Son upon the Vengeful Spirit

On the bridge of the Vengeful Spirit, Sanguinius — Primarch of the Ninth Legion, the Emperor's most beloved "Angel," and the brother Horus had once respected above all others — placed himself before the Emperor first. Horus slew him with his own hand. That blow cut down not only Sanguinius's life, but the last trace of humanity remaining within Horus.

The Emperor looked upon his most beloved son killing another of his most beloved sons. He finally accepted that Horus was beyond redemption. Unleashing the full measure of his psychic power, he obliterated Horus from every level of existence — not killing, but erasing. Horus's body, his soul, every trace of him within the Warp — all were burned away, utterly and forever. The Chaos Gods withdrew their blessings at the last instant, for before the Emperor's blow, no Dark God wished to be caught in the blast.

## Fate & Present Status

Horus Lupercal is utterly dead. Not slain. Not missing. Not sealed in stasis awaiting resurrection. He was erased from the universe. The Chaos Gods staked everything on him, and when the bet was lost, they discarded him. The Emperor, after delivering that blow, was left mortally wounded and installed upon the Golden Throne — suspended between life and death ever since, ruling in this state for ten thousand years.

Horus's Legion was hunted by the Loyalists after his death. Its remnants reorganized within the Eye of Terror into the Black Legion, taken over by his former lieutenant Abaddon. But Abaddon refused to carry the mantle of "Sons of Horus" — he would be his own Warmaster, not a second Horus.

## Verdict

Was Horus a puppet of Chaos, or did he make his own choice?

There is no clean answer. Seen from without, he was indeed Chaos's meticulously chosen prey — from the corrupted blade on Davin, to Erebus's deception, to the doubt planted in the vision, the entire process was as precise as a surgical operation. Chaos spent decades laying the trap, all to drag this one Primarch into the abyss.

But saying Horus was "corrupted" — and leaving it at that — lets him off too easily. The Emperor secretly returned to Terra for the Webway Project and told his Primarch sons nothing. He appointed a Warmaster without explaining what he himself intended to do. He handed Horus the entire future of the Imperium and gave him not a single word of "why" — these are the Emperor's failures, not fabrications of Chaos. The doubt and anger in Horus's heart grew from his own soil. Chaos merely watered them.

Perhaps the most accurate description is this: Chaos handed him a knife, and he chose to plunge it in.

This also makes the relationship between the Emperor and Horus the most chilling father-son tragedy in the Warhammer universe. The Emperor's most beloved son was Horus. The son the Emperor destroyed with his own hands was also Horus. The Emperor hesitated a long time before delivering that blow — not because Horus was too strong to defeat, but because he could not bear to admit that the person he trusted most had become the thing he loathed most. The death of Sanguinius forced his hand. The Angel's blood bought the Emperor's final resolve.

Horus is a man destroyed by his finest qualities — ambition, confidence, and a hunger for glory. What Chaos excels at is not manufacturing evil, but twisting virtue. Horus's fall did not begin with "becoming evil." It began with the feeling of being unvalued. He did not die on a battlefield. He died inside his own heart, in a doubt someone else had planted and he himself had dug deeper.
