# 14 · Jaghatai Khan

## Overview

Jaghatai Khan, Primarch of the V Legion, the White Scars, and of all the Emperor's sons the fastest, the least governed, and the hardest to categorise. He grew up on the vast steppes of Chogoris and believed in two things his entire life: speed and freedom. During the Great Crusade, he was famed across the galaxy for lightning warfare and high-speed manoeuvre. During the Horus Heresy, both Horus and the Emperor scrambled to win him over—and he chose the Emperor, not out of blind obedience, not out of fear, but because he had thought his way to one clear conclusion: the Emperor represented order, while Chaos was pure annihilation. After the war, he pursued the Dark Eldar into the Webway, hunting them there to this day. The White Scars have kept the Khan's seat empty for ten thousand years—they believe he is still riding through the Webway, and will eventually ride back.

## Origin

Chogoris is a world ruled by wind. No oceans, no forests, no cities—only endless steppe, and nomadic tribes who gallop across it chasing the horizon. In Imperial records it is called Mundus Planus, but nobody uses that name. It is called Chogoris—the Storm Land.

Jaghatai was found on this steppe by a tribal chieftain. A baby fallen from the sky, landed in the middle of a herd of horses, not crying, not fussing, just staring up at the sky with open eyes. The chieftain took him as his adopted son and named him Jaghatai—in the tribal tongue, roughly meaning "the Swift Wind."

He grew up on horseback. Other Primarchs learned strategy in laboratories; he learned on the steppe to read the movement of clouds and the shift of winds. Other Primarchs were trained by their adoptive fathers to be generals and statesmen; he was trained to be a hunter and a rider. The tribes of Chogoris warred constantly over water sources, grazing lands, and honour, and Jaghatai watched these wars from childhood and fought in them too. He rode faster than anyone, shot truer than anyone, but the thing that earned him the most awe from the tribes was the question he always asked before drawing his blade: "The steppe is vast enough. Why can we not ride together?"

By the time he reached adulthood, he did not unify Chogoris with an iron fist—he launched a Great Hunt that spanned the entire continent. At each place he reached, he challenged the local khan to contests: horse-racing, archery, wrestling, debate. He won almost every time, but he never humiliated those he defeated. He would stay to drink and sing the old songs of the steppe. By the time his horse's hooves had touched every blade of grass on the continent, Chogoris was unified—not through conquest, but through being ridden into unity.

When the Emperor descended upon Chogoris, Jaghatai was among the calmest of all Primarchs regarding the matter of "you are my son"—but his calm contained something other Primarchs struggled to understand. He submitted, but he did not surrender himself. He said to the Emperor: I can fight beneath your banner, but my people must wage war in our own way. The Emperor agreed. In the entire Great Crusade, no other Legion preserved such an intact native culture as the White Scars: their armour bore the totems of Chogoris, their tactical councils began with fermented mare's milk before discussing deployments, and their battle-cry was not "For the Emperor"—it was "For the Khan and the steppe."

## Deeds

### The White Scars: A Legion Like the Wind

The first thing Jaghatai did after taking command of the V Legion was to strike every slow thing—heavy tanks, siege artillery, fortress defence manuals—from the order of battle. The White Scars rebuilt their entire tactical doctrine around speed and mobility. Their signature equipment was not Terminator Armour, not the Land Raider, but the Jetbike and the Storm Eagle. They swept across the battlefield like steppe cavalry, struck, and vanished, then thirty seconds later rolled back in from an entirely different direction. The Imperium's standard tactical doctrine was: deploy, advance, hold, advance again. The White Scars' logic was: let the enemy never know where you are—by the time they figure it out, you have already torn through them.

During the Great Crusade, the White Scars were tasked with the war zones that most demanded speed. Other Legions spent six months besieging a single planet; Jaghatai took three systems in three days. He did not care about occupation—that was the Ultramarines' and the Imperial Fists' affair. What he wanted was breakthrough. In his own words: "I have no desire to conquer the steppe. I only wish to ride across it. Conquest is merely a byproduct."

The White Scars transplanted Chogoris's nomadic culture to a galactic scale without modification. They built no fortress-monasteries on the worlds they conquered, considering such things cages—the fleet was the steppe, and planets were merely grazing stops along the way. They composed poetry—real poetry, using Chogoris's ancient metres to describe interstellar war and Warp storms. Before battle, they did not pray. They sang.

In the eyes of the other Primarchs, Jaghatai was an outsider. He disliked attending his brothers' councils, aligned with no faction, delivered no grand speeches about the future of the Imperium. Mortarion in particular held him in contempt, considering the White Scars' hit-and-run tactics to be utterly devoid of military discipline—cheap opportunism, barbaric. Jaghatai fired straight back: you are the hypocrite—you claim to rely on nothing but your own will and no external force, yet your body cannot even stand straight without toxins; you've merely grown accustomed to an external dependency you lack the courage to admit. The two despised each other for a lifetime, but also never came to actual blows—because both knew the other was not entirely wrong.

Among all his brothers, Jaghatai was close to only one: Magnus the Red. A steppe rider and a psychic scholar—on the surface, wholly incompatible. But Magnus and Jaghatai shared one thing: curiosity. Magnus wanted to understand the secrets of the Warp; Jaghatai wanted to know what lay beyond the steppe. The two would often sit and talk for hours between campaigns—poetry, philosophy, the nature of the universe. This friendship became a lethal variable during the Horus Heresy: when Magnus's Thousand Sons were officially declared traitors by the Emperor, Jaghatai was the only loyalist Primarch who did not immediately accept the verdict.

### The Horus Heresy: Standing at the Crossroads

When the Horus Heresy broke out, Jaghatai and his fleet were prosecuting a campaign on the most remote edge of the Imperium. Communications were severed, intelligence was fragmentary—all he knew was that the Imperium was fighting a civil war, and everyone was pressuring him to pick a side.

The first to contact him was Horus. Ciphered channels from the traitors bombarded his fleet repeatedly, and the terms were tempting: join the rebellion, and you will have far more freedom than you ever had under the Emperor—no more decrees from Terra, no more bureaucratic dogma, no longer an eagle in a cage. Jaghatai did not answer immediately. He was not the kind of man who signed a contract just because he had heard the terms.

But the signals from the loyalist side unsettled him even more than the traitors' did. Rogal Dorn, in the Emperor's name, ordered him to bring his entire Legion back to Terra immediately and submit to unified Imperial command—no explanation, no dialogue, just an order. This tone was what Jaghatai loathed most of all. He decided to investigate the truth himself.

First, he diverted his fleet to Prospero—Magnus's home world. He wanted to see with his own eyes whether his friend had truly turned traitor. What he found was a wasteland burned to black charcoal. Leman Russ's Space Wolves had already razed the place flat. No one could tell him whether Magnus had deserved his fate or had been forced into rebellion—but in the ashes, Jaghatai caught a scent. A scent he did not like. The acrid stench of Imperial justice.

It was at this juncture that Mortarion came for him in person.

Mortarion boarded Jaghatai's flagship as a "brother," attempting to persuade him to turn traitor. The arguments were not new: the Emperor was a hypocrite, mouthing prohibition of god-worship while the things he was building beneath Terra were more like sorcery than any religion; he claimed everything was for humanity, but truly cared only for himself; we were all his tools, to be discarded once the war was over. Some of this, Jaghatai had thought about himself. But the moment Mortarion pushed his argument to the extreme, Jaghatai saw through it: you are not rebelling against a tyrant—you are making excuses for yourself. If you truly believed the Emperor was a tyrant, you would overthrow him and build a better order. But you are turning to Chaos—and Chaos is no liberator. Chaos is pure dissolution, the void of all order and meaning. The difference between you and the Emperor is not "freedom versus oppression." It is that the Emperor at least still believes in order, while you have abandoned even that, and cannot admit it to yourself.

It was this judgement that sealed Jaghatai's final choice. When he refused Mortarion, he did not shout "For the Emperor." He said something colder and more honest: "You have hated your adoptive father your entire life—hated him so much that you used him to define everything you are. Your rebellion is nothing but a protracted adolescent revenge. I am not you."

He chose the Emperor not because the Emperor was perfect—he had seen with his own eyes, in the scorched earth of Prospero, the cold ruthlessness of the Imperium. He chose the Emperor because, on the galactic scale, Chaos is not an "alternative." Chaos is the end of choice. He could not push everyone inside the cage out into the void just because the void lay beyond it.

This would later be passed down through the Legion for ten millennia, distilled into a terse maxim: "The void beyond the cage is not the steppe."

### The Battle of the Lion's Gate

The Siege of Terra was the moment the White Scars truly showed what they were made of.

When the traitors encircled Terra, the Lion's Gate Spaceport fell into Horus's hands. Loyalist reinforcements could not land; the Imperial Palace was doomed to fight alone to the death. Dorn's defensive doctrine was helpless here: the spaceport had been nailed down by the traitors with fleet assets and heavy armour in layer upon layer; a frontal assault was suicide.

Jaghatai proposed a plan to Dorn: let him take a strike force in from the flank, using speed to bypass the frontal defences and go straight for the spaceport's central control tower. Dorn thought this was lunacy—Jaghatai was a Primarch, not an expendable asset to be thrown behind enemy lines. Jaghatai said only one thing: "You have been in the cage too long. You have forgotten how fine a crack the wind can slip through."

He took the White Scars' finest riders, mounted on jetbikes, and raised a storm in the traitors' rear. The objective was not kills—it was disruption. Shatter the traitor command chain. Make them fight themselves. Make them misread everything in the chaos. By the time the defenders inside the spaceport saw the lightning-bolt icon of Jaghatai rising from behind enemy lines, the entire port had been swallowed by the dust storm he had engineered. The Lion's Gate was retaken. The lifeline for loyalist reinforcements was opened again.

It was one of the few reversals in the Siege of Terra—achieved not through defence, not through sacrifice, but through pure speed.

### Pursuit into the Webway

After Horus's death, the great chaos subsided. But Jaghatai did not stay for the victory celebrations, nor take part in the postwar carve-up of power. On the pretext of "hunting down remnant xenos raiders," he took his most elite strike force and sailed towards the Imperium's edge.

Those raiders were the Dark Eldar—Drukhari—pouring out of the Webway, the ancient interstellar tunnels built by the Aeldari, exploiting the Imperium's civil-war weakness to harvest human colonies in droves. Jaghatai caught up with them, cut his way into the Webway, and then—vanished.

No word has come since.

No one knows whether he is lost, dead, or fighting an eternal guerrilla war in some unreachable Webway node. The official Imperial record reads "Missing in Action." But the White Scars do not accept that phrase. For ten thousand years, they have insisted that the Khan still hunts deep within the Webway—not missing, but pursuing. They believe that on the day the Imperium truly needs him, he will ride out of one of the Webway's gates, and the wind behind him will carry the scent of Chogoris's grasslands.

## Fate & Present Status

The White Scars were divided into multiple Chapters during the Second Founding, but every single one has preserved the nomadic traditions of Chogoris in full. They do not believe in fortresses. They do not believe in dogma. They do not believe in bureaucrats who issue decrees while seated on thrones. They believe only in the wind, in speed, and in the brothers at their side. Among all the Space Marine Chapters of the Imperium, they remain to this day the fastest.

Jaghatai Khan's seat has been empty for ten thousand years. The Chapter has elected no replacement. The tribes of Chogoris have an old saying: the Great Khan is not dead. The Great Khan has merely ridden beyond the horizon we can see.

## Verdict

Jaghatai Khan is perhaps, of all the loyalist Primarchs, the one whose loyalty was least "clean."

He was no blind follower. He did not treat the Emperor's will as the sole truth like Rogal Dorn. He did not love his father with a son's devotion like Sanguinius. He had examined the cracks in the Imperial machine with his own eyes, had smelled the acrid stench of Imperial justice in the ashes of Prospero with his own senses. He stood at the crossroads for a long time, weighing everything over and over—not out of weakness, but because he was too clear-eyed. He knew the Emperor was not perfect. He may even have dimly understood that even if Horus were defeated, the galaxy would not become paradise—it would simply not become hell.

He chose the Emperor not because the Emperor was worthy of his loyalty. He chose him because the alternative was worse.

This is Jaghatai's deepest insight. The Imperium of Man is a cage. He knew it. But at least it is a cage—with boundaries, with shape, with bars to hold back the winds of Chaos. And the freedom that Chaos promises is a place where you do not need bars—because there is no ground there at all. He stood at the door of the cage and looked out. What he saw was void upon void. Then he turned around, and pulled the cage door shut himself.

From one angle, he is the loyalist who least resembles a loyalist. From another, he is the loyalist who understood most clearly what he was being loyal to. He did not lie to himself and say he was fighting for a god. He did not lie to himself and say he was fighting for a father. He did not even lie to himself and say he was fighting for humanity. He was fighting to keep the thing outside from getting in. That reason is not heroic. It is not suitable for carving into stone monuments. But it holds.

The herders of Chogoris say that the eagle is the freest creature on the steppe. But even an eagle needs a tree to land on. Jaghatai flew his entire life and, in the end, chose a tree he did not like to alight upon—because every other tree had already been snapped by the wind from outside.
