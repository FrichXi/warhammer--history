# 19 · Leman Russ

## Overview

Leman Russ was the most savage of the Loyalists and the most loyal of the savages. He was the Emperor's Executioner, and all his life he was trapped by his own blade.

## Origin

The place where Leman Russ landed was one of the most hostile worlds to living things in the galaxy. Fenris — an eternally frozen death world of ice, where only a brief season each year melts a sliver of water, and the rest is blizzard, tundra, and man-eating sea-beasts. Every breath was ice-shard; every step could be through cracking ice.

When the Primarch's gestation pod punched into Fenris's permafrost, a she-wolf was the first to reach the crater's edge. The she-wolf did not devour the swaddled infant. Instead, she carried him back to her pack and nursed him on her own milk. Leman Russ's first home was not a palace, not a tribe — it was a wolf-den.

He grew among the wolves into a feral child who could run barefoot through blizzards, whose sense of smell was keener than any hound's, who could scent the fear of an enemy from ten kilometres away. The local Fenrisian tribes eventually discovered him. The tribal king, Thengir, took him as a foster son and gave him the name Leman Russ. But he never truly left the wolves — the grown Russ was always flanked by two giant wolves, Freki and Geri, brothers he had tamed with his own hands in the fang-years.

Russ unified Fenris. He used no negotiation, no parchment — only fists and bloodthirsty howls. He beat every tribe on the planet into submission and declared himself the Wolf King. When the Emperor arrived, Russ did not kneel or welcome him as the other Primarchs had done — he demanded the Emperor fight him. The two are said to have battled for a full day and night. Finally, the Emperor pinned Russ to the ground. Russ grinned: *"Fine. You're my father."*

## Deeds

### The Emperor's Executioner

During the Great Crusade, the Space Wolves carried a role no other Legion wished — or could — assume: the Emperor's Executioner.

What did this title mean? It meant that when another Primarch erred, when a Legion overstepped its bounds, when the Emperor needed one son to discipline another — Russ was the one sent. He led the Space Wolves in sanctioning wayward Legions, overseeing worlds that had crossed the line, and, when necessary, carrying out purges. He was not the Imperium's most powerful Primarch, but he was the one most willing to let himself be dirtied.

To the outside world, Russ always presented the face of an illiterate Viking savage — drinking, roaring, pounding axe-hafts on tables, calling starship corridors "hunting grounds." But anyone who had fought him knew this barbarian's face was a carefully maintained mask. Russ was ferociously intelligent; his tactical instincts were keener than those of most Primarchs. He deliberately played the role of a wild man who "couldn't follow a complex plan," because that way, he could drive the blade home while the enemy was busy underestimating him.

The "courtesy duel" with Lion El'Jonson says everything about his character. The two had once argued during a campaign over who would lead the first charge. Russ proposed they fight it out — whoever won would go first. After three days and three nights of combat, the Lion beat Russ to the ground. Russ lay there laughing. The Lion demanded to know what was funny. Russ said: *"I wasn't arguing about who charges first. I wanted to see whether you, the straight-backed knight, could actually fight."* The Lion stalked off in fury. Russ got up, took the Space Wolves, and charged first anyway. From that day, the two Legions had a standing arrangement: whenever such a situation arose again, fight it out — the victor would let the loser go first. This tradition endured for a hundred years. That is the Space Wolves' idea of "courtesy."

Russ also fought Angron. The Emperor sent him to "discipline" the Primarch of the World Eaters, because Angron had turned every compliance into unrestrained slaughter. Russ brought the Space Wolves and drew Angron into an encirclement — the Butcher's Nails made Angron incapable of tactical thought; he just kept swinging forward. Only when Angron was surrounded on all sides by wolf fangs and bolter muzzles did he realise he had lost. Russ could have said, *"A man like you doesn't deserve to command a Legion."* Instead, he only said: *"You see? You can fight. But you have to learn to win."*

Of course, Angron never learned. The Primarch tortured his whole life by slavers and the Primarch whom no one had ever been able to leash — the gap between them was not tactical proficiency. They did not even inhabit the same universe. But Russ tried. Among all the Primarchs, he was one of the few truly willing to reach out and pull a brother back — even if his method was always a punch.

### The Burning of Prospero

The greatest act of Russ's life was also the act he most bitterly regretted. Its name was the Burning of Prospero.

The affair did not originate with him. Magnus the Red — Primarch of the XV Legion, the Thousand Sons, the crimson cyclops — had, with good intentions, driven a psychic lance through Terra's Warp-warding. He meant to warn the Emperor: Horus had turned traitor. But the Emperor was deep beneath Terra, labouring on the Webway Project, and Magnus's psychic blow shattered the wards. A Warp-incursion swallowed entire levels of the subterranean works. The bodies of Custodians and Adeptus Mechanicus adepts piled into mountains.

The Emperor's fury was cold and still. He commanded Russ to take the Space Wolves to Prospero and *bring Magnus back.* Back to Terra. To stand trial. The Emperor needed his son to return and explain.

But the order passed through Horus's hands in transit. By this point, Horus had long since turned. He did not alter the command's heading, nor its seal — only changed *"bring back"* to *"exterminate."*

Russ received the order and did not question it. He and Magnus had never been on good terms. Magnus was the most powerful psyker in the galaxy; Russ was the man who hated sorcery most in the galaxy — in Fenrisian tradition, spells were unclean things, psychic power the omen of corruption. Russ had always believed Magnus's fire-play would sooner or later burn himself. When the order read *"exterminate,"* he nearly felt it was only natural: *So. Father has finally seen through this witch.*

The Space Wolves fleet blackened the skies of Prospero. Russ did issue an ultimatum — he called Magnus through a channel he believed would relay the message, demanding his surrender. But Magnus either did not receive it, or received it and chose silence. (He saw that his sons were about to be punished, and a titanic guilt had nearly paralysed his will.)

The bombardment began. The golden armour of the Custodians and the soulless null-aura of the Sisters of Silence suppressed the psychic power upon which the Thousand Sons most relied. The Space Wolves' berserk charges poured through breaches in the walls of this city of scholars. Libraries burned; colonnades collapsed; Thousand Sons warriors hurled entire towers at the wolf-packs with their minds, and the wolf-packs carved open the throat of every sorcerer who tried to cast.

Magnus never fought back at full strength. He sat within his pyramid as the city above him burned and the warriors below him died. What was he thinking? Perhaps that he had made a mistake. Perhaps that his father had no right to send a barbarian to pass his death sentence. Perhaps nothing at all — merely broken.

When everything was beyond recall, Magnus struck his bargain with Tzeentch. The remnants of the Thousand Sons were wrenched into a Warp-vortex and cast into Tzeentch's domain.

Russ stood in the ashes of Prospero. He had killed an entire brother's homeworld. He should have won. But he stood there for a long time, saying nothing. Perhaps he had already understood — the order was false. Perhaps he was only thinking that, true or false, he had personally pushed the Thousand Sons into the arms of Chaos.

The Burning of Prospero is the cleanest tragedy in the galaxy. There is no villain. Everyone acted for reasons that made sense to themselves. All the reasons, added together, equal hell.

### The Battle He Missed

After the Horus Heresy erupted in full, the Space Wolves fought across every front. Russ led his Legion through dozens of brutal engagements — the Battle of the Alaxxes Nebula, the Yarant Gap, and more. On the eve of the most critical battle of all, the Siege of Terra, Russ was grievously wounded in combat and could not return to Terra in time.

By the time he recovered from his wounds, the battle for Terra was over. The Emperor was upon the Golden Throne, Sanguinius was dead, and Horus had been obliterated from existence. He had failed to stand at the battle that decided humanity's fate. The Executioner had missed the execution.

### The Last Hunt

The Russ who emerged from the Horus Heresy was no longer the Wolf King who drank and roared with laughter. The smoke of Prospero and his absence from the battle for Terra had become two thorns lodged in his heart. He grew quieter, drank more, spoke less. He spent days and nights among the wolves. Wolves do not ask, *"Are you alright?"* Wolves only lick his wounds.

Then one day, he left.

In the Fenrisian tradition of the Last Hunt, an aged warrior walks into the ice wastes on a blizzard night, never to return. Russ did not walk into the ice wastes. He walked into the Eye of Terror. He took his personal retinue — the finest warriors of the Wolf Guard — and steered his fleet into the deepest fissure of the Warp.

Before he left, he left only one line. No lengthy proclamation. No mandate to his Legion. Only a single line, spoken to all the Space Wolves watching from the storm: **"I will return at the Wolftime."**

The *Wolftime* is a concept from Fenrisian myth — that world is too cold, too cruel, and the Fenrisians possess their own end-time prophecy. They believe that at the final day, the Wolf King shall return at the head of an army of the dead, to fight the last battle that cannot be won. And then the world begins again from the start.

Russ did not borrow this prophecy casually. He was telling his sons: *Do not search for me. Do not wait. When the day comes that I am truly needed — when everything has reached its darkest point — I will appear.*

As for his purpose within the Eye of Terror, the Space Wolves' sagas hold a more specific account: **he is seeking the Tree of Life**, a fabled entity said to be capable of healing anything — even restoring a dying god to life. The Emperor had been bound to the Golden Throne for too long. Russ believed the only cure that could save his father lay in the deepest reaches of the Warp. His loyalty to the Emperor never discounted — having failed as Executioner to protect his father, he would go as a son to find the medicine that could make him stand again.

Whether the Tree of Life truly exists, whether it might be nothing more than a hallucination dangled before him by Chaos — no one knows. But Russ believed, and walked into the Eye of Terror to find it. He spent his whole life making decisions that others found mad and he found perfectly natural.

## Fate & Present Status

Ten thousand years have passed.

Leman Russ has not returned. No confirmed report of his death has emerged from the Eye of Terror; no signal of his survival has surfaced either. But across this long millennium, from time to time, chilling threads of evidence have floated to the surface.

Certain Rune Priests — the Space Wolves' own psykers, who of course insist their power is not sorcery but "the work of the world-spirit of Fenris" — have perceived in their divinations the form of a great wolf, roaming somewhere in the Warp, hunting across infinite dimensions for ten thousand years.

In the chaos following the Thirteenth Black Crusade and the tearing open of the Great Rift across the galaxy, more than one isolated Imperial force has reported glimpsing a blurred giant on the battlefield, a figure with the silhouette of a Primarch, wielding an immense axe and chainsword, ripping a daemon in two with a single blow. No one has confirmed it was Leman Russ — but every Space Wolf who hears the news nods.

The Space Wolves have still not elected a new Wolf King. In the Fang, the fortress-monastery on Fenris, Russ's throne remains empty. Every Great Wolf who has sat upon it since has occupied only a regent's seat — the true throne waits for the one who said he would return.

## Verdict

Among all twenty Primarchs, Leman Russ is perhaps the most easily misunderstood. Outsiders see him — a barbaric Viking, a hard-drinking brawler, an axe in the Emperor's hand. But every Primarch who has truly fought alongside him — even Magnus, who loathed him most — would never use the word "stupid" to describe him.

The tragedy of Russ is that he understood his own role too clearly, and accepted it too completely. The Emperor needed an axe, and Russ willingly became the axe. The Emperor needed an executioner, and Russ swung the blade without hesitation. It was not that he did not think — it was that he buried his thinking so deep he even fooled himself. Because he knew: an executioner whose head is full of moral quandaries cannot swing.

Prospero was the ultimate eruption of that fault line. Russ carried out the order believing he was doing holy work; only when it was done did he realise he had been used as a gun — and the way this gun had fired had directly shoved a brother who might have been brought back into Chaos's embrace. From that moment on, he was never again the certain axe. He hesitated — before Horus, he hesitated — and thus he never stood on the battlefield of Terra's final battle.

But it was also from that moment that Leman Russ completed his second growth. In the golden age of the Great Crusade, he was a suffocating enforcer; after the Heresy, he finally became a man capable of remorse, of seeking a cure, of wagering his old life on a search that might have no object at all.

His quest for the Tree of Life — the act itself — is his complete portrait. He knew he might never find it. He knew he might never come back. He went anyway — because he is a wolf. A wolf does not tell the dying alpha of its pack, *"I can only sit beside you till dawn."* A wolf keeps searching, keeps searching, keeps searching until it collapses.

Whether the Emperor can be saved remains an open question. But that the Imperium should have a son who would walk into hell to find the cure — that, in this dark galaxy, is something approaching a luxury. The Space Wolves' understanding of it is the simplest of all: **The Wolf King is not dead. The Wolf King is hunting. The Wolf King will return on the coldest dawn.**
