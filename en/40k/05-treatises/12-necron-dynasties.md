# 12 · The Necron Dynasties

## Overview

The Necrons possess the galaxy's most advanced technology and are simultaneously afflicted by its most primitive senescence and decay. They do not die—but are slowly forgetting everything. When a dynasty rises from its tomb world, the most terrifying enemy it faces is often not the Imperium, not Chaos, but the corrupted protocols festering within its own mind.

---

### I. The Dynastic System: Shards of a Sixty-Million-Year Mandate

Necron society is a living fossil of ancient Necrontyr—the elder galactic civilisation. Every dynasty is an independent polity, with its own Phaeron, Overlord, court, and army, its own heraldry and strategic preferences. After biotransference, the social hierarchy was cast intact into living metal: those who were kings in life remain kings after death; those who were soldiers in life remain soldiers after death—there is no upward mobility, because the entire society has been sealed shut for sixty million years.

Four dynasties are of paramount importance. The **Szarekhan Dynasty** is the direct royal court of Szarekh the Silent King (see the *Biography of the Silent King*). Its mandate is supreme, yet since Szarekh's self-imposed exile, it slumbered for tens of millions of years, only re-emerging in M42 with the King's return. The **Sautekh Dynasty** is currently the most powerful, its de facto ruler being Imotekh the Stormlord—a strategic genius who has conquered dozens of Imperial worlds and repelled two Imperial Crusades. The **Mephrit Dynasty** was renowned in antiquity for star-igniting weaponry; though most of that lore has been lost, the surviving solar-forge armaments remain lethally potent. The **Nihilakh Dynasty** controls the galaxy's wealthiest stellar region; its motivation for warfare is often not conquest but the question of "whether this planet holds anything worth putting in the vault"—Trazyn the Infinite (see the *Biography of Trazyn*) hails from this dynasty.

The post-awakening relationships among these dynasties are neither alliance nor enmity: everyone knows, in theory, that they should obey the Silent King, but sixty million years without a supreme commander have bred independent will and independent interests in every dynasty.

---

### II. The Awakening Mechanism: Who Set the Alarm Wrong

Necrons do not live on planets—they sleep inside them. A **Tomb World** is a planet whose entire volume, from core to crust, has been remade into a colossal dormancy facility, its interior layered with innumerable stasis-crypts, reanimation furnaces, translocation beacons, and armouries. A typical Tomb World can house billions of Necron Warriors.

The awakening protocol is an automated programme: detection systems confirm low external threat, high energy reserves, subsystem self-checks passed—then proceed to wake the Necrons in staggered hierarchical tiers. But sixty million years have abraded this system to a state of near-paralysis. Some dynasties have only half-awakened—the Overlord still slumbers in the deep tiers while Warriors drift aimlessly through the mid-level corridors. Some dynasties woke too early—energy reserves only at thirty percent, Imperial colonists already building hive cities overhead. The most lethal afflictions are the cerebral viruses: the **Flayer Virus** compresses the infected's consciousness into a single directive—"slay all organic life"; the **Destroyer Curse** drives the infected to systematically exterminate every living thing, transforming them into mindless, self-perpetuating killing machines.

Necrons in the awakening process are acutely vulnerable. A Warrior fresh from the stasis-crypt requires several minutes to bring combat systems online; the necrodermis is still in a semi-fluid state—a single bolt round can reduce it to a puddle of scrap. And a Tomb World in the process of awakening emits powerful energy signatures—effectively broadcasting to the entire sector: "Dormant Necrons here. Attack now."

---

### III. Caste Structure: The Soul Has Been Drained

Necron caste is locked—not by law, but by hardware.

At the lowest tier is the **Warrior**. Devoid of independent consciousness, incapable of thought, hesitation, or fear, the Warrior simply advances in lockstep upon receiving a directive, firing as it walks. If the command chain is severed, the Warrior halts in place—or continues executing its last received order, forever. When one Warrior is cut down, the one behind steps over the wreckage and continues forward—not out of bravery, but because the "self" that could be threatened no longer exists.

Above them are the **Immortals**, the heavy elite infantry. They retain a greater quotient of self-awareness, enabling independent tactical judgement; when the command chain breaks, they can organise their own firing line. Their armour is heavier, their gauss weaponry more powerful.

Higher still is the **Royal Court** stratum—Overlords, Lords, and Crypteks. An Overlord is the supreme theatre commander, his necrodermis body a premium-grade version: taller, harder, faster to repair. Lords are Legion-level tactical executors. Crypteks are the engineer-scientists, responsible for maintaining equipment, authoring protocols, controlling Canoptek constructs, and tending the C'tan shards.

At the apex is the **Phaeron**—supreme ruler of an entire dynasty. A Phaeron retains near-complete personhood and intellect, though his soul was still devoured by the C'tan. A Phaeron can live sixty million years—so long as the body is not utterly annihilated, it can recompile infinitely. But herein lies the deepest tragedy: you remember that you once loved someone, but that sentence is now merely a data-string, devoid of warmth.

---

### IV. Reanimation Protocols & Self-Repair

When a Necron is struck down, it does not die—it will stand back up before your eyes. Living metal—**necrodermis**—when severed, automatically seeks its fragments and re-fuses. The energy source is the long-range transmission array at the Tomb World's core—so long as the signal remains unbroken, a Necron can reform an infinite number of times.

But the critical weakness lies here: **sever the translocation signal, and the Necron truly dies.** The signal has a range limit; the farther the distance, the weaker the transmission. If an expeditionary Legion is cut off from its rear-echelon tomb connection, every Necron destroyed cannot reanimate. More lethally, a Necron's central consciousness is stored in a micro-core called the **engrammatic matrix**—typically buried deep within the thoracic cavity. If the matrix is physically destroyed, even with the signal intact, the consciousness cannot return to the body.

The most terrible fate is to be pulverised to the point where the engrammatic matrix can no longer yield readable data. The body will still self-repair, but what is repaired is classified by the system as aberrant—this is one genesis path of the **Flayed One**: a metal zombie driven by nothing but a single fragment of skin-hunger code, shunned by its own dynasty, unable to interface with the command network, the only thing it still "remembers" being to find something with skin and peel it off to drape over its own frame.

Another fate is quieter: a Warrior blown in half at the waist, lower body intact, upper body's engrammatic matrix destroyed. Those two legs, within signal range, reanimate infinitely—standing there, a half-Warrior with no upper body, standing in place for ten thousand years.

### V. Technology & Weaponry: Got the Keys, Can't Find the Car

Necron technology stands at the galactic apex, yet they face a predicament both ludicrous and tragic: they have forgotten how to use much of their own equipment.

The *Gauss Flayer* is the Necron signature weapon. It fires no projectile—its principle is the application of a reverse gravitational field at the subatomic level, stripping the target's molecular bonds layer by layer. A victim does not bleed—in a searing green glare, skin, then muscle, then bone are peeled away successively, dissolving into ashen smoke. Terminator plate and flak armour are stripped at roughly the same pace by a gauss weapon, because the disassembly rate is determined not by material density but by atomic bond energy.

Space-time technology is even more staggering: phasic translocation allows a unit to switch positions in microseconds without traversing intervening space; pocket dimensions can store an entire reserve army inside a personally-carried extradimensional fold; chrono-fragments can locally distort the temporal vector. But the problem: the protocol code that drives these devices was manually purged as non-essential data during the Great Sleep sixty million years ago. The result: the keys are still in your hand, but you personally deleted the vehicle.

---

### VI. C'tan Shards: Gods in a Cage

The C'tan were once the Necrons' masters; the relevant background is detailed in the *Annals of the Necrontyr*. Here we focus on their present form: **Shards**.

After the intact C'tan were shattered, each fragment was individually imprisoned within a **Tesseract Vault**—a labyrinth of infinite dimensional folds. A Shard possesses no free will; it has been reduced to a kind of controllable, harnessable "demigod-class reactor." On the battlefield, a Cryptek opens a corner of the vault, releases the shard, assigns a direction and time-limit—typically a few seconds—then immediately withdraws it.

Those few seconds are enough. A single C'tan Shard's power is sufficient to distort the laws of physics within the volume of a small star system: gravity instantly reverses, light solidifies, time abruptly halts over a particular column of troops. In one recorded case, a shard of the Deceiver was released for three seconds, during which an entire family of six Imperial Knights spontaneously turned their weapons on their allies—the pilots afterwards had no memory of their actions, only a memory that, in that moment, a voice told them the enemy stood behind them.

Each release, however, weakens the seals. The moment a C'tan Shard is freed, it frenziedly absorbs all available energy to reconstitute itself. If re-absorption is delayed by a single second, the Shard may already have recovered enough power to shatter the vault—and the first thing it will do is exact vengeance upon the Necrons who imprisoned it for sixty million years. History records multiple incidents where an uncontrolled C'tan Shard annihilated the entire Legion that had released it, then physically vaporised the planet itself along with them.

---

### VII. Conclusion

The Necron contradiction is the deepest irony in the galaxy: they wield infantry weapons capable of disassembling molecular bonds, yet cannot repair the corrupted awakening protocols festering within their own brains; they imprison cosmic deities as armaments, yet can be transformed into skin-obsessed zombies by a cerebral virus. With every passing millennium, their engrammatic matrices undergo irreversible data loss—memories, knowledge, who they are—like a hard drive with perpetually spreading bad sectors.

The Necrons do not die. The Necrons simply grow ever closer to being hollow shells. One hundred million Warriors, and not a single one can die—but among those hundred million Warriors, perhaps only a few hundred still remember why they are alive. The rest are executing an order issued several hundred thousand years ago, marching toward a direction that no longer exists, walking for ten thousand years.

They are one of the most powerful forces in the galaxy—and also the one most slowly destroying itself. Not defeated by enemies, but defeated by time and forgetting.
