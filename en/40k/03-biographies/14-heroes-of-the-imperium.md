# 14 · Heroes of the Imperium

> The Astra Militarum is the Imperium's wall of flesh and blood. Trillions of ordinary humans, without genetic enhancement, without the impenetrable protection of ceramite, stand against the darkest corners of the galaxy on nothing but mortal bodies and faith. Three among them stand especially tall: Yarrick, whom even the Orks revere as something like a god; Cain, whose luck may be the most extraordinary in all of recorded history; and Gaunt, who led an iron regiment with a human heart. None of these three are transcendent beings like the Primarchs, nor immortal warriors like the Space Marines. They are simply mortal. But the light of mortals can sometimes ignite a lamp bright enough to illuminate an entire age of darkness. Their biographies are joined here.

---

## Sebastian Yarrick — The Orks' Terror

### Overview

Sebastian Yarrick, Commissar of the Astra Militarum, known as "Old Man Yarrick." He is the only human in the entire galaxy whom the Orks genuinely fear — not a Space Marine, not a Custodian, but an old man. The Ork Warlord Ghazghkull Mag Uruk Thraka calls him "Ol' Bale Eye" and once, with the power to kill him, chose to let him go — because, in Ghazghkull's own words, a fight without a worthy enemy is no fight at all.

### Origin

Yarrick had already served with distinction across dozens of campaigns before his name became legend. A veteran commissar forged in the endless brushfire wars of the Imperium, he had survived longer than most Imperial Guard soldiers and certainly longer than most commissars. By the time his true test arrived, he was already old — old enough that any other man would have retired behind a desk. Yarrick did not.

### Deeds

Yarrick's legend was forged on Armageddon. This industrial hive world, at the close of the 41st Millennium, became the site of the most brutal meat-grinder in galactic history. The First War for Armageddon was fought against Angron, the Daemon Primarch of Khorne, and his World Eaters; the Second brought Ghazghkull. When the green tide crashed against Armageddon's hive cities, Yarrick was already an old man — but he did not retire, did not hide in the rear, did not delegate his duty to anyone younger. He stood on the front line, bolt pistol in hand, firing into the Ork horde.

In one engagement, Yarrick was captured. Ghazghkull did not kill him. The Ork prophet said, in person: *Go. Go back. Heal up. We'll fight again — otherwise it's no fun.* To an Ork, releasing a worthy enemy is the highest form of respect. And to Yarrick, that scene was the precise mirror of his entire life's way of war: he did not fight "enemies." He fought "opponents." Between him and Ghazghkull there existed a kind of mutual recognition that crossed species, a bond that only extreme violence could produce.

Yarrick then did something that left the entire Astra Militarum speechless: he replaced his severed arm with the power klaw of a destroyed Ork mek. From that moment on, Yarrick killed Orks with an Ork's own weapon. The psychological impact this had on greenskins far exceeded any artillery barrage — the Orks' collective gestalt psychic field, the WAAAGH!, generated a near-superstitious dread of the old man. They began to believe Ol' Bale Eye could not be killed. And in the Ork psychic field, if enough Orks believe you cannot be killed — you genuinely become harder to kill.

The Third War for Armageddon saw Ghazghkull return in even greater force. The Imperium committed dozens of Space Marine Chapters; the Space Wolves and Blood Angels arrived in full strength. And Yarrick, a white-haired old human commissar, remained the spiritual anchor of the entire war effort. Not because of his rank, not because of tactical genius — but because as long as the soldiers of Armageddon saw that old man with the Ork power klaw standing on the line, they knew the fight was not yet lost.

### Fate & Present Status

Yarrick's current status is the subject of conflicting reports. His legend long outlives any single battlefield. His bale eye and Ork power klaw remain symbols that rally the Astra Militarum across the Segmentum Solar. Some say he died in the closing stages of the Third War; others insist Ghazghkull would never permit such an anticlimax.

## Ciaphas Cain — The Lucky One

### Overview

Ciaphas Cain, Commissar of the Astra Militarum, one of the longest-serving heroes in Imperial history — over a hundred years of active duty, decorated beyond counting, a living legend to the Guard. But if you read the memoirs he wrote himself (naturally, sealed by the Inquisition), you find an entirely different man: a cowardly, self-serving wretch who spent his entire life trying to stay away from the front line and was somehow always pushed into the heart of the storm. Every single one of Cain's heroic deeds, by his own account, was an accident that happened while he was running away.

But no one "accidentally" becomes a hero for over a hundred years. That is no accident.

### Origin

Little is known of Cain's early life; he was a product of the Schola Progenium, the Imperium's orphanage-military-academy, and commissioned as a commissar in the standard fashion. From his very first posting, the pattern was set.

### Deeds

Cain's first mission laid bare his "true character." Assigned to a world infiltrated by Tyranids, his instinct was to find somewhere safe to hide. Blundering blindly, he stumbled straight into the heart of a Genestealer nest and, in a state of total confusion, coordinated with local forces to wipe out the entire brood. By the time he realised what had happened, he was standing in the middle of a cheering crowd of Guardsmen, receiving his first medal.

The pattern repeated itself for over a century. Every time Cain tried to shrink back, his "escape route" happened to lead directly to the enemy's weakest point. When he tried to desert, he ran straight into the moment his allies most desperately needed reinforcement. When he tried to hide behind the command vehicle, the enemy targeted it as the primary assault axis, forcing him to fight — and he won. His aide Jurgen, a malodorous and stolid blank who naturally nullified psychic powers, became his constant companion; together, this odd pair of a craven commissar and his stolid orderly repeatedly survived encounters with daemons and psykers that should have annihilated them.

Cain's tragedy — or comedy — is that the greater his reputation grew, the more his superiors assigned him to the most lethal postings. The entire Imperium spread the word: *Commissar Cain is coming — there is hope.* So he was dispatched to locations of ever-increasing fatality: Ork WAAAGH!, Chaos incursions, Drukhari raids, Necron awakenings — he stepped into every hellscape the galaxy had to offer, tried to flee every single time, failed every single time, and came back with another medal each time.

His most honest sentence is written in the preface to his memoirs: *"The greatest desire of my life has been to find a safe planet and retire. Fate, it seems, considers this wish excessively extravagant."*

### Fate & Present Status

Cain is recorded as having died in the line of duty and been buried with full honours. His memoirs remain classified by the Inquisition, annotated extensively by Inquisitor Amberley Vail. Whether he truly died or engineered one final escape is, appropriately, a matter of some debate.

## Ibram Gaunt — The Soldiers' Commissar

### Overview

Ibram Gaunt, Colonel-Commissar of the Tanith First and Only. In the structure of the Astra Militarum, a commissar's role is never that of a "friend" — the commissar is the living embodiment of the Imperial will, tasked with maintaining discipline, executing deserters, ensuring that the troops never retreat under any circumstances. Between commissar and soldier there exists a wall by design: you fear me, you obey me, you do not love me.

Gaunt broke that wall.

### Origin

Gaunt was a promising officer, educated at the Schola Progenium and commissioned as a commissar. He had served capably in prior postings, but nothing marked him for legend — until Tanith.

### Deeds

Gaunt's legend began in an unmitigated tragedy. Tanith — a beautiful forest world — came under the shadow of a Chaos fleet. The Imperium ordered a total evacuation. Gaunt, tasked with raising regiments from Tanith, faced an impossible choice: wait for everyone to embark, and the planet and all its people would be reduced to cinders by orbital bombardment; evacuate immediately, and the only souls he could save would be a single light infantry regiment. He chose the latter. The Tanith First departed their homeworld alive. The planet of Tanith burned to ash behind them.

From that moment, the soldiers of the Tanith First and Only were people without a home. And Gaunt was their colonel — and their home.

Gaunt's philosophy of command ran counter to every commissariat manual. He did not stand behind his soldiers with a pistol; he stood in front of them. He did not live in a separate command tent; he slept in the mud alongside his men. He knew the name of every soldier in his regiment — not the roster-sheet kind of knowing, but the kind that meant knowing where they came from, who in their family was still alive, what they feared, what they excelled at. When a Tanith soldier hesitated before charging into a kill-zone, Gaunt did not draw his bolt pistol — he charged in first, to show them the way. He never needed to execute a deserter, because his soldiers could not bear to let him down.

Under Gaunt's leadership, the Tanith First became a legendary elite light infantry unit — the Ghosts, as the enemy came to call them. They excelled at infiltration, disruption, decapitation strikes, moving like wind through forests and ruins. On countless missions, Gaunt led his people into positions no one could survive — and they came out alive. The cost was real — the Tanith regiment's casualty rate was never low — but every Ghost who fell died knowing what they were dying for: not the abstract concept of the Imperium, but the commissar standing in their midst.

Gaunt himself took mortal wounds. He lost his eyes in a close-quarters engagement and later received a pair of bionic replacements. Those glowing red eyes became the regiment's standard — the Ghosts no longer needed to see the colours to know where to stand; they only needed to find those twin red points of light and know the Old Man was still there.

### Fate & Present Status

Gaunt continues to lead the Tanith First and Only, now a veteran regiment of legendary status. The Sabbat Worlds Crusade grinds on, and the Ghosts remain its sharpest blade. Gaunt's bionic eyes still glow red on the front line.

## Final Verdict

Three men, one chronicle, one common thread: all mortal, all Astra Militarum, all survivors against impossible odds who became legends. Yet their paths could not be more different.

Yarrick's heroism springs from hatred transmuted into will — he killed the enemy with the enemy's own weapons, sharpened his resolve on the enemy's fear. In the Orks' eyes, he is an unkillable monster; in his own, he simply kept refusing to die. This kind of hero is not built on talent — it is built on sheer stubbornness.

Cain represents a different equation: in the Warhammer 40K universe, survival itself is a form of victory. His hundred years are not a hundred years of heroism — they are a hundred years of endurance. Fear never left him, but he never truly broke. He proved that courage and fear are not contradictory — a coward who has been afraid for his entire life has overcome fear in every single second of that life. Every second, he was a hero.

Gaunt is the one among the three closest to the "ideal." He found something in the identity of the commissar that was never supposed to exist: love. His love for his soldiers — the specific, individual kind, the kind willing to trade his own life for theirs, never spoken aloud but etched into every action — is the rarest force in the Astra Militarum. The Imperium asks you to die for the Emperor. Gaunt asks you to live for him.

Three men, three paths, a single destination: the Imperium still stands because they once stood here.
