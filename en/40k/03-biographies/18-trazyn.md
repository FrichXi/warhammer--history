# 18 · Trazyn the Infinite

## Overview

Trazyn, called the Infinite, Overlord of the Nihilakh Dynasty, self-styled "curator"—but to everyone else in the galaxy, whether the Imperium of Man, Chaos warbands, Aeldari craftworlds, or Ork warbosses, the word that follows his name is simply: thief. Beneath the tomb world of Solemnace, he has built a museum on a planetary scale, the Prismatic Galleries, housing specimens spanning the entire sweep of galactic history—from the War in Heaven to the Horus Heresy, from the Fall of the Aeldari to the 13th Black Crusade. Artifacts, weapons, starships, entire armies frozen mid-charge in stasis fields, the last surviving individual of an ancient xenos species, original, authentic Imperial heroes "collected" in the heat of battle, and, it is whispered, possibly even something that once belonged to the Emperor Himself. He does not conquer. He does not rule. He takes no interest in dynastic wars. He does one thing: collect. Any moment in the galaxy he deems historically significant, he will be there—and by the time you realise it, your soldiers, your weapons, perhaps you yourself, are already in an exhibit case in the Prismatic Galleries.

## Origin

Trazyn hails from the Nihilakh Dynasty, which in ancient times controlled a region of space richest in resources and densest in relics, notorious for its wealth, its treasures, and its possessive obsession with all things precious. Before biotransference, Trazyn was already a figure of prominence in Necrontyr society: chief archivist, archaeologist, royal chronicler, keeper of the dynasty's knowledge and memory. If a civilisation's obsession with immortality could be described as pathological, Trazyn is the purest cross-section of that pathology—he never sought martial immortality; he simply wanted everything worth remembering to be preserved forever.

When the War in Heaven came, when Szarekh, the Silent King, signed the pact of biotransference with the C'tan on behalf of the entire race, Trazyn walked into the furnaces like every other Necrontyr. What emerged from the living metal was a skeletal frame of necrodermis. But unlike other Necron overlords, he did not emerge furious or despondent at the loss of his soul—he discovered that his obsession with collecting had survived, purer even than when he had a soul. No emotional interference. No physical fatigue. No lifespan limit. He could collect forever.

After the War in Heaven ended, the C'tan were shattered, and the Necron dynasties entered their sixty-million-year Great Sleep, Trazyn was among the first to awaken. The first thing he did upon waking was not conquer a sector, not muster armies—it was to take inventory of his collection. Then he discovered that across sixty million years, a great deal of fine material had been... interfered with. Countless new species had risen in the galaxy, staging countless magnificent dramas. He embarked on a voyage spanning tens of millions of years.

## Deeds

### The Prismatic Galleries

Trazyn constructed the Prismatic Galleries in the subterranean tunnels of Solemnace—perhaps not the largest museum in the galaxy, but indisputably the most comprehensive. Every exhibition hall is a complete historical diorama: a xenos empire from aeons past preserved at the moment its last emperor was devoured by his subjects (all participants rendered as living specimens); an entire company of Space Marines suspended in mid-air, locked in a charging posture, the muzzle flash of their boltguns frozen in stasis fields. The Galleries are so vast that even using the most advanced teleportation technology, walking them all would take centuries.

Trazyn's collecting philosophy is unambiguous: no replicas, no reproductions, no records. He wants originals. To collect a battle, he wants the actual combatants of that battle. To collect an era, he wants the most representative figures and objects of that era. As for the method of acquisition—he commands an elite corps of operatives called the Atoners, equipped with chrono-displacement devices and Tesseract Cubes, capable of materialising at the densest point of an enemy battle line, selecting a target, encapsulating it, and returning to the Galleries in a matter of seconds.

If you are ever on a battlefield and witness an entire company of a famous Imperial regiment vanish without warning, only to find their holographic likenesses on display in some unheard-of xenos museum the next day—then you will know: Trazyn was here.

### Creed and Cadia

At the close of M41, Trazyn's collecting career entered a golden period: Abaddon the Despoiler launched the 13th Black Crusade, aimed at annihilating the Imperium's last bastion before the Eye of Terror—Cadia. For Trazyn, this was a once-in-a-millennium procurement opportunity. Cadia was not merely the most heavily defended fortress world in the galaxy, but the birthplace of the Imperium's most tragic military legend, and this battle was writing its final chapter. What collector could possibly miss the final chapter?

He arrived in person in the subterranean catacombs beneath Cadia, intending to quietly harvest a batch of high-quality exhibits while both sides were locked in blood-drenched combat. But he quickly discovered something profoundly displeasing: Abaddon did not merely want to conquer Cadia—he wanted to smash the entire planet, together with its civilisation, its history, its military legacy, into rubble. This, to Trazyn, was unacceptable. You may make war. You may kill people. But you may not destroy an exhibit with sixty million years of provenance.

To preserve Cadia—or rather, to preserve the value of "Cadia as a historical specimen"—Trazyn did something that stunned both sides: he released one of the most powerful exhibits in his collection, a C'tan Shard. This was no trinket. A C'tan Shard is a fragment of a shattered ancient star-god, possessed of power sufficient to tear the laws of physics apart. As Abaddon's Chaos fleet prepared to hurl Cadia into the Eye of Terror, Trazyn loosed the Shard to aid the Imperial defence—a Necron Overlord, expending his most prized possession to protect an Imperial fortress world from being consumed by Chaos.

Naturally, he did not waste the opportunity. At the height of the fighting, Ursarkar E. Creed—the legendary commander of the Cadian Shock Troops, a veteran whose tactical genius had humiliated countless Chaos warbands—found himself abruptly sealed inside a Tesseract Cube. "You are dead, as far as the Imperium is concerned," Trazyn later remarked to Creed's frozen image in the Prismatic Galleries (yes, he really does talk to his exhibits), "but in my galleries, you shall live forever in your finest moment."

### Known Acquisitions

How many items Trazyn actually possesses is known only to him. But based on scattered eyewitness reports from the Inquisition, Aeldari craftworlds, and various Chaos warbands, the following exhibits are broadly confirmed:

An entire living clone regiment of an Imperial Guard force—now the sole surviving specimen available for Imperial military study, since the original regiment was annihilated millennia ago. Trazyn holds the complete company, frozen in assault formation.

An uncorrupted Emperor's Children Legionary—from the era of the Great Crusade, before the Horus Heresy, before the Emperor's Children were infected by the corruption of Slaanesh. Were anyone in the Imperium aware of this, they would trade their homeworld for it.

A fully intact Custodian—Trazyn acquired this one in some unrecorded engagement. No one knows how he pulled it off.

Tens of thousands of holographic historical records and original artifacts from diverse xenos civilisations—including relics predating the Fall of the Aeldari (the birth of Slaanesh), and ancient constructs that even the Aeldari themselves believed lost forever.

It is said—only said—that deep within his Galleries lies something that once belonged to the Emperor Himself. As to what, Trazyn has never uttered a word, but every Inquisitor who has heard the rumour refuses to speculate further.

### The Eternal Curator, at Peace with the Universe

Another peculiarity of Trazyn is this: he has no real enemies. Not because no one wants him dead—the list of those who do stretches from the Eternity Gate to the Eye of Terror. The Inquisition has classified him as a xenos fugitive of the highest order. Chaos warlords swear to unearth and dismember him. The Aeldari Harlequins consider him the most dangerous hoarder in the galaxy. But Trazyn's strength lies precisely here: he never seeks combat, but he is never afraid of it either. Any attacker who reaches the orbit of Solemnace discovers, before their ships even finish translating, that they are not facing a Necron Overlord—they are facing a complete arsenal from the era of the War in Heaven, and the master of that arsenal keeps producing items you have never seen before.

Within Necron dynastic politics, Trazyn occupies an equally peculiar position. When the Silent King Szarekh called for all dynasties to unite against the Tyranids, Trazyn's response was roughly: "The Hive Fleets threatening the entire galaxy? Would it be possible to reserve one live specimen for my collection before they are annihilated?" Imotekh the Stormlord regards him as an unreliable romantic fantasist but is reluctant to openly break with him—because you can never be certain that during your next campaign, a legion won't mysteriously vanish from your flank and reappear in Trazyn's display cases.

Trazyn is utterly indifferent to these assessments. To him, the galaxy is an eternal exhibition, war is merely pre-exhibition curation, and the difference between heroes and tyrants is simply that the former belong in the main hall. The rise and fall of dynasties, the survival or extinction of empires, the eternal war between Chaos and Order—all of these are different acts of the same play. His job is to record, to preserve, to fix in permanence every scene that deserves to be seen by those who come after.

## Fate & Present Status

Trazyn remains active in the 41st Millennium, the Prismatic Galleries expanding with every major galactic event. The Great Rift has not disrupted his operations; if anything, the Era Indomitus has provided a wealth of new acquisition opportunities. He continues to appear uninvited at pivotal battles, selectively collecting specimens while occasionally intervening—always in ways that serve his curatorial interests. No faction has ever successfully assaulted Solemnace, and none is likely to. Trazyn is, for all intents and purposes, as immortal as his title suggests.

## Verdict

In the grand tragedy of Warhammer 40,000, every major player is fighting in their own desperate way: the Emperor burns on the Golden Throne, Guilliman rebuilds amid despair, Abaddon drives his crusades forward in vengeance, Szarekh returns in penance. Only Trazyn maintains, in this mad galaxy, a tranquility that borders on cold indifference.

It is not that he lacks the capacity to intervene—in truth, with knowledge accumulated across tens of millions of years and an arsenal dating back to the War in Heaven, his strength would give even the Stormlord of the Sautekh Dynasty pause. Nor is it that he does not understand what is happening in the galaxy—every major war, every species-wide crisis, he is almost always on site, arriving earlier than most of the actual participants. He simply chooses not to participate. To him, the battle you pour every ounce of strength into winning, the world you shed rivers of blood to defend, the tomorrow purchased with the sacrifice of a generation—all of it will eventually become one more display in the Prismatic Galleries. A few thousand years ago you were throwing stones; a few thousand years later you pilot starships—he has seen it all. He has been watching for so long that, for him, nothing has the concept of an "ending." There is only "has the exhibit been acquired."

This stance is almost impossible to evaluate morally. You could call him cold-blooded—he seals living beings in eternal stasis prisons merely so he can admire their final poses at leisure. Or you could call him the most objective chronicler in the galaxy—in an age when every civilisation destroys and forgets, he alone truly seeks to preserve. Trazyn himself probably thinks of it this way: if, one day, the entire galaxy finally burns to ash, and a single museum still has its lights on—then everything he has done will have been worth it.

On that day, he will gaze with those hollow eye sockets at the last visitor to walk in, and say: "Welcome. The War in Heaven is in the first hall. This way, please."
