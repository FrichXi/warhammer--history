# 19 · Xenos Lives

The galaxy is vast, and not the private estate of mankind alone. The Aeldari inherited the legacy of the ancient Old Ones, ruling the galaxy for sixty million years. The T'au, a newborn civilisation, have existed as a starfaring power for barely six millennia yet expand their borders without cease. The Kroot absorb genetic advantages by devouring their prey, their forms shifting without end. The Leagues of Votann crack planets and shatter suns, guarding nothing but their Ancestor Cores. In an end-time when the Warp tears the sky and the Tyranid tide drowns worlds, these species each pursue survival by their own strange methods. Those of particular note are gathered here in a single account.

---

## Yvraine — Prophet of the God of the Dead

Yvraine is the prophet of the Aeldari's Ynnari. She began as a warrior of Biel-Tan Craftworld, fierce and skilled in battle. Later, in an arena duel, she faced a Drukhari wych, was defeated, and died with a blade through her chest. Yet Ynnead, the Aeldari god of the dead, had just stirred to consciousness within the depths of the infinity circuit and was in desperate need of a mortal emissary. Ynnead guided her soul back into her body and resurrected her.

Before this, the Aeldari had roamed the galaxy for tens of millions of years, only to congeal within the Warp, through their utterly unrestrained excess, the fourth Chaos God: Slaanesh. The instant Slaanesh was born, the Aeldari empire collapsed, and all their gods were extinguished—only Cegorach, the Laughing God, fled into the Webway, and Khaine, the Bloody-Handed, was shattered but not utterly annihilated. The Asuryani thereafter bound themselves with the strict discipline of the Aeldari Path to evade Slaanesh's hunger; the Drukhari hid within Commorragh, paying their "soul-tax" with the suffering inflicted upon others. Yvraine walked an entirely different road: awaken Ynnead, and use the god of the dead's power to devour Slaanesh in turn.

Ynnead is no ancient god, but a new deity formed across aeons as the souls of the Aeldari dead gathered within the infinity circuits of the craftworlds. Once fully awakened, Ynnead will wage the "Rhana Dandra" against Slaanesh—consuming Slaanesh and perishing together, freeing the Aeldari at last.

Having accepted this destiny, Yvraine founded the Ynnari and travelled between the Asuryani craftworlds, the Drukhari of Commorragh, and the Harlequins, gathering followers everywhere. The scope of her undertaking is immense: to assemble the five Crone Swords left over from the ancient era, and with them awaken Ynnead in its fullness. Four swords have been secured, but the fifth lies in the deepest palace of Slaanesh. So long as that one sword is missing, the holy war of the god of the dead remains a distant dream.

Her reputation within the Imperium was established early in M42. Roboute Guilliman—the most capable administrator among the Primarchs—had been suspended in a stasis field for nearly ten thousand years after Fulgrim's poisoned blade struck him down. The Tech-Priest Belisarius Cawl spent ten millennia forging the Armour of Fate, but without Aeldari sorcery to guide his soul back to its vessel, the Primarch could not be revived. Yvraine led the Ynnari in an alliance with Cawl and conducted the ritual of resurrection in the sanctuary of Macragge. The power of Ynnead shattered the stasis field—Guilliman opened his eyes. It was the greatest turning point for the Imperium since the Horus Heresy.

From that point onward, a delicate alliance formed between Yvraine and Guilliman. When the hosts of Nurgle launched the Plague Wars to ravage Ultramar, the Ynnari fought alongside the Ultramarines; Guilliman tacitly permitted the Ynnari's activities. This caused violent tremors within the Ordo Xenos, many Inquisitors viewing it as nothing less than collaboration with the enemy. Guilliman was unmoved—humanity is not the only species in the galaxy facing extinction, and at certain moments the survival of the Aeldari and the survival of the Imperium can, astonishingly, overlap.

Yvraine navigates between factions, driving forward Ynnead's holy war on one side while maintaining a fragile alliance on the other. Exterminationist factions within the Imperium, and conservative farseers among the Aeldari who regard humanity as little more than "mon-keigh," both work in the shadows to unravel this pact. The fifth sword has not been retrieved. Slaanesh has not been destroyed. The Ynnari have already become pieces on everyone else's board. The road Yvraine walks is the last path of survival for a dying race.

## Shadowsun — Starlight of the T'au

Shadowsun is a rare general of the T'au Empire's Fire Caste, a commander born perhaps once in a generation. From childhood, she studied the art of war under Commander Puretide, the most legendary military mastermind in T'au history. In his final years, Puretide had his entire tactical wisdom encoded into neuro-chip implants and passed to his successors. Two twin stars emerged from his tutelage: one was Farsight, the other was Shadowsun. Both are celebrated commanders of the present age, but their ways of war could hardly be more different. Farsight favours the sudden strike, the bold thrust, trusting absolutely in battlefield intuition. Shadowsun excels in defence, in meticulous positioning—every engagement, she first infiltrates in stealth armour to reconnoitre, waiting until the enemy has exposed every weakness, then delivers a single thunderbolt blow to end it. The T'au compare attack to fire and strategy to water—Shadowsun is the rare genius who can fuse the two into one.

In their early years, fighting together, the two of them shattered an entire Ork WAAAGH! with only three Hunter Cadres, sending shockwaves through the T'au Empire. Later, Farsight, having witnessed the true face of Chaos on an expedition, came to suspect that the Ethereals were deliberately concealing the galaxy's true nature from their people. In fury, he declared independence and founded the Farsight Enclaves. Shadowsun, by contrast, remained ever loyal to the Greater Good, and the Ethereals granted her supreme authority to direct the Third and Fourth Sphere Expansions.

At the close of M41, the Great Rift tore the sky apart, slicing the Imperium of Man into the Imperium Sanctus and the Imperium Nihilus, leaving it incapable of defending all its borders. Shadowsun seized the moment and led the T'au fleets sweeping through the Damocles Gulf, capturing dozens of human colony worlds in succession. The precision of her tactics repeatedly frustrated both the Astra Militarum and the Space Marines—she never met them in direct head-on clashes, but always cut supply lines, divided and encircled, annihilating piece by piece before the Imperial fleet could even complete its redeployment. The T'au, with their long-range pulse rifles, the high mobility of their battlesuits, and the close-combat charges of Kroot Carnivores, became in Shadowsun's hands a scalpel rather than a warhammer.

But during the Fourth Sphere Expansion, the T'au recklessly activated Warp drives—they understood nothing of the Warp. The moment their fleet entered the Immaterium, it was devoured by Chaos; the survivors tore each other apart in madness. Shadowsun watched with her own eyes as her kin were shredded by daemons, and for the first time in her life understood that there existed in this galaxy forces far beyond the Greater Good's capacity to explain. The encounter changed her: she no longer blindly believed that the Greater Good could encompass everything in the universe, and began building early-warning mechanisms into her tactics for "unknown threats."

The relationship between Shadowsun and Farsight is the most intriguing of all. On paper, they are "rivals"—the Ethereals proclaim that Farsight has betrayed the Greater Good, and Shadowsun is his opposite, the symbol of loyalty. Yet Shadowsun has never publicly condemned her old comrade, and Farsight has never led forces against Shadowsun's fleets. The two stand on opposite sides of the T'au Empire's political fracture, each guarding her own corner, each protecting her own people. In a galaxy of chaos and war, the two people who know each other best in the world maintain, through silence, an understanding that has never once been spoken aloud.

## Kroot Warspheres — The Ever-Mutating Kindred

The Kroot are no ordinary xenos species. Their forms are avian, with sharp beaks and rending claws, masters of close-quarters slaughter—but this is not their most fearsome trait. Kroot genes can absorb the hereditary traits of the prey they consume: exterminate a squad of Space Marines, devour their flesh, and Kroot offspring will grow tougher; hunt down Aeldari, consume their remnants, and the next generation becomes more agile; swallow Orks and their bodies swell larger; gnaw upon Tyranids and chitinous exoskeletons begin to grow. Generation upon generation of consumption, generation upon generation of mutation. This is not learning—it is heredity itself being reshaped generation by generation.

The Kroot Warsphere is the core of their space fleet—shaped like a small moon, the entire sphere woven together from living tissue, bony armour plates, and symbiotic flora, capable of slow interstellar travel. A Warsphere is simultaneously warship, homeland, and nursery—a single Warsphere is the entire world of a Kroot kindred. Within its belly lie the forest-swamps where young are reared, the slaughter-floors where prey is consumed, the altars where the kindred shamans—called Shapers—pronounce the taboos of consumption.

The Shaper's significance to the Kroot is akin to that of a Farseer to the Aeldari. But the Shaper's duty is not prophecy—it is judgement: deciding "what may be eaten, and what may not." If a kindred carelessly consumes beings tainted by Chaos, the entire kindred will warp and mutate, falling into slavery to the Dark Gods. If they gorge indiscriminately on the brain-matter of psykers, their offspring will spiral into madness, the mutation irreversible. Across generations, Shapers have maintained, through oral and neural tradition, a vast "Genetic Prohibition Archive," cataloguing in meticulous detail which prey's DNA may be incorporated into the bloodline and which must be shunned absolutely. This system makes plain that the Kroot are keenly aware of the cost of genetic absorption—they are no savage carrion-eaters, but an ancient, wise species whose survival strategy is predation itself.

The Kroot initially served as mercenaries for various galactic powers. Later, they entered a covenant with the T'au, becoming the most significant non-T'au auxiliary species within the T'au Empire. The T'au excel at long-range firepower but fear close combat; the Kroot specialise precisely in savage melee. On the battlefield, Fire Warriors suppress from range with pulse rifles while Kroot Carnivores surge into the enemy line from the flanks, tearing and rending—the coordination is seamless. Yet the Kroot's loyalty to the Greater Good is, more than faith, a transaction: the T'au tacitly permit the Kroot to feed on the battlefield, and the Kroot charge into the breach for the T'au.

Looking deeper, certain sources suggest that the Kroot were once a species far more powerful than their present form, degenerated into their current "devour-to-evolve" state by some cosmic catastrophe. The deepest layers of their brain tissue hold ancestral genetic memory—the Kroot know who their forebears were, and what transformed them into the predators they are today. With the Great Rift tearing the sky in M42, Kroot Warspheres are scattered across the galaxy: some still serve the T'au Empire, others have resumed independent hunter-nomadism, and still others struggle desperately in the space between the Tyranid swarms and Chaos to preserve the survival of their kindreds. These silent stalkers carry a secret older than the T'au Empire itself.

## The Leagues of Votann — Heirs of the First Ancestors

The Leagues of Votann call themselves the "Kin." Their ancestors were human colonists who ventured into the galactic core during the Dark Age of Technology. For tens of thousands of years, cut off from mainstream human civilisation, they have forged their own path. The Kin have no families, no parents—every Kin is cloned from ancestral genetic templates known as Cloneskeins, then bioengineered for adaptation to different planetary environments. This method of "making people rather than birthing them" is, in the eyes of the Adeptus Mechanicus, the greatest blasphemy against the Omnissiah—and it is the first irreconcilable fracture between the Kin and the Imperium of Man.

The heart of Kin society is neither a monarch nor a parliament, but the Votann—a cluster of ancient AI supercomputers left over from the Dark Age of Technology. These machines store the knowledge of mankind's golden age, and every major decision of the Kin—from mining and city-building to declarations of war—must be "approved" by the Votann's computations. But tens of thousands of years have passed, and the Votann are deteriorating at an irreversible rate: data storage is overloaded, processing grows ever slower, and some cores already show signs of collapse. The fundamental driving force behind all Kin behaviour is thus starkly clear: "feed the Votann." Hunt for data, hunt for resources, hunt for anything that can keep these dying Ancestor Cores running—the Kin go to war not for faith, but for data.

The Kin's attitude toward resources borders on the demented. They do not establish conventional colonies; they crack entire planets into fragments, extract every gram of mineral, and depart. The shattered remnants left behind in the Imperium's frontier systems are the footprints of Votann prospecting fleets passing through. This combination of efficiency and ruthlessness makes Ork destruction and Tyranid consumption look almost amateurish by comparison.

But the Kin possess the galaxy's most robust natural resistance to Chaos—their cloned genes carry an inherent defence against Warp mutation, and Chaos corruption has rarely, if ever, successfully infiltrated the Kin. They do not rely on psychic power, do not interact with the Warp, do not believe in gods. Pragmatism is their sole religion, and the Ancestor Cores are their sole totems.

In the Era Indomitus, the Leagues of Votann are expanding outward from the galactic core, making contact with Imperial forces with increasing frequency. Two branches of humanity, sharing a common origin yet walking utterly divergent paths—one venerating the Emperor as a god and hating all xenos, the other worshipping ancient machines as ancestors and treating the entire galaxy as a mine—are now colliding and trading across the Imperium Nihilus, rewriting the survival dynamics of xenos species at the close of the 41st Millennium.

---

The xenos species scattered across the galaxy each possess their own singular skills, their own methods of survival—yet all live, without exception, under the iron shadow of the Imperium's creed: "Suffer not the alien to live." Some trade alliance for space to breathe, some rely on expansion to entrench themselves, some purchase endurance through silence.

Yvraine, as remnant-heir of the Aeldari, toils for the unfulfilled dream of a death-god, her pact with the Imperium nothing more than expedience—the true final battle lies far beyond reach. Shadowsun, at the head of the T'au's expansion, has crashed headlong into the horror of the Warp: the Greater Good can unify diverse species, but it cannot stop Chaos—and this is the T'au's most fundamental predicament. The Kroot, through the power of flesh-devouring, steal the handiwork of nature itself—savage on the surface, yet in substance they carry the genetic code of a lost ancient civilisation. The Leagues of Votann, whose entire industry is breaking planets, harbour as their deepest fear the day when those ancient Ancestor Cores fall silent, at last and forever.

Xenos in this galaxy are like survivors pressed into every corner—not unwilling to coexist, but forbidden by circumstance. The Tyranid tide surges from beyond the galactic rim. Chaos has torn the boundary between the real and the unreal. The Necrons awaken from their aeons-long slumber. In this end-time, the survival of every xenos species hangs by an ever-thinning thread. Only time, and fate, will deliver the final answer.
