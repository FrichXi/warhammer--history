# 09 · Belisarius Cawl

## Overview

Belisarius Cawl, Archmagos Dominus of the Adeptus Mechanicus, is one of the longest-lived humans in the galaxy — over ten thousand years old, and showing no signs of stopping. He is the creator of the Primaris Space Marines, the engineer behind Roboute Guilliman's resurrection, the bearer of a secret mandate issued by the Primarch himself, and a techno-heretic whom even his own Mechanicus would gladly put on trial. In an empire that has been rusting for ten millennia, Cawl may be the only craftsman still mass-producing new parts.

He is not a good man. But without him, the Imperium of Man would have been finished by M42.

## Origin

Cawl did not begin at the top. Over ten thousand years ago, during the Horus Heresy, he was a young scientist on Mars — not the most powerful kind, but clever enough, and bolder still. The Mechanicus of that era was already a conservative institution that regarded "innovation" as heresy; any deviation from established STC (Standard Template Construct) blueprints could earn the label of techno-heresy, with consequences including but not limited to: excommunication, memory-core wipe, and summary execution.

What set Cawl apart was meeting the right person at the right time. In the closing years of the Great Crusade, before Horus had openly rebelled, Guilliman was already making a decision few could comprehend — he sensed an immense danger approaching, one that would not vanish with loyalty alone. He needed a scientist to help prepare a contingency: a new generation of warriors, stronger and more stable than the existing Adeptus Astartes, capable of confronting threats no one had yet imagined.

He chose Cawl.

This was not a formal contract. It was a secret command. Guilliman himself probably never expected that the young magos he had entrusted with this task on something of a whim would actually spend ten thousand years carrying it out.

## Deeds

### The Ten-Thousand-Year Task

Guilliman's order was breathtakingly vague: improve the genetic engineering of the Space Marines. No technical pathway, no budget approval, no timeline. The Mechanicus would never sanction such research, and no Space Marine Chapter would accept that "someone intends to improve upon the Emperor's own design." Cawl's entire project was underground from the start.

Then the Horus Heresy erupted. The Emperor was mortally wounded; Guilliman was stabbed through the throat by Fulgrim's poisoned blade and sealed in stasis. The war ended. The Imperium descended into ten thousand years of darkness. Every person who had known about the secret task — if one did not count Cawl himself — was either dead or lying in a stasis casket.

A normal person would have abandoned the mission. Cawl did not.

Over the millennia that followed, he used every means available to extend his life. When his human body failed, he replaced it with machinery — piece by piece, until the original flesh-and-blood became a philosophical question. When machinery ran its course, he resorted to consciousness transfer: backing up his complete personality into multiple mechanical bodies, distributed across the galaxy and working in parallel. Whether these "copies" possess independent self-awareness is a subject Cawl himself declines to discuss, and few dare to ask.

Mechanicus conservatives attempted to put him on trial more than once. But his technology was too valuable, the secrets he held too lethal, and he never truly "stayed dead" — every time you thought you had destroyed him, another Cawl would emerge from a different Forge World and resume the work.

### The Primaris Space Marines

Ten thousand years later, Cawl turned in his assignment.

Primaris Space Marines — taller, stronger, with more stable gene-seed and longer lifespans than traditional Astartes. The gene-seed of conventional Space Marines had suffered varying degrees of degradation over ten millennia of replication (some Chapters were on the brink of extinction); the Primaris solved this problem and even added new organs: Sinew Coils for explosive strength, and the Belisarian Furnace, which automatically releases life-sustaining hormones when the bearer is critically wounded.

This was not "improvement." This was an upgrade. The genetic template personally designed by the Emperor had been refurbished by a mechanical madman who had lived far too long. In any other era, Cawl would have driven a blade straight into the lung of Imperial faith — Space Marines are the Emperor's sacred creations; who dares to alter them?

But the Imperium of M42 had no choice left. The Great Rift had torn the galaxy in two. Countless Chapters were being annihilated in wars of attrition against Chaos, Tyranids, and Orks. Guilliman's first military decision upon awakening was to deploy every product of the order he had placed ten thousand years ago — the full technology and finished Primaris forces — onto the battlefield. The Indomitus Crusade could never have been sustained without Primaris Marines to fill the line.

### Resurrection of Guilliman

Cawl's "after-sales service" for Guilliman went beyond the Primaris. After Guilliman was impaled by Fulgrim's poisoned blade, the Apothecaries of the Ultramarines sealed him in a stasis field at the exact threshold between life and death. For ten thousand years, that stasis casket was the most precious relic on Macragge, the Ultramarines' homeworld — indistinguishable from a death watch, except that it had not yet given up.

Yvraine, leader of the Aeldari Ynnari, possessed the Warp-lore to guide Guilliman's soul back from the brink of death. But psychic power alone was insufficient; a device was needed that could simultaneously suppress the poison, sustain organ function, and interface with psychic intervention — the Armour of Fate.

Cawl built that armour.

The collaboration between Yvraine and Cawl made the entire Imperium and Aeldari alike profoundly uncomfortable: the Archmagos Dominus of the Mechanicus joining hands with a xenos psyker to experiment on the Emperor's most hallowed creation — before any tribunal of the Inquisition, the two of them could have been burned a dozen times over. Cawl did not care. The list of things Cawl does not care about is long: Mechanicus dogma, Imperial religion, what others think of him. What he cares about is whether the task is done. Guilliman told him to do three things — build stronger warriors, build a device that could save him, and wait for him to awaken — and ten thousand years later, Cawl delivered all three.

Guilliman opened his eyes inside the Armour of Fate. Cawl stood beside him. It was probably the closest this ancient, too-long-lived construct of metal has ever come to the thought: *I won.*

### The Ultimate Goal: Voice of the Machine God

But Cawl was never a loyal servant.

The Mechanicus worships the Machine God — also called the Omnissiah — and in Martian doctrine, the Emperor is recognised as the Omnissiah's embodiment. Cawl's faith does not quite align with orthodox Mechanicus teaching. He does not believe the Emperor is the endpoint. He believes the true essence of the Machine God is pure knowledge, and that humanity must evolve toward that ideal by continually pushing the boundaries of technology, rather than reciting ten-thousand-year-old STC blueprints as though they were scripture.

The list of forbidden technologies Cawl has dabbled in is long enough to earn him three hundred death sentences from the Inquisition: artificial intelligence research (AI is an absolute prohibition in the Imperium, because humanity was nearly annihilated by its own AIs during the Dark Age of Technology), psychic-machine fusion, xenos-tech reverse-engineering, self-learning algorithms — anything the Mechanicus says "do not touch," he has probably touched extensively.

His ultimate goal is not to resurrect the Emperor, nor to assist Guilliman, nor to save the Imperium. It is himself — to become the true voice of the Machine God. What he seeks is not merely a position of power, but the power of definition: the authority to define what the next stage of human technology should be, and what "knowledge" itself means. The breadth and depth of what he has accumulated over ten thousand years — genetic engineering, psychic physics, Warp mechanics, xenos technology, artificial intelligence — likely exceeds that of any other institution in the Imperium.

Guilliman knows this. He uses Cawl, uses his Primaris Marines and his Armour of Fate, but has never truly trusted him. There is too much "too much" about Cawl: too old, too knowledgeable, too transgressive, and not nearly loyal enough to be reassuring.

## Fate & Present Status

Cawl remains active in the Era Indomitus, continuing his research across multiple Forge Worlds through his distributed consciousness network. He operates with a degree of autonomy that no other figure in the Imperium enjoys — Guilliman needs him and cannot afford to rein him in, and the Mechanicus lacks the means to destroy him permanently. He is, for all practical purposes, untouchable.

## Verdict

Belisarius Cawl is the smartest person in the Imperium — this statement may not be hyperbole. His knowledge spans genetics, mechanics, psychic phenomena, and xenos technology, and he has produced results continuously across a ten-thousand-year span. There is no second example of such research productivity in all of human history.

But he is also one of the Imperium's greatest threats.

A being who has lived ten millennia, bound by no institution, untroubled by moral ambiguity, in possession of vast forbidden knowledge and actively seeking to "surpass the Emperor" — that is roughly the recipe for the Horus Heresy in new packaging. Cawl will not defect to Chaos (he has no interest in it whatsoever), but his loyalty to the Imperial Truth is equally thin. He serves the "Machine God" as he himself defines it — an ultimate ideal that does not yet exist, but is achingly clear inside his mind.

Guilliman is fully aware of his situation: the Imperium's life-support apparatus is in the hands of a ten-thousand-year-old madman who is bound to make history sooner or later. But he has no choice. Neither does Cawl — a mission of ten thousand years has hollowed him into a being for whom nothing remains except "keep going."

This is not a story of loyalty. It is a symbiosis conducted at the edge of the abyss. The Imperium needs Cawl's mind; Cawl needs the Imperium's platform. Both know that their paths must eventually diverge. Neither knows how loudly that divergence will explode.
