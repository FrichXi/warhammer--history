# 03 · Commander Dante

## Overview

Dante, Chapter Master of the Blood Angels, is the oldest living Space Marine currently known to the Imperium — he has lived over fifteen hundred years, eleven hundred of them seated in a position he never wanted. He is the victor of the Defense of Baal, the Regent of Imperium Nihilus, and the man Roboute Guilliman personally named "the highest authority in the Imperium." But if you asked Dante what he truly desires, he would not answer. He would fall silent, because the answer is too light to speak.

## Origin

Dante was born on Baal Secundus, the second moon of Baal. Calling that place a "wasteland" would be generous: irradiated salt pans crusted the surface, the water tasted of metal, and the twin moons hung overhead like a pair of dead eyes. That was not his birth name. It belonged to who he was before he became a Space Marine — an ordinary boy of a Baalite tribe, whose daily tasks were finding water, dodging mutant scorpions, and praying to survive tomorrow.

He entered the Blood Angels' selection trials — or rather, the Blood Angels' culling. On Baal, countless young men were taken every year to undergo the ordeal of gene-seed implantation. Dante passed. He did not know what it meant. He only knew that from that day on, his blood was no longer his own. Through his veins began to flow the gene-code of Sanguinius — and the curse along with it.

Fifteen hundred years ago on Baal, nobody could have imagined that the gaunt young boy would one day hold dominion over the entire Imperium Nihilus. He didn't imagine it either.

## Deeds

### From Warrior to Chapter Master

Dante's career spans so long that even the Imperial bureaucracy cannot fully trace it. How many campaigns has he fought? No one knows. How many enemies has he killed? He has lost count. He has been Chapter Master for longer than the entire natural lifespan of most Space Marines. The warriors of the Blood Angels age in battle — not in body, but in soul. The Black Rage and the Red Thirst hang like twin nooses around the neck of every son of Sanguinius, tightening with each passing year until one day they snap shut for good.

Dante has held out for eleven hundred years without snapping.

That fact alone is something close to a miracle. His mask — the golden Death Mask of Sanguinius — conceals his face, but his voice has never wavered. He has awarded gene-seed to recruits, signed death notices, delivered pre-battle prayers for generations upon generations. Those who joined the Chapter when he was young are long dead. Their grandsons' grandsons are dead. And Dante stands in the same place.

He has no friends left. Everyone went ahead of him.

### The Inner War Against the Black Rage

The Blood Angels' greatest enemy does not exist outside the Imperium. It is written into their genes.

Every Blood Angels warrior experiences dreams that eventually, inevitably, collapse into one fixed scene: the deck of the Vengeful Spirit, Horus's shadow bearing down like a mountain, Sanguinius being torn apart beneath the ceiling. It is the psychic imprint of their gene-father's final seconds of life, branded into the gene-seed and passed down the line. When a warrior's will can no longer suppress that torrent of memory, he falls into the Black Rage — trapped forever on that final deck, never to return.

Dante has seen too many brothers go mad this way. He has personally killed comrades consumed by the Rage in the middle of battle — not because they lacked loyalty, but because they had nothing left but loyalty. In those moments he stays silent for a long time. Then he gives the next order.

He dreams of that deck too. But he has not fallen.

No one knows how. Perhaps it is sheer will. Perhaps it is something crueler: duty. A Chapter Master cannot afford to go mad. Behind him stands the entire Chapter, a thousand years of legacy, the honor-name of Sanguinius. He does not have permission to fall.

### The Defense of Baal

In the late 41st Millennium, Hive Fleet Leviathan of the Tyranids bore down on the Baal system from beyond the galactic plane. The scale of this hive fleet was beyond even the Imperium's most demented strategic analysts — it was not an invasion force, it was a living, starving cosmic phenomenon, grinding through all organic matter.

Dante made the decision: he summoned every successor Chapter of the Blood Angels back to Baal. It was the most extreme act within his authority — suspending all combat operations, ordering every Chapter born of Sanguinius's gene-seed to return and defend the homeworld. It was the largest gathering of the IX Legion since the Horus Heresy. It would be the last. If they lost, Sanguinius's bloodline would be erased from the galaxy entirely.

Dante stood on the walls — what remained of them — of Baal, wearing the golden mask, watching the sky darken with mycetic spores falling like rain. The warriors beside him grew fewer. One Chapter Master after another of the successor Chapters fell in battle. Baal's surface became the hunting ground of Tyranid bioforms. Sanguinius's statues dissolved under acid. Sanctuaries were choked with spores. The psychic shadow of the Hive Mind pressed down on every psyker until their skulls screamed.

Dante was ready to die here. In truth, he had been hoping for it.

Fate did not grant him that mercy.

At the most desperate moment, Roboute Guilliman arrived in the Baal system at the head of the Indomitus Crusade's reinforcement fleet. Ultramarines and other Loyalist Chapters descended from the sky. The hive fleet was crushed between two jaws. Baal held.

After the battle, Dante stood among mountains of corpses — human, Astartes, Tyranid — layered and caked into Baal's salt flats, forming a new stratum of organic matter. Guilliman walked toward him. The image of the two of them standing together captured both faces of the Imperium: one a Primarch who had just woken from a nine-thousand-year sleep, young, potent, carrying the will to rebuild a golden age; the other a Chapter Master old enough to be weathering off his soul, who had lived in battle and among the memories of the dead for too, too long.

Guilliman looked at Dante and made an unexpected decision. He did not retire Dante. He did not let Dante rest. He appointed Dante as Regent of Imperium Nihilus — charged with governing the half of the Imperium severed from the Imperial core by the Great Rift.

In other words, Guilliman handed half the human Imperium to a fifteen-hundred-year-old veteran. Not out of cruelty, but because only Dante could bear it. Guilliman knew what kind of ruler Imperium Nihilus demanded: an old warrior who had spent fifteen centuries wrestling with darkness, a man of iron who was not afraid to die but would not die easily. Any young, energetic soul thrown into that abyss would be swallowed in moments — only someone like Dante, who had already said goodbye to the light, could hold the line in eternal night.

### Regent of Imperium Nihilus

The Great Rift is a Warp-wound slashed across the galaxy, cutting the Imperium of Man in two. Every Imperial world north of the Rift — including Baal — is cut off from the Astronomican's light from Terra. Warp travel is nearly impossible. Communications are severed. Supplies are gone. That half of the Imperium is called Imperium Nihilus. No Imperial bureaucracy to back it, no decrees from the High Lords of Terra, nothing. Just darkness, solitude, and endless enemies.

Dante's title now is "Regent of Imperium Nihilus." Translation: you are in charge of half the Imperium of Man. Survive on your own. No thanks.

He is doing it. With fifteen hundred years of momentum. He does not show excitement, does not complain, does not reveal weariness in public. But every time he removes the golden mask and sees in the mirror a face so aged it no longer looks like an Astartes, there is only one thought deep in his heart:

Enough.

He wants to die. He wants to lie down in the sand of Baal, beside all the brothers who should have been forgotten long ago, close his eyes and never open them again. He wants to feel no more pull of the Black Rage in his final peace, hear no more voices of the dead, no longer have to say "For Sanguinius" to fresh recruits while his heart echoes hollow like an iron can.

But he cannot.

Because Guilliman called his name. Because Sanguinius's face still wears the golden mask — the mask he inherited from the last Chapter Master, the death mask of Sanguinius himself. Because he knows too well that Imperium Nihilus needs him, the Blood Angels need him, and the living mortals who know he is still here draw just a little more courage from that fact.

A veteran's greatest curse is not death. It is wanting to die and not being allowed to.

## Fate & Present Status

Dante remains the Regent of Imperium Nihilus, commanding the Blood Angels and their successor Chapters across the dark half of the galaxy. His body ages beyond all Astartes norms, his soul bends under the weight of fifteen centuries of unending war — but he does not stop. He leads, he fights, he endures. An ancient prophecy whispers that a "golden warrior" will stand before the Emperor at the end of all things — and many believe that warrior is Dante. Whether true or not, it is a truth Dante himself may cling to in his darkest hours: that his suffering has a page in fate's book not yet reached.

## Verdict

Dante is the oldest living Space Marine in the Imperium, but that is not what makes him unique. What makes him unique is this: he has lived fifteen hundred years, witnessed the deaths of countless comrades and enemies, borne burdens that might have broken even a Primarch — and he is still fighting.

Not for honor. Honor faded eight centuries ago. Not for revenge. The debts he could repay have been repaid, and those he cannot are not for lack of ability — they are simply endless. He fights on because if he does not, more people who should not have to die will die in his place.

An ancient prophecy claims that in the final hour, when the Emperor rises from the Golden Throne, a "golden warrior" will stand before Him. Many readings — and perhaps Dante himself secretly believes — identify that warrior as him.

If true, then Dante's pain is not in vain. He cannot die not merely because the Imperium needs him to fight, but because fate has not yet turned to the page where he is called.

But the book of fate, at this point, is far too thick. Every page Dante has turned is soaked in blood. The first word of every chapter is always "Endure a little longer." He no longer expects a reward at the end. He simply turns the next page. Until the day the Emperor no longer needs him.

Or until the day the Golden Throne shatters. Whichever comes first.

On Baal there stands a golden statue of Sanguinius, wings unfurled, facing the sea of stars. Every generation of Blood Angels recruits kneels before it for their final oath. Dante has knelt before that statue more times than he can count. Long ago, he was a recruit, kneeling to swear. Later, he was a Captain, kneeling to confess. Now he is Chapter Master, Regent, commander of half the Imperium. He kneels before the statue and says nothing. He has no promises left to offer. He only looks at the face of Sanguinius — the face identical to the golden mask on his own — and thinks: Father, how much longer do you need me to hold on.
