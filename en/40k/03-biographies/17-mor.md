# 17 · Alicia Dominica — Mother of the Adepta Sororitas

## Overview

Alicia Dominica, founder of the Adepta Sororitas, one of the first Living Saints ever recognised by the Imperium. She had no Primarch's transhuman blood, no ceramite of the Astartes, no trillions-strong armies of the Imperial Guard. What she had was a blade and a faith. Yet in the 36th Millennium's Age of Apostasy, this woman used her sword to sever the bloodiest tyranny in Imperial history, and used that same faith to secure for the Ecclesiarchy the legal right to bear arms. The very existence of the Sisters of Battle—the Imperium's most singular armed force, a legion of warrior women driven entirely by faith rather than gene-forged augmentation—is her legacy.

## Origin

Dominica's early life has almost no verifiable official record. The Ecclesiarchy's hagiographies are vague where they do not contradict each other, and Imperial historiographers can only assemble a blurred outline: she was born on San Leor, a remote and barren world whose inhabitants were known for their extreme, fervent worship of the Emperor. San Leor was home to an all-female warrior order who called themselves the Daughters of the Emperor, training in combat and spirituality under a discipline as severe as any monastic rule. Dominica grew up in that environment: prayer and swordsmanship were equally the breath of her life.

In that era, worship of the Emperor had already spread across the Imperium for ten thousand years. The Ecclesiarchy's power had swollen across those millennia, and by the 36th Millennium it was no longer a mere religious institution—it was a leviathan of vast wealth, independent armies, and absolute political influence. And the man riding atop that leviathan was named Goge Vandire.

## Deeds

### The Daughters Fall into Vandire's Hands

Vandire was, in every sense, the archetypal Imperial tyrant. He held both the office of High Lord of the Administratum and that of Ecclesiarch, establishing absolute personal rule on Terra. He believed himself the sole voice of the Emperor in the mortal realm; anyone who disagreed ceased to exist, physically, within the day.

As Vandire consolidated power, his attention fell upon the Daughters of the Emperor on San Leor. These warrior women were, to him, the perfect instrument: zealously devout and easily co-opted under a religious mantle; female, thus sidestepping certain delicate prohibitions around armed personnel; and above all, ferociously capable in battle. Vandire arrived on San Leor as Ecclesiarch and reorganised the Daughters into the "Brides of the Emperor"—a name that chills the blood, suggesting these warriors had been wedded to the God upon the Golden Throne. Dominica and her sisters, under Vandire's religious manipulation, became the sharpest blade of his personal dictatorship.

In the days that followed, the bloodletting of the Age of Apostasy began. Vandire deployed Dominica's forces to crush any world that dared question his rule. The Daughters, cocooned in Vandire's meticulously spun lies, believed with all their hearts that they fought for the Emperor—each "heretic" they slaughtered brought them, they thought, one step closer to Him. Dominica's hands were drenched in blood, and she did not know it was innocent blood.

### Face to Face with the Emperor: The Turn at the Golden Throne

The true turning point of the Age of Apostasy came not from without, but from a man Vandire had never once regarded: Sebastian Thor. Thor was an obscure preacher from the world of Dimmamar. When he first began publicly denouncing Vandire's tyranny, no one believed he would amount to anything. But Thor's power lay not in the sword, but in words. His sermons spread like wildfire through the Imperium's lowest strata—farmers, labourers, rank-and-file soldiers, planetary governors crushed under Vandire's heel—all flocked to his banner. A civil war against Vandire erupted, known to history as the Wars of Apostasy.

Amid the flames of this war, a critical figure stepped forward: the Captain-General of the Adeptus Custodes. The Custodians, who had not left the Imperial Palace in ten thousand years, who had never interfered in Imperial politics—even they were stirred. The Captain-General extended Dominica an invitation: come to the Palace. See the "god" you believe you serve.

Dominica took her five closest sisters—Katherine, Lucia, Mina, Arabella, and Silvana—into the depths of the Imperial Palace. They passed through the Eternity Gate, walked past the silent ranks of ten thousand years of Custodians, and at last stood before the Golden Throne.

No one can describe with certainty what happened in that moment. The Custodians present recorded only one line: "They knelt. Then they rose—no longer Vandire's, but the Emperor's."

Dominica never spoke of the experience afterward. But every version of the legend converges on the same truth: when she emerged from the Throne Room, the confusion in her eyes was gone. The Emperor—the real Emperor, the one who truly sat upon the Golden Throne, whose psychic will had sustained mankind's Imperium for ten thousand years—had touched her soul directly. Every word Vandire had ever spoken to her, every interpretation, every theological argument, burned through like paper in that instant. Dominica saw the truth: Vandire had never spoken a single word on the Emperor's behalf. He was merely a mortal tyrant hiding behind the Emperor's name.

### The Execution of Vandire

Dominica returned with her sisters to Vandire's palace. Vandire greeted them with his customary arrogant smile—he thought this was a routine audience, that these warrior women were still his puppets on strings.

Dominica drew her sword.

Vandire never understood, even as he died. His last words, it is said, were shouted at Dominica—that he would not accept "judgement from a woman." Dominica's answer was to run her blade through his throat. The tyrant who had ruled the Imperium for decades, who had unleashed a bloody civil war, died at the hands of a woman he believed he controlled utterly.

That moment was not only Vandire's end, but the purification of the Daughters of the Emperor. What Dominica severed was not merely one man's throat, but the lies of an entire era that had been layered upon them. They were no longer anyone's brides—they were the Emperor's Daughters. They always had been.

### The Birth of the Adepta Sororitas

Vandire's death did not end the civil war immediately. Thor's uprising seized control of more and more systems, ultimately forcing the Imperium's remaining High Lords back to the negotiating table. But the Age of Apostasy left behind a thorny question: the Ecclesiarchy, under Vandire, had abused armed force and nearly destroyed the Imperium—what now?

In the negotiations between Thor and the High Lords, the most critical clause was this: the Ecclesiarchy would forever be forbidden from keeping "men under arms." This was meant to prevent a second Vandire, to ensure the Ecclesiarchy could never again mount a military rebellion. But if the Ecclesiarchy were fully disarmed, who then would protect the faithful civilians on the battlefields of the galaxy? Who would guard the Imperium's shrines against Chaos, xenos, and heresy? With what, exactly, was faith itself to be defended?

Dominica gave the answer.

She led her warrior women forward and, with their deeds in the Wars of Apostasy as proof, proposed a solution unprecedented in Imperial political history: retain arms, but only for women. This clause was later formally enshrined in the Decree Passive—the Ecclesiarchy may not keep men under arms, but may maintain combat forces composed entirely of women. Because, in the most technical reading of the text, women are not "men."

This was one of the cleverest feats of legal arbitrage in political history. Dominica was no politician versed in legal terminology, but she understood better than any bureaucrat present that faith stripped of its sword ceases to be faith and becomes mere rhetoric. With one precise legal wedge, she preserved for the Ecclesiarchy the sole means of resisting the galaxy's darkness.

Under Thor's authority, the Daughters of the Emperor were formally reorganised as the Adepta Sororitas, with Dominica appointed as the first Abbess. The Sisters of Battle thus became a unique presence on the Imperium's military landscape: they were not Astartes—no gene-forged superhuman physiques; they were not Astra Militarum—no trillion-strong massed armies. They relied on pure faith. Every Battle Sister, from the day she swore her vows, was forged into a being that could not be corrupted by Chaos, could not be broken by terror, could not be undone by defeat. The Emperor was their sword; faith was their shield.

### Martyrdom and Canonisation

Dominica did not live long to savour her victory. Shortly after the founding of the Adepta Sororitas, she and her five sisters—the same five who had walked with her into the Golden Throne Room—were caught in a ferocious ambush by Chaos forces during a crusade. Outnumbered, out of ammunition, all six fell in battle. But they did not yield a single step. Their six bodies formed a defensive line where they fell, holding the enemy long enough for Imperial reinforcements to arrive.

After her death, the Order venerated her as a Living Saint, one of the first generation to be formally recognised by the Imperium. Within the Orders, the tradition of venerating "The Six Martyrs" endures to this day—Dominica and her five sisters stand side by side in the murals of their shrines, forever young, forever gripping their blades.

## Fate & Present Status

Dominica died in battle shortly after the Adepta Sororitas' founding, ambushed by overwhelming Chaos forces. Canonised as a Living Saint, she remains the eternal patron of the Orders Militant. The Six Martyrs are among the most venerated figures in the Ecclesiarchy, their shrine on Ophelia VII drawing millions of pilgrims each year. The Decree Passive holds to this day: the Ecclesiarchy keeps no men under arms, but the Sisters of Battle remain the Imperium's most incorruptible warriors of faith.

## Verdict

In the ten-thousand-year history of the Imperium of Man, the vast majority of heroes are remembered for transcendent martial power: Primarchs splitting mountains, Astartes grinding xenos to pulp, Custodians holding the Eternity Gate. Dominica's legend is entirely different. She began not as a hero—she was a tyrant's blade, a warrior raised on lies. She killed people. Innocent people. And at the time, she genuinely believed she was doing the right thing.

What changed her was not strength. It was truth. When she stood before the Golden Throne and the Emperor's will touched her soul, the choice she made was not the one most people would make in that position: defending herself, rationalising, or breaking. She chose to draw her sword, turn around, and kill the man who had deceived her all her life. That required not courage—it required the courage to admit "I was wrong," which is far harder than trading blows with a daemon of Chaos.

Her second legacy is equally significant. After Vandire's death, the Imperium's power structure faced a genuine dilemma: an unarmed faith cannot survive in this galaxy, but an armed faith could at any moment become the next Vandire. Dominica sliced through this deadlock with one stroke of precise legal intuition: "Men shall not bear arms, but women may." The formulation sounds almost like sophistry, yet it has functioned for ten thousand years without collapsing. In ten millennia, not a single Battle Sister has ever turned traitor. Their corruption rate is the lowest of any Imperial military organisation, lower even than most Space Marine Chapters. This is not the triumph of law—it is Dominica's design. She was not building an army. She was building a way of fusing faith into the very structure of a fighting force, so that the structure itself resists corruption.

She proved that faith can be a sword—when Vandire used faith as a pretext to butcher, she used faith as a weapon to strike back. She also proved that faith can be a shield—when she fought to preserve the Ecclesiarchy's right to bear arms, she did it not with brute force, but with a kind of wisdom one can only call "righteous cunning." In the universe of the 41st Millennium, fists and ceramite are hard currency, but Dominica tells you: when a soul burns bright enough, even the Custodians who have not left the Palace in ten thousand years will open the gates for you.
