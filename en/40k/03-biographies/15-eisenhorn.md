# 15 · Gregor Eisenhorn

## Overview

Gregor Eisenhorn is an Inquisitor of the Imperial Inquisition's Ordo Xenos. He wrote a book — the *Malleus* — that influenced the Imperial Inquisition for centuries, and was the finest Puritan theoretician of his generation. But his true legacy is not his book. It is that he spent his life completing the most chilling of all the possible endings an Inquisitor can meet: from the Imperium's purest Puritan, step by step, he became the very monster he once hunted.

The Eisenhorn Trilogy (*Xenos*, *Malleus*, *Hereticus*) by Dan Abnett is a foundational work of Warhammer 40,000 fiction. It is not merely an Inquisitor's casebook — it is a surgical dissection of "corruption." The most terrifying thing about the trilogy is that you follow Eisenhorn's perspective the entire way, and every single decision he makes sounds, in the moment, like the right one.

## Origin

What Eisenhorn experienced in his early life is almost entirely blank in the official record. An Inquisitor is not required to disclose his origins, and Eisenhorn's character carries a deliberately constructed silence — he does not want anyone to know where he came from.

We know only that he trained under Inquisitor Hapshant and was inducted into the Ordo Xenos. Hapshant belonged to the old-school Puritan mould that treated dogma as oxygen: xenos must be exterminated, the pyre is the only destination for heretics, forbidden knowledge must not be touched — because knowledge itself is already sinful. The young Eisenhorn inherited this rigid creed wholesale and became known among his fellow Inquisitors as "the man least likely to be corrupted." He was cold in manner, precise in enforcement, and never crossed the line. He even wrote the *Malleus*, a field manual used by the Imperial Inquisition for centuries — systematically teaching you how to identify traces of the Warp, how to deal with Daemonhosts, how to distinguish true loyalty from false on a corrupted world. Within the Puritan camp, he was the gold standard.

Had he died gloriously in the line of duty twenty years into his career, his name would have become a footnote on the "Model Inquisitor" page of Imperial textbooks.

He did not die.

## Deeds

### The *Malus Codicium*: The First Line

Eisenhorn's first major case was the investigation of House Glaw's conspiracy on the planet Gudrun. House Glaw was a hereditary heretic dynasty entrenched at the highest levels of society — outwardly cultivating noble wealth and prestige, while underground reconstructing an ancient legacy of forbidden knowledge. One member of the family, a being named Pontius Glaw, had been killed and his soul subsequently imprisoned inside a psychic crystal, creating an entity without a body but with its will fully intact. And the intelligence he held pointed directly to a xenos species the Imperium had long since expunged from every record: the Saruthi.

The Saruthi possessed the ability to warp physical laws. Their architecture refused Euclidean geometry; their weapons were not projectiles or energy beams, but some form of rearrangement of reality itself. When the pursuit dead-ended, Eisenhorn faced a question that short-circuited the entire Puritan creed: he had in his possession a specific forbidden tome — the *Malus Codicium* — containing the language, rituals, and weaknesses of the Saruthi. What should a Puritan do? Burn the book, and then charge headlong against a completely unknown enemy with nothing but faith.

Eisenhorn opened it.

He used the contents of the *Malus Codicium* as a weapon on the battlefield and defeated the Saruthi. The price was that from that moment on, he was no longer the "incorruptible" Inquisitor. Something in the book left a mark on his soul. He did not yet feel it.

### Daemonhost: Knowledge Is Poison

The second major case dragged Eisenhorn into trouble deeper than any xenos of the Warp. On the planet Thracian Primaris, a Daemonhost disaster erupted in the most densely populated hab-zones. This was not xenos contamination — this was true Chaos manifestation. A psychic entity called Cherubael, powerful enough to tear open rifts in realspace, had already destroyed the ecology of at least one entire world. It was a Daemonhost, originally human, forcibly bound by heretics into a physical vessel through sorcery, deployable as a weapon at its master's whim.

In this hunt, Eisenhorn confronted, up close for the first time, an inescapable fact: the Imperium forbids you to understand Chaos — but if you do not understand Chaos, you have no chance of defeating it. The Puritan dogma teaches you how to burn a heretic, but it does not teach you how to walk away alive when a daemon is already standing in front of you.

He destroyed Cherubael's current host vessel. But Cherubael's essence cannot be killed — it merely recoiled back into the Warp, waiting for the next vessel. That battle left Eisenhorn with twin wounds, physical and psychic. His nerves had been touched by the Warp; a scar of some kind was growing beneath the skin, slowly.

At the same time, certain eyes within the Inquisition began to study him in earnest. Not scrutinising his achievements — scrutinising his methods. His toolkit was now packed with forbidden items that "should have been surrendered and destroyed." His journals contained growing passages that even his peers could no longer parse. The question every Inquisitor most dreads being asked in court was creeping toward him: *What, exactly, is the difference between the means you use and the target you hunt?*

### The Fire That Burns Inward

By the opening of the third volume, Eisenhorn was no longer the paragon. The Inquisition's internal scrutiny of him had escalated from "discreet observation" to "formal pursuit." His nemesis — the Puritan extremist Inquisitor Osma — had openly placed his name on the Radical list. He was stripped of all official authority and resource allocation; the hunter became the hunted.

His former protégé, Gideon Ravenor — who would inherit his cause and his convictions — later summarised his mentor with a sentence that cuts to the bone: *"Eisenhorn crossed every line with a perfect justification, delivered on the spot, that left everyone in the room speechless. The problem is that he crossed too many of them — and by the time he wanted to stop and count back, he could no longer see where the first line had been."*

Backed into a corner, Eisenhorn made a decision that even the most extreme Radicals would hesitate to take: he created a Daemonhost with his own hands. He took Cherubael — the same Warp entity he had once destroyed — and forcibly bound it into a new vessel through a binding ritual, then turned it into his own weapon.

The man who wrote the *Malleus* became a daemon's master. The man once labelled "incorruptible" walked through his days with a Daemonhost at his side.

He did not wake up one morning and decide to become a monster. He made countless correct decisions — each one appearing unconnected to the last — and screwed his life, bolt by bolt, down a corridor with no exit. He loves the Imperium. He wants more than anyone to protect it. But the weapons he reaches for are precisely the things the Imperium commands you to destroy.

### Becoming the Abyss

By the end, Eisenhorn had lived beyond four hundred years — propped up by psychic power and semi-licit life-extension technologies. He wore long black robes. At his side walked Cherubael, bound by the tightest psychic chains. He still hunted the Imperium's true enemies, still served the Emperor in his own fashion. But the Inquisition had placed his name on the "pending" list. He himself could no longer say what he was — a heretic? He still killed heretics. A Puritan? His hands were no cleaner than any heretic's.

An Inquisitor's ultimate fate is not to be killed by heresy. It is to spend all your strength fighting the abyss — and in the end, to have become it.

## Fate & Present Status

Eisenhorn's current whereabouts are unknown. He operates beyond the reach of any Inquisition sanction, a rogue agent who still believes himself to be serving the Imperium. Cherubael remains at his side. He is hunted by both Imperial and Chaos forces alike. The Inquisition has neither caught him nor closed his file.

## Verdict

*"He who gazes into the abyss finds the abyss gazing back."* Across the entire galaxy of the 41st Millennium, no one embodies this line more completely than Gregor Eisenhorn.

What chills the spine is not that "he is guilty" — it is that there is no *single* sin you can point to. He was not seduced by Slaanesh. He never knelt to Tzeentch. He never raised the banner of rebellion at the final hour. Every step of his fall was a rational decision made to solve a more urgent, more righteous problem: the Saruthi could only be defeated through the *Malus Codicium*; daemons can only be fought by understanding daemons; when hunted across the hunting grounds by your own colleagues, you bring out the sharpest blade you can find — even if that blade is a daemon. Isolate each frame and you find nothing to fault. String them together into a film, and you see, in this "incorruptible" Inquisitor, a figure closer to Chaos than any heretic he ever personally consigned to the flames.

Eisenhorn's fate is the Inquisition's own parable. An Inquisitor wields power closer to "absolute" than anything else in the Imperium — with a glance he can sign the death warrant of a planet's population; with a word he can nail an entire Legion to the list of traitors. And the more competent the Inquisitor, the more that power drives him into a death spiral: the closer you draw to the abyss, the more darkness you see — so what guarantee do you have that you will not sink into that darkness yourself?

His protégé Ravenor inherited many of the master's methods and spent his own life trying to avoid arriving at that same destination. Late in his life, Ravenor spoke a line that reads simultaneously as an epitaph for his teacher and a warning to himself: *"Eisenhorn will never admit, not for a single day, that he was wrong. Deep down, he knows — but he cannot fight that war. Because the moment you admit you took the first wrong step, you admit that everything in your hands is wrong. And no one can keep standing under that kind of weight."*

Eisenhorn does not regret. The most terrifying thing about his life is precisely that he has never regretted anything.
