# 24 · The Inquisition

## Overview

The Inquisition—formally the Holy Orders of the Emperor's Inquisition—is the most feared institution in the Imperium of Man. Its remit is to defend humanity against threats from within: xenos contamination, daemonic incursion, heretical thought, genetic mutation. If the Astra Militarum is the Imperium's fist and the Adeptus Astartes is the Imperium's sword, then the Inquisition is the Imperium's immune system—present in every organ, ready at any moment to excise the infected tissue before the cancer spreads.

An Inquisitor wields an absolute authority unique in Imperial law. Any Inquisitor may, at any time and in any place, on their own judgement alone, declare a planet subject to Exterminatus—billions of souls reduced to ash beneath their signature. No superior to approve the order. No retrospective tribunal. No oversight committee. An Inquisitor answers only to the Emperor Himself, and the Emperor has not spoken in ten thousand years.

This system sounds insane. In the galaxy of the 41st Millennium, it may be the only reason the Imperium has survived this long.

## Origin

The Inquisition was not designed by the Emperor's hand. The Emperor, during the Unification Wars and the Great Crusade, established every core institution of the Imperium—the Legions, the Custodians, the alliance with the Mechanicum—but the Inquisition was not among His plans.

After the Horus Heresy ended in M31, the Imperium was broken to its bones. The nine Loyalist Legions were reduced to remnants. The Emperor had been interred on the Golden Throne, a half-dead religious symbol. Half the Primarchs were dead or damned. Malcador the Sigilite had been reduced to ash by the Golden Throne. The two greatest minds in Imperial history—the Emperor and Malcador—were, respectively, silent and gone.

Yet the threats did not die with Horus. The Traitors fled into the Eye of Terror, and Chaos worship took root like fungi on countless worlds. Xenos expanded aggressively into the Imperium's weakened state. Most lethal of all: humanity's own fear and greed, sprouting madly in the ruins of order.

The Inquisition's direct predecessor was a set of clandestine "special operations cells" that Malcador had secretly assembled during the chaos of the Heresy. Their members included loyalist Space Marines, Custodians, Sisters of Silence, and even certain "former heretics" who had been granted absolution. Their task was not frontal combat but infiltration, pursuit, interrogation, assassination—handling precisely those matters that could not be solved with fleets. Malcador himself put it bluntly: "The Imperium needs someone to do its dirty work."

After Malcador's death, these loose cells were formally reorganised into the Inquisition by the survivors. The founding Inquisitors laid down one iron law: the Inquisition would never have a "Supreme Inquisitor." They had watched, with their own eyes, how Horus—a Warmaster granted absolute authority—had fallen and become a slave to Chaos. To concentrate ultimate power in one person was to hand Chaos a ready-made instrument of corruption. So the Inquisition was designed as an aggressively flat, cellular network: every Inquisitor holds exactly equal authority. There is no chain of command, only intelligence-sharing and voluntary cooperation.

This design had two consequences: first, no single rogue Inquisitor could destroy the entire institution the way Horus destroyed the Great Crusade. Second, no one—no one—could stop an Inquisitor when they made a mistake.

## The Three Major Ordos

The Inquisition's core is divided into three Ordos Majoris, each corresponding to the deadliest threats facing the Imperium. These are not headquarters buildings and uniforms—they function more as labels. An Inquisitor affiliates with an Ordo based on their primary quarry; in practice, the boundaries between the three are perpetually blurred.

**The Ordo Malleus** monitors the threat of Chaos—specifically, daemonic incursion into realspace. When a daemonic manifestation occurs, the Ordo Malleus will seal off the entire sector, execute every witness and contact (innocent or otherwise), and destroy all related records. The Imperium officially maintains that daemons "do not exist"—the Ecclesiarchy's public position is that the Chaos Gods are merely "xenos false deities." The Ordo Malleus is the violent enforcer of this lie. Its most famous chamber militant is the Grey Knights—an entire Chapter of psychic Space Marines cultivated from the Emperor's own gene-seed, whose very existence is classified at the highest level of secrecy.

**The Ordo Xenos** deals with alien civilisations. Imperial ideology demands the eradication of all xenos, but reality is nothing but compromises: the Aeldari craftworlds are too vast to destroy, the T'au Empire is expanding too fast to contain, and Ork WAAAGH!s come in endless waves. The Ordo Xenos' task is to decide when to wage war, when to tolerate temporarily, and when to use xenos against xenos. Its chamber militant is the Deathwatch—a cross-Chapter special forces body composed of Space Marines seconded from their parent Chapters.

**The Ordo Hereticus** is the youngest of the three Ordos and arguably the busiest. It was founded in the aftermath of the Age of Apostasy in M36—when the Ecclesiarch Goge Vandire nearly tore the Imperium apart while claiming to speak with "the Emperor's will." The Ordo Hereticus monitors ideological contamination within the Imperium: heretic cults, unsanctioned variants of Emperor-worship, political sedition, mutants. Its chamber militant is the Adepta Sororitas; though nominally part of the Ecclesiarchy, the Sisters of Battle operate in tight alignment with the Ordo Hereticus.

The division of labour among the three Ordos is a tangled mess in practice. A daemon-worshipping heretic cult triggers both Malleus and Hereticus simultaneously. A world tainted by xenos technology—if the contaminant is a daemon-possessed xenos relic—involves all three. Relations between Ordos range from cordial cooperation to mutual assassination. The Inquisition's greatest enemy is sometimes another Inquisitor.

## Puritans and Radicals

The deepest fracture within the Inquisition lies not between Ordos but along ideological lines: Puritan versus Radical.

Puritans hold to a single, brutal principle: everything of Chaos and xenos must be destroyed by pure means alone. You do not fight daemons with daemons. You do not use xenos technology against xenos. Their logic is hard-won: anyone who makes a deal with Chaos, no matter how noble the intention, is eventually consumed by that deal—Horus is the warning for all time. "Just this once" today becomes "one more time" tomorrow, and the day after that you can no longer tell whether you are defending the Imperium or serving Chaos.

Radicals hold the opposite view: the Imperium is fighting a war it cannot possibly win. The Chaos Gods are eternal, and the Imperium is at an absolute disadvantage in both the material realm and the Warp. Adhere to pure means, and the Imperium simply slides into extinction at a slightly slower pace. Radicals will use Daemonhosts, xenos-tech weaponry, forbidden psychic arts, and even limited cooperation with xenos. Their logic is equally clear: the Imperium is already sustaining the Astronomican with psykers (mutants) and already waging war with Space Marines (gene-forged superhumans)—the notion of "pure means" is itself a lie. The true red line is not the method but the outcome: does the Imperium still stand.

The moderates satisfy neither camp. They consider Puritans too rigid and Radicals too dangerous, yet lack a clear position on specific issues. Most Inquisitors self-identify as moderates—while simultaneously accusing everyone else of extremism.

The classic case study of this internal war is Gregor Eisenhorn. For the first hundred years of his career he was the archetypal Puritan, authoring a treatise on daemonology that shaped Inquisitorial thinking for centuries, its core thesis being: "any engagement with the tools of Chaos leads to corruption." Over two hundred years later, he had himself become a hunted renegade sorcerer—to bring down a powerful daemon, he was forced to bind another daemon, and slid step by step into the abyss. His story is a forbidden parable within the Inquisition: today's Puritan may be tomorrow's Radical, and the day after's heretic.

## The Power and the Loneliness of the Inquisitor

An Inquisitor's operational procedure runs like this: they arrive in orbit aboard a small, fast vessel and demand the entire planetary government assemble aboard within the hour. Failure to appear constitutes a declaration of rebellion, authorising immediate orbital bombardment by the Imperial Navy. The governor arrives. A few questions are asked. A judgement is made as to whether the planet is tainted. If the answer is yes, Exterminatus may be signed into effect.

Perfectly legal. An Inquisitor's authority derives from the Emperor, and the Emperor cannot say no—He cannot say anything.

But the price is absolute solitude. An Inquisitor has no superior to consult, no colleague with whom to share the burden (even fellow Inquisitors of the same Ordo are equals; there is no "assistant" relationship), no court of appeal, no populace to confide in—the vast majority of ordinary citizens are not even permitted to hear the word "Inquisition." Any mistake an Inquisitor makes—faulty judgement, flawed intelligence, loss of emotional control—can mean millions of lives. And no one will ever tell them "you were wrong." They must make their decisions alone in an endless grey zone, and then live with the consequences.

Many Inquisitors go mad. Not corrupted by Chaos—crushed by their own authority. After you have destroyed your hundredth world, it becomes very difficult to keep believing that every Exterminatus you signed was correct.

An Inquisitor's manner of death is more statistically telling than their lifespan: a minority fall in combat. Some are consumed by their own Radical methods and become what they once hunted. A significant proportion are executed by another Inquisitor (because "they went too far"). And many simply disappear—gone to hunt some daemon or xenos that can never be caught, and never heard from again.

## Verdict

The Inquisition is the most concentrated paradox in the Imperium of Man.

It was designed as the Imperium's last line of defence, yet every brick in that line can detonate inward against its own. An Inquisitor, in the course of defending the Imperium, may kill more people than an entire Ork WAAAGH!. To prevent Chaos corruption, they may employ forbidden arts whose distinction from Chaos worship lies solely in intention—and that intention is known only to them.

The Imperium has never solved the question of "who watches the Inquisition." In theory, Inquisitors watch each other—but this mechanism is a near-comedy in practice. A Radical Inquisitor makes a decision that can destabilise an entire sector; a Puritan Inquisitor may spend two hundred years gathering sufficient evidence to execute them—and in those two hundred years, the sector has already burned to ash.

But abolish the Inquisition? The Imperium would not survive a decade. In a world without the Inquisition, Genestealer Cults would infiltrate half the Imperium's planets decades before the Hive Fleets even arrived. Chaos cults would incubate daemon-gates in the underhive of every hive city. Xenos civilisations would build, in the Imperium's blind spots, interstellar alliances larger than the T'au Empire. The "normal functioning" of the Imperium in the 41st Millennium—if that phrase still has meaning—is sustained, in half its measure, by the terror of the Inquisition. An Inquisitor does not need to actually appear on every planet. So long as everyone knows an Inquisitor *could* appear, that invisible chain holds the entire Imperium bound.

The classic argument defending absolute power throughout human history has been "power requires checks." The Inquisition's founding principle is the opposite: in the face of certain existential threats, checks themselves become a luxury—even a fatality. When a Warp rift is dragging an entire world into the Eye of Terror, spending three months on due process means the world is already gone.

Thus the Inquisition's logic is forced to the extreme: grant an individual unchecked authority; accept that this individual may become a second Horus; stake everything on that individual's judgement and moral resilience. In any other era of human history, this system would be incomprehensible tyranny. In the 41st Millennium, it is a surviving rational choice.

The Grey Knights have a maxim: "We are the hammer. We endure the horror so that others may live." The Inquisition endures something still harder to digest—absolute power. The cost it has exacted from the Imperium, and the salvation it has bought, remain unsettled after ten thousand years.
