# 13 · Erebus — The First Mortal Prophet of Chaos

## Overview

Erebus — in the galaxy of the 41st Millennium, this name is synonymous with "loathsome." He is the Dark Apostle of the XVII Legion, the Word Bearers, the first true mortal prophet of the Chaos Gods, the hand that personally injected the corruption of Chaos into Horus. If the Horus Heresy were a plague, he is Patient Zero — not the victim, but the one who carried the virus into the room. He has never ascended to daemonhood; ten thousand years later, he remains a purely mortal worshipper of Chaos.

## Origin

Erebus was born on Colchis — a world steeped in religion to the marrow. Before Lorgar's arrival, the old faith of Colchis had been running for millennia, and Erebus grew up in that environment, mastering the skill-set most essential to a priest of the old creed: eloquence, patience, a preternatural nose for the human heart, and a piety utterly unconstrained by limits.

After Lorgar unified the faith of Colchis, Erebus immediately aligned himself with the Primarch and became the central operative within the Word Bearers' religious apparatus. He translated Lorgar's doctrine into executable ritual systems — the grand masses after every compliance, the consecration rites of every Imperial temple, the compilation of every volume of the Imperial Creed — the organisational machinery behind all of it bore his hand. More critically, he created and expanded the Warrior Lodges across the Legions — outwardly a cross-Legion fraternal network, in truth a clandestine web spanning eighteen Legions. While Lorgar was still openly kneeling to the Emperor, Erebus was already in the shadows, weaving the channels through which the faith of Chaos would later spread.

## Deeds

### Into the Eye of Terror

After the Emperor destroyed Monarchia, Lorgar collapsed into a crisis of faith. Erebus did not collapse — his faith had never been built upon the Emperor. He spoke to Lorgar the words that changed the fate of the galaxy: *If the Emperor refuses to be a god, it is because He is not a god. The true gods are elsewhere.*

Lorgar dispatched him with a fleet into the Eye of Terror to find those "true gods." The Eye is the wound where realspace and the Warp bleed into each other; most who enter go mad, or die, or both simultaneously. Erebus did not go mad. Deep within the Warp, he made direct contact with Chaos — not seduced, not deceived, but actively seeking and comprehending everything he found. He returned with a cold conclusion: the Chaos Gods are real, and their power is real. He was the first human in history to comprehend the nature of Chaos with full lucidity, as a mortal, and choose conversion of his own volition. In his worldview, Chaos is not evil — it is the cosmic truth the Emperor deliberately concealed. The Emperor's Imperial Truth is a burial shroud wrapped over the eyes of all humanity. The second half of Erebus's life has been devoted to tearing that shroud away for everyone.

### The Corruption on Davin

The knowledge of Chaos did not stay theoretical. Erebus and Lorgar together formulated the plan: plant Chaos inside Horus's body.

The Warmaster Horus was wounded on the moon of Davin by a xenos-wrought blade, the Anathame, and the injury was so severe that the Legion's Chief Apothecary could do nothing. Erebus approached the sickbed in the guise of a Word Bearers Chaplain, claiming he had a remedy — an ancient ritual from Colchis. In truth, it was an elaborately staged Chaos rite. He drew the Anathame's edge across the Warmaster's wound and poured Warp-corruption along the cut. Horus's soul, in his coma, was cast into the depths of the Warp, where the Chaos Gods, wearing the face of the Emperor, showed him a false future in which the Emperor abandoned His sons and ascended to godhood alone.

When Horus awoke, he was no longer the Horus who had been. The seed of doubt had been planted. The hand that planted it was Erebus's. Without that blade, the entire Horus Heresy might never have happened.

### The Web of Conspiracy

During the civil war, Erebus's battlefield was not the planetary surface — it was the shadows within the traitor camp itself. He moved among the rebel Primarchs, passing messages, stoking antagonisms, smoothing over fractures. An alliance of nine demigods who distrusted one another — Erebus was the only man who could navigate among them without being killed by any of them. Horus himself eventually recognised the danger he posed and nearly killed him with his own hands — the hideous scar that runs from Erebus's forehead down across his face was left by Horus's Lightning Claws. He did not die. He returned to the Word Bearers' camp and kept working.

Within the Word Bearers, the warrior Argel Tal — the Primarch's most trusted — grew wary of Erebus's conspiratorial methods and became a variable in the plan. Erebus did not hesitate. He personally drove a dagger into this brother who had fought beside him. In the Word Bearers' doctrine, brother-betrayal is an unforgivable sin. Erebus does not care about doctrine — doctrine is something he wrote for other people.

His hands are soaked in the blood of the entire civil war. The wound on Davin bled from his blade. The Burning of Prospero carried misdirection he fed through the Lodges. The Word Bearers' participation in the Dropsite Massacre on Isstvan V was his arrangement. If one were to draw up a ledger of responsibility for every trillion deaths of the Heresy, the top three names would be the Emperor, Horus, and Erebus.

### Never Ascended

This is Erebus's most distinctive feature. All the Primarchs eventually ascended into Daemon Princes — he did not. The Chaos Gods have extended their offer to him countless times; he has declined every time. On the surface, this is the arrogance of a Chaos Undivided devotee — he serves no single god, therefore he accepts no single god's gift. But deeper lies his attachment to his identity as the "mortal prophet." In his own narrative, he is not Chaos's slave — he is Chaos's voice in the mortal realm. Once he ascends, he moves from "the one who reveals the truth" to "part of the truth." He wants to stand beside the truth and speak for it, not become it.

For ten thousand years, he has dwelled within the Eye of Terror, continuing to devise rituals, compile scripture, and orchestrate conspiracies for the Word Bearers. His face is covered in Colchisian ritual glyphs — carved into his own flesh, stroke by stroke, each one a reminder: this body will age, will wound, will be killed. The masters of Chaos have granted him no deathless form, because he does not need one.

## Fate & Present Status

Erebus remains active in the Eye of Terror, a fixture of the Word Bearers' Dark Council. He continues to write, to scheme, to preach. He is still mortal, still ageing, still bearing the scar Horus gave him. In a pantheon of ascended demigods and immortal daemons, he is enduring proof that faith alone — pure, undiluted, fanatical faith — is a force that requires no physical transcendence to reshape the galaxy.

## Verdict

In the entire Warhammer universe, you will struggle to find a character who inspires a purer desire to simply *kill him* than Erebus. Other villains carry some tragic undertone — Angron was dragged from the corpses of his comrades by the Emperor; Magnus shattered the Imperium's greatest project with good intentions; Lorgar had his faith personally ground to dust by the Emperor. They all possess the dimension of "if only that thing hadn't happened, he might have remained loyal."

Erebus does not.

He chose, from beginning to end. The old faith did not force him into darkness — he grew up inside it, but he was more clear-eyed than anyone in that faith. Lorgar did not push him toward Chaos — Lorgar himself was falling apart; it was Erebus who, like a drover hauling a wounded beast, pulled Lorgar toward new water. The horror of the Eye of Terror did not corrupt him — he walked in, looked around, decided *this is the truth*, and came back with the dossier. He is not a tragic figure pushed along by events; he is the driver who kept his foot on the accelerator the whole way down.

He would tell you: *I did not start the rebellion — I merely showed the evidence to a son who did not know his father was lying. I did not learn from devils — I merely found the true masters of the universe and told my Primarch. I did not kill anyone — I merely gave the tiller a gentle nudge at every fork in the road. You should not hate me. You should hate the God-Emperor who could let a single blade destroy His entire Imperium.*

The logic is seamless. The morality is unspeakable. And Erebus knows the gap between the two. It is that gap that makes him the perfect specimen of the mortal Chaos prophet: his faith requires no reward; his piety needs no immortality as proof. The Chaos Primarchs are demigods, so dazzling that people stare at the battlefield and forget who lit the fire. Erebus is the one who lit it — absent from every mural, every monument. But trace the Horus Heresy from its root to its final branch, and you see: the hand on the blade was not Horus's hand. It was Erebus's.

The most hateful man in the galaxy is, by coincidence, one of the most important mortals in Imperial history. You can hate him. He does not mind. He may even take it as a kind of praise — because in the doctrine of Chaos, to be loathed by the entire galaxy is the true fulfilment of a prophet's mission.
