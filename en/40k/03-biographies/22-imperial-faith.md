# 22 · Lives of the Imperial Faith

> The Ecclesiarchy, worshipping the Emperor as God, commands the hearts of trillions and spans ten thousand years. Yet the Emperor, in life, enforced the Imperial Truth—atheism absolute—and forbade all worship. Only when He was entombed upon the Golden Throne did He become, against everything He ever stood for, a god. The irony of this may rank as the deepest in galactic history. Three figures within it illuminate the wellspring of faith: one, Euphrati Keeler, a remembrancer, from whose terror burst the first sacred fire; one, Sebastian Thor, a confessor in sackcloth, who raised his voice and toppled a tyrant; one, Alicia Dominica, blade of the Emperor, who beheld the face of her God and executed the usurper with her own hand. Three mortals—no Space Marines, no Primarchs. Yet the edifice of Imperial faith rests upon a foundation laid by three pairs of mortal hands. Their lives are gathered here.

---

## Euphrati Keeler — The First Living Saint

### Overview

Euphrati Keeler, a remembrancer attached to the fleets at the close of the Great Crusade, the Imperium of Man's first Living Saint. She was no warrior, no psyker, no being designed to carry a messianic burden. She was simply a person who happened to be there when the Horus Heresy erupted—a journalist with a pict-recorder and audio equipment. But terror drew out of her something she did not know she possessed, and that something would define Imperial faith for the next ten thousand years.

### Deeds

In the era of the Great Crusade, the Emperor dispatched artists, poets, photographers, and writers to accompany the Expeditionary Fleets—to illuminate the conquered worlds with the light of reason and art. This was the original purpose of the remembrancer institution. In the Emperor's vision, science, art, and empirical record would form the bedrock of the Imperial Truth: no gods, no superstition, only the radiance of human civilisation. Keeler was assigned to the 63rd Expeditionary Fleet, operating alongside a support flotilla attached to the Warmaster Horus Lupercal's flagship, the *Vengeful Spirit*. That ship would later become the greatest tragic stage in galactic history.

When the Horus Heresy broke out, Keeler's vessel came under attack by Chaos forces. The interior of the ship was penetrated by the Warp, and a daemon—Chaos' formless horror congealed into physical reality—appeared in the corridors. The Space Marines were fighting elsewhere. Mortals scattered in panic. Keeler had no weapon, no psychic training, no theological doctrine to lean upon.

But in her terror, she did one thing: she raised her hand and cried the Emperor's name.

Not an incantation. Not scripture. Just the Emperor's name. And the daemon was burned to ash by a blaze of golden light.

This was not psychic power—Keeler had no psyker gene. It was not a weapon—her hand was empty. It was not a phenomenon any known science could explain. The first miracle of the first Living Saint occurred thus, without warning, in a woman consumed by terror, who thought of nothing but instinctively believed the Emperor could protect her.

The weight of this event cannot be overstated. At that time, the Emperor still lived in His Palace on Terra, and the Imperial Truth remained official ideology—explicitly prohibiting all forms of religious worship. Yet at this very moment, the Emperor's own will manifested a miracle through a mortal's faith. In other words: the Emperor, even as He ruled the Imperium in the name of godlessness, had Himself already begun to "become a god."

Keeler survived. She could not explain what she had experienced, but she knew what she had experienced. Thereafter she began to tell the survivors what she had seen—not as a preacher, but as an eyewitness. What she told them was simple: the Emperor is not just a man. He is becoming something greater. And we—we mortals in the dark—have only Him to rely upon.

Her words ignited a wildfire. The Horus Heresy was the bloodiest civil war in Imperial history; people crushed under the weight of terror, despair, and death seized upon anything they could hold. Keeler provided an answer. What she transmitted was not the theological treatise Lorgar Aurelian had written in the *Lectitio Divinitatus*—Lorgar had reasoned the Emperor was a god through philosophical argument, and the Emperor had publicly humiliated him for it. Keeler did not reason. She saw. She saw a light burn through indescribable darkness. The power of eyewitness let her voice pierce the rational walls the Imperial Truth had erected.

Her final fate is unknown. Some say she died in the chaos. Some say she was erased from history in a later purge by the Inquisition. But her influence never vanished. The Ecclesiarchy later canonised her as the first Living Saint—the first manifestation of the Emperor's will in a mortal. The holy fire she lit still burns, ten thousand years later, in every Battle Sister, every confessor, every Imperial subject kneeling in prayer before the statues of the Emperor.

## Sebastian Thor — Confessor of the Age of Apostasy

### Overview

Sebastian Thor, a wandering preacher of the 36th Millennium, a man in sackcloth who stood against the bloodiest tyranny in Imperial history—the Age of Apostasy—and ultimately brought down the Ecclesiarchal tyrant Goge Vandire. If Keeler was the first spark of Imperial faith, Thor was the man who, in the darkest hour when faith had been trampled to ash by its own guardians, gathered it back together, one ember at a time. Without him, the Ecclesiarchy might have destroyed itself in civil war and self-corruption.

### Deeds

The Imperium of the 36th Millennium faced a catastrophe that had nothing to do with Chaos or xenos. Three thousand years after the Horus Heresy, the Ecclesiarchy had mutated from a faith institution centred on the worship of the Emperor into a monstrous military-political-economic hybrid. It owned private armies, independent worlds, and a vast treasury outside the Imperial bureaucracy—and the man controlling all of it was Goge Vandire, supreme leader of the Ecclesiarchy and self-appointed High Lord.

Vandire did not deserve the name of believer. He was a political predator in red robes. Through conspiracy, assassination, and coup, he seized the Ecclesiarchy's highest office and then simultaneously grabbed multiple seats on the High Lords' Council. In a window of time that still stupefies historians, this one man controlled, near-simultaneously, the Imperium's religious apparatus, its administrative machinery, and its military resources. He then launched the terror purge called the Reign of Blood: anyone who questioned him—High Lord, Chapter Master of Space Marines, or Planetary Governor—was executed by his private army. For millennia, the Imperium had fought external enemies. Vandire made it start eating its own flesh.

The one who rose amid this catastrophe was no Primarch, no Astartes Chapter Master, not even anyone of note within the Imperium's power structure. It was a confessor named Sebastian Thor—the lowest tier of itinerant preacher in the Ecclesiarchy, dressed in rough cloth robes, walking barefoot across the muddy surfaces of planets, telling miners and farmers stories of the Emperor. That was Thor: no army, not a single soldier under his command; no wealth, everything he owned fit in one bundle; no high holy title, the only thing he could be called was "Confessor Thor."

But he had a mouth that could speak.

Thor began to preach. What he said bore no resemblance to standard Ecclesiarchal doctrine—he spoke not of obedience, not of tithes, not of Imperial glory. What he said was this: Vandire is not the Emperor's voice. Vandire is a thief. The Emperor has sat in silence upon the Golden Throne for ten thousand years—not so His bishops could slaughter His people in His name. If you truly believe in the Emperor, then stand up. Now.

To the ears of the Ecclesiarchy's power core, these were the ravings of a madman. But in the Imperium's lowest hive cities, mining colonies, and agri-worlds, they spread like wildfire. People who had suffered enough of Vandire's bloody rule began to flock to Thor's banner—not soldiers, but miners, farmers, labourers, refugees. Thor led this rabble of believers from world to world, their numbers swelling from hundreds to thousands, tens of thousands, hundreds of thousands—and at last, as his host neared Terra, entire Imperial fleet detachments began to mutiny and defect.

Vandire descended into his final madness within the Imperial Palace. His personal guard—the Daughters of the Emperor, an elite force of fanatical warrior women—still served him. Vandire ordered them to slaughter uncounted numbers of Terran civilians, hoping to quell the uprising with terror.

Then the decisive moment arrived. The Captain-General of the Adeptus Custodes could no longer stand by, yet was shackled by the ten-thousand-year prohibition against Custodian interference in politics. So he did something exquisitely subtle: he brought the Daughters' leader, Alicia Dominica, and her lieutenants into the deepest sanctum of the Imperial Palace—the Golden Throne Room. In ten thousand years, only Custodians and a vanishingly small number of others had ever set foot there. What Dominica saw within, she never fully described. But when she emerged, her face had changed. She led her warriors back to Vandire's palace, drew her blade, and took Vandire's head.

Thor's tide of believers flooded into Terra. He walked into the hall where Vandire had once sat and did not take that seat. He did something the Imperial bureaucracy had not seen in ten thousand years: he announced comprehensive religious reform. He disbanded the Ecclesiarchy's private armies—thus giving rise to the famous Decree Passive, forbidding the Ecclesiarchy from ever retaining "men under arms." The Adepta Sororitas survived legally because they were entirely female; this was no loophole, but the design of the Emperor. He purged thousands of corrupt bishops from Vandire's system and re-anchored the Ecclesiarchy in faith, not power. He personally refused, for his entire life, any title beyond "Confessor."

Thor died a natural death. He did not become a saint. He left no miracles behind. He did not live forever in the Warp. He was simply a mortal standing in the right place at the right time. And that, precisely, is what constitutes his greatest strength: Keeler proved the Emperor could be a god. Thor proved the people who believe in that god must not become devils.

## Alicia Dominica — Blade of the Emperor

### Overview

Alicia Dominica, founding leader of the Daughters of the Emperor, the first patron saint of the Adepta Sororitas. Her story interlocks with Thor's, but what she contributed to Imperial faith was something entirely different: not preaching, not confession. One blade.

### Deeds

Dominica was, at the start, the person Vandire trusted most. During the Age of Apostasy, Vandire, knowing he had made endless enemies, needed a guard that was absolutely loyal, could never betray, and knew no fear. He scoured the Imperium's most remote worlds and gathered a group of women—widows who had lost husbands and sons to war, orphans from the hive bottoms who had lost all reason to live, people with nothing left in them except burning faith. Vandire soaked them in the theology of the Ecclesiarchy: you are not soldiers. You are the "Brides of the Emperor." The sole purpose of your existence is to protect the Emperor's voice in the mortal realm—which is me.

These women believed him. And Dominica was the strongest, the most devout among them.

She killed countless people on Vandire's behalf. High Lords, planetary governors, even Space Marine commanders marked as "heretics" by Vandire—Dominica struck without hesitation. Not cruelty. She believed too completely. In her understanding, Vandire was the Emperor's mouthpiece in the mortal world. To question Vandire was to question the Emperor. You cannot reason with someone who truly believes. Or rather, you can only shatter the illusion before her eyes with a higher order of truth.

When Thor's host reached the gates of Terra, Dominica arrayed the Daughters of the Emperor outside the Palace. She was prepared to shed her last drop of blood for Vandire. At that moment, the Custodians appeared. They did not attack her. They simply said: come with us.

The Custodians led Dominica and her five lieutenants into the heart of the Palace. The full details remain sealed by the Ecclesiarchy to this day. But piecing together all sources confirms this: they were brought before the Golden Throne. The Emperor—the broken, half-dead figure who had not spoken in ten thousand years—communicated directly with Dominica. Not a sign. Not an oracle. Not a vision in fire. A direct infusion of will. The Emperor showed her everything: Vandire's lies, Vandire's massacres, every atrocity Vandire had committed in the Emperor's name. And then the Emperor gave her an order.

The moment Dominica stepped out of the Throne Room, she was no longer a "Bride of the Emperor." She was the blade of the Emperor.

She led her sisters through the Palace corridors. No one stopped them—the Custodians had cleared the way. She entered Vandire's bedchamber. Vandire saw her eyes and, as the hagiographies enjoy recounting, soiled himself on the spot. Dominica said not a word. She swung her sword and took Vandire's head.

That single sword-stroke defined every tradition of the Adepta Sororitas that followed. Dominica and her sisters did not disband; they were legitimised under Thor's reforms as the one armed branch the Ecclesiarchy was permitted to retain—the Sisters of Battle. The Decree forbade "men under arms." The Sisterhood was entirely female and therefore legal. This was no accident of coincidence but the laying-down of the Emperor's will.

Dominica was martyred in a later campaign. The Ecclesiarchy canonised her as Saint Alicia the Protector, eternal guardian of the Sisters of Battle. But the hagiographies almost never mention the most critical truth: she became a saint not because she was pious, but because the Emperor was willing to see her. Across ten thousand years, the number of beings the Emperor has actively communicated with can be counted on a few hands. And this woman—once blinded by lies, once a killer for a tyrant—the Emperor did not give up on her.

## Verdict

These three are gathered in one account for what they share: all are mortal. All were thrust onto the stage of history in the moment the Imperium most needed faith. None chose to become legend.

Keeler's story is the origin point of Imperial faith. A mortal, in terror, inadvertently triggered the Emperor's divinity. Her miracle was unplanned, unpremeditated, unrepeatable. But as a witness, she proved one thing: faith is not Lorgar's theological argument. Faith is a true light that burned through the darkness.

Thor's story is Imperial faith's self-correcting mechanism. When the religious institution had rotted to its core, the lowest-ranked preacher, armed with pure moral courage, gathered the fury of the entire Imperial underclass and toppled the tyrant who committed the devil's work in the Emperor's name. He proved that the power of religion to purify itself lies not in sceptre and crown—but in the human heart.

Dominica's story is the sharpest edge of Imperial faith. A fanatic fed on lies, the moment she saw her God with her own eyes, became His most lethal weapon. She represents the most direct, most violent method of the Emperor's will: you are wrong. I am telling you you are wrong. Go and correct it now. You are forgiven because you can still be used.

The three divide between them the three wellsprings of Imperial faith: Grace—Keeler, light descending from above. Penitence—Thor, light born from the human heart. Judgement—Dominica, light delivered by the sword. And the shared undertone of all three paths is the truth that has burned in silence upon the Golden Throne for ten thousand years: the Emperor never wanted to become a god, but He is becoming one. Three mortals—a remembrancer, a confessor, a warrior woman—are the witnesses He would not acknowledge but cannot erase.

When Saint Celestine spread her golden wings in the 42nd Millennium and swept across the skies of the Imperium Nihilus, the road beneath her feet was paved by these three.
