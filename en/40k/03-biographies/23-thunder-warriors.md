# 23 · Taranis — Last of the Thunder Warriors

## Overview

The Thunder Warriors were the first generation of gene-enhanced warriors the Emperor created—before the Primarchs, before the Legiones Astartes. They were larger, stronger, and more savage than the Space Marines who would follow. They were the true architects of the Unification Wars—without them, the Emperor could not have even walked out of His gene-laboratories beneath the Himalayas, let alone retake Terra from the techno-barbarian warlords.

But the Thunder Warriors had one fatal flaw: genetic instability. Their bodies and minds would both collapse within a short span—muscle growth spiralling out of control, organs failing, minds consumed by rage. The Emperor had never intended for them to live long. They were designed as disposable weapons. When the Unification Wars ended and the Emperor required a more stable, more enduring breed of warrior suited to the conquest of the galaxy, the Thunder Warriors' fate was already sealed.

Taranis is the only Thunder Warrior whose name survives in any record, and the living embodiment of all their fates. He survived—but he lived through a betrayal crueller than death on any battlefield.

## Origin

The Thunder Warriors were the product of the Emperor's earliest gene-craft, forged in the subterranean laboratoria beneath the Himalayan Mountains during the Age of Strife. Unlike the Astartes who would follow—recruited as adolescents and transformed over years through organ implantation and hypno-indoctrination—the Thunder Warriors were adults subjected to a far cruder, far more violent process: direct injection of the Emperor's gene-enhancement compounds. Their muscles swelled within days to the point where a Thunder Warrior could tear a man in power armour apart with bare hands. Bone density was reinforced to withstand direct hits from anti-materiel weapons. Hearts pumped combat-stimulants and adrenaline compounds that gave them reaction speeds beyond any unmodified human. The cost of this process was a mortality rate that killed most subjects outright. Those who survived had, at most, a few decades of life—some only a few years. The Emperor did not care. He needed a tool to conquer Terra within a fixed window of time, not a legion to pass down through the ages.

Taranis was among the finest of these creations. He was not a supreme commander of any particular campaign—rather, he was the type who survived, who watched his brothers fall in droves to genetic collapse and battlefield carnage, yet kept standing, kept obeying.

## Deeds

### The Unification of Terra

In the early years of M30, Terra was a hell more terrible than any xenos world. The Age of Strife had shattered humanity's cradle into countless warlord domains. Techno-barbarians—such as Cardinal Tang, the gene-warlord of the Urals; the cult of the "Unspeakables" along the Pacific coast; the irradiated raider tribes of the equatorial zone—ruled over the wreckage of human civilisation. These warlords wielded surviving weapons from the Dark Age of Technology, gene-sculpted slave armies, and even certain unnameable xenos or Chaos-derived technologies.

When the Emperor emerged from His laboratories beneath the Himalayas, He had only three things: His own psychic power, the first handful of Custodians, and the Thunder Warriors.

The Thunder Warriors did what they were made to do. From the Himalayas eastward to the Pacific, westward across the Eurasian heartland, south to swallow the scorched-earth empires of Africa, north to exterminate the mutant tribes beneath the Arctic ice—they were the battering ram that broke every fortress. They needed no complex tactics; the Thunder Warrior himself was the siege engine. They charged into enemy strongholds, tore heavy defence turrets apart with their bare hands, ripped the heads from warlords and hung them from their chests as they kept charging forward. The Emperor directed from the rear with His Custodians; the Thunder Warriors at the front bought victory with mountains of their own dead. Every battle saw waves of Thunder Warriors fall to genetic collapse or violent overexertion, but the Emperor could always make more.

And they won. The Unification Wars ended. Terra was unified. The Emperor ascended to supreme authority over mankind.

### The Purge at Mount Ararat

After the Unification Wars, the Emperor signed the Treaty of Mars with the Mechanicum, gaining access to the technology and industrial capacity to produce true Space Marines—the Legiones Astartes. He no longer needed the Thunder Warriors. These genetic time bombs, kept too close, would become nothing but a destabilising liability—they were already exhibiting mass mental breakdowns and physical degeneration, some even lashing out indiscriminately due to cerebral degradation.

The Emperor's plan was cold and clean: eliminate all Thunder Warriors. Erase them as a redacted chapter of history.

The final purge took place at Mount Ararat on Terra. Under the guise of a "Final Muster and Honours Parade," the Emperor summoned all surviving Thunder Warriors to assemble. When they were arrayed in formation, awaiting the Emperor's commendation, what appeared on the ridgeline were not honours but Custodians and the first generation of Space Marines. The Emperor had never given the Thunder Warriors a Legion numeral, a designation, a name. Now these new arrivals—more stable, more refined, the "Astartes"—had replaced them.

The slaughter began. The Thunder Warriors fought back with everything they had. Even in their degraded state, their individual combat power still exceeded that of most Space Marines—a dying Thunder Warrior's last blow could punch clean through a ceramite chestplate. But the numbers had never been equal, and combined with the Custodians and the Sisters of Silence, the Thunder Warriors were cut down from the base of the mountain to its peak. Their blood painted the entire western slope of Mount Ararat red.

There were almost no survivors.

Taranis was among the very few who lived. Not by luck—by having smelled the scent of betrayal before his brothers did. While the other Thunder Warriors still stood proudly in formation, he had already drifted to the edges. When the killing began, he crawled out from beneath the dead and vanished into the depths of Terra's ruins.

### Long Concealment and Futile Vengeance

For decades thereafter, Taranis hid in Terra's underbelly. His body was visibly disintegrating—scaly keratinous growths spreading across his skin, organs failing and rallying in cycles, his mind intermittently drowned in berserker fury. Around him gathered a handful of other Thunder Warrior remnants who had likewise escaped the purge. They lived in sewers, abandoned air-raid shelters, the ancient tunnel systems running directly beneath the Imperial Palace itself. The irony was exquisite: the Emperor's deepest secrets lay no more than a few kilometres above their heads, and the Emperor had no idea they were still alive.

Taranis attempted to organise a revenge. He gathered every Thunder Warrior remnant he could find in the deep places and even made contact with certain Terran native factions who harboured grievances against the Emperor's rule. The plan had a single objective—not to overthrow the Imperium, which was impossible—but to punch a hole in the Emperor's triumphal history. To make it known that the Thunder Warriors had existed. That there had been a betrayal.

When the Siege of Terra came during the Horus Heresy, Taranis saw his chance. As the Traitor Legions pounded the surface and the Emperor's defences were stretched to breaking point, Taranis led his last Thunder Warriors in a sudden uprising, attempting to tear the Imperial defence line apart from the rear. Their assault was ferocious beyond measure—even the Space Marines were stunned by the primordial savagery of it.

But the vengeance failed. The Thunder Warriors were too few, too ill-equipped, their genetic collapse too far advanced. Taranis disappeared again into the fire and rubble. This time, no record survives of whether he died or continued to live as a failed avenger. All traces of Taranis after the Siege of Terra go cold.

The only certainty: no one has ever seen Thunder Warriors in significant numbers again.

## Fate & Present Status

The Thunder Warriors are officially deemed extinct—their very existence systematically expunged from Imperial records. Taranis' fate after the Siege of Terra is unknown. A handful of fragmented legends, preserved in the deepest oral traditions of Terran underhive gangers, speak of a giant who still walks the lowest levels, a broken king of a dead brotherhood. The Imperium's official histories acknowledge no such figures. The Thunder Warriors are, in the eyes of the Imperium, a chapter that was never written.

## Verdict

The Thunder Warriors were the true architects of the Emperor's unification of Terra—and the Imperium's earliest, dirtiest secret.

To understand this tragedy, one must see the Emperor's logic for what it was. The Emperor's construction of the Imperium was, from beginning to end, an engineering project that did not count costs: everything was a tool, everything had a service life. The Thunder Warriors' design specification contained no parameter for "longevity." They were a weapon system meant to be expended. Once the Astartes blueprint had proven its viability—can fight, can live, can reproduce gene-seed, can follow orders—the old weapon was to be retired. In the Emperor's mind, disposing of an obsolete military asset was no different from dismantling a worn-out machine.

But this logic overlooked the most basic fact: the Thunder Warriors were human beings. They had names. They had memories. They had brothers with whom they had shed blood. They had a hunger to be recognised. They fought the dirtiest war in Terra's hell on the Emperor's behalf, purchased the birth of the Imperium with the lives of most of their kind. When they formed their ranks on Mount Ararat, what awaited them was not medals, not honourable discharge, not even "you may rest now." What awaited them was slaughter.

The Emperor erased every trace of evidence that they had ever existed. Official Imperial history has never acknowledged any formation called the "Thunder Warriors." The legend of the Unification of Terra stars, always, the Emperor Himself—and the Space Marines who came after. The men who died in the most brutal unification war in human history did not even leave their names behind.

Taranis is the final footnote of this tragedy. He survived—survived as a broken blade embedded in the foundations of the Imperial edifice. He did not win, but he was never quite killed. He has simply always been there, a living historical correction: the Imperium was not built upon nobility. Its first brick was mortared with the blood of its own.

Perhaps that is why the Emperor never looked back at what He had done. Because if He looked back just once, He would see the faces of the men He had killed on the western slope of Mount Ararat.
