# 12 · Champions of Chaos

> The four Chaos Gods each keep their claws sharp. The sharpest among them are not the Primarchs, not mortal generals — but these four. Khârn slaughtered his brothers on an ice field. Ahriman cursed his entire Legion with the Rubric of red dust. Typhus offered his own gene-father to the Garden of Rot. Lucius wears the faces of every enemy who ever bested him. Each of these four, by a single act of rupture, reshaped ten thousand years of war. Thus they are gathered here, examined together.

---

## Khârn the Betrayer

### Overview

Khârn is the Eighth Captain of the World Eaters Legion, Khorne's most favoured mortal warrior, and the purest killing instrument in the galaxy. His epithet "the Betrayer" derives from a single infamous atrocity — on the ice fields of Skalathrax, he butchered his own World Eaters alongside the Emperor's Children. But what makes this figure compelling is the other side of him — the man he was before the fall.

### Origin: Before the Fall

Before the Twelfth Legion became the frothing madness it is today, Khârn was known throughout the Legion as the "normal one." The World Eaters' gene-seed, combined with Angron's Butcher's Nails, left every warrior in a state of near-explosive fury. To serve as a captain in such a place was not a matter of being the best fighter — everyone could fight. It was a matter of being able to hold onto a stretch of lucidity amid the Nails' relentless drone. Khârn could do that. He was even regarded by other companies as "an officer you could reason with." The older lore contains a classic line to the effect that "Khârn was a really nice guy" — this is not sarcasm. In an environment where the entire Legion had gone insane, he genuinely was.

His relationship with Angron also merits attention. Angron hated everything, but Khârn was one of the few subordinates who could communicate normally with the Primarch. He understood Angron's pain, and he understood what the Emperor had done to this Legion.

### Skalathrax and the Name of Betrayer

In the latter stages of the Horus Heresy, the World Eaters had degenerated into indiscriminate killing machines. On the ice fields of the planet Skalathrax, the World Eaters and the Emperor's Children were fighting a civil war over the division of spoils — two Traitor Legions tearing into each other. Halfway through the battle, in the midst of a blizzard, Khârn posed a simple but devastating question: if it is so cold that no one can hold a weapon, why keep fighting?

He was the first to pick up a flamer and begin indiscriminately torching the shelters and tents of both World Eaters and Emperor's Children. In that frozen night, whatever Legion you belonged to, whatever colour your armour — those who stood before him died beneath his axe. When the sun rose, those who remained had either fled back to their ships or frozen solid on the ice.

From that moment, his title changed from "Captain of the Eighth Company" to "the Betrayer." And that night, more than any other, elevated him above all other warriors in Khorne's eyes — not for the number he killed, but because he did not even leave himself a fallback.

### Present Status

Today, Khârn is Khorne's most beloved mortal champion.

> Blood for the Blood God! Skulls for the Skull Throne!

His chainaxe Gorechild — Angron's own old axe, a weapon the Primarch himself could no longer wield, now swung one-handed by Khârn wherever he goes — has shattered the skulls of Imperium and xenos alike beyond counting. Every skull added to the Brazen Throne moves Khârn one step closer to the pure concept of slaughter. He has not ascended to daemonhood, and he does not need to — he is already pure enough.

## Ahzek Ahriman — Master of the Rubric

### Overview

Ahriman is the Chief Librarian of the Thousand Sons Legion, one of the most powerful mortal psykers in the galaxy, and Tzeentch's most brilliant champion. He created a spell — the Rubric — originally intended to save his Legion forever. And "forever" it was: the majority of the Thousand Sons were transformed into suits of power armour filled with dust, un-living, un-dying, un-thinking.

### The Flesh-Change and the Spell of Salvation

The Thousand Sons Legion was born with a genetic curse called the Flesh-change — warriors would suffer uncontrollable physical mutation, eventually transforming into mindless monsters. The problem grew worse throughout the Great Crusade. After the Burning of Prospero, Magnus the Red brought the Thousand Sons to the Planet of the Sorcerers, deep within Tzeentch's domain, and on that Chaos-saturated world the Flesh-change began to accelerate at a terrifying rate. The entire Legion faced extinction.

Ahriman, together with his most trusted cabal of Librarians, secretly formulated a plan. He did not tell Magnus — he knew Magnus would stop him. The plan's core was a grand spell: in a single working, bind the entire Legion's genetic and psychic essence, sealing away the possibility of the Flesh-change forever. He succeeded. The instant the spell took effect, the physical mutation of every psychic warrior in the Legion was locked in perpetuity.

The price: warriors with weak psychic ability were drained dry by the spell itself, their flesh reduced to fine dust, their residual souls sealed forever inside their power armour — becoming the Rubric Marines, hollow automata capable of only the simplest commands, unable to speak. The strong psykers preserved themselves, but in the same moment that the Flesh-change was sealed, every one of them was branded with Tzeentch's irrevocable mark.

When Magnus learned of this, his fury was absolute; he would have torn Ahriman apart. Tzeentch intervened — true to form, the Changer of Ways had been waiting for precisely this moment.

### The Eternal Quest

Ahriman's fate ever since has revolved around a single theme: find a way to undo the Rubric. He has delved into countless forbidden grimoires, sought entry to the Black Library (the repository of knowledge controlled by the Aeldari Harlequins — he remains barred from it to this day), and crossed timelines to peer into parallel universes. Every time he seems close to an answer, he discovers that the answer itself is another layer of the deception — which is exactly why Tzeentch loves him most. A sorcerer forever chasing truth and forever denied it feeds the Changer of Ways more richly than ten thousand kneeling worshippers ever could.

Ahriman is the being most thoroughly toyed with by Tzeentch in the entire galaxy — and for that very reason, the one Tzeentch values most. He does not worship Tzeentch; he hates Tzeentch. But Tzeentch does not care about worship — what Tzeentch wants is the *seeking* itself.

## Typhus, the Herald of the Plague God

### Overview

Typhus is the First Captain of the Death Guard, Nurgle's most faithful herald, and the only one among the four Champions of Chaos who actively sold his own Primarch.

### Half-Human, Half-Xenos

Typhus's relationship with the Death Guard Primarch Mortarion was unusual from the start. Both grew up on the world of Barbarus, both suffered under the oppression of xenos overlords. Mortarion viewed his xenos foster-father as the ultimate curse of his existence; his entire life's driving force was vengeance, resistance, and escape. Typhus was different.

Typhus was half-human, half-xenos. His birth-mother was human; his birth-father was one of the local xenos Overlords of Barbarus — genetically, culturally, he was not purely "one of the oppressed humans." This torn identity taught him from childhood to hide — to pass as an ordinary human, while deep down he had never truly belonged to either side. Mortarion did not know this, or perhaps never thought to ask — Mortarion never delved into the inner lives of his subordinates.

### The Betrayal of Mortarion

During the Horus Heresy, the Death Guard fleet needed to traverse the Warp to reach Terra. Typhus raised Nurgle's plague-banner aboard his flagship, the *Terminus Est*. Using sorcery, he seized control of the entire fleet's navigation and dragged the Death Guard deep into the Plague Garden, Nurgle's domain.

There, Nurgle imprisoned the Legion with the cruelest form of "love": the incurable Destroyer Plague erupted simultaneously in every body. Warriors found their wounds refusing to close, their organs beginning to suppurate, and yet death stubbornly refused to come — for Nurgle's essence is not death, but *rotting eternally*. Soldiers screamed in the agony of undeath; the walls of the ships bloomed with visible rot.

Typhus stood before Mortarion and said: *Surrender. This is the only way out.* Mortarion looked upon the subordinate he had raised for decades and saw a walking hive of pestilence — Typhus's flesh had been reshaped by the Destroyer Plague into the "Destroyer Hive," his abdomen a living nest, every insect that crawled from it a gift of Nurgle.

Mortarion knelt before Nurgle at last. Not out of faith — the Primarch who hated tyrants above all else was imprisoned, tormented, drained of all will within that rotting interstitial space, and surrendered so that his entire Legion would not be driven to madness by unending agony. But Typhus — Typhus was an active participant. In Nurgle's garden, he found his one and only home.

### Nurgle's Herald

Today, Typhus is Nurgle's foremost herald in realspace. Where he walks, visible plagues-storms wreathe his form. He does not need to attack — wherever he stands, Nurgle's garden extends.

## Lucius the Eternal

### Overview

Lucius is the most perfect swordsman of the Emperor's Children Legion, and the bearer of one of Slaanesh's most unique designs: he has been given an ability that ensures no one can truly defeat him — whoever kills Lucius has their soul overwritten by his will, eventually becoming him. The surface of his armour is studded with screaming faces, every single face belonging to someone who once "beat" him.

### The Man on the Path of Perfection

Before the Emperor's Children degenerated into Slaanesh's playthings, Lucius was the one who rose to the very top of the Legion purely on skill. He was born with a fanatical perfectionism — every angle of every cut, the rhythm of his footwork, the cadence of his breathing, all had to be aesthetically beyond reproach. Fulgrim privately considered him the Legion's truly finest swordsman. Fulgrim admitted he himself was stronger — but that was the advantage of a Primarch's physique. On pure technique, Lucius had no equal in the Third Legion.

This extreme narcissistic perfectionism was, in the late Great Crusade, easily transmuted by the corrupting touch of the Laer Blade into a Slaaneshi obsession with sensation. Killing had long since ceased to be a tactical concern for Lucius — it had become an experience. Every duel was a ritual to prove to himself: *I am still the most perfect.* The scars on his face were self-inflicted, because ordinary battle wounds were not "artistic" enough.

### The Curse of Immortality

Slaanesh's ultimate gift — or ultimate punishment — operates as follows: whenever Lucius is slain in a duel, the person who killed him will, in the days that follow, begin to notice Lucius's smile forming on their own armour or skin. Over time, that person's entire consciousness and sense of self is consumed by Lucius's soul; their body collapses and reshapes into Lucius. And a new screaming face appears on the armour.

There is only one way to escape: if you kill Lucius and feel absolutely nothing — no thrill of victory, no rush of conquest, not even the faintest emotional flicker — Slaanesh has nothing to work with. But that is precisely Lucius's trump card: on a battlefield, facing an opponent so perfect, so ritualistically sublime, who could possibly feel nothing?

Lucius continues to wander the galaxy, seeking worthy opponents. He is not courting death — he is verifying that even after ten thousand years, there is still no second soul anywhere in existence who can surpass him at the blade.

## Final Verdict

Four Champions of the Four Gods — four faces, yet their cores share two common threads.

First: all four were, before their fall, the most *essential* figures within their respective Legions — not outcasts. Khârn was the World Eaters' only lucid soul; Ahriman was the pinnacle of the Thousand Sons' intellect; Typhus was the first captain Mortarion trusted from the beginning; Lucius was the superlative endpoint of the Emperor's Children's swordsmanship. Chaos does not turn mediocrity into champions — it specifically demands the best. It was at the very summit of each Legion, at their innermost heart, that Chaos completed its most total inversion.

Second: each one's "immortality" is an exact miniature of their patron god's essential nature. Khârn's immortality is slaughter without end — Khorne is not an endpoint, but an eternal process. Ahriman's immortality is an answer never found — Tzeentch is not the answer, but the eternal cycle of seeking. Typhus's immortality is rot that never dies — Nurgle is not death, but putrescent life. Lucius's immortality is a self forever replaced and reborn — Slaanesh is not satisfaction, but the infinite loop of desire and displacement.

None of the four are driven by power alone. What drives them is deeper than rage or greed: Ahriman — obsession. Typhus — a hunger for belonging in the void. Lucius — the pathological pride of proving himself unsurpassable. Khârn — Khârn wants, in the eternal screech of the Butcher's Nails, to find a single instant of absolute silence, free of pain.

The Four Gods made them into immortal mythic symbols. The price is that all four can never again die as human beings.
