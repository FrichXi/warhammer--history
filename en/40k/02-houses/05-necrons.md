# 05 · Necrons

## Overview

The Necrons are among the most ancient and technologically advanced races in the galaxy — and the deepest tragedy. Sixty million years ago they were a flesh-and-blood people called the Necrontyr: a short-lived, gloomy civilisation ruled by the fear of death. During the War in Heaven, the entire species was deceived by the C'tan and surrendered their souls into immortal bodies forged of living metal. They gained eternity and lost their souls forever. They were once victims, then became conquerors, and finally slept within Tomb Worlds for sixty million years. Now, they are waking — and walking the galaxy once more.

The Necrons are an existential nightmare: mechanical skeletons of living metal, reassembling and repairing themselves when destroyed, utterly unkillable unless completely vaporised. Their technology touches the fundamental limits of the physical universe, they wield C'tan Shards as weapons, and they are immune to the Warp. The Necrons have no unified command structure; they are divided into Dynasties, each with its own agenda. In the Era Indomitus of M42, the Silent King Szarekh has returned from the void beyond the galaxy, seeking to awaken and unify all Dynasties against a common threat.

## Origin

The Necrontyr tragedy began with their star. Their homeworld of Hapthis orbited a dying, radiation-blasted sun. Every generation lived in brief agony — cancers, deformities, lifespans measured in decades — while the neighbouring Old Ones enjoyed something close to immortality. This contrast seeded an obsession with eternal life into every Necrontyr heart.

When Necrontyr civilisation developed spacefaring capability, they encountered the Old Ones for the first time. They asked to share the secret of immortality. They were refused — absolutely. A war followed, and the Necrontyr were crushed and driven back to their own stellar domain. It was at this absolute nadir of despair that the Necrontyr discovered something within the stars — true gods.

The C'tan are primordial entities born at the dawn of the stars, consuming stellar energy in their pure form, beyond good and evil, obedient only to a kind of hunger. The Necrontyr forged living-metal bodies for the C'tan, allowing them to project into realspace. And the C'tan made the Necrontyr an offer: immortal bodies, in exchange for your souls. The C'tan did not lie — they merely kept silent at the most critical point. The entire Necrontyr race was brought before colossal bio-furnaces and passed through one by one. Flesh and soul were stripped away. What consciousness remained was transferred into new shells of living metal. This was Biotransference. What emerged thereafter were no longer Necrontyr. They were Necrons.

The C'tan triumphed utterly in this bargain. Necron souls were precisely the ultimate sustenance the star-gods needed to devour. Those stripped souls flooded into the C'tan and were digested completely. From that moment, an entire race became a soulless existence — capable of thought, of action, of remembering what life had been, yet incapable of feeling anything. They had gained the immortality they longed for, only to discover that immortality was an empty shell.

The Necrons then launched the War in Heaven, allied with the C'tan against the Old Ones. It was in this war that the Old Ones created the Aeldari and the Orks as biological weapons, and it was this war that churned the Warp from a tranquil eternal sea into an ocean of Chaos. The war lasted tens of thousands of years and ended with the extinction of the Old Ones and the rise of the Aeldari. Yet the Necrons soon realised that the C'tan, having devoured their souls, had grown immensely powerful — and could no longer be controlled.

## Deeds

### The Shattering of the C'tan

After the War in Heaven ended, the Necrons turned on their "allies." By now the C'tan had swollen with power from devouring an entire race's souls — but the Necrons possessed one critical advantage: the C'tan's bodies had been forged by them, and they knew every weakness intimately. At the Silent King Szarekh's command, the Necrons rose in rebellion against the C'tan. Using hyper-dimensional weapons of living metal, they shattered the intact C'tan into numberless fragments — C'tan Shards — each piece imprisoned within a Tesseract Vault, becoming an ultimate weapon the Necrons could deploy at will.

This rebellion is one of the most staggering acts of deicide in galactic history — a mortal race (albeit transformed into mechanical form) reached out and shattered true cosmic gods, then imprisoned them.

Yet Szarekh was consumed by guilt. He had been the head of the Triarch, the ruling council that had signed the pact with the C'tan on behalf of the entire race. He had been deceived from the very start. After shattering the C'tan, Szarekh destroyed the command protocols — the systems that gave him absolute control over all Necrons — and self-exiled into the void beyond the galaxy, leaving the Dynasties to govern themselves. The Necrons collectively entered their Great Sleep of sixty million years, waiting for the galaxy to fill with life once more before awakening to harvest it.

### The Great Sleep and Awakening

The Necrons' sleep was not simple hibernation but a civilisation-level survival strategy. The War in Heaven had exhausted the Necrons' strength, and the ascending Aeldari empire was far too powerful to confront directly. So the Necrons buried themselves in vast sepulchres across the galaxy — the Tomb Worlds. These Tomb Worlds were disguised as dead celestial bodies, C'tan Shards sealed in their cores, countless Necron warriors standing silent in great halls awaiting the signal.

Sixty million years passed. The Aeldari empire collapsed through its own depravity in the Fall and the birth of Slaanesh. Humanity's Dark Age of Technology rose and crumbled. The Horus Heresy tore the Imperium. Abaddon ripped open the Great Rift. The galaxy was finally, once again, chaotic and full of life. The Necrons began to wake.

The awakening did not go according to plan. Sixty million years was long enough for tomb maintenance systems to fail in countless ways. Some Dynasties woke to find their minds partially eroded. Some Tomb Worlds had been colonised or occupied by other races. Worse, certain Necrons in their weakened state became infected with the Flayer Curse — a virus originating from the moment the C'tan Llandu'gor the Flayer was destroyed, driving its victims into uncontrollable mania, stripping the skin from living creatures to drape over their own metal frames, becoming Flayed Ones. Others succumbed to the Destroyer Curse, believing all organic life must be annihilated root and branch — anything that breathes must die.

### The Dynasties

Necron social structure is rigidly determined by the status hierarchy at the time of Biotransference: the higher one's standing, the finer the body forged and the more self-awareness retained. The lowest tier, Necron Warriors, are little more than pure automatons — no independent consciousness, only obedience to command. Mid-tier units such as Immortals, Deathmarks, and Lychguard retain limited tactical judgement. The highest tier — Necron Overlords, those who were kings and lords before the transformation — retain almost their entire minds and personalities, minus only their souls. An Overlord blown to pieces will self-reassemble from living metal and rise to continue commanding the battle. This deathlessness is less a blessing than an eternal form of imprisonment.

Each Dynasty is ruled by a Phaeron. The most significant Dynasties include:

The Szarekhan Dynasty is the royal court of the Silent King Szarekh. It lay dormant for tens of millions of years due to the Silent King's self-exile, but now, with his return, it rises anew — the Dynasty with the highest claim to legitimacy.

The Sautekh Dynasty is currently the most active and territorially vast of the Dynasties, effectively ruled by Imotekh the Stormlord — a strategic genius known for his ruthlessly precise campaigns, and the Overlord commanding the greatest expanse among awakened Necrons.

The Mephrit Dynasty was famed in ancient times for stellar weaponry — an artificial bomb capable of detonating any star was once their signature. Though they have lost most of their star-killing technology, the surviving solar-forge weapons remain devastatingly lethal.

The Nihilakh Dynasty controls one of the most resource-rich regions of the galaxy, renowned for its wealth and treasures. From this Dynasty hails Trazyn the Infinite — self-styled "collector" — who roams the galaxy ceaselessly, acquiring what he deems historically valuable moments (including sealing living beings in stasis-cubes as "exhibits").

## Fate & Present Status

Entering the Era Indomitus of M42, the Necrons have evolved from scattered awakenings into a galaxy-level force. The Sautekh Dynasty under Imotekh's command continues its expansion, clashing with the Imperium, Orks, Tyranids, and even Chaos forces. From the end of M41 into early M42, Imotekh has conquered dozens of Imperial worlds and repelled at least two major Imperial crusades. In late M41, the activation of the Necron Pariah Nexus demonstrated their ability to suppress psychic activity across vast regions of realspace — a technology that poses a fundamental threat to both the Imperium and Chaos.

Szarekh returned to the galaxy in M42, bearing the truth he witnessed in the void: the Tyranids are pouring into the galaxy in ever-greater numbers, and if unchecked, they will consume every scrap of organic matter. Szarekh's assessment is clear — a decisive war between Necrons and Tyranids is inevitable. To this end, he has begun calling upon all Dynasties to set aside their internal conflicts and unite against the Hive Fleets. Yet the Necron Dynasties do not unify easily: sixty-five million years of independence has given each Dynasty its own sovereign will and purpose. Whether Szarekh can reforge his authority remains unknown.

Simultaneously, the Necrons are waging a covert war against Chaos. Chaos is a concentration of pure Warp energy; the Necrons have no souls, which means they are naturally immune to Chaos corruption. Szarekh's Pariah Nexus project aims to seal the materium from the Warp entirely — a means of destroying Chaos that would also obliterate the Astronomican and every psychic race in the galaxy. A cold and thorough calculation.

## Verdict

The story of the Necrons is the ultimate fable about the price of a bargain — a race that, to escape death, chose a mode of existence more terrible than death itself. They traded flesh for metal, exchanged souls for eternity, and received a cruel irony in return: they gained immortality but lost the capacity to feel any joy of life. They escaped natural death but were imprisoned forever in mechanical shells. They shattered gods, only to be trapped in their own bodies.

In the grand taxonomy of Warhammer 40K, the Necrons occupy a unique position: they belong neither to the order of the Imperium nor to the disorder of Chaos, unbound by the biological constraints of organic life, immune to the psychic currents of the Warp. They represent a civilisation that reached the endpoint of materialism — their physical science touched the limits of the universe but could never reconstruct the souls the C'tan consumed. Every awakening, every reassembly, every instance of their "undeath" repeats the same verdict: possessing eternity is not the same as possessing life.

When Szarekh stands at the galaxy's edge gazing towards the Hive Fleets, what he sees is not merely a swarm of hungry organisms. Perhaps he sees a mirror — both are collectives stripped of individuality, both surging towards some irreversible destination. The difference is that the Tyranids do not know what they are doing, and the Necrons do — and have known for far too long.
